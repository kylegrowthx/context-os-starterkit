# Culture of Documentation

At GrowthX, our Culture of Documentation is a foundational aspect of how we work. We are a fully-remote, asynchronous, and international company, and our ability to thrive relies on **clear, comprehensive, and well-organized knowledge** that is accessible to everyone. Cultivating this culture means each of us actively contributes to capturing, sharing, and maintaining valuable information, ensuring it remains easy to find and use.

---

## Why Documentation is Crucial for Us

### Working Asynchronously at GrowthX

Our asynchronous approach means that we can accomplish our work without the constant need for meetings or real-time communication. Here's why this matters:

- **Efficiency**: With good documentation, we can solve problems or share updates without waiting for synchronous interaction.
- **Freedom**: Team members work in different time zones, and not everyone is online simultaneously. Solid documentation allows us to eliminate blockers, even if someone is unavailable when you need an answer.
- **Scalability**: This is how we function effectively as a remote organization without the constraints of a physical office or fixed working hours.

---

## What Does It Mean to Have a Culture of Documentation?

Having a *Culture of Documentation* means we collectively work towards:

- Ensuring our knowledge base is **useful, tidy, and up to date**.
- Fixing errors whenever they arise.
- Adding missing information as soon as we identify the gap.
- Enabling others to learn and build upon our work by making sure knowledge is captured and accessible.

Documentation is our way of creating a **shared brain**—it eliminates confusion, accelerates progress, and enables us to work more effectively across the globe.

---

## What is Documentation?

**Documentation** is anything written down that others can read, learn from, and expand upon. It might be an in-depth Notion page explaining a workflow, notes from a meeting, or a quick reference guide on a project. Simply put, it's about **writing things down** in a way that others can understand and use.

---

## When Should I Document?

You should document whenever:

- You're working on something that others might need to reference later.
- A process or decision has been made that could impact others.
- You've learned something useful that others can benefit from.

Our goal is to **capture the knowledge** that will help others understand what we did, why we did it, and how they can build on it.

---

## Single Source of Truth (SSoT)

### What is SSoT and Why Does It Matter?

We work with various tools like Google Drive, Slack, Notion, and others, but it is important to have **one designated place** as the **Single Source of Truth (SSoT)** for our most crucial data. At GrowthX, our SSoT is **Notion**. Here's why we chose it:

- **Availability & Consistency**: Everyone at GrowthX has access to Notion, and they know that it is the most up-to-date and consistent repository of information.
- **Quality Assurance**: Revision control helps ensure that our data remains accurate, tidy, and easy to follow.
- **Extensibility**: Notion's data can easily be connected or exported to other systems.
- **Built-in AI**: Notion AI is available to help everyone easily create, edit, and improve documentation.

We use Notion to avoid scattering our information across multiple platforms. While we may work with other tools for specific needs, **Notion remains our primary hub for documentation**.

---

## Who Owns Documentation?

### Ownership and Maintenance

- **Page Owners**: Every page in Notion has an assigned owner responsible for keeping it accurate and part of the current workflow. Owners can change over time, but their role is to ensure documentation is always up to date.
- **Department Ownership**: Ultimately, documentation is owned by the department or subject matter experts (SMEs) where it originates. If there are errors, **those empowered to edit should fix them**.
- **Contribution**: If you aren't empowered to edit a page but notice something that needs improvement, **let the page owner know** so they can address it.

If you need to create new documentation, **first check** if a page on the topic already exists. If it does, update it. If not, consider starting a new document, ensuring that it aligns with our standards for clarity and accessibility.

## Best Practices for Documentation

- **Be Clear and Concise**: Make sure your documentation can be easily understood by anyone reading it. Avoid jargon when possible and provide context for any complex concepts.
- **Add Value**: Only document what is valuable. Documentation should help others move forward more effectively, not create unnecessary noise.
- **Keep It Updated**: Stale information can be misleading. If you come across outdated documentation, either update it yourself or inform the relevant owner.
- **Organize Wisely**: Documentation is only as good as its accessibility. Structure information logically and link related documents to make it easier to navigate.
- **Include Dates**: Add timestamps or revision notes where applicable so everyone knows how recent and reliable the information is.

## Benefits of a Documentation Culture

- **Reduces Dependency**: Team members are less reliant on each other for day-to-day answers because the information they need is readily available.
- **Accelerates Onboarding**: New team members can get up to speed faster by accessing detailed documentation, reducing the need for repetitive questions.
- **Enhances Collaboration**: A shared knowledge base facilitates better collaboration. Everyone has the same access to key information, reducing misunderstandings.

The more we invest in our culture of documentation, the better we all become at our work. Let's work together to keep our knowledge accessible, accurate, and evergreen.
