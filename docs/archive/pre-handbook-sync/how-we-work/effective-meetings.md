# Effective Meetings

## Introduction

Meetings are an important part of our work culture, but they need to be intentional and effective to add value. As highlighted in the *Art of Gathering*, "gathering well isn't a chill activity."

A good meeting organizer has a clear purpose, ensures the right people are present, and is mindful of meeting dynamics. Below are best practices for organizing and leading effective meetings at GrowthX.

### Asynchronous First, Synchronous When Needed

Default to **asynchronous communication** and use meetings only when there is a clear need to transform rather than transmit information. Ask yourself: "Does this really need to be a meeting?" If not, opt for async solutions like shared documents or collaborative platforms.

However, for project kick-offs, complex problem-solving, or team bonding, meetings are still invaluable. Just remember to prepare well, respect participants' time, and ensure every meeting adds value.

---

## Before the Meeting

### 1. Decide if the Meeting is Necessary

Only schedule or attend a meeting if:
- You need to **teach or learn** something crucial.
- You need **critical input**, such as brainstorming or decision-making.
- You want to **build a social connection** or achieve something that cannot be done asynchronously.

If possible, consider if you could handle the subject using asynchronous communication like Notion or Slack.

### 2. Create a Detailed Google Calendar Invite

A meeting invitation should include:
- **Description**: Meeting objective, **agenda** (time-boxed if needed), and any preparation participants need.
- **Participants**: Only invite those who need to be there.
- **Location**: For larger groups, use Google Meet or another suitable virtual room.
- **Date & Time**: Use "Speedy Meetings" in Google Calendar to set meetings for 25 or 50 minutes to allow breathing space between sessions.

### 3. Prepare Content Ahead of Time

Provide materials ahead of the meeting to give participants time to understand and contribute. Examples include:
- **Miro Board** for brainstorming sessions.
- **Notion Document** outlining key points for decision-making.
- **Google Sheet** for financial presentations.

### 4. Limit the Number of Attendees

The more attendees, the less effective the decision-making. Use these guidelines:
- **Decision-making**: Less than 7 people.
- **Brainstorming**: Up to 15-18 people.
- **Information Sharing**: More than 18 people.

### 5. Avoid Unnecessary Exclusion

People don't like missing out. To avoid the feeling of exclusion:
- **Timed-Agenda**: Invite people to the parts of the meeting that are relevant to them.
- **Ordered Agenda**: Share information first, then transition to a smaller discussion.

## During the Meeting

### 6. Start the Meeting Right

Begin with a quick **check-in**. Ask participants to share two words about how they are feeling. This helps establish a connection and energy level. After that, **share the agenda** and **assign roles** like facilitator, note-taker, and time-keeper.

### 7. Stick to the Schedule

Start and end on time. Assign a **time-keeper** to keep everyone aware of how much time is left. If you can't cover all agenda items, set a follow-up plan instead of running over.

### 8. Take Clear Notes

Assign a **note-taker** (not the meeting owner) to document key points:
- **Goals**
- **Decisions Made**
- **Action Items** (with assigned owners)
- **Questions and Answers**

Distribute notes immediately after the meeting.

### 9. Introduce Breaks for Long Meetings

If a meeting runs longer than 1 hour, provide a 5-minute break for every 50-55 minutes.

### 10. Invite Silence for Deeper Thinking

During brainstorming or decision-making, introduce moments of silence for reflection or written input. Techniques include:
- **Brainwriting**: Write down ideas silently on stickies before sharing.
- **Silent Reading**: Read the material together in silence to align everyone.
- **Voting**: Use tools like Mentimeter or Miro for anonymous feedback.

### 11. Own Your Meetings

The meeting owner is responsible for keeping the meeting productive. **Listen actively**, **encourage constructive conflict**, and **call on quieter participants** for input.

### 12. Engage Participants

Combat distractions by making your meetings interactive:
- Start with an **icebreaker**.
- Use **live polls** or ask questions in the chat.
- Acknowledge and highlight participants' input.

### 13. Be an Active Participant

If you are attending, **follow the lead** of the meeting owner, **pay attention**, and **speak up when appropriate** without overplaying politeness.

### 14. Use Non-Verbal Signals

Non-verbal cues are crucial for effective online meetings:
- **Raise Hand**: Indicate a desire to speak.
- **Jazz Hands**: Show agreement.
- **Time-Out Gesture**: Highlight an issue.

### 15. End the Meeting with Summary and Action Steps

Leave the last 5 minutes for **summarizing key takeaways** and **aligning on next steps**. End with a clear closure and make sure to ask for any remaining concerns.

---

## After the Meeting

### 16. Gather Feedback

Meeting owners often overestimate the effectiveness of meetings. Consider running a quick post-meeting survey to gather feedback. A simple three-question survey can do wonders.

### 17. Share Results with Stakeholders

After the meeting, communicate key outcomes with those not present via Slack or Notion. Keep the message concise with bullet points and links to supporting documents.

### 18. Take Time to Review

Take time after the meeting to review notes and outcomes. Block time in your calendar to reflect and adjust future plans as necessary.
