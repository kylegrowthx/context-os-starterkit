# Internal Communication

The average employee loses 46 workdays per year to ineffective communication. Not vacation. Not sick days. Forty-six days spent in meetings that should have been memos, deciphering Slack threads that should have been docs, and re-explaining decisions that were never written down.

Internal writing is a different craft than external writing. External content sells. Internal content *aligns*. It needs to be faster to consume, more transparent about uncertainty, and structured for decision-making. Most teams get this wrong because they treat internal comms like an afterthought. The companies that get it right treat writing as infrastructure.

---

## Why internal writing is different

External writing persuades strangers. Internal writing aligns people who already share context. That difference changes everything about how you write.

Three rules separate good internal writing from bad:

**Speed over polish.** Your reader is a colleague with 47 unread Slack messages and three meetings in the next two hours. They need your point in the first sentence, not the third paragraph. The military calls this [BLUF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BLUF_(communication)): Bottom Line Up Front. State your conclusion, then support it. Never bury the lead.

**Transparency over confidence.** External content projects authority. Internal content should project honesty. If you're 60% sure about a recommendation, say so. "We believe X based on Y, but we haven't validated Z" is more useful than false certainty. Teams make better decisions when they know what's solid and what's a guess.

**Decisions over information.** Every internal doc should answer one question: "So what?" A status update that lists activities without conclusions is just noise. A memo that presents three options without a recommendation is a homework assignment, not a decision tool. Our writing style guide puts it simply: lead with the point.

## The channel decision

The biggest communication mistake isn't bad writing. It's choosing the wrong channel. A strategy discussion in Slack. A quick yes/no question in a meeting. A time-sensitive request buried in a doc nobody checks until Friday.

Here's when to use what:

| Channel | Use when... | Don't use when... |
|---------|------------|-------------------|
| **Slack message** | You need a quick answer (< 2 sentences). You're sharing a link or FYI. You're coordinating logistics. | The topic needs more than two back-and-forth replies. The decision needs to be findable in six months. |
| **Email** | You're communicating with external stakeholders. You need a permanent record. You're sending a formal announcement. | You need a fast response from a teammate. You're brainstorming. |
| **Document** | The topic requires structured thinking. Multiple people need to review or reference it later. You're making a decision that affects the team. | The answer is simple and time-sensitive. Nobody will read it. |
| **Meeting** | You need real-time discussion to resolve ambiguity. The topic involves conflict or nuance that text can't carry. You're building relationships. | You could get the same result with a doc and comments. Only one person is talking. There's no agenda. |

The test is simple. If a Slack thread hits five replies, it should have been a doc. If a meeting has no agenda, it should have been an email. If a doc sits unread for a week, it should have been a Slack message with a deadline.

> **Tip:** **The channel rule of thumb:** Use the slowest channel that still meets the deadline. Slower channels (docs, email) create better records and require more structured thinking. Faster channels (Slack, meetings) are better for ambiguity and quick coordination. Default to slower when you can.

## The memo

Jeff Bezos banned PowerPoint at Amazon in the early 2000s. His reasoning: slides let presenters gloss over gaps in logic. A narrative memo doesn't. "There is no way to write a six-page narratively structured memo and not have clear thinking," he said. That's the core insight. Writing is thinking. If someone can't write a clear memo, they haven't thought clearly about the problem.

Amazon's 6-pager format works like this: every meeting starts with 30 minutes of silent reading. Everyone reads the memo, writes their questions in the margins, and then the group discusses. No pre-reads that nobody does. No presentations where the loudest voice wins. The memo carries the argument.

You don't need to copy Amazon's exact format. But you need a structure. Three frameworks work well for internal memos:

### BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front)

Start with the conclusion. Then provide supporting evidence. The military invented this because operators don't have time to read three pages of context before getting the point. Neither do your colleagues.

**Structure:** State the decision or recommendation in the first sentence. Then explain why. Then address objections or alternatives.

### The Pyramid Principle

Barbara Minto developed this at McKinsey in the 1960s. Start with the answer. Group supporting ideas into logical clusters. Make each cluster mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive (MECE). The reader gets the headline first, then drills into detail only where they need it.

**Structure:** One key message at the top, supported by 2-4 arguments, each supported by evidence. Readers who only read the top level still get the point.

### SCR (Situation-Complication-Resolution)

Another McKinsey framework, useful when you're proposing a change. Describe the current situation. Explain what's wrong or what's changing (the complication). Present your recommended path forward (the resolution).

**Structure:** "Here's where we are. Here's the problem. Here's what I recommend." Three paragraphs. Clear and persuasive.

All three frameworks share one principle: lead with the conclusion. Internal memos are not mystery novels. Don't make your reader wait for the reveal. Put the answer up front and let them decide how deep to go.

## Weekly updates

A good weekly update takes 15 minutes to write and 5 minutes to read. It tells your manager or team what happened, what's coming, and what's blocked. That's it.

The worst weekly updates are what Visier calls "productivity theater." A 2023 survey found that 83% of employees engage in at least one performative work behavior. The update looks busy but says nothing. It lists meetings attended and emails sent, not outcomes produced or problems solved.

**A good weekly update has three sections:**

1. **What shipped.** Concrete outcomes, not activities. "Published the Q4 client report" beats "Worked on Q4 deliverables."
2. **What's next.** The 2-3 things you'll focus on this week. Specific enough that your manager could check next week.
3. **What's blocked.** Anything that needs help, a decision, or someone else's input. This is the most important section. It turns a status report into a coordination tool.

Warren Buffett writes his Berkshire Hathaway shareholder letters as if talking to his sister. Conversational. Specific. Honest about what went wrong. Your weekly update should read the same way. No jargon. No hedging. Just what happened and what you need.

## Strategy docs and decision records

Some decisions are too important for Slack and too complex for a weekly update. They need a dedicated document.

### The one-pager

Condense a complex topic to a single page. The constraint forces clarity. If you can't explain your strategy in one page, you don't understand it yet. One-pagers work well for project proposals, quarterly priorities, and client recommendations.

### The decision record (RFC)

Tech companies call these RFCs (Request for Comments). Google calls them design docs. The format varies, but the purpose is the same: write down *what* you decided, *why* you decided it, and *what alternatives you considered*. Six months from now, when someone asks "Why did we do it this way?", the answer lives in the doc, not in someone's memory.

Amazon takes this further with their PR/FAQ format. Before building anything, the team writes a press release announcing the finished product, plus a FAQ answering likely questions. If the press release doesn't sound compelling, the idea needs more work. It tests the value proposition on paper before anyone writes code.

### Communicating uncertainty

The hardest part of strategy docs is handling what you don't know. Bad docs hide uncertainty behind confident language. Good docs call it out.

A useful format: "We believe [recommendation] because [evidence]. Key risks: [what could go wrong]. What we'd need to see to change course: [conditions]." This gives decision-makers the full picture. It also protects the writer. If the recommendation fails, the risks were documented. If it succeeds despite the risks, the thinking holds up.

## How to apply it at GrowthX

### Client-facing alignment

Before kicking off any new engagement, write a one-pager that answers: What problem are we solving? What does success look like? What's our approach? Share it with the client for sign-off. This prevents the "I thought you were doing X" conversation three weeks in.

Our creating good content guide covers the quality standards for external content. But internal alignment docs matter just as much. A content strategy memo that clearly articulates the "why" behind a keyword list saves hours of revision later.

### Cross-team coordination

GrowthX runs multiple client engagements in parallel. Information gets lost when it lives in Slack threads. The fix: every engagement should have a living doc (strategy brief, running notes, decision log) that any team member can find. When someone new joins an engagement, they read the doc instead of asking five people for context.

### Pipeline and process documentation

Our agentic pipelines guide walks through building AI workflows for clients. The first step is "Gain context." That context needs to be written down. The more rules and guidelines you bake into context artifacts and prompts, the less rework the delivery team faces. In pipeline work, writing is the product.

## Common Traps

### Writing for the file, not the reader

Some memos exist because someone felt they "should" document something. Nobody reads them. Before writing, ask: who will read this, and what do they need to decide or do? If the answer is "nobody" and "nothing," don't write it. Our writing craft guide calls this the "earn-it test." Every paragraph earns its place or gets cut.

### Slack as a documentation system

Slack is a river, not a lake. Messages flow past. Decisions made in Slack threads are invisible to anyone who wasn't in the thread at the time. When a conversation produces a decision, someone needs to write it down in a permanent location (doc, wiki, project brief). The Slack thread is the discussion. The doc is the record.

### Meetings without memos

A meeting without a written agenda is a conversation. A meeting without written notes is a rumor. If the topic matters enough to gather six people in a room for an hour (at roughly $82 per minute, per HBR's estimate), it matters enough to write down what was decided and who's doing what.

### Confusing length with rigor

A 10-page memo is not more rigorous than a 2-page memo. It's usually less rigorous. Longer docs often pad the argument with repetition and hedging. The Amazon 6-pager has a page limit for a reason. Constraints force precision. Write the shortest document that carries the full argument, and stop.

### Hiding uncertainty behind confident prose

Teams often write recommendations as if they're certain when they're not. This causes two problems. First, decision-makers can't assess risk. Second, when the recommendation turns out wrong, trust erodes. State your confidence level. Flag your assumptions. The goal is transparency, not persuasion.

## Practice It

### 1. This week: Audit one Slack thread

Find a Slack thread from this week that went longer than five messages. Rewrite the key decision or conclusion as a two-sentence summary. Post it in the thread with a note: "Capturing the decision here for reference." Notice how much faster it is to understand the summary versus re-reading the thread.

### 2. Next meeting: Write a BLUF memo

For your next team meeting, write a one-page memo using the BLUF framework. State your recommendation in the first sentence. Support it with 3-4 points. Share it before the meeting and use meeting time for questions, not presentation. Compare the discussion quality to your usual meetings.

### 3. Every engagement: Create a decision log

Start a running document for each client engagement that captures key decisions, who made them, and why. When someone asks "Why did we go with approach X?", the answer is in the log. This compounds over time. Six months in, the log becomes the most valuable document on the engagement.

## Further Reading

**[Working Backwards](https://www.workingbackwards.com/)** — Colin Bryar and Bill Carr. The definitive account of Amazon's writing culture, including the 6-pager and PR/FAQ format. Shows how writing became the operating system for decision-making at scale.

**[The Pyramid Principle](https://www.amazon.com/Pyramid-Principle-Logic-Writing-Thinking/dp/0960191024)** — Barbara Minto. The foundational framework for structured business writing. Developed at McKinsey in the 1960s, still the standard for consulting communications and executive memos.

**[The 37signals Guide to Internal Communication](https://basecamp.com/guides/how-we-communicate)** — Basecamp / 37signals. A short, opinionated guide to how a remote company handles internal comms. Practical rules about when to write, when to meet, and how to keep communication from eating your workday.

**[Deep Work](https://calnewport.com/deep-work-rules-for-focused-success-in-a-distracted-world/)** — Cal Newport. The case for protecting focused work from communication interruptions. Backed by research showing context switches cost 23 minutes of recovery time per interruption.

**[Writes and Write-Nots](https://paulgraham.com/writes.html)** — Paul Graham. A short essay on why writing ability will matter more as AI changes work. "What people call good writing is actually good thinking."
