# Why Prompts Matter

The quality of your AI visibility tracking depends entirely on the quality of the questions you track.

This sounds obvious. In practice, it's where most companies go wrong.

---

## The Prompt Problem

When brands start tracking AI visibility, they usually do the same thing: brainstorm a list of prompts that seem relevant.

"Best [our category] software."
"[Our brand] vs [competitor]."
"[Our brand] reviews."
"Top [our category] tools 2026."

These aren't bad prompts. But they're only a small slice of what buyers actually ask. And they tend to be the most generic, least useful slice.

Real buyers don't ask generic questions. They ask specific ones. They include context about their company, their industry, their budget, their team size, their technical requirements. They ask follow-up questions. They change direction mid-conversation.

If you're only tracking "best CRM software," you're missing the buyer who asks "which CRM is best for a 20-person real estate brokerage that needs Zillow integration." That's a much more actionable signal. And it's the kind of question where smaller brands can win against market leaders.

---

## Vanity Prompts vs. Buyer Intent Prompts

There's a useful distinction between two types of prompts.

**Vanity prompts** are the ones that make you feel good when you show up. "Best [category] software" or "[our brand] reviews." They're broad, generic, and everyone tracks them. They tell you whether AI knows your name. They don't tell you much about actual buying behavior.

**Buyer intent prompts** reflect how real people research and make decisions. They include context. They have specificity. They sound like something an actual buyer would type into ChatGPT at 10pm when they're shortlisting vendors.

"I'm evaluating project management tools for a remote engineering team of 40. We need GitHub integration, async communication features, and SOC 2 compliance. What should I look at?"

That's a buyer intent prompt. It tells you whether AI recommends your brand for a specific, real use case. If you're not appearing for questions like this, it doesn't matter how often AI mentions your name in generic contexts.

---

## Why Most Companies Guess Wrong

The problem starts before the first prompt is written.

Most AI visibility tools have shallow understanding of your company, your market, and your buyers. They don't know your actual buyer personas. They don't know which use cases drive your revenue. They don't know what your competitors' real weaknesses are.

Without that context, they can't help you write good prompts. So they give you a blank slate and tell you to figure it out. Or they auto-generate prompts based on keyword patterns, which produces volume without quality.

Garbage in, garbage out. If you're tracking the wrong questions, it doesn't matter how often you check the dashboard. You'll know your visibility for questions nobody is actually asking.
