# How AI Decides What to Recommend

When a buyer asks AI for a software recommendation, the engine doesn't flip a coin. It draws on patterns in its training data, real-time web retrieval, and a set of signals that influence which brands appear and how they're described.

Understanding those signals is the first step to improving your visibility.

---

## It's Not Just Your Website

The most common mistake brands make: assuming AI recommends them based on their own website content.

Your website matters. But it's one input among many. AI engines form their view of your brand from a wide range of sources: review platforms, forums, social media, news articles, comparison sites, Wikipedia, analyst reports, customer testimonials, and more.

The brands that appear most consistently in AI answers are the ones with strong signals across many sources. Not just the ones with the best product page.

---

## Third-Party Mentions Carry More Weight

AI engines are built to be skeptical of self-promotion. When your website says "we're the best expense management tool," AI treats that as a marketing claim. When G2 reviews, Reddit threads, industry analysts, and news articles say the same thing, AI treats that as evidence.

Third-party mentions are the currency of AI visibility. The more credible sources that discuss your brand positively, the more likely AI is to recommend you.

This has a practical implication: investing in PR, reviews, community presence, and analyst relationships directly impacts your AI visibility. These aren't just brand-building activities anymore. They're visibility signals.

---

## Each Engine Is Different

Ask ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, and Google AI the same buyer question. You'll get different answers.

Each engine has different source preferences, different training data, and different retrieval approaches. A brand that's prominent on Reddit will perform differently across engines than a brand with strong Wikipedia presence.

Some patterns we've observed:

**ChatGPT** has the largest user base and draws heavily from web content and public databases. It tends to recommend well-known brands with broad web presence.

**Perplexity** retrieves information in real time from the web and cites sources inline. It favors recent content and tends to surface Reddit discussions and recent articles.

**Claude** is more cautious about recommendations and tends to reason through trade-offs. It's growing quickly, especially among technical buyers and decision-makers.

**Google AI** integrates with traditional search results and draws from Google's own index. Strong organic search performance tends to correlate with visibility here.

The implication: there's no single "AI ranking" to optimize for. Visibility requires presence across multiple engines, which means investing in a range of signals rather than optimizing for one platform.

---

## Brand Authority Matters

The strongest predictor of whether AI recommends a brand isn't any single piece of content. It's overall brand authority.

Brands with strong name recognition, high search volume, and broad mentions across the web tend to appear more consistently across all engines. This isn't surprising. AI engines reflect the web, and the web reflects brand authority.

This doesn't mean small brands can't compete. It means the strategy is different. A small brand can win on specific, niche questions where the big players don't have depth. "Best expense management tool for Series A startups with international teams" is a question where a focused challenger can outperform a market leader that hasn't addressed that specific audience.

---

## Freshness Matters

AI engines favor recent information. Content that was accurate two years ago might no longer reflect your current pricing, features, or positioning. When AI gives outdated information about your brand, it can be worse than not appearing at all.

Keeping your core content current is one of the most practical things you can do. Update your website. Refresh your comparison pages. Make sure your pricing information is accurate across review platforms. Publish content regularly so AI engines have a recent signal to draw from.

---

## Structure Helps AI Find You

AI engines extract information from web pages. They don't read pages the way humans do. They pull chunks of text, look for direct answers, and prioritize content that's easy to parse.

Pages with clear headings, direct answers to specific questions, and structured formats (tables, lists, FAQ sections) tend to get cited more than long narrative pages where the key information is buried in the middle.

This doesn't mean you need to rewrite everything. It means that when you create content aimed at AI visibility, structure it so the answer comes first. Make key claims easy to extract. Use headings that match the questions buyers actually ask.

---

## The Bottom Line

AI engines don't have a single ranking algorithm you can reverse-engineer. They synthesize information from many sources and form recommendations based on a mix of authority, recency, third-party validation, and content quality.

The brands that perform best aren't trying to hack any one signal. They're building genuine authority across multiple channels: great content, strong reviews, active community presence, current information, and structured data that's easy for AI to interpret.
