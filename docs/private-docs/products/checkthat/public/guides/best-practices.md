# Best Practices

Simple habits that make CheckThat more useful.

---

## Start with the Shared Library

Before writing custom prompts, spend time with the shared prompt library. Filter by your category. See what's already tracked. Identify the buyer questions that matter most for your brand.

Most of the high-value prompts in your category are already there. Custom prompts should add depth to what exists, not duplicate it.

---

## Track Competitors, Not Just Yourself

Your visibility only makes sense in context.

Add your top competitors to your tracking. See how AI engines describe them. Compare your positioning to theirs for the same buyer questions. Identify where you lead and where they lead.

The most useful insights come from the gaps between your visibility and theirs.

---

## Focus on Buyer Questions, Not Vanity Prompts

"Best [category] software" is a prompt everyone tracks. It tells you whether AI knows your name. It doesn't tell you much about real buying behavior.

The prompts that drive decisions are more specific: "best [category] for [specific use case]," "which [category] tool works best for [industry/company size]," "[your brand] vs [competitor] for [context]."

Track the questions your buyers actually ask. If you're not sure what those are, look at your sales calls, support tickets, and Reddit threads where people discuss your category.

---

## Review Weekly, Not Daily

AI visibility doesn't change hour by hour. Daily checking creates noise and anxiety without actionable signal.

Set a weekly cadence. Look at trends, not individual data points. Ask: Where did my visibility improve? Where did it decline? Did any competitor make a significant move? Are there new questions in my category that I'm not visible for?

Weekly reviews with a clear set of questions are more useful than daily dashboard refreshes.

---

## Use Insights to Inform Content Strategy

The point of tracking AI visibility isn't to track AI visibility. It's to do something about it.

When you find buyer questions where you're invisible, that's a content opportunity. Create content that directly answers those questions. Make it specific, comprehensive, and easy for AI to extract.

When you find questions where AI describes you inaccurately, that's a correction opportunity. Update your website, your review profiles, and your public content to reflect current reality.

When you find questions where competitors dominate, that's a competitive intelligence signal. Understand what they're doing differently and decide whether to compete directly or find adjacent opportunities.

---

## Don't Over-Track

More prompts isn't always better.

50 well-chosen prompts that reflect real buyer behavior will give you more useful data than 500 generic keyword variations. Quality of questions determines quality of insights.

Start with fewer prompts. Get a baseline. Add more as you understand your category better. Retire prompts that aren't producing useful signal.

---

## Check for Accuracy

AI sometimes gets things wrong about your brand. Wrong pricing. Outdated features. Incorrect positioning. Old leadership information.

Periodically check what AI engines say about you and look for inaccuracies. When you find them, update the sources AI draws from: your website, your Wikipedia page, your review platform profiles. Inaccurate AI recommendations are worse than no recommendations.

---

## Think Long-Term

AI visibility is a compounding asset. The data you track today becomes more valuable over time. Trends that emerge over weeks and months are more meaningful than any single data point.

Start tracking now, even if you're not ready to act on everything you learn. The history you build can't be created later.
