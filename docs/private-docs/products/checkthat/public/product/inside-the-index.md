# Inside the Index

You can browse CheckThat right now, without signing up. Here's what you'll find.

---

## Brand Pages

**[Browse brands](/brands/)**

Every brand in the index has a dedicated page. This is more than a company listing. It's a research profile built with editorial depth.

A brand page includes: what the company does, what products they offer, how they position themselves, who their buyers are, and what their competitive landscape looks like. Then the layer no one else provides: how AI engines describe this brand when buyers ask about it across ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, Google AI, and Gemini.

Not every brand page is identical. We recognize three types of brands:

- **Products** like Ramp, Notion, or Linear. These have pricing, features, reviews, and alternatives.
- **Organizations** like Anthropic, Microsoft, or Sequoia. These have company profiles but not product comparison pages.
- **Websites** like Product Hunt, G2, or Hacker News. These have visibility data but different contexts than products.

The page adapts to the brand type. "Ramp pricing" is a page that should exist. "Sequoia pricing" is not.

Brand pages also expand into sub-pages based on what buyers actually search for: pricing breakdowns, alternatives and comparisons, reviews, product features, AI visibility data. We build these where there's genuine buyer interest, not for every brand in every direction.

## Category Pages

**[Browse categories](/categories/)**

The index maps 1,740 B2B software categories. Each one is a window into how AI views a competitive landscape.

A category page shows you: which brands compete in this space, how AI engines rank them, which brands get mentioned most often when buyers ask questions in this category, and how those rankings have shifted over time.

Categories are the connective tissue of the index. Every brand page links up to the categories it belongs to. Every category links down to all its brands. This creates a browsable structure where you can start anywhere and explore the full map of B2B software through AI's eyes.

Example categories: AI Agents, Contact Center, Foundation Models, Identity and Access Management, Live Chat, Low-Code/No-Code, Product Analytics, Website Builders. And 1,700+ more.

## Answer Pages

**[Browse AI answers](/ai-answers/)**

This is where CheckThat is most different from anything else on the market.

Answer pages have two layers.

**Layer one: Our answer.** For important buyer questions, we create the most comprehensive answer we can. "Best expense management tools for startups." "Ramp vs Brex for Series A companies." "Which CRM is best for real estate agents."

These aren't summaries of what AI says. They're original editorial content. Researched, written, reviewed. We aim to create the best answer to each question on the internet.

**Layer two: How AI answers.** Alongside our editorial answer, we show how each AI engine responds to the same question. Which brands they mention. Which sources they cite. How their answers differ from each other. How those answers have changed over time.

This is data that exists nowhere else. When someone wants to know what ChatGPT says about expense management tools, we show them. When they want to see how that answer has shifted over the past three months, we show them that too.

The two layers together create something unique: a page that's both the best answer to a buyer's question and a transparency window into how AI is answering that same question.

## How Everything Connects

The index isn't a collection of isolated pages. It's an interconnected graph.

Brand pages link to their categories. Category pages link to all their brands. Answer pages link to every brand mentioned in the response. Brands link to the answers where they appear.

You can start at any point and follow connections to explore the full landscape. Look up a brand and see which categories they compete in. Browse a category and see which brands AI favors. Read an answer and discover brands you hadn't considered.

Every link strengthens the index. Every connection makes the data more discoverable. The result is a browsable, interlinked map of how AI sees B2B software.

Not a dashboard you log into. An index you explore.
