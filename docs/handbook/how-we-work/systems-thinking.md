# Systems thinking

## Understanding systems

A well-run organization is fundamentally a system — a collection of elements, interconnected through relationships, all in service of a shared purpose. Systems are everywhere: from the human body to a championship team. Elements change, but relationships and goals remain consistent, driving behavior.

### Key concepts

- **Elements**: The observable components — people, tools, processes, workflows
- **Relationships**: How elements connect and interact to achieve objectives
- **Purpose**: The ultimate mission driving the system, often inferred through actions rather than statements

**What**: Systems are interconnected groups working toward a common goal.

**So what**: Understanding systems helps you see beyond the surface to comprehend why things truly work — or don't.

**How to apply**: Identify the systems in your work. Look at the elements, how they interact, and what purpose they serve.

## Stocks and flows

In systems, **stocks** are the assets you accumulate — content pieces, leads, capital. **Flows** are what change these stocks — publishing new articles (inflow), outdated posts losing relevance (outflow). Stocks and flows form the foundation for understanding how a system evolves over time.

**Example:**
- **Stock**: Your library of evergreen content
- **Flow**: Adding fresh content weekly (inflow) or archiving outdated content (outflow)

**What**: Stocks represent your assets; flows represent the changes affecting them.

**So what**: Knowing your stocks and flows helps you manage resources efficiently — avoiding underutilization or depletion.

**How to apply**: Look at your marketing assets. Are you replenishing faster than you're losing? Can you amplify inflows or mitigate outflows to maintain growth?

## Feedback loops

**Feedback loops** drive system behavior. **Balancing feedback** stabilizes the system. **Reinforcing feedback** drives growth — positive or negative.

- **Balancing feedback**: Ensures stability, like quality control in a content strategy
- **Reinforcing feedback**: Fuels growth — think of a viral post gaining traction as shares increase exponentially

**What**: Feedback loops are forces that either stabilize or amplify system dynamics.

**So what**: Recognizing them helps you maintain control during growth or restore stability when needed.

**How to apply**: Identify what drives growth in your system and what balances it. Use reinforcing loops to scale, but don't neglect balancing loops — or you'll hit burnout or quality issues.

## Resilience and self-organization

The best systems are **resilient**, capable of **self-organization**, and structured in **hierarchies**. Resilience means bouncing back. Self-organization means adapting and evolving. Hierarchies manage complexity.

**Examples:**
- **Resilience**: Your content team adapting after an underperforming campaign
- **Self-organization**: Writers independently finding new angles without a formal directive
- **Hierarchy**: The content team contributing insights that shape broader strategic goals

**What**: These traits ensure a system can endure disruptions, adapt, and handle complexity efficiently.

**So what**: A resilient system grows sustainably even amidst challenges.

**How to apply**: Empower your teams to make decisions. Encourage self-organization by giving them freedom to experiment and adapt.

## System traps

Even great systems fall into traps. **Policy resistance** happens when parts of the system work against each other. **Linear thinking** expects outcomes to be directly proportional to inputs — but the real world is nonlinear.

**Examples:**
- **Policy resistance**: Teams prioritizing contradictory goals, like brand voice versus SEO optimization
- **Linear thinking**: Assuming doubling your marketing budget will double your results — when diminishing returns often apply

**What**: System traps are behaviors or patterns that undermine effectiveness.

**So what**: Awareness of these traps lets you anticipate conflicts and adjust proactively.

**How to apply**: Regularly assess team goals for alignment. Are different parts of your organization unknowingly working against each other?

## Leverage points

**Leverage points** are strategic areas where small changes yield massive results — the gears you turn to transform an entire process.

- **Rules and policies**: Simplify processes, like fast-tracking content approval
- **Information flow**: Implement dashboards so everyone sees performance in real time

**What**: High-impact areas in a system where change leads to significant improvement.

**So what**: Finding leverage points creates substantial efficiency gains without proportional increases in effort.

**How to apply**: Audit your workflows. Where do bottlenecks come from? Find small tweaks that remove obstacles and amplify productivity.

## Challenging mental models

Our **mental models** shape how we see the world and design systems. Systems thinking pushes us to challenge these models — to see interconnected dynamics rather than isolated parts.

- **Linear vs. nonlinear**: Moving from simple cause-and-effect to understanding complex growth dynamics
- **Boundaries**: Realizing content, marketing, and sales aren't separate silos but part of a connected growth engine

**What**: Mental models are the perspectives and assumptions that influence decisions.

**So what**: By questioning them, you discover new opportunities and see past constraints.

**How to apply**: Question existing assumptions — especially about what's possible. Are outdated beliefs holding your strategy back?

## Connecting the frameworks

**Systems thinking**, **first principles thinking**, and **the flywheel concept** are interconnected strategies that transform how you approach growth.

### How they work together

- **Holistic insight + breakthrough thinking**: Systems thinking shows what's holding you back. First principles creates new ways to solve those problems.
- **Reinforcing loops for growth**: Systems thinking reveals the loops. First principles optimizes every component within them.
- **Sustained effort + clarity**: Flywheels need clear, repeated pushes. Systems thinking gives the framework. First principles provides clarity for every move.

## Bringing it all together

Systems thinking isn't just theory — it's a way to reshape how you approach challenges. Whether through stocks and flows, feedback loops, or leverage points, there's always an opportunity to create lasting impact.

**What**: An approach that emphasizes the interconnected nature of everything you work on.

**So what**: It gives you the ability to see beyond the immediate and understand how changes ripple through your work.

**How to apply**: Start by mapping your processes, questioning old assumptions, and leveraging small changes to yield significant growth.
