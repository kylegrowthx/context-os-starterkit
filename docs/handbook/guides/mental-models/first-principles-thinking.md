# First principles thinking

Most advice you'll get is wrong. Not because the person giving it is stupid. Because they're reasoning from analogy. They saw something work somewhere else and assumed it'll work here.

First principles thinking is the fix. You decompose a problem to its fundamental truths and build your reasoning from there. No copying. No "best practices." Just: what do we actually know, and what follows from that?

Every engagement, every strategy conversation, every product decision at GrowthX runs on this. It's how we avoid giving clients recycled advice dressed up as insight.

---

## What It Actually Is

The base facts that remain true after you strip away every assumption. Aristotle called them "the first basis from which a thing is known." Really, it's asking **"What do I know is true?"** and refusing to accept anything else as a starting point.

The process is simple. Executing it is hard.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Identify your assumptions">
    What are you taking for granted? What "everyone knows" about this problem?
  </Step>
  <Step title="Break them down">
    Why do you believe each one? Is it because you have evidence, or because someone told you?
  </Step>
  <Step title="Find the fundamentals">
    What remains true after you've questioned everything? These are your first principles.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Reason up">
    Build your solution from those truths, ignoring what's conventional.
  </Step>
</Steps>

The hard part is step 2. Your brain will resist. It likes shortcuts. Analogy is comfortable. "Company X did this, so we should too." First-principles thinking requires you to think from scratch every time.

---

## Why It Matters Here

GrowthX is a growth advisory company. Clients hire us because they're stuck. Usually because they're reasoning from analogy. They copy a competitor's playbook, follow "best practices" from a blog post, or keep doing what worked three years ago in a different market.

If we reason the same way, we're useless. Our value comes from seeing what they can't.

**In advisory work.** A client says "we need to improve our SEO." First principles response: What problem are we solving? Are buyers finding you through search? What's the actual discovery path? Maybe the answer is AEO, not SEO. Our [buyer evaluation playbook](/guides/marketing/aeo-buyer-evaluation) shows how to trace the real discovery journey. Maybe the answer is partnerships. "Improve SEO" is a solution dressed as a problem. Peel it back.

**In product decisions.** "We need to add feature X because competitors have it." First principles: What job is the customer hiring our product to do? Does feature X serve that job? Or are competitors building for a different customer segment? Our [AI product leadership guide](/guides/ai/product-leadership) goes deep on this. Start from the problem, not from what exists.

**In growth strategy.** "We should hire more salespeople to grow revenue." What's the actual constraint? If it's pipeline, more salespeople won't help. If it's conversion, the problem is positioning or product. If it's churn, you're filling a leaky bucket. The [MRR and churn guide](/guides/business/mrr-churn-retention) walks through the math on why this kills compounding. The first-principles answer starts by diagnosing the real bottleneck.

## The Battery Example

In 2008, battery packs cost \$600 per kWh. Nobody questioned it. At that price, electric cars couldn't compete with gas. The conventional conclusion: wait for battery technology to improve.

Musk asked a different question: What are batteries made of? Cobalt, nickel, aluminum, carbon, some polymers, a steel can. What do those materials cost on the commodity market? About \$80 per kWh.

The \$600 wasn't a fact. It came from how batteries were manufactured and who manufactured them. The materials said \$80 was possible. The industry said \$600 was inevitable. First principles pointed to the gap.

<Tip>
**The pattern that transfers:** When an industry accepts a constraint as fixed, ask what it's made of. Decompose it into raw components. The constraint usually isn't fundamental. It's structural. Structural problems have structural solutions.
</Tip>

## How to Apply It at GrowthX

### In Client Discovery

You discover more when you refuse to accept the client's framing.

| Client Says | Analogy-Based Response | First Principles Response |
|-------------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| "We need better content" | Audit their content, suggest improvements | What outcome are you trying to drive? Who needs to see this? What changes their behavior? |
| "Our CAC is too high" | Optimize ad spend, negotiate rates | What does acquisition actually cost you? Break down every component. Which piece is inflated? Why? |
| "We need to go enterprise" | Build enterprise features, hire enterprise sales | What do enterprise buyers actually need? Is it features, or is it trust, compliance, support? What's the minimum credible offering? |
| "Competitors are beating us" | Competitive analysis, feature comparison | What job are customers hiring them for that they're not hiring you for? What would make you the obvious choice for your segment? |

Clients bring you the first column. A mediocre consultant delivers the second. Value lives in the third. It takes discipline to question the premise before solving the problem.

Think of it as the Pyramid Principle in reverse. Before you can lead with the answer, you need to make sure you're answering the right question. The [operator's handbook](/guides/operations/operators-handbook) covers the full framework.

### In Strategy

Richard Rumelt wrote in *Good Strategy/Bad Strategy* that most companies confuse goals with strategy. "Grow 3x" is a goal. Strategy is the diagnosis of what's happening, a guiding policy for dealing with it, and coherent actions that follow.

**Start with diagnosis.** What is actually happening in our market? Not what we wish were happening. Not what the board deck says. What do the numbers show? What are customers actually doing?

**Find the constraint.** Every system has a bottleneck. At \$12M ARR, it might be pipeline. At \$36M, delivery capacity. At \$100M, management depth. The constraint shifts. If you're solving last year's constraint, you're wasting effort.

**Build from truth, not from precedent.** "Companies at our stage typically do X" is analogy. "Our data shows Y is the constraint, and Z is the most efficient way to address it" is first principles.

### In Writing

Our [writing style guide](/guides/writing/style) says it directly: "Decompose the idea into fundamental parts. Question assumptions. Start from what you know is true, then reason up."

If you can't write a clear first-principles argument, you don't have one. The writing shows the thinking. Or the lack of it.

## Common Traps

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Confusing first principles with contrarianism">
    First-principles thinking sometimes leads to contrarian conclusions. But being contrarian isn't the point. If the analysis confirms conventional wisdom, good. The value is in the rigor, not in being different.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Going too deep">
    You can decompose anything to the atomic level. That's rarely helpful. Stop when you hit truths you can act on. "Atoms exist" is a first principle, but it won't help you fix churn.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Ignoring useful analogies">
    First-principles thinking doesn't mean ignoring what others have learned. It means not *starting* there. Use frameworks and patterns, but treat them as tools to test, not answers to accept.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Analysis paralysis">
    Decomposing everything from scratch takes time. Save it for the decisions that matter. One-way doors where getting it wrong is costly. The [operator's handbook](/guides/operations/operators-handbook) covers the one-way vs. two-way door framework for knowing when this level of rigor is worth it. For reversible decisions, pattern matching is faster and good enough.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## The Mental Model Stack

First-principles thinking doesn't work alone.

<Info>
**These are layers, not alternatives.** Decompose with first principles. Pressure-test with inversion. Project consequences with second-order thinking. Reconnect with [systems thinking](/how-we-work/systems-thinking).
</Info>

- **Inversion.** Once you have your answer, flip it: "How would this fail?" Attacking your own conclusion is how you stress-test it.
- **Second-order thinking.** First principles tells you what's true. Second-order thinking tells you what happens next. "If we lower price, we'll get more customers" is first-order. "If we lower price, we'll attract price-sensitive customers who churn faster" is second-order.
- **[Systems thinking](/how-we-work/systems-thinking).** First principles decomposes. Systems thinking reconnects the pieces and shows how they interact. You need both.

## Practice It

You get better at this by doing it. Not by reading about it.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Daily: Ask 'why' three times">
    When you hear a claim or assumption, ask "Why is that true?" Then ask again. And again. Three layers of "why" usually gets you past the surface.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Weekly: Audit one decision">
    Pick one decision or recommendation you're making. Write down every assumption behind it. Circle the ones you can't prove. Those are your investigation targets.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Every engagement: Challenge the premise">
    Before proposing a solution, list the assumptions embedded in the client's problem statement. Challenge at least two. The best insight? Usually behind an assumption nobody questioned.
  </Step>
</Steps>
