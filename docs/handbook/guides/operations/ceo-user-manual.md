# CEO user manual

## About This Document

This guide synthesizes best practices for creating a CEO/executive "user manual"—a document that codifies how you work, what you value, and how others can work effectively with you.

**Why this matters for scaling yourself:**
- **Reduces repetition**: Stop explaining your preferences to every new hire
- **Accelerates onboarding**: New team members understand you in days, not months
- **Creates psychological safety**: Predictability builds trust
- **Forces self-awareness**: Writing it reveals patterns you may not have articulated
- **Enables delegation**: Others can make decisions aligned with your preferences

**Core insight:** As Claire Hughes Johnson puts it: "The vast majority of issues are a result of poor or infrequent communication." A user manual is communication at scale.

---

## Part 1: The Origin and Evolution of CEO User Manuals

### Where It Started: Claire Hughes Johnson's "Working with Claire"

Claire Hughes Johnson first wrote a version of her user manual in 2010 when managing global online sales and operations at Google. When she joined Stripe as COO in 2014, she adapted it and shared it as an open document with everyone working closely with her.

**What happened next:**
- The document spread quickly through the organization
- People started asking "Why don't we have more of these?"
- Non-managers began writing guides about themselves
- It became viral, organic adoption—not mandated from the top

**Key insight from Claire:** "I think if you don't get feedback on it from the people who work with you, you're not going to get the bigger goal accomplished." The document should evolve based on real feedback.

The concept was also championed by **Ivar Kroghrud** in Adam Bryant's New York Times "Corner Office" column, where he explained: "This shortens the learning curve when you build new teams and bring new people on board. If you give them this 'how to work with me' page on the first day, they get a different perspective on who you are."

### Notable Examples in Practice

| Leader | Company | Notable Element |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| **Claire Hughes Johnson** | Stripe/Google | Communication channel hierarchy, career session structure |
| **Sid Sijbrandij** | GitLab | Public "flaws" section, radical transparency |
| **Jay Desai** | PatientPing | Detailed expectations for direct reports, hiring philosophy |
| **Steph Smith** | HubSpot | Strengths, weaknesses, favorite quotes in slides format |

## Part 2: Mental Models for Why This Works

### Mental Model 1: The Trust Battery (Tobi Lütke, Shopify)

Think of trust as a battery that charges and depletes:

- **Charging**: Meeting commitments, good communication, delivering results
- **Depleting**: Missing deadlines, surprises, misaligned expectations
- **User manuals charge the battery**: They reduce misunderstandings before they happen

A user manual front-loads trust by making your operating system transparent. People don't have to guess—they know.

### Mental Model 2: Task-Relevant Maturity (Andy Grove, Intel)

From *High Output Management*, Grove teaches that how you manage someone should depend on their experience **with the specific task**, not their seniority.

| TRM Level | Profile | Your Approach |
|-----------|---------|---------------|
| **Low** | New to this task | Structured direction, frequent check-ins |
| **Medium** | Building experience | Coaching, explain "why" not just "what" |
| **High** | Proven track record | Set goals, minimal intervention |

**Application to user manuals:** Your document should clarify how you'll adjust your management style based on someone's experience, so people understand why you're hands-on in some areas and hands-off in others.

### Mental Model 3: Barrels vs. Ammunition (Keith Rabois)

Rabois distinguishes between two types of people:

- **Barrels**: Can take an idea and drive it to completion independently
- **Ammunition**: Support and complement barrels' efforts

**Application:** Your user manual can explain how you identify and develop barrels, what autonomy they earn, and what you expect from people at different stages.

### Mental Model 4: The CEO as Editor (Keith Rabois)

"A manager should be an editor, not a writer. An editor's job is to not write, but to clarify, simplify, and above all, remove."

**Application:** Your user manual should explain your editing philosophy:
- How you prefer work to be brought to you
- What "good enough" looks like for different contexts
- Where you want final approval vs. where you trust the team

### Mental Model 5: Radical Transparency (Ray Dalio, Bridgewater)

Dalio's formula: **Idea Meritocracy = Radical Truth + Radical Transparency + Believability-Weighted Decision Making**

- Everything visible builds trust
- The more people see what's happening—good, bad, ugly—the better they decide
- Transparency accelerates learning for everyone

**Application:** GitLab's Sid Sijbrandij models this by publicly listing his flaws: "Transparency and directness are part of our values and I want to live them by sharing the flaws I know I have."

### Mental Model 6: Zone of Genius (Matt Mochary)

From *The Great CEO Within*:

| Zone | Definition | Action |
|------|------------|--------|
| **Incompetence** | Others do better | Delegate immediately |
| **Competence** | You're fine, others equally good | Delegate if no joy |
| **Excellence** | Better than others but don't love it | **DANGER ZONE**—delegate even this |
| **Genius** | Uniquely good AND love it | Maximize (target 75-80% of time) |

**Application:** Your user manual can share what's in your Zone of Genius (where you'll engage deeply) and what's not (where you'll trust the team).

## Part 3: Framework for Building Your User Manual

### The Two Essential Categories

According to Claire Hughes Johnson, every working-with-me guide needs at least two sections:

1. **The ways you like to work, literally** (logistics, channels, timing)
2. **The ways you handle work, emotionally** (stress, conflict, feedback)

### Comprehensive Section Framework

Based on synthesis across leading examples:

#### Section 1: My Role and What I'm Here For

**Purpose:** Clarify what value you provide to your direct reports and the organization.

**Example from Jay Desai (PatientPing):**
> "I try very hard to hire leaders that I would like to work for myself and are meaningfully better than me at the function you lead. As a result, it's unlikely I will be a mentor to you in your role. My biggest value to you is to be a strong vocal advocate for your success, get you the resources you need to be successful, empower you to make impact without friction, remove any blockers to your success."

**Questions to answer:**
- What is my primary job as CEO/leader?
- What value do I uniquely provide to my direct reports?
- What should people NOT expect me to do?

#### Section 2: Communication Preferences

**Purpose:** Eliminate the guessing game about how to reach you.

**Example from Claire Hughes Johnson:**
> Channel hierarchy (most → least urgent): Call → Text → Slack → Email. I like fast turnaround and acknowledgement on written communications.

**Questions to answer:**
- What channel should people use for what type of communication?
- What response times do you expect/commit to?
- When is it appropriate to interrupt you?
- How do you prefer to receive information (written vs. verbal, detailed vs. summary)?
- What's your communication style under stress?

**From Sid Sijbrandij (GitLab):**
> "I am a visual person much more than auditory, and I am a top-down person much more than bottom-up."

#### Section 3: Decision-Making Philosophy

**Purpose:** Help people understand when to involve you and when to proceed independently.

**Frameworks to reference:**
- **One-way vs. two-way doors** (Bezos): Reversible decisions should be fast; irreversible ones need care
- **Disagree and commit**: Voice disagreement, then commit genuinely once decided
- **Conviction vs. consequence matrix**: Low consequence + low conviction = delegate; high consequence + high conviction = you decide

**Questions to answer:**
- When do you want to be consulted vs. informed?
- How do you handle disagreement?
- What does "disagree and commit" look like with you?
- What decisions do you want final approval on?

#### Section 4: Meeting Preferences and 1:1 Structure

**Purpose:** Make meetings with you more productive from day one.

**Example from Claire Hughes Johnson:**
> "We'll do a career session at some point in our first few months of working together—your history, why you've made choices you have made, what your ambitions are for the future. These help me know where you are in terms of personal development interests and ambitions."

**Questions to answer:**
- What's your preferred 1:1 cadence and duration?
- Who owns the 1:1 agenda?
- What topics are best for 1:1s vs. other forums?
- How do you prefer status updates delivered?

#### Section 5: How I Give and Receive Feedback

**Purpose:** Create psychological safety around feedback.

**Adam Bryant's "CEO Test" insight:** Great leaders master "the inner game"—calm and self-awareness that enable them to be simultaneously confident and humble, urgent and patient, compassionate and demanding.

**Questions to answer:**
- How do you prefer to give feedback (immediately, in 1:1s, written)?
- How direct is your style? Should people read between the lines or take feedback at face value?
- How do you prefer to receive feedback?
- What makes you most receptive to pushback?

**From Claire:**
> "I like data and dashboards so that there is one (ideally) objective way to measure progress and results, but I dislike being bogged down in data and torturing the numbers."

#### Section 6: Accountability and Expectations

**Purpose:** Be explicit about what you expect so people can deliver.

**Example from a CEO user manual:**
> "Accountability is uber important. If you say you will do something, I expect you to do it. Don't tell me at the last minute you won't make it—it's too late to help and negatively affects the rest of the team."

**Questions to answer:**
- What does accountability mean to you?
- How should people handle blockers or delays?
- What's the difference between a mistake and a pattern you won't tolerate?
- How do you distinguish effort from results?

#### Section 7: How I Operate Under Stress

**Purpose:** Help people predict your behavior when things get hard.

**Example:**
> "Under stress, I become hyper-collaborative and bring the team together to work on hard problems."

Alternatively, some leaders become more directive, more detail-oriented, or withdraw to think.

**Questions to answer:**
- What does stress look like on you?
- How should people work with you differently when you're stressed?
- What do you need from your team during crises?

#### Section 8: My Known Flaws and Development Areas

**Purpose:** Model vulnerability and reduce fear of raising concerns.

**Example from Sid Sijbrandij (GitLab):**
> "Transparency and directness are part of our values and I want to live them by sharing the flaws I know I have. I'm fully responsible for improving the things below, listing them is no excuse. They are listed here for two reasons. The first one is so that people know it is not them but my fault. The second one is so I can improve—I hope that listing them lets people know I appreciate when people speak up about them."

**Questions to answer:**
- What are your known weaknesses?
- What behaviors are you actively working to improve?
- How can people help you improve?

#### Section 9: Values and What I Care Deeply About

**Purpose:** Help people understand what drives you.

**Questions to answer:**
- What 3-5 principles guide your decision-making?
- What do you value most in the people you work with?
- What are non-negotiables for you?
- What kind of company are you trying to build?

#### Section 10: Personal Context (Optional but Powerful)

**Purpose:** Build connection and understanding.

**Suggested by Claire Hughes Johnson for new hires:**
- Important dates (birthday, anniversaries)
- Personal circumstances that affect work
- Hobbies and interests
- What energizes you outside of work

## Part 4: Implementation Guide

### Step 1: Write Your First Draft (2-3 hours)

Don't aim for perfection. Write quickly through each section. You can refine later.

**Prompt questions to get started:**
1. If someone shadowed you for a week, what would surprise them about how you work?
2. What do you wish people understood about you from day one?
3. What misunderstandings have you had with past colleagues that a document could have prevented?
4. What do you need from others to do your best work?
5. What are you working on improving about yourself?

### Step 2: Get Feedback (Critical Step)

**Claire Hughes Johnson's advice:** "If you don't get feedback on it from the people who work with you, you're not going to get the bigger goal accomplished."

- Share with 2-3 trusted colleagues or direct reports
- Ask: "Does this match your experience of working with me?"
- Ask: "What's missing that would have helped you?"
- Incorporate their input

### Step 3: Share Openly

- Make it accessible (Google Doc, company wiki, onboarding materials)
- Share proactively with new hires and new team members
- Reference it when patterns emerge: "As I mentioned in my working-with-me doc..."

### Step 4: Update Regularly

- Review quarterly or when you notice recurring friction
- Update after significant feedback
- Treat it as a living document, not a static artifact

### Step 5: Encourage Others to Create Their Own

At Stripe, after Claire shared hers:
- People started asking "Why don't we have more of these?"
- Non-managers began writing their own
- It became part of the culture

**Consider:** Making personal user manuals part of your onboarding process or team rituals.

## Part 5: Common Pitfalls to Avoid

### Pitfall 1: Writing an Aspirational Document

Write about who you **are**, not who you wish you were. Your team will notice the gap.

**Fix:** Get feedback from people who've worked with you closely.

### Pitfall 2: Making It a Demand List

The document should help people work **with** you, not just comply with you.

**Fix:** Include what they can expect from you, not just what you expect from them.

### Pitfall 3: Never Updating It

You change. Your role changes. Your team changes.

**Fix:** Set a calendar reminder to review quarterly.

### Pitfall 4: Writing It and Forgetting It

The document only works if people know about it and you reference it.

**Fix:** Actively integrate it into onboarding and 1:1s.

### Pitfall 5: Being Too Generic

Statements like "I value honesty" don't help. Everyone says that.

**Fix:** Be specific. "When you disagree with a direction, I expect you to voice it in the meeting, not after. I'd rather have uncomfortable conversations than discover misalignment later."

## Part 6: Related Frameworks for CEO Self-Scaling

### McKinsey's Personal Operating Model

Four drivers to manage:

1. **Priorities**: The highest-impact problems to solve
2. **Roles**: Where you should personally engage
3. **Time**: How you allocate your calendar
4. **Energy**: What sustains vs. drains you

**Application:** Your user manual can articulate your priorities and where you choose to spend time.

### The CEO Test (Adam Bryant & Kevin Sharer)

Seven challenges that make or break leaders:

1. Can you develop a simple plan for your strategy?
2. Can you make the culture real—and matter?
3. Can you build teams that are true teams?
4. Can you lead transformation?
5. Can you really listen?
6. Can you handle a crisis?
7. Can you master the inner game of leadership?

**Application:** Your user manual can address how you approach several of these—especially culture, teams, listening, and the inner game.

### Keith Rabois' CEO Operating Principles

From his famous "How to Operate" Stanford lecture:

1. **CEO as Editor**: Clarify, simplify, remove
2. **Barrels vs. Ammunition**: Identify who can drive things to completion
3. **Task-Relevant Maturity**: Match management intensity to experience with the task
4. **Decision-Making Framework**: Assess conviction levels and potential consequences
5. **Transparency**: Share board decks, metrics, meeting notes
6. **Details Matter**: If you get all the small things right, the big things follow

**Recommended reading:** Bill Walsh's *The Score Takes Care of Itself*

## Part 7: Template Outline

Use this structure as a starting point:

```
# Working with [Your Name]

## My Role
What I'm here to do. What value I provide. What you shouldn't expect from me.

## Communication
- Preferred channels and when to use them
- Response time expectations
- How I prefer to receive information

## Meetings & 1:1s
- Cadence and structure
- Who owns the agenda
- What I expect you to bring

## Decision-Making
- When to involve me vs. proceed independently
- How I handle disagreement
- My philosophy on speed vs. thoroughness

## Feedback
- How I give feedback
- How I prefer to receive feedback
- How direct is my style

## Accountability
- What I expect when you commit to something
- How to handle blockers
- The difference between mistakes and patterns

## Under Stress
- What stress looks like on me
- How to work with me during crises

## Known Flaws
- What I'm working on
- How you can help me improve

## Values & Principles
- What I care most deeply about
- Non-negotiables

## About Me (Optional)
- Personal context
- What energizes me outside work
```

## Summary

Creating a CEO user manual is one of the highest-leverage activities you can do to scale yourself. It:

1. **Reduces friction** by eliminating the guesswork in working with you
2. **Accelerates onboarding** so new team members are productive faster
3. **Builds trust** through transparency and predictability
4. **Forces self-awareness** about how you actually operate
5. **Creates culture** when the practice spreads to your team

The best user manuals share three qualities:
- **Honest**: They describe who you are, not who you wish you were
- **Specific**: They give actionable guidance, not generic platitudes
- **Alive**: They evolve as you grow and get feedback

As Claire Hughes Johnson says: "Writing a user guide is an exercise in self-awareness. What teams want from their leaders more than anything else is predictability and authenticity. This leads to trust. With trust, you can be unstoppable."

---

## Quick-Start Action Items

1. **Block 2 hours** to write your first draft
2. **Share with 2-3 people** who know you well for feedback
3. **Publish it** in your company wiki or onboarding docs
4. **Reference it** in your first meeting with new team members
5. **Update quarterly** or when patterns emerge
6. **Encourage your leadership team** to create their own
