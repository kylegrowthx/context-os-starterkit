# Design engineer

## TL;DR

- Design engineers blend product design with engineering. You design and build, not just one or the other.
- Part of the product lane — building ContentOS (Atlas) and CheckThat.ai.
- Work in 4-week Shape Up cycles with 2-week cooldowns.
- Own the user experience from concept to shipped code.

## What this role does

Design engineers sit at the intersection of product design and frontend engineering. You don't hand off mockups to developers — you design the interface and build it. This means you own the user experience end-to-end, from understanding the problem to shipping the solution.

You're part of the [product lane](/epd/product-lane), working on ContentOS and CheckThat. The products you build are used by internal teams (delivery, editors) and external customers.

## Key responsibilities

- **Product design** — Understand user needs, design interfaces, create interaction patterns. Think through the full user experience, not just screens.
- **Frontend engineering** — Build what you design. React components, responsive layouts, interactive elements. The design isn't done until it's live.
- **Prototyping** — Build quick prototypes to test ideas before committing to full implementation. Shape Up cycles reward this — solve the hard UX problems early.
- **Design system** — Contribute to and maintain the shared design system. Components should be consistent and reusable.
- **User research** — Talk to users (internal and external). Watch them use the product. Let real usage inform design decisions.

## Accountabilities

| What you own | How it's measured |
|-------------|-------------------|
| User experience quality | Usability, clarity, and effectiveness of shipped features |
| Design-to-ship speed | Features move from design to live product within a single cycle |
| Design system contribution | Consistency and reusability of components |
| User satisfaction | Qualitative feedback from users on product quality |

## What great looks like

- Features ship with great design and great code in the same cycle — no handoff lag
- Users find the product intuitive without needing documentation
- You prototype quickly and kill bad ideas before they consume engineering time
- The design system grows through your contributions, making future work faster
- You understand the business context — why a feature matters, not just what it looks like

## How Shape Up works for you

Each 4-week cycle, the shaping team defines the work (pitches with clear appetite and boundaries). You and the execution team have full autonomy to solve the problem within those boundaries.

**What this means in practice:**
- You won't get pixel-perfect mockups to implement. You'll get a shaped pitch with the problem and constraints defined.
- You decide how to solve the design and engineering challenges.
- You ship something real within the cycle — no multi-month projects.

See the [product dev process](/epd/product-dev-process) for the full methodology.

## Tools you'll use

- **Figma** — Design exploration and component design
- **React** — Frontend framework
- **Rails** — Backend (full stack when needed)
- **Cursor / AI tools** — AI-assisted development
- **GitHub** — Code management and collaboration

## Key relationships

| Who | How you work together |
|-----|----------------------|
| Full-stack engineers | You work in parallel during cycles. You own the frontend experience; they handle backend and infrastructure. |
| Shaping team | They define the work. You solve it. Frequent dialogue during cycles. |
| Other design engineers | Peer review, design system collaboration, shared patterns. |
| QA | Validate that what shipped matches design intent and works correctly. |
| Delivery teams | They use the products you build. Their feedback drives product decisions. |

## Career path

- **Senior design engineer** — Take on more complex product challenges, lead design direction for major features
- **Design lead** — Set design vision across products, mentor other design engineers
- **Product engineering** — Expand into full-stack work if you want to go deeper on engineering
- **Product management** — Transition to shaping and strategy if you want to define the work rather than build it
