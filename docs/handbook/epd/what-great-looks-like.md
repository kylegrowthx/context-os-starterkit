# What great looks like

We're a small team shipping multiple products in one of the fastest-moving spaces in tech. Some of our products have been in 0-1 mode for months. Many are now approaching or past their initial launch. The product direction has been hard to crack — even for leadership. That ambiguity isn't fully going away. It's the nature of what we're building and the fast-paced market we're in.

That's also what makes this exciting. We're at the point where the things we build reach real users, generate real revenue, and shape a market that barely existed two years ago. A team this small shouldn't be able to do what we're doing. But we can — if everyone operates at the level this moment demands.

The traits that mattered in the 0-1 phase — deep thinking before building, visible planning, fast context absorption — don't stop mattering once a v1 of these products launch. They matter more. The cost of drifting in the wrong direction only goes up as products have real users and real revenue attached to them.

So far, we haven't clearly defined what we expect from everyone on this team. So here it is.

This isn't a career ladder or a leveling framework. It's how we need people to operate — now and going forward.

Performance will be judged from now based on how you incorporate these traits.

---

## The Bar

### Think first, build second

This is the most important trait on this team. It's the foundation of everything else. It's also what lets a 20-person team compete with companies 10x our size.

AI has fundamentally shifted where the hard work lives. AI has made building fast and cheap. Writing code is no longer the bottleneck. Thinking is. Planning is. Understanding the problem deeply enough to point the tools in the right direction — that's the job now. The planning can still be done fast, but it can't be skipped. It's the highest-leverage thing you do.

Before you build anything, invest real time understanding the problem. Study the context. Research what great looks like. Understand the business reason behind what you're doing. Then work backwards from that into a detailed plan. If you don't feel like you have enough grasp on the problem, it's your job to figure out how to get it — ask questions, read the docs, talk to customers, study the market. Don't guess. Don't assume. Go get the understanding.

The best people on this team navigate fluidly between levels of detail. They can zoom into the implementation specifics and zoom back out to tie every decision to why the business needs it. They think in systems — from the code to the product to the customer to the business model. That range is what makes them dangerous.

We're also a vertically integrated company — our software serves our services, our services inform our software. Simplistic solutions don't solve anything here. You need the grit to dig into the real problem and find what delivers actual outcomes.

The worst thing you can do here is sprint into building without that understanding. Speed without direction is drift. You end up going quiet for days, building something in isolation, and surfacing with output that missed the mark. That's not a speed problem — it's a thinking problem.

### Work in the open

We're a remote company. Nobody can see you work. The only way the team knows what's happening is if you make it visible. When this works well, it's one of our biggest advantages — the whole team moves in sync without anyone being in the same room.

Share the plan before you execute. Share progress while you execute. A rough plan shared early beats a polished plan shared after the work is done wrong. If leadership has to chase you down to find out what happened, the process already failed.

This also means routing information to the right people. Know when something belongs in a channel, in a doc, or in front of leadership. Follow up to make sure it landed. The job isn't done when you hit send — it's done when the people who need the information have it and reviewed it.

<Note>
**The floor:** Weekly reflections and updates and at least 2 Geekbot updates per week. Leadership reads these. That's the bare minimum to operate on a remote team. If you can't maintain that cadence, nothing else on this list matters.
</Note>

### Chase the context

Read the docs. Understand the product, the space, the business, the customer. Build a mental model of why things exist, not just what they do. Know why the company makes money, who the customers are, and what matters strategically. The deeper your context, the better every decision you make gets — and the more autonomy you earn.

We work across multiple products in a market that changes monthly. Nobody is going to sit you down and explain everything. The context is there — in docs, in Slack, in the product itself. Go get it. The people who thrive here are the ones who close their own knowledge gaps without being prompted. They stay curious about everything: the product, the market, the tech, the customer. They stay current with the tools that matter — especially AI.

We have lanes, not silos. Not all 20 engineers build all 4 products — that's by design. But the best people keep tabs on their lane but still carve time to poke their heads out and see the forest from the trees. At minimum, read Daniel's Geekbot updates and his EPD channel summaries — that's an easy way to get the tactical company-wide status updates. Beyond that, read the channels that overlap with your work, ask questions, test other products, join QA when you can. The more you understand what's happening around you, the better your decisions get.

Dog food everything. Nothing we build is so far removed from our own work that you can't use it yourself. Working on CheckThat? Use it to track CheckThat's own AI visibility. Building Output.ai? Migrate a real part of our system onto it. Working on ContentOS? Use it with one of our own websites. If you feel like you don't understand what another team does, ask to be embedded there for a while. The fastest way to close a context gap is to experience the product as a user, not just as a builder.

We're too small for anyone to just write code or push pixels without understanding the business. If you can't explain what we do and why it matters, you can't make good decisions about what to build.

### Know when to sprint and when to be careful

Can-do attitude matters. So does knowing when "can-do" means slowing down to think about scalability, architecture, and the risk of what you're changing.

The best people on this team move fast on the right things and pump the brakes on the things that could break. They don't over-engineer low-relevancy tasks. They don't under-think complex or critical ones. That judgment — when to sprint and when to be careful — is critical. It's also what makes senior people here genuinely senior, regardless of title.

### Clean, intentional output

Planning is the highest-leverage phase of any project. Reviewing what comes out of execution is the second. Both require real attention.

Code is well-organized and easy to follow. Specs are clear and complete. Designs are deliberate. Abstractions are simple — if you can't explain why an abstraction exists in one sentence, it probably shouldn't. We've seen too many projects buried under unnecessary complexity that made the next person's job harder instead of easier. Keep it simple. Keep it clean. Follow the company's conventions and tech choices — not personal preferences. Consistency across the codebase matters more than individual style.

When you're working with AI tools, the output still needs to meet the same bar. AI can generate a lot of code fast — that makes reviewing it more important, not less. Read what it produces. Understand it. Refactor it. Don't merge output you wouldn't be comfortable defending in a review. The speed gain from AI is real, but only if the quality holds.

QA your own work. Manually. Before it reaches anyone else, click through it, break it, test the edge cases. Don't assume it works because. The best people on this team ship things they've personally used and verified — not things they've only looked at in a code editor.

This follows naturally from thinking first. If you understood the problem before building, the output is cleaner. If you didn't, no amount of review catches the deeper issue.

## How This Connects to Our Values

This isn't new. It's what our values mean in practice for EPD.

**Communicate with Clarity** means working in the open — planning visibly, routing information to the right people, and writing that doesn't require follow-up questions.

**Own the Outcome** means thinking before building and executing independently. Not waiting for direction. Not treating your scope as someone else's problem once it leaves your hands.

**Relentless Excellence** means clean work that ships without someone checking behind you. Standards you hold yourself to before anyone else does.

**Never Stop Learning** means chasing the context, staying curious, and closing your own knowledge gaps without being prompted. Especially when it comes to AI — the tools are changing fast and we expect everyone to keep up.

---

## The Bottom Line

Not everyone thrives in this kind of environment. That's not a judgment on talent or experience. But this is how we need to operate, and that's not changing.

Title and tenure don't determine your impact here. How you operate does. If this way of working energizes you, you're in the right place. If it doesn't, that's worth an honest conversation.

The people who thrive here will have more impact, more autonomy, and more growth than at companies five times our size. That's the trade. The bar is high because the opportunity is real.
