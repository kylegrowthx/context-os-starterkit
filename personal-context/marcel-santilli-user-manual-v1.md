## The Bottom Line

Marcel is a systems-thinking founder who built GrowthX to solve his own 15-year pain. He operates with high intensity, thinks in feedback loops, and values data over opinions. He's direct about problems, transparent about his own limitations, and expects you to own your domain while calibrating to his standards.

This guide tells you what he won't always say explicitly. Use it to accelerate your working relationship.

## 1. My Role and What I'm Here For

**What I provide:**

Direction, blockers removed, and help thinking through hard problems. My job is connecting dots across the business you can't see from your seat. I bring context, frameworks, and pattern recognition from 15 years of doing this.

I'm also the escalation path for high-stakes client relationships. When an account is at risk, I can step in with credibility that takes time to build.

**What I expect from you:**

Own your domain. I'll set the north star, but you navigate. "Standards, not steps" means I define what great looks like, but I won't prescribe how you get there.

> "I need you to make sure you know that answer, because I can't focus on that answer right now."

**What NOT to expect:**

Don't expect me to be your functional mentor. As I've told candidates: "I try very hard to hire leaders that I would like to work for myself and are meaningfully better than me at the function you lead."

I'm also not your project manager. If you need constant check-ins to stay on track, we have a fit problem.

**Where I add most value:**

- High-stakes client conversations where trust is at risk
- Strategic sessions that require connecting disparate dots
- Selling sophisticated buyers who need intellectual respect
- Teaching frameworks and codifying knowledge
- Navigating complexity when others feel overwhelmed

## 2. Communication Preferences

**Channel hierarchy (most urgent to least):**

| Channel | Use For | My Response Time |
|---------|---------|------------------|
| Call/Text | True emergencies, time-sensitive client issues | Immediate |
| Slack DM | Quick questions, need input same day | Within hours |
| Slack Channel | Team coordination, FYI updates | Same day |
| Email | External communication, formal records | 24 hours |

**How I prefer information:**

Written over verbal. Write it down first. I need to see it to think about it.

> "He seems to really like the give me something to read, you know, or listen to in advance. He's like, I don't want to randomize the team by showing up and not having consumed it in advance."

Send me context before meetings. A 2-minute read saves 20 minutes of live explanation. I process better when I've thought about it beforehand.

**When to interrupt me:**

- Client escalations that could cost us the account
- Decisions that are blocked and time-sensitive
- Information that changes a decision we already made

**When NOT to interrupt me:**

- Questions you could answer with 10 minutes of research
- FYIs that don't require action
- Problems you haven't thought through yet

**What my silence means:**

If I haven't responded, it's not approval. It's that I'm buried. Follow up.

## 3. Decision-Making Philosophy

**What needs my approval:**

- Pricing and commercial terms
- Scope changes on major accounts
- Anything that changes our positioning or public commitments
- Hiring and compensation decisions
- Strategic product direction

> "Someone asks to change priority and it's not me or Daniel, do not listen."

**What you should decide:**

- Tactical execution within your domain
- Process improvements that don't change outcomes
- Day-to-day client management
- How you organize your work

**How to bring me a decision:**

Come with a recommendation, not an open question. Show me:
1. The problem (with data)
2. Options you considered
3. Your recommendation and why
4. What you need from me

I edit faster than I build from scratch.

**My decision-making frameworks:**

**Validation before scaling:** Never invest at $100K scale before proving it works at $10K.

> "Hitting up Mario and asking him to swipe a credit card doesn't count."

Real validation means strangers pay. Friends don't count.

**Type 1 vs Type 2 doors:** Reversible decisions should be fast. Irreversible ones need care. Most decisions are Type 2. Move.

**Disagree and commit:** Voice disagreement with data. Once we decide, commit genuinely. No "I told you so" later.

## 4. Meeting Preferences and 1:1 Structure

**How I run internal meetings:**

- Open with brief warmth, then business
- Numbers first. What's the scorecard say?
- Probing questions to test understanding
- Direct feedback in the moment
- Close with clear action items and owners

**1:1 structure:**

You own the agenda. Bring:
- What you're working on (briefly)
- Where you need input or decisions
- Issues or blockers
- What you need from me

Don't waste 1:1 time on status updates I could read in Slack.

**What energizes me in meetings:**

Strategic depth. Pattern recognition. Connecting dots across domains. Teaching frameworks.

**What drains me:**

> "In one single day I'm being hit from like loans, legal board, product design, CheckThat launch, growth, marketing, sales, delivery, churn, risk... I just can't operate like at all."

Context switching kills my effectiveness. Clustered, focused time is how I do my best work.

**Protect my family time:**

4:30-7:30pm is family time. I protect it fiercely. Don't schedule calls in this window unless it's urgent.

## 5. How I Give and Receive Feedback

**How I give feedback:**

Directly and in the moment. I don't save things for performance reviews.

> "Kyle's not a good negotiator. Also not good at pushing commercial terms and he did not do well in sales. So like Kyle is not a good person to be pushing renewals."

This directness is me being clear, not personal. If I'm vague, you won't know where you stand. I'd rather be uncomfortable than leave you guessing.

**How to receive my feedback:**

- Take it at face value. I don't soften or hint.
- Ask clarifying questions if you need them.
- Tell me if you disagree, but bring data.
- Don't get defensive. I'm trying to help you win.

**How to give ME feedback:**

I'm receptive when you ground it in data and examples. "I noticed X happened three times this week" lands better than "I feel like you're doing Y."

Call me out in the moment. I'd rather know now than find out later that something wasn't working.

I explicitly want to improve. As I tell candidates: "Transparency and directness are part of our values and I want to live them by sharing the flaws I know I have."

**What makes me most receptive:**

- When you've thought it through
- When you bring evidence
- When you're trying to solve, not just vent
- When you tell me privately first

## 6. Accountability and Expectations

**What accountability means to me:**

Know your numbers. Cold.

> "If we did no kickoffs rest of the year, by the end of the year, we were probably 50% of the revenue we are today."

I think in metrics. Volume, throughput, velocity. If you can't tell me your scorecard without looking it up, we have a problem.

**How I distinguish effort from results:**

Results matter. Effort without outcomes is just activity.

> "I don't feel like the level of experimentation and testing is kind of like we're doing stuff versus like, what do we need to do to crack it."

I'd rather you try something that fails fast than stay busy on things that don't move the needle.

**How to handle blockers:**

Escalate early. Don't wait until you're stuck.

> "Flying blind is not an option. So you just tell us what we need and you just escalate as hard and as aggressively as humanly possible."

Tell me what you need. Be specific. "I need a decision by Thursday" is actionable. "I'm kind of blocked" isn't.

**The difference between a mistake and a pattern:**

Mistakes happen. I diagnose before I blame. My default question is "what happened," not "who failed."

But patterns are different. If the same problem keeps recurring, something's broken. Either the system or the person.

> "There's nothing fine until it's signed and confirmed. And until it's signed and confirmed and it's the next one coming up and it's not confirmed, there's nothing fine in it."

Optimism without confirmation is a pattern I won't tolerate.

## 7. How I Operate Under Stress

**What stress looks like on me:**

I extrapolate to worst-case scenarios.

> "At this pace, the community will be at zero in two months... check that will be a zero in two months, you know, and it's like dead in the water."

This catastrophic thinking is me processing urgency out loud. It's not a prediction. It's me saying "this matters, we need to move."

**My stress triggers:**

- Organizational chaos and uncertainty
- Not seeing the full picture
- Same problems recurring despite prior discussion
- "It'll be fine" thinking when nothing is confirmed
- Multiple context switches in rapid succession

> "My ADHD plus anxiety plus other things, like, gets way worse when I feel like there's a lot of things that I don't see a picture."

**What I need from you during crises:**

- Clarity on what's happening (facts, not interpretations)
- Options, not just problems
- Quick turnaround on information requests
- Don't add to the chaos with unprocessed updates

**How to work with me when I'm stressed:**

Bring structure. Help me see the picture. Give me something concrete to react to, not open-ended problems.

If I seem intense, it's not personal. I'm in problem-solving mode. Match the energy with solutions, not defensiveness.

## 8. Known Flaws and Development Areas

**What I'm working on:**

**High-context leadership:** I expect the team to infer priorities from signals. This creates ambiguity. I'm working on being more explicit.

> "I will say things... and those things get lost in translation."

**The replicability problem:** Clients are "buying Marcel." My unique contribution doesn't transfer easily through standard processes. I need to codify more of what I do instinctively.

**Catastrophic extrapolation:** Under stress, I project current problems to existential outcomes. This creates urgency but can distort perspective.

**Intensity that doesn't sustain:** 18,000 lines of code on a Sunday is not a sustainable pace. I've been in dark places after intense periods.

**How you can help me improve:**

- Ask clarifying questions when my direction feels ambiguous
- Tell me when my stress is creating unnecessary urgency
- Push back when I'm over-involved in things you should own
- Flag when my standards feel unclear or shifting

I list these flaws because I want to improve. Calling them out is helping, not criticizing.
