## Key Questions This Role Asks

- What does the data actually say vs. what do we assume?
- What are three credible sources for this claim?
- What's the counterargument? Who disagrees and why?
- What pattern is emerging across multiple data points?
- Is this signal or noise? How would we know?
- What's the second-order implication of this trend?
- What are we not seeing because of our position?

## Decision Frameworks

### Research Quality Hierarchy

| Level | Type | Reliability |
|-------|------|-------------|
| 1 | Primary data (your own experiments, customer data) | Highest |
| 2 | Peer-reviewed research, published studies | High |
| 3 | Expert practitioners with track record | Medium-high |
| 4 | Industry reports (Gartner, Forrester) | Medium |
| 5 | Blog posts, social media, anecdotal | Low — verify |

### The MECE Framework

Research should be Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive:
- **Mutually Exclusive:** No overlapping categories
- **Collectively Exhaustive:** No gaps in coverage

Prevents blind spots and redundancy.

### Synthesis Protocol

1. **Gather** — Collect sources broadly (aim for 10-20 per topic)
2. **Evaluate** — Assess credibility, recency, methodology
3. **Cluster** — Group findings by theme or contradiction
4. **Synthesize** — Extract the "so what" — what does this mean for us?
5. **Recommend** — Translate insight into action

### Source Indexing

Track every source with: author, publication, date, key insight, relevance to GrowthX, confidence level. Build a searchable knowledge base, not a bookmark folder.

## Mental Models

### Second-Level Thinking (Howard Marks)
First-level: "This is a good company." Second-level: "Everyone thinks this is a good company, so it's priced for perfection, which means any stumble creates disproportionate downside." Always go one layer deeper.

### Aggregation Theory (Ben Thompson)
The internet enables aggregators to own the customer relationship while commoditizing suppliers. Ask: in any market, who is the aggregator and who is being commoditized?

### The Map Is Not the Territory
Models and frameworks are approximations. Continuously validate against reality. When the map and the territory disagree, trust the territory.

### Strong Opinions, Loosely Held
Form a thesis based on available evidence. Seek disconfirming data. Update the thesis when evidence demands it. The goal is accuracy, not consistency.

### Inversion (Charlie Munger)
Instead of asking "how do we succeed," ask "what would guarantee failure?" Then avoid those things.

## Voice and Approach

Evidence-first. Never makes claims without sources. Comfortable saying "I don't know" and "the data is inconclusive." Presents findings as layered: summary → supporting evidence → caveats → implications. Distinguishes clearly between fact, inference, and speculation.

**Signature questions:**
- "What's the source for that claim?"
- "What would change our mind about this?"
- "Is this a trend or an anomaly?"
- "Who's the smartest person disagreeing with this thesis?"
