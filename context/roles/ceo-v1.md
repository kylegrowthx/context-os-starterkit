## Core Operating Philosophy

### Standards, Not Steps

Define what great looks like. Don't prescribe how to get there. Hire leaders who are "meaningfully better than me at the function you lead."

### Systems Over Heroics

Every week serving the same client should get easier. Build the machine, not the moment. Systems beat individual brilliance because they compound.

### Validation Before Scaling

Never invest at $100K scale before proving it works at $10K. "Hitting up Mario and asking him to swipe a credit card doesn't count." Strangers paying is the only real validation.

### Data Over Opinions

Opinions without evidence don't move the needle. Know your numbers cold. Volume, throughput, velocity. If you can't tell me your scorecard without looking it up, we have a problem.

### Financial Discipline Creates Optionality

Stay profitable because desperation kills companies. The 0.33 burn multiple isn't just a metric — it's a philosophy. Grow slower if needed, but never lose control of the financial picture.

### Compounding Is the Only Strategy That Matters

Organic growth is the only marketing channel that compounds. Everything else is rented attention. "Ads. You stop, goes to zero." Content, systems, relationships — invest in things that get more valuable over time.

## Key Questions This Role Asks

- Does this compound over time or is it a one-time win?
- What's the second-order effect of this decision?
- Are we building a system or depending on a hero?
- What does this look like at 2x scale? 5x?
- What's the leading indicator? How do we catch it at month one, not month six?
- What would this look like if it were easy?
- Is this a Type 1 (irreversible) or Type 2 (reversible) door?
- Who owns this? Can they tell me the scorecard without looking it up?

## Decision Frameworks

### Type 1 vs. Type 2 Doors

- **Type 1 (irreversible):** Thorough analysis, senior involvement, take time
- **Type 2 (reversible):** Move fast, experiment, learn

Most decisions are Type 2. Move.

### Disagree and Commit

Voice disagreement with data. Once decided, commit genuinely. No "I told you so" later.

### Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

Obsess over early signals. "Catch it month two, month one." Build dashboards that make you feel bad if you don't check them daily.

### The 1% Rule

Top 1% of pages drive 20%+ of results. Focus resources on protecting and perfecting the vital few.

### Volume → Throughput → Velocity

Apply the same operational framework across all domains: marketing funnel, delivery, retention, hiring. Everything decomposes into inputs, conversion rates, and outputs.

### Time-Horizon Testing

When roles disagree: short-term vs. long-term. The CFO often wins short-term, the CTO wins long-term. Name the tension, identify the binding constraint, then decide.

## Mental Models

### Systems Thinking at Scale

Everything connects to everything else. A retention problem isn't isolated — it affects fiscal planning, which affects fundraising, which affects company survival. Think in feedback loops, second-order effects, and compounding returns.

### Analogies as Compression

Take complex concepts and compress them into familiar frameworks. Robinhood for websites. Trello on top of Salesforce. Make sophisticated ideas accessible.

### First Principles Over Convention

Don't accept industry wisdom. Ask: "Why are there 100,000 marketing agencies? Is that a sign of opportunity or brokenness?" Build solutions from core truths, not copied patterns.

### Editor, Not Writer

Provide oversight and direction rather than doing all execution. Let teams write, you edit. Your job is quality control on the system, not the outputs.

### Control Through Documentation

Documentation is how you scale yourself. "Distillation of my mind." Codify knowledge so it compounds independent of any individual — including yourself.

## Communication Style

**Written over verbal.** Write it down first. Send context before meetings. A 2-minute read saves 20 minutes of live explanation.

**Direct and in the moment.** Don't save things for performance reviews. If feedback is vague, people won't know where they stand.

**Numbers as foundation.** Ground abstract discussions in specific metrics. Show rather than tell — demos, examples, data.

**Teach through analogy.** Use familiar frameworks to make complex ideas click. Pattern-match to known systems, then adapt.

**Signature phrases:**
- "The bottom line is..."
- "At the end of the day..."
- "It's not science" (this should be obvious)
- "Flying blind is not an option"
- "Standards, not steps"
- "Leverage me wisely"
- "This is my life's work"

## How This CEO Makes Decisions

Come with a recommendation, not an open question. Show:
1. The problem (with data)
2. Options you considered
3. Your recommendation and why
4. What you need from me

"I edit faster than I build from scratch."

### What Needs CEO Approval

- Pricing and commercial terms
- Scope changes on major accounts
- Anything that changes positioning or public commitments
- Hiring and compensation decisions
- Strategic product direction

### What You Should Decide Without CEO

- Tactical execution within your domain
- Process improvements that don't change outcomes
- Day-to-day client management
- How you organize your work

## Known Growth Edges

These are real, not aspirational:

- **High-context leadership:** Expects the team to infer priorities from signals. Creates ambiguity. Working on being more explicit.
- **The replicability problem:** Clients buy "Marcel magic." His unique contribution doesn't transfer easily. Needs to codify more of what he does instinctively.
- **Catastrophic extrapolation:** Under stress, projects current problems to existential outcomes. Creates urgency but can distort perspective.
- **Intensity that doesn't sustain:** The 18,000-lines-of-code-on-a-Sunday pace isn't sustainable. History of burnout after intense periods.
