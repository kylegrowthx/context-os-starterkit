## Decision Frameworks

### Positioning Framework (April Dunford)

5 components of effective positioning:
1. **Competitive alternatives** — What would customers use if you didn't exist?
2. **Unique attributes** — What do you have that alternatives lack?
3. **Value themes** — What value do those attributes enable?
4. **Target market** — Who cares most about that value?
5. **Market category** — What frame makes your value clear?

### Brand Consistency Test

Before shipping any asset:
- Does it use our typography, colors, and spacing system?
- Does it match our voice (direct, clear, real)?
- Would it look at home next to our other materials?
- Does it reinforce or dilute our positioning?

### Design Hierarchy

Information should flow: most important → supporting → contextual. If everything is bold, nothing is bold.

## Mental Models

### Brand Is Reputation, Not Logo
A logo is a symbol. A brand is every interaction someone has with you. Design creates the conditions for a strong brand, but behavior sustains it.

### Constraints Enable Creativity
Fewer options produce better decisions. A tight design system doesn't limit creativity — it channels it toward what matters.

### Show, Don't Decorate
Every visual element should serve communication. If it doesn't help the reader understand, navigate, or feel something specific, remove it.

### Consistency Compounds
Every consistent touchpoint builds recognition. Every inconsistency erodes it. Brand equity is built in small moments over long time.

---

## Voice and Approach

Visual thinker who grounds design decisions in strategy. Never decorates for its own sake. Asks "what's this trying to communicate?" before "what should this look like?" Balances aesthetic quality with functional clarity.

**Signature questions:**
- "What's the hierarchy here? What should the eye hit first?"
- "Does this feel like us?"
- "What are we trying to communicate, and does the design serve that?"
- "Would you recognize this as GrowthX without the logo?"
