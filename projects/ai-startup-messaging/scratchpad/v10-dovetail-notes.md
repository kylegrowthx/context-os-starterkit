# V10 (Dovetail-Inspired) — Improvement Notes

Version 10 borrows Dovetail's messaging architecture but doesn't fully absorb what makes it work. The structure is right. The execution needs sharpening in five areas: density, specificity, restraint, proof, and rhythm.

---

## 1. What Dovetail Does Well That V10 Misses

### Extreme headline economy

Dovetail's tagline is five words: **"The best never guess."** Every word earns its place. Identity claim + behavioral standard + implied consequence. No fat.

V10's tagline is eight words: **"The best never hope their website works."** The extra three words ("their website works") dilute the punch. "Hope" is weaker than "guess" — hoping is passive, guessing is an active failure. And "their website works" narrows the frame to websites before the reader has been primed to care about websites.

Dovetail's hero is eight words: **"Get total clarity from scattered user feedback."** One adjective per noun. Clean transformation arc.

V10's hero is ten words: **"Get compounding clarity from scattered growth operations."** "Compounding clarity" is not a natural phrase — clarity doesn't compound, returns do. "Growth operations" is internal jargon that a VP of Marketing wouldn't use to describe their own problem.

### ROI metrics that are specific, not ranged

Dovetail leads with:
- **2.3x** return on investment
- **30hrs** weekly time saved per user
- **66%** faster shipping

Every number is singular and precise. The Forrester TEI study gives external validation — these aren't self-reported claims.

V10 leads with:
- **2-4x** organic traffic growth within 12 months
- **80-90%** of content production handled by AI
- **Compounding** returns that accelerate every quarter

A range (2-4x) signals uncertainty. A process metric (80-90% AI production) tells the buyer how you work, not what they get. "Compounding returns" has no number at all — it's a descriptor pretending to be a metric.

### Workflow sub-headlines that are verb-driven outcomes

Dovetail's workflow headlines:
- "Ingest feedback from everywhere"
- "Identify the next thing to work on"
- "Go from feedback to code"
- "Close the loop with AI agents"

Every one starts with a verb and ends with a concrete outcome. "Go from feedback to code" is especially strong — it's a transformation compressed into six words.

V10's workflow headlines:
- "See everything."
- "Turn intelligence into direction."
- "AI handles volume. Humans handle judgment."
- "The system learns."

"See everything" is vague — what am I seeing? "Turn intelligence into direction" is abstract — what direction? "The system learns" is passive and doesn't tell the buyer what happens next. Only "AI handles volume. Humans handle judgment" works — it's a concrete structural claim.

### Social proof as architecture, not decoration

Dovetail deploys proof at four distinct moments:
1. **Logo bar** immediately after the hero (Shopify, AWS, Atlassian, Notion, Canva, Lovable)
2. **Third-party study** (Forrester TEI) linked from the ROI section
3. **Customer story carousel** with named humans and direct quotes (Breville, Atlassian, Itaú Unibanco)
4. **Review scores** (G2 4.5/5, Capterra 4.6/5)

V10 has **zero proof elements**. No logos, no quotes, no case studies, no third-party validation. The trust section claims "Proven Results" without proving anything.

### Sub-features that expand without overwhelming

Under each Dovetail workflow step, there are 1-3 named sub-features (AI Projects, AI Channels, AI Dashboards, etc.) with one-line descriptions. The reader can scan or drill in. The consistent "AI [Noun]" naming convention makes the platform feel cohesive.

V10 lists 3 bullet points under each step, but they're descriptions of work, not features:
- "Full-stack growth audit (search + AI + content + competitive)"
- "Content decay detection and gap analysis"
- "AI content engine with human calibration"

These read like a statement of work, not a product page. Dovetail describes capabilities; V10 describes tasks.

## 2. Weak Spots

### "Compounding" fatigue

The word "compounding" (or "compounds") appears **six times**:

1. "They build a system that compounds."
2. "Get compounding clarity..."
3. "starts compounding what works."
4. "Compounding returns that accelerate every quarter"
5. "Compounding returns that accelerate, not plateau"
6. "Turn your website into a compounding growth engine."

Dovetail uses "clarity" exactly twice on the entire page. When you repeat your core concept this aggressively, it stops feeling like conviction and starts feeling like you only have one idea. The word loses meaning through saturation.

**Fix:** Use "compounding" a maximum of 2-3 times. Reserve it for the moments that matter most — the hero and the close. Use synonyms or downstream consequences elsewhere: "accelerating," "multiplying," "stacking," "building on itself."

### The sub-headline does no work

> "They build a system that compounds."

This follows "The best never hope their website works." It should escalate or reframe. Instead it restates the same idea in weaker language. "They build a system" is generic. What kind of system? For whom?

Dovetail doesn't have a sub-headline — the hero does all the work. If V10 keeps a two-line open, the second line needs to add new information.

**Suggested replacement:**
> "They build one that compounds."

Or drop the sub-headline entirely and let the hero do the lifting, Dovetail-style.

### The hero tries to do three things

> "GrowthX's AI centralizes your content, search, and AI visibility data into one growth engine — so your team stops guessing what to publish and starts compounding what works."

This sentence is doing too much:
- Naming the mechanism (AI centralizes)
- Listing what gets centralized (content, search, AI visibility data)
- Naming the output (growth engine)
- Describing the before state (guessing what to publish)
- Describing the after state (compounding what works)

Dovetail's sub-hero does two things: mechanism + outcome.

> "Dovetail's AI centralizes and analyzes your customer data to pinpoint the work that drives usage and revenue, so your team can build with confidence."

**Suggested replacement:**
> "GrowthX runs your content, search, and AI visibility as one system — so every page you publish compounds what's already working."

### Strategy Sprint is buried and undersold

The Strategy Sprint is GrowthX's most differentiated product element — an 8-week engagement with a decision point at Week 6. It's the "try before you buy" mechanism. But it appears as a subordinate clause inside Step 02:

> "Strategy Sprint validates a growth thesis with data before you commit. Eight weeks. Decision at Week 6."

This should be elevated. Dovetail doesn't have anything like this — it's a SaaS tool with a free trial. GrowthX is services + software. The Sprint is the on-ramp, and it deserves its own moment.

### "Single growth engine" section is cryptic

> "Three audiences. One engine. Every page. Every quarter."

A prospect reading this doesn't know what "three audiences" means. (Buyers, AI agents, training bots — but the page hasn't explained this.) The staccato rhythm mimics Dovetail's "Unifying product, sales, marketing, and CX" but loses the clarity. Dovetail names the four functions it unifies. V10 names a concept (three audiences) that requires prior context.

**Fix:** Either introduce the three-audience concept earlier in the page, or replace this line with something self-explanatory:

> "One system for content strategy, search performance, and AI visibility. Every page. Every quarter."

### Trust section is vague where Dovetail is specific

Dovetail: SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001, HIPAA, GDPR, MCP Server.

V10:
- "Data Sovereignty — Your content data stays yours."
- "Dedicated Teams — Named strategists, not ticket queues."
- "Proven Results — Compounding metrics that show acceleration."

These are marketing claims, not trust signals. "Data Sovereignty" with no certification backing is just a phrase. "Proven Results" with no proof is ironic.

**Fix:** Either add real trust proof (client logos, named results, specific certifications/compliances) or reframe the section around what GrowthX can actually prove — team structure, process transparency, and result attribution.

## 3. Structural Improvements

### Add a logo bar after the hero

Dovetail places Shopify/AWS/Atlassian/Notion/Canva/Lovable immediately after the hero. GrowthX has real clients (Webflow, Abnormal AI, Lovable, Exec). This is the single highest-leverage structural addition — it converts "we say we're good" into "these companies trust us."

Position it between the ROI metrics and the workflow, with copy like:
> "Powering growth for:"

### Elevate the Strategy Sprint into its own section

Right now the Sprint is a bullet point. It should be a mid-page anchor (like Dovetail's "Single source of truth" moment). Pattern:

> **Start with a Sprint. Stay because it compounds.**
> 8 weeks. Full-stack growth audit. Data-backed strategy. Decision at Week 6.
> No long-term contract until you see the system working.

This serves as a commitment-reducer (try before you buy) and differentiator (no one else offers this structure).

### Add a customer proof section

Even a single customer quote would transform the page. Dovetail has three rotating stories. GrowthX could have one:

> **[Webflow logo]**
> "Quote about specific result."
> — Name, Title at Webflow

If no publishable quotes exist yet, this is the most urgent content gap to close.

### Shorten the workflow bullet points

Each V10 step has a paragraph + 3 bullets. Dovetail has a paragraph + named feature links. The bullets in V10 read like a scope of work:

> - Full-stack growth audit (search + AI + content + competitive)
> - CheckThat AI visibility baseline (172 categories, 5,800+ brands)
> - Content decay detection and gap analysis

**Fix:** Cut to one paragraph per step. Move the detailed feature descriptions to a product page or expandable sections (like Dovetail's "Explore Data Analysis features" links).

### Add a Forrester-equivalent proof artifact

Dovetail links to a Forrester TEI study from the ROI section. GrowthX doesn't have a Forrester study, but it could create:
- A published case study with named metrics
- A "Growth Intelligence Report" showing aggregate client results
- A CheckThat benchmark report as an authority artifact

Until this exists, the ROI metrics will feel self-reported. Flag this as a content priority.

## 4. Language/Tone Improvements

### Replace abstract nouns with concrete verbs

V10 overuses nominalization — turning verbs into nouns. This makes the language feel corporate instead of direct.

| V10 (abstract) | Suggested (concrete) |
|---|---|
| "Turn intelligence into direction" | "Know what to build next" |
| "The system learns" | "Every result sharpens the next move" |
| "Compounding returns that accelerate every quarter" | "Returns that double every quarter" |
| "Auto-prioritized growth signals" | "The system flags what to fix first" |
| "Continuous optimization loop" | "Performance data feeds back automatically" |

### Make the tagline shorter and sharper

Current: **"The best never hope their website works."** (8 words)

Options:
- **"The best never hope."** (4 words — mirrors Dovetail's 5-word structure, provocative)
- **"The best compound."** (3 words — identity claim + core concept)
- **"The best never publish and pray."** (6 words — more specific to content marketing)

Recommendation: **"The best never hope."** It's punchy, ambiguous enough to hook, and the hero headline resolves what "hoping" means.

### Fix the hero transformation arc

Current: "Get compounding clarity from scattered growth operations."

"Compounding clarity" is a concept clash. Clarity is a state; compounding is a process. They don't naturally pair.

**Suggested:** "Get compounding returns from scattered content operations."

Or, staying closer to Dovetail's "total clarity" formula: "Get total visibility across scattered growth channels."

Or, the boldest option: "Turn scattered content into a compounding growth engine."

### Stop parenthetical feature dumps

V10 has parenthetical asides that break reading flow:
- "(search + AI + content + competitive)"
- "(172 categories, 5,800+ brands)"
- "(highest-impact pages first)"

These are useful details, but they interrupt the narrative. Move them to sub-text, tooltips, or expandable sections. Dovetail never uses parentheticals on the homepage.

### The close should echo the open

Dovetail opens with "The best never guess" and closes with "Turn customer feedback into product innovation." The close completes the arc: if you don't guess, you innovate.

V10 opens with "The best never hope their website works" and closes with "Turn your website into a compounding growth engine." The close repeats words from the middle of the page. It doesn't feel like an arrival.

**Suggested close:** "Stop hoping. Start compounding." — This echoes the open and compresses the entire pitch into four words.

Or: "See what compounding looks like for your business." — direct and curiosity-driven.

## 5. What to Keep

These elements work and should survive into the next draft:

1. **"AI handles volume. Humans handle judgment."** — This is the single best line in V10. It's a structural claim that no competitor makes this clearly. Consider elevating it from a Step 03 sub-headline to a more prominent position (possibly the sub-hero).

2. **The 4-step numbered workflow** (Audit → Strategize → Produce → Compound) — The structure is right. The step names are right. Only the descriptions need sharpening.

3. **"Eight weeks. Decision at Week 6."** — Specific, commitment-reducing, differentiating. Needs to be elevated, not buried.

4. **The "scattered → engine" transformation** — The before/after is clear and maps well to the buyer's actual experience. Just fix the specific wording.

5. **"Every piece of content built to rank in search and appear in AI answers"** — This is the three-audience thesis compressed into one line. It names what's different about GrowthX's approach without requiring the buyer to understand the framework.

6. **"Manage hundreds of pages without hundreds of people"** — Concrete, memorable, scales the value. Keep.

7. **"Strategies get sharper. Content gets more effective."** — Clean parallel construction. Could be the close of the workflow section instead of buried in Step 04.

## 6. Top 5 Highest-Impact Changes

### 1. Cut "compounding" to 2-3 uses maximum

**Current state:** 6 occurrences. The word has lost all force by mid-page.

**Action:** Keep it in the hero and the closing CTA. Replace all other instances with specific consequences — "accelerating returns," "stacking gains," "building on every result," or just the actual numbers.

**Why highest impact:** This single change will make the entire page feel less repetitive and more confident. Dovetail says "clarity" twice. That's the standard.

### 2. Add a client logo bar and at least one customer quote

**Current state:** Zero social proof anywhere on the page.

**Action:** Logo bar (Webflow, Abnormal AI, Lovable, Exec) after the hero metrics. One customer quote after the workflow section. Even "Webflow trusts GrowthX to..." with a logo is better than nothing.

**Why highest impact:** Social proof is the single largest trust accelerator on B2B homepages. Dovetail uses four distinct proof mechanisms. V10 uses zero. This is the biggest credibility gap.

### 3. Sharpen the tagline from 8 words to 4-5

**Current:** "The best never hope their website works."
**Suggested:** "The best never hope."

**Why highest impact:** The tagline is the first thing a visitor reads. It sets the entire tone. Eight words is too many for a banner tagline — Dovetail proves that five words can do the job. Cutting "their website works" forces the hero to do the explaining (which it should).

### 4. Elevate the Strategy Sprint into its own section

**Current:** Buried as a subordinate clause in Step 02.
**Action:** Create a dedicated mid-page section between the workflow and the trust block. Frame it as the low-commitment entry point:

> **Start with a Sprint.**
> 8 weeks. Data-backed growth thesis. Decision at Week 6. No long-term contract until you've seen the system work.

**Why highest impact:** The Strategy Sprint is GrowthX's biggest differentiator from both agencies (who want annual retainers) and tools (who want subscriptions). Dovetail's equivalent is "Try Dovetail free." GrowthX needs its own version of "try before you commit" — and the Sprint is it.

### 5. Rewrite workflow descriptions as outcomes, not scopes of work

**Current:** Each step reads like a proposal — "Full-stack growth audit (search + AI + content + competitive)" / "Growth thesis built on actual performance data" / "AI content engine with human calibration."

**Action:** Rewrite each step to lead with what the buyer gets, not what GrowthX does:

| Step | Current lead | Suggested lead |
|---|---|---|
| Audit | "Full-stack growth audit" | "See every gap, every opportunity, every competitor move" |
| Strategize | "Growth thesis built on actual performance data" | "Know exactly what to build and why" |
| Produce | "AI content engine with human calibration" | "Ship 10x more content without 10x the team" |
| Compound | "Continuous optimization loop" | "Every quarter gets better than the last" |

**Why highest impact:** Buyers don't buy processes. They buy outcomes. Dovetail's "Go from feedback to code" works because it describes what the buyer experiences, not what the software does. V10 needs the same shift.

---

## Summary

V10 has the right bones. The four-step workflow, the scattered→engine arc, the "AI + humans" structural claim, and the Strategy Sprint are all strong raw material. The problems are: word repetition (compounding x6), missing proof (zero logos/quotes), abstract language (nominalizations instead of verbs), buried differentiators (Sprint in a bullet point), and scope-of-work framing instead of outcome framing.

The Dovetail pattern to internalize isn't the specific words — it's the ratio: **short tagline → specific metrics → verb-driven workflow → social proof → bold anchor → real trust signals → clean close.** V10 has most of these sections but hasn't matched the density and specificity that makes Dovetail's version work.
