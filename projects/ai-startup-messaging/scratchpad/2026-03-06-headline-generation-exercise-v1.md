# Headline Generation Exercise — GrowthX V3 Homepage

## Audience

- Founder / CEO of a fast-growing B2B company
- Head of Marketing / Head of Growth under pressure to grow
- Company that feels the squeeze: content isn't working, traffic plateaued, competitors moving faster, AI changing the game

## Scoring Rubric (7 Dimensions, 1-10 each, max 70)

| Dimension | What it measures | 10 looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **Clarity** | Can a founder/CMO understand the promise in <3 seconds? | Instantly obvious what GrowthX does or delivers |
| **Differentiation** | Does this separate GrowthX from agencies, tools, consultants? | Could only be said by GrowthX — not interchangeable |
| **Emotional Punch** | Does it create urgency, curiosity, or "I need this"? | Stops the scroll, creates a gut reaction |
| **Credibility Signal** | Does it convey deep expertise, not marketing fluff? | Feels like it was written by someone who's done the job |
| **Specificity** | Does it name a concrete pain, outcome, or mechanism? | Names a real number, timeframe, pain, or result |
| **Memorability** | Could someone repeat this to a colleague after one read? | Sticky — the kind of phrase people borrow |
| **Action Pull** | Does it make the reader want to scroll/learn more? | Creates an open loop or a desire to understand how |

## Stack Ranking: Patterns and Analysis

### What the top 20 reveal

The highest-scoring headlines cluster around three patterns:

**Pattern 1: The Three-Audience Insight (7 of top 22)**
Headlines #76, #87, #79, #94, #84, #78, #81 — all variations of the three-audience framework. This is GrowthX's most differentiated idea. When it's stated clearly, it scores high on differentiation (8-9), emotional punch (8-9), and action pull (8-9) because it reframes the buyer's entire worldview. The best versions state the insight as a revelation, not a fact dump.

**Pattern 2: AI-as-Threat-to-You-Specifically (5 of top 22)**
Headlines #10, #96, #7, #236, #81 — these don't talk about "AI" abstractly. They make it personal: AI is answering YOUR buyer's questions, training on YOUR competitor's content, recommending THEM instead of you. The personalization is what creates the gut punch. Generic "AI is changing marketing" scores low. "AI is doing X to YOUR business right now" scores high.

**Pattern 3: Credibility Through Specificity (5 of top 22)**
Headlines #177, #152, #37, #205, #213 — these use specific numbers, timeframes, or autobiographical detail that can't be faked: "15 years as a CMO," "80/20 split," "ranks in Google AND ChatGPT," "8 weeks or walk away." Specificity signals credibility without claiming it.

### What doesn't work

**Category labels as headlines** score lowest (22-36 range). "The growth operating system," "Content marketing infrastructure," "The AI growth engine for B2B" — these are category shelf labels, not headlines. They tell the reader where to file you, but don't make them care.

**Generic transformations** ("From X to Y") average 31-38 unless the X and Y are viscerally specific. "From scattered content to compounding growth" is wallpaper. "From invisible to inevitable" has punch but no clarity.

**Questions without stakes** ("What if your content compounded?") score lower than questions with implied threat ("Does AI recommend you? Or your competitor?"). The question needs to expose a gap the reader didn't know they had.

### The ideal headline formula for GrowthX

Based on what scored highest, the winning headline:

1. **Names a specific, personal threat or insight** the reader hasn't fully processed
2. **Uses the three-audience framework or AI-visibility angle** as GrowthX's unique territory
3. **Implies deep expertise** through specificity (numbers, timeframes, autobiographical detail)
4. **Creates an open loop** that demands the reader learn more
5. **Is under 15 words** — the top 10 average 10.4 words
