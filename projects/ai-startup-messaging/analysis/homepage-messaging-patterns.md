# Homepage Messaging Patterns — Cross-Site Synthesis

Six AI-era startups, six different approaches. Here's what they share and where they diverge.

---

## Shared Patterns

### 1. "OS for X" or System-Level Framing

Four of six sites position as a system, not a tool:
- Sanity: "The Content Operating System for the AI era"
- brand.ai: "The operating system for modern brand work"
- Rox: "Revenue OS" (architecture diagram)
- Actively: "System of intelligence For Enterprise-Scale GTM"

Even Midday and Maze, which don't use "OS" explicitly, position as all-in-one replacements rather than single-feature tools. The pattern: claim the category by naming the system.

### 2. Hero = Category Claim, Not Feature Description

Every site leads with positioning, not product features:
- "Structure powers intelligence" (Sanity)
- "Revenue Agents for the Global 2000" (Rox)
- "GTM Superintelligence for the Enterprise" (Actively)
- "Research at the pace of change" (Maze)
- "For the new wave of one-person companies" (Midday)

No one leads with "Our tool does X." They all lead with "We are the X for Y."

### 3. Two-Tier Product Architecture

Five of six sites split their product into an intelligence/foundation layer and an execution/action layer:
- Sanity: Content operations + Content backend
- brand.ai: Brand OS + Brand Studio
- Rox: System of Context + Agent Swarm
- Actively: Custom Model + Per-Account Agents
- Midday: Financial intelligence + Feature set (implicit)

The pattern: separate "thinking" from "doing" — the system understands first, then acts.

### 4. Trust Bars Are Universal

All six sites have logo walls. Four place them immediately after the hero. The logos serve as permission to keep reading, not decoration.

### 5. Enterprise Security as Table Stakes

Five of six mention SOC2, GDPR, or both. It's no longer a differentiator — it's a checkbox. The interesting variation is in how they present it:
- Rox: Makes it a full section with 3 pillars
- Sanity: Inline badges
- Maze: Dedicated security section with 6 items
- Actively: Three badges at the bottom
- Midday: Badges in footer

### 6. Outcome Metrics in Social Proof

The best sites don't just show quotes — they show numbers:
- Sanity: "300% faster release cycles," "80 hours saved per month"
- Rox: "90% reduction in rep prep time"
- Midday: "4-6 hours per week" saved (quantified pain, not quantified result)

## Standout Techniques

### Sanity: Numbered Feature Progression

Five features presented as "01, 02, 03, 04, 05" with tab navigation. Creates a sense of completeness and sequential discovery. The numbering implies "we thought about this in order" — not a random feature dump.

### brand.ai: Use Cases by Team, Not by Feature

Instead of listing features, brand.ai lists what each team (Strategy, Marketing, Creative, Communications, Governance, Sales Enablement) can do — with 4 specific sub-use-cases each. This says: "We understand your org chart." Much more powerful than "Here's what our tool does."

### Rox: Explicit Legacy Replacement

"Consolidate your legacy SaaS workflows" names the six categories Rox replaces. This is aggressive positioning — they're saying "you don't need those other tools anymore." The time-to-value commitment ("Revenue outcomes in 90 Days") adds urgency.

### Actively: Process Revelation

The 4-step "Researches → Understands → Reasons → Improves" sequence shows how the AI thinks. This builds trust by making the black box transparent. The implied message: "We're not just running prompts — there's a real reasoning process."

### Midday: Quantified Pain Before Solution

"Less admin. More focus." section breaks down time wasted: chasing receipts (45 min), cleaning transactions (1 hr), preparing invoices (1-2 hrs), explaining numbers (1 hr) = 4-6 hours per week. The user sees their own pain quantified before seeing the solution. Visceral.

### Maze: Movement Language

"Research at the pace of change" and "Don't keep up with change, shape it" position Maze as a movement, not a product. The four pillars (Versatility, Scale, Influence, AI) read like a manifesto. This creates emotional buy-in beyond features.

## Divergent Approaches

| Dimension | Developer-Led | Executive-Led | Creator-Led |
|-----------|--------------|---------------|-------------|
| Sites | Sanity | Rox, Actively, brand.ai | Midday, Maze |
| Hero tone | Technical credibility | Bold outcome claims | Empathetic, identity-based |
| CTA style | "Start building" | "Contact Sales" / "Book a demo" | "Start your trial" / "Sign up" |
| Proof type | Code snippets, metrics | Case studies, ROI | User testimonials, time saved |
| Page length | Long, detailed | Medium, architecture-focused | Medium, feature-walkthrough |

## What This Means for GrowthX

The strongest pattern across all six: **claim the system, not the feature**. GrowthX already has "OS for website-driven growth" — that's the right framing. The question is which execution pattern fits best:

1. **Sanity's numbered features** — if showing platform depth and developer credibility
2. **brand.ai's team-based use cases** — if speaking to different buyer personas on one page
3. **Rox's replacement narrative** — if positioning against fragmented agency/tool stacks
4. **Actively's process revelation** — if showing how the AI actually works (transparency as trust)
5. **Midday's quantified pain** — if selling time savings and operational relief
6. **Maze's movement language** — if building community/category beyond the product

GrowthX likely needs elements from several: the OS framing (Sanity/brand.ai), the replacement narrative (Rox), the three-audience urgency (unique to GrowthX), and the compounding engine story (unique to GrowthX). The three-audience framework (buyer, AI agent, training bot) is a differentiator none of these six sites have.
