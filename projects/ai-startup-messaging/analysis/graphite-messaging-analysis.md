# Graphite — Homepage Messaging Analysis

## Hero / Headline

**Title tag:** "Code review for the age of AI"
**Hero headline:** "The next generation of code review."
**Sub-hero:** "Graphite is the AI code review platform where teams ship higher quality code, faster."
**Trust note:** "Free for your first 30 days. No credit card required. Synced with your GitHub account."

Two moves at once. The title tag uses era framing ("for the age of AI") to position Graphite as the inevitable next step — not a choice, a generational shift. The hero headline doubles down: "the next generation" implies the old way is done. The sub-hero names the category ("AI code review platform") and the outcome ("higher quality code, faster") in one sentence.

The trust note removes three objections in 14 words: cost ("free"), commitment ("no credit card"), and setup ("synced with your GitHub account"). Elegant friction removal.

Dual CTA: "Get started for free" + "Request a demo."

## Positioning and Category

**Category claim:** "AI code review platform"

Graphite doesn't try to invent a new category. It takes an existing one (code review) and claims the AI-era version. The positioning is generational, not categorical — "we're not a different thing, we're the next version of the thing you already do." This is low-risk, high-clarity positioning. No one has to learn what Graphite is.

The era framing ("for the age of AI") does the category expansion work. It says: this isn't just code review with AI bolted on — this is what code review looks like when AI is native.

## Product Hierarchy

No two-tier architecture. Instead, a feature portfolio presented as outcome cards:

1. **"The AI reviewer you can collaborate with"** — Graphite Chat
2. **"Review faster, ship sooner"** — AI reviews
3. **"Merge without conflicts or delays"** — Merge queue
4. **"Stay unblocked with stacked PRs"** — Stacked PRs
5. **"Fast, focused reviews in a modern PR page"** — PR page

Then a feature grid under "Everything you need to ship faster":
- "Never wait on review again" (CLI + stacking)
- "A review experience built for teams" (inbox)
- "Don't miss a beat" (notifications)
- "Smarter CI" (CI optimizer)
- "One platform. All of your review essentials." (unification)

Every feature is named as an outcome, not a capability. "Never wait on review again" is more powerful than "Stacked PR management." "Don't miss a beat" is more vivid than "Slack integration." This is the key technique: features described by what they eliminate, not what they provide.

## Information Architecture

1. **Hero** — Generational claim + dual CTA + trust note
2. **Trust bar** — "Trusted by leading engineering teams at" (logo wall)
3. **Video thumbnail** — Feature overview (plays on click)
4. **Feature tabs** — Stacked PRs, PR page, AI code review, Chat, Merge queue, PR inbox, Dev metrics
5. **Case study carousel** — Semgrep, Shopify, Ramp, Asana, Tecton (each with a speed/productivity metric)
6. **Outcome feature cards** — 5 cards, each with outcome headline + Learn more link
7. **"Everything you need to ship faster"** — Feature grid (5 items)
8. **AI collaboration section** — Chat feature as a collaborative AI reviewer
9. **Infrastructure section** — GitHub sync, Git-native, developer toolchain
10. **Closing CTA** — "Built for the world's fastest engineering teams, now available for everyone."

The flow: claim the era → prove adoption (logos + case studies) → show outcomes (feature cards) → demonstrate depth (feature grid) → anchor on AI collaboration → reassure on infrastructure → close with exclusivity-to-access arc.

## Tone and Voice

Developer-forward, velocity-obsessed. Every paragraph is about speed: "ship faster," "ship sooner," "stay unblocked," "gain momentum," "don't miss a beat," "accelerate your workflow." The vocabulary is Git-native: PRs, stacking, branches, CI, merge queues. No business jargon — this speaks to engineers directly.

Confident but not aggressive. "The next generation" is bold but not combative. They don't name competitors or say "replace your tools" — they just position as the obvious next step.

## Standout Techniques

### 1. Generational Positioning

"The next generation of code review" is a masterclass in positioning by era rather than by feature. It doesn't say "we're better than X" — it says "the world moved on." This forces the competitor into the position of being "last generation" without naming them.

### 2. Outcome Headlines for Features

Every feature card opens with what it does for you, not what it is:
- "Never wait on review again" (not "stacking CLI")
- "Don't miss a beat" (not "Slack notifications")
- "Merge without conflicts or delays" (not "merge queue")

This makes the feature list feel like a list of problems solved.

### 3. Case Studies as Speed Metrics

Each customer story has a velocity metric baked into the headline:
- "Ramp ships code 3x faster"
- "Asana engineers save 7 hours per week"
- "How Shopify scaled their developer productivity"

The case study carousel doubles as a proof-of-speed section.

### 4. "Built for X, Now Available for Everyone"

The closing line creates an exclusivity-to-access arc. "Built for the world's fastest engineering teams" — that's aspiration. "Now available for everyone" — that's invitation. The reader feels like they're being let into an exclusive club.

### 5. Friction Removal in 14 Words

"Free for your first 30 days. No credit card required. Synced with your GitHub account." Three objections eliminated in one line, placed directly under the CTA.

## What's Borrowable for GrowthX

1. **"For the age of AI" era framing** — GrowthX equivalent: "Website growth for the AI era" or "The growth engine for the age of AI." Positions the shift as generational, not incremental. The old approach (hire agencies, publish content, hope) is "last generation."

2. **"The next generation" opening** — "The next generation of website growth." Simple, bold, makes everything else sound outdated.

3. **Outcome headlines for capabilities** — Instead of "Content Production," say "Never run out of content again." Instead of "AI Visibility Tracking," say "Know exactly how AI sees your brand." Feature names as problems solved.

4. **Case study velocity metrics** — "2-4x organic traffic in 12 months." "80% of content production automated." Bake the speed into every proof point.

5. **"Built for X, now available for Y"** — "Built for the companies shaping their markets. Now available as a platform." Or: "Originally built for our own clients. Now open as a growth engine."

6. **14-word friction removal** — "Start with a free growth audit. No commitment. No contract. Results in two weeks."

7. **Velocity-obsessed vocabulary** — Make every section about speed and momentum: "ship," "compound," "accelerate," "unblock," "gain momentum." The page should feel like it's moving forward.
