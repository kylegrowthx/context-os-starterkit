# Maze — Homepage Messaging Analysis

## 1. Hero / Headline Messaging

**Primary:** "Research at the pace of change"

**Sub-hero:** "In a world moving faster than ever, Maze turns insights into clarity your team can act on"

The hero is aspirational and tension-based. "Research at the pace of change" names the core problem — research is slow, the market moves fast — and promises resolution in five words. No product category, no feature list, no company name in the hero. Pure outcome framing.

The sub-hero introduces the value chain: insights → clarity → action. "Your team" signals this is a collaborative tool, not a solo researcher's instrument. "Can act on" is the payoff — research that actually changes decisions.

**Movement tagline (mid-page):** "Don't keep up with change, shape it." This escalates the hero's promise from speed to agency. You're not reacting — you're leading.

## 2. Positioning & Category Framing

Maze positions as a **research platform** but frames research as a strategic capability, not a department function.

The positioning layers:
- **Category:** Research platform (implied, never explicitly stated on the homepage)
- **Reframe:** Research as a shared, continuous, decision-driving activity — not a bottleneck
- **Democratization angle:** "Make research a shared capability" — anyone can run studies, not just researchers
- **AI angle:** "Research amplified by AI" — AI removes busywork, humans drive insight

The four value pillars reveal the positioning strategy:

| Pillar | What it means |
|--------|--------------|
| Versatility | Any study type — surveys to one-on-ones |
| Scale | Research as a company-wide capability, not a team function |
| Influence | Insights that actually change decisions |
| Research-grade AI | AI that automates the tedious parts, not the thinking |

"Research-grade AI" is a smart modifier — it separates Maze's AI from generic AI by anchoring it to research rigor. This is the same move Actively makes with "Superintelligence" — adding a qualifier that elevates the AI claim above commodity.

## 3. Product Hierarchy & Naming

Three-stage pipeline model:

| Stage | Name | Description |
|-------|------|-------------|
| 1 | Recruit | "Reach the right people, effortlessly" — 6M+ participant panel |
| 2 | Research | "Gain the clarity to shape change" — interviews, prototype tests, usability studies |
| 3 | Analyze | "Influence key decisions with evidence" — automated reports, clips, stats, stories |

The pipeline is the product. Recruit → Research → Analyze maps directly to the buyer's workflow. Each stage gets its own name, description, and outcome. This is effective because it shows the product as a complete system, not a collection of features.

Beneath the pipeline, Solutions are organized by three axes:
- **Use Cases:** Concept Validation, Usability Testing, Copy Testing, User Satisfaction
- **Industries:** Financial Services, Tech, Insurance
- **Roles:** Researchers, Designers, Product Managers

This three-axis structure (use case × industry × role) creates a lot of surface area for SEO and persona-specific landing pages without cluttering the homepage.

## 4. Information Architecture

Page flow:

1. **Hero** — Aspirational claim + outcome promise
2. **Product pipeline** — Recruit → Research → Analyze (3 stages, each with detail)
3. **Movement tagline** — "Don't keep up with change, shape it"
4. **Unified value prop** — "With Maze it all comes together"
5. **Four value pillars** — Versatility, Scale, Influence, Research-grade AI (each with dedicated section)
6. **Enterprise logos** — Atlassian, Volvo, Cisco, Revolut, etc.
7. **Security** — Encryption, GDPR, SSO, private workspaces
8. **CTA** — "Shape change together"

The architecture follows a storytelling arc: aspiration (hero) → mechanism (pipeline) → philosophy (movement) → depth (pillars) → trust (logos, security) → invitation (CTA). It's less linear than Actively's page and more layered than Midday's.

Notable structural choices:
- The pipeline section does double duty as both product tour and value prop
- Each of the four pillars gets a full dedicated section — this is where the page invests most of its real estate
- Enterprise logos appear late, after the value story is told — logos validate, they don't lead
- CTA echoes the movement language: "Shape change together," not "Start free trial"

## 5. Tone & Voice

**Energetic, movement-oriented, inclusive, democratic.**

Key characteristics:
- **Change as motif** — "Pace of change," "shape change," "don't keep up with change." The word "change" appears throughout and functions as the page's narrative engine.
- **Inclusive language** — "Your team," "shared capability," "together." Research isn't for researchers; it's for everyone.
- **Aspirational but grounded** — The hero is aspirational ("shape change"), but the product sections are concrete (6M+ panel, automated reports, clips and stats). The page oscillates between vision and mechanism.
- **Active verbs** — "Reach," "gain," "influence," "shape." Everything is about agency and action.
- **Restrained enterprise signals** — Logos and security appear but don't dominate. The tone stays closer to a design tool (Figma energy) than an enterprise platform (Salesforce energy).

The CTA "Shape change together" is the tone distilled — collaborative, action-oriented, movement-framed. It invites rather than sells.

## 6. What's Borrowable for GrowthX

**Tension-based hero.** "Research at the pace of change" names a real tension (research is slow, change is fast) and resolves it in one line. GrowthX could use the same structure: "[Growth/Marketing] at the pace of [AI/change/competition]." The tension is the hook.

**Pipeline-as-product architecture.** Recruit → Research → Analyze is the product, presented as a workflow the buyer already understands. GrowthX could map its product to a similar pipeline: Audit → Strategy → Execute → Measure. Showing the product as a complete system (not a feature list) builds confidence that it handles the whole job.

**Four-pillar value framework.** Versatility, Scale, Influence, Research-grade AI — each gets a dedicated section with its own framing. This is a strong pattern for GrowthX: pick 3-4 strategic values (e.g., Speed, Quality, Intelligence, Integration) and give each a full section on the homepage. It's more memorable than a feature grid and more persuasive than bullet points.

**Movement language in CTAs.** "Shape change together" and "Don't keep up with change, shape it" turn a product into a mission. GrowthX could adopt similar movement framing — especially for the founder-led marketing angle. Something that invites the buyer into a shared belief rather than a transaction.

**Three-axis solutions structure.** Organizing by use case × industry × role creates natural entry points for different buyers without cluttering the homepage. GrowthX could use a similar structure: solutions by function (SEO, content, demand gen), by stage (early, growth, enterprise), and by role (CMO, content lead, growth lead).

**"Research-grade AI" as qualifier pattern.** Adding a domain-specific qualifier to "AI" elevates the claim. GrowthX could do the same: "Growth-grade AI" or "Marketing-grade intelligence" — signals that the AI is built for the domain, not generic.
