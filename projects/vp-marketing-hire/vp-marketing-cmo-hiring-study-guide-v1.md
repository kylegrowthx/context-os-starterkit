# How to Hire a VP of Marketing / CMO for Your Series A Startup

> **For:** Series A CEOs/Founders hiring their first marketing leader
> **Goal:** Build a rigorous, evidence-based hiring process that finds the right person and avoids a $500K+ mistake
> **Time Investment:** ~4 hours to read, 4-8 weeks to execute
> **Last Updated:** 2026-03-03

## How to Use This Guide

Read this sequentially the first time. It builds from foundations to process to specifics. Once you understand the full approach, use the appendices as templates and reference material while you hire. The most important section is Part 3 on assignments—that's where you'll actually see how someone thinks, not just how they interview. Budget 4-8 weeks for the full process. That feels slow, but rushing this hire is how founders end up paying $500K+ for someone who leaves in 18 months and leaves the pipeline in worse shape than they found it.

## Part 1: Foundations — What You're Actually Hiring For

### The $500K Mistake You're Trying to Avoid

Hiring the wrong VP of Marketing is one of the most expensive mistakes a Series A founder can make. The numbers are brutal. According to research from SaaStr and Avenue Talent Partners, between 60-70% of first VP-level marketing hires at Series A companies fail within their first 18-24 months. When a VP fails, you're not paying a salary—you're paying for a full year of lost pipeline growth, team damage, broken sales relationships, and the entire hiring process again. Direct costs run $500K to $1M when you factor in salary, misallocated marketing spend, and opportunity cost.

Here's what actually happens. You hire someone based on an impressive resume and interview performance. First 30 days feel fine—they're learning the business. Month 2-3, you realize they don't understand your specific market or how to generate pipeline in a capital-efficient way. By month 4-5, sales is frustrated because leads are low quality. By month 6-8, you know it's wrong but you hold hope. By month 12, you pull the trigger and start over, having wasted critical growth months. The team they started building falls apart. You've burned through recruiting fees. And now you're hiring a second marketing leader, which carries its own stigma.

The good news: this is almost entirely preventable. Most first marketing hires fail because founders don't vet for the specific skills their stage requires, or they hire on resume/pedigree instead of demonstrated capability. This guide is designed to fix that.

### VP Marketing vs. CMO vs. Head of Marketing: What's the Right Title?

Start with clarity on what role you actually need. These titles mean different things at different stages.

A **Head of Marketing** is typically an IC role—very hands-on, running campaigns, managing two to four people, still doing some of the work themselves. A **VP of Marketing** is a leadership role—building strategy, managing a small team, setting direction. A **CMO** is typically the chief marketing officer—usually reporting to the CEO, owning the entire go-to-market function, often with P&L responsibility.

At $10M ARR scaling to $30M, you almost certainly want a VP of Marketing or Head of Marketing, not a CMO. This is a critical distinction. Many founders mistakenly title the first hire as CMO, which can actually cause problems. When you call someone a CMO, they often expect broader authority than is realistic at your stage. They expect significant budget flexibility, control over multiple functions, and board visibility. They expect compensation benchmarks aligned with later-stage companies. Title inflation creates misalignment from day one.

First Round Review's research on scaling marketing in high-growth SaaS recommends starting with a VP or Head of Marketing title, even if they'll eventually evolve into a CMO role. This gives you clarity on scope. The person you hire is accountable for marketing, period. They report to you. They have specific outcomes. There's no ambiguity about who owns what. If they succeed, the CMO title comes naturally after Series B when the function has scaled and perhaps you've layered in a brand or communications function.

The only exception: if you're already $15M+ ARR and have capital-efficient demand gen dialed in, and you need someone to manage an expanded team with multiple sub-functions (brand, PMM, content, etc.), then CMO might be the right title. But that's not you.

### The Three Types of Marketing Leaders

Not all marketing leaders are built the same. Dave Kellogg's framework (outlined in his blog Kellblog) suggests that great marketing leaders are strongest in some subset of four pillars: Product Marketing, Demand Generation, Brand & Awareness, and Digital Programs (events, partnerships, etc.). Most marketers are truly exceptional in one, strong in one other, and adequate in the remaining two. The "five-five" CMO—someone who's excellent at all four—is a statistical unicorn. You're not finding that person at any price point.

OpenView's research on scaling marketing adds another useful frame: the **Builder** (creates new motions from scratch), the **Scaler** (takes a working playbook and makes it bigger), and the **Optimizer** (squeezes incremental gains from existing systems). Early-stage companies need Builders or early Scalers. Optimizers fail at startups because there's nothing built yet to optimize.

At your stage ($10M to $30M ARR), here's what matters: you need someone who is strong in Demand Generation and either Product Marketing or Digital Programs. You do not need someone strong in Brand. At $10M ARR, you have one job: generate capital-efficient pipeline. That's the foundation. Jason Lemkin at SaaStr calls this the non-negotiable first priority. Everything else is secondary.

This means when you're evaluating candidates, you're looking for someone who can:
- Articulate how they'd build or optimize a lead generation motion specific to your ICP
- Explain funnel mechanics and how to improve conversion at each stage
- Run efficient paid programs (usually Google Ads and LinkedIn at this stage)
- Work hand-in-glove with sales, understand their process, and commit to lead quality SLAs
- Maybe layer in product marketing to sharpen positioning or differentiation, but that's secondary

If a candidate leads with brand, positioning, or thought leadership, they're optimizing for scale you don't have yet. They're a Scaler or Optimizer, not a Builder. Wrong profile for this stage.

### What You Need at $10M → $30M ARR Specifically

SignalFire's research on marketing at high-growth startups identifies the specific profile that works: someone with 6+ years of experience, who has operated at a company that went from roughly $5M-$10M to $30M+ ARR. This is crucial—they've lived through exactly the transition you're about to make. They know what changes in the function as you scale. They understand the rhythm of hiring, specialization, and team structure across that range.

This person should be a Builder or early Scaler, not an Optimizer. They should have startup experience—having worked at a large company (Fortune 500) makes failure more likely, not less, because the constraints are completely different. At a big company, you have budget, headcount, and brand awareness you can take for granted. At a startup, you have none of those things. The skills required are almost opposite. A marketer who's spent their entire career at a big company often struggles with resource constraints, ambiguity, and the need to do execution work that they'd normally delegate.

The best candidates have brought people with them before. This signals that they're a real leader—people want to work with them, and they know how to recruit. By the time you're hiring them, you should be exploring whether they have one or two people they could bring along (especially someone who can run demand gen while they focus on strategy). That's not a requirement, but it's a green flag.

Your goal is someone who can bring demand gen from 2-3% of ARR (roughly $200-300K marketing spend) to 7-10% (roughly $700K-$1.2M) while maintaining CAC payback under 12 months and LTV/CAC ratio above 3:1. That's the real metric—not that they're a famous person or have a big resume, but that they've actually optimized that particular lever at a company at your stage.

Compensation benchmarks from Betts Recruiting and TopStartups.io: base salary $200K-$300K (median $250K), equity 0.5-1.2% (non-founder), bonus 10-30% (median 20%). The equity percentage feels counterintuitive to founders—you're thinking 0.1-0.2%—but you're competing with other Series A companies for similar talent, and 0.5-0.8% is table stakes. You're also competing with some larger companies making retention offers to their rising stars. You want someone talented enough that other places would try to keep them.

### Fractional vs. Full-Time: When Each Makes Sense

There's been a trend toward fractional CMO services in recent years, especially post-2020. It's tempting. You pay $5K-$15K a month for strategy and leadership without the salary commitment. When does that actually make sense?

Fractional works if you're under $5M ARR and trying to figure out whether marketing needs a dedicated full-time hire, or if you're in a very specific situation like a vertical SaaS where you only need 10-15 hours a week of strategic marketing leadership. It does not work at $10M ARR. You need someone embedded in the company, in strategy calls, in the weeds, understanding the product and sales process at a level that fractional CMOs simply can't achieve while splitting time across four clients.

There is an emerging hybrid model that works better: fractional CMO (6-12 hours/week for strategy, hiring, and quarterly planning) plus a full-time Head of Demand Gen (someone who runs the demand generation machine day-to-day). This can make sense if you're between $5M-$10M ARR and don't need a full VP yet, but that's not your situation. You're at $10M heading to $30M. You need a full-time VP or Head of Marketing. Everything else is false economy.

### The Profile That Actually Works

Synthesize the above into a clear profile. You're looking for:

**Experience & Track Record:** 6-8 years of marketing experience, with at least one role at a company that scaled from roughly $5M to $30M+ ARR. Demonstrated ability to build or scale demand generation. Clear before-and-after metrics (pipeline growth, CAC reduction, LTV/CAC improvement).

**Startup DNA:** Has worked at a startup, understood ambiguity, built things with limited budget. Can do execution work—not just strategy. Resourceful and scrappy. Knows how to prioritize ruthlessly because you can't do everything.

**Sales Alignment:** Understands the sales process deeply. Has worked hand-in-glove with sales leaders. Can articulate what great marketing looks like from a sales perspective. Has committed to and delivered on lead quality SLAs.

**Leadership:** Has built a small team (3-8 people). Can recruit. Has managed people and shown they can develop talent. Has strong opinions about how to build a marketing organization, but isn't dogmatic about it.

**Self-Awareness:** Can articulate strengths and weaknesses honestly. Knows where they're not strong and either has developed that skill or is clear about the gap. Coachable. Asks good questions.

**Demand Gen Orientation:** For you, this is non-negotiable. Demand gen (lead generation, pipeline, funnel efficiency) should be in their top-2 strongest areas. Everything else is secondary.

This person probably costs $250K base + 0.6-0.8% equity + 20% bonus. They're probably not available immediately—the good ones have notice periods of 4-8 weeks. You might need to recruit for 6-8 weeks before you find them. It's worth it.

## Part 2: The Hiring Process — Stage by Stage

### Step 1: Build the Scorecard Before You Source

Before you post a job or reach out to a single candidate, define what you're actually hiring for using the ghSMART "Who" method from Geoff Smart's book. This is the single most important step, and most founders skip it. They go straight to sourcing. That's a mistake. It's like building a house without blueprints—you end up with something, but probably not what you wanted.

Build a scorecard with three components: **Mission** (one sentence summary of the role), **Outcomes** (3-5 specific, measurable results the person should achieve in their first 12 months), and **Competencies** (5-8 behavioral traits required to be successful).

For your situation, a sample scorecard might look like this:

**Mission:** Build and lead the marketing function to generate sufficient capital-efficient pipeline to support $30M ARR revenue targets while establishing the foundational brand and positioning for long-term growth.

**Outcomes:**
- Generate $X in marketing-sourced pipeline by month 12 (this should be roughly 30-40% of your total pipeline, depending on your sales motion)
- Reduce CAC from current baseline to under $X and maintain LTV/CAC above 3:1
- Build marketing team from current 2-3 people to 5-7 people, with clear specialization in demand gen, content, and analytics
- Establish and execute marketing-sales SLA, with at least 90% of marketing-sourced leads meeting quality standards
- Position company for Series B through thought leadership and brand building (blogs, speaking, content, web presence)

**Competencies:**
- Demand Gen Builder (can create lead motion, optimize funnels, manage paid programs)
- Sales Alignment (understands sales process, can work through objections with sales team, not territorial)
- Strategic Thinking (can diagnose problems, prioritize options, build a plan, not just execute)
- Resourcefulness (gets things done with constraints, doesn't over-index on budget)
- Team Builder (can recruit, develop talent, create culture, not just manage tasks)
- Data-Driven (makes decisions based on metrics, understands CAC/LTV, can communicate with finance/ops)
- Coachability (seeks feedback, adapts, doesn't need to be right all the time)

Spend time on this. Get your board, your CFO, your VP Sales involved. Make sure you're aligned on what success looks like. This scorecard is your North Star for the entire hiring process. You'll use it to evaluate candidates, to interview, to assess assignments. If you haven't done this clearly, every interview will go in a different direction and you'll end up comparing apples and oranges.

### Step 2: Source Candidates (Don't Just Post a Job)

The best candidates in the market aren't applying to jobs. They're either happy where they are, or they're looking quietly and only moving for something exceptional. If you just post a job on LinkedIn or AngelList, you'll get a decent funnel of people, but the very best candidates won't be in that funnel.

Use your network. Ask every person you know who works with marketing leaders if they know someone exceptional who might be interested. Ask your VCs—they know founders who know marketing leaders, and they have financial incentive to help you succeed. Ask your existing team. Ask your board. Ask other SaaS founders you know. This is called recruiting warm, and it's more effective than cold sourcing by about 3:1.

If you don't have sufficient warm leads, hire an executive recruiter who specializes in marketing leadership at Series A/B companies. This will cost you $50K-$80K in fees (typically 33% of first-year comp), but the good recruiters will source the market aggressively, pre-screen rigorously, and set expectations clearly. Firms like Betts Recruiting, Executive Levels, and others have deep benches of marketing executives. For one of your most critical hires, the recruiter fee is worth it.

As you build your candidate pipeline, aim for 15-20 credible candidates who match your scorecard. You'll probably screen 12-15, interview deeply with 5-8, and make an offer to 1-2. This funnel takes time, but rushing it by settling on a weaker candidate earlier is how you end up with the wrong person.

### Step 3: The Screening Call (30 minutes)

The first call is a filter for fit and interest only. Don't try to do deep evaluation yet. You're just trying to understand whether they're interested, whether they match the profile, and whether it's worth investing more time.

Jason Lemkin's best screening question is this: "What are the top three things you'd upgrade in our marketing if you joined?" Listen to their answer carefully. If they say "branding and thought leadership," that's a yellow flag. You need someone who talks about demand gen and pipeline first. If they say "better attribution" or "marketing automation platform," that's a green flag—they understand the mechanics.

Also ask in the screening: "What attracted you to our company?" and "What would your ideal next role look like?" If their answer to the first question is vague (they didn't do homework) or if their ideal role doesn't match yours, save time and move on.

End the screening by explaining the process: "If we move forward, here's what to expect: two more interviews, we'll ask you to do an assignment, we'll do reference calls, and this takes about 4-6 weeks total. Are you still interested? Do you have any concerns?" This filters for people who are genuinely available and interested vs. people who are casually exploring.

Screen 12-15 people. Spend 30 minutes each. You'll quickly get a sense of who's serious and who's not.

### Step 4: The Deep Interview — Questions That Actually Matter

The deep interview is where you actually understand whether someone can do the job. This is usually one 60-90 minute conversation, though you might split it into two interviews if you're thorough. The key is asking questions that reveal how they actually think, not just what they know.

For each category of questions, below I'll explain what you're testing and what good answers look like.

#### Demand Generation & Pipeline Questions

"Walk me through a time when you had to generate more pipeline from marketing. How did you approach it? What channels did you focus on? How did you prioritize? What were the results?"

What you're testing: Can they actually build a lead generation motion? Do they understand channel economics? Do they know how to prioritize across tactics? Are they clear on results?

What good looks like: They walk you through a specific situation, not a generic strategy. They talk about diagnosis first ("we had $X pipeline, we needed $Y, the gap was Z, so I started by understanding where the best leads came from historically"). They mention testing and iteration, not just executing one big plan. They talk about measurement—CAC, LTV, conversion rates—not just vanity metrics like MQLs. They can explain why certain channels worked and others didn't.

"Tell me about your ideal funnel. What's the mix of inbound, outbound, partnerships, events? How would you build that at our company?"

What you're testing: Do they understand your specific business model? Can they think through channel economics? Are they creative but grounded?

What good looks like: They ask clarifying questions first: What's your sales cycle? How big is the market? Who's buying? Then they give a hypothesis: "At your stage with a 6-month sales cycle and small sales team, I'd probably start with 50% Google/SEM, 30% LinkedIn, 20% content, and we'd test ABM and events once we had better brand. Here's why..." They're not dogmatic—they frame it as a hypothesis to test. They reference your specific constraints.

"What's your view on marketing-qualified leads (MQLs) vs. sales-qualified leads (SQLs)? How would you define them at our company?"

What you're testing: Do they understand the sales-marketing handoff? Can they work with sales on this or would they be territorial?

What good looks like: They don't have a dogmatic answer. They say something like: "It depends on your sales process, but the key is that sales and marketing agree on the definition. MQL is basically 'fits the ICP and has shown intent.' SQL is 'sales has deemed them worth meeting.' We'd work with your sales team to define both. The important thing is that we have an SLA around conversion rates and quality so we can keep improving the funnel together. My philosophy is that marketing owns the top of the funnel, sales owns the qualification, and we own the handoff together." That's collaborative, pragmatic, and process-oriented.

"Here's our current funnel: 20K visitors/month, 500 leads, 50 MQLs, 15 SQLs, 3 customers. Walk me through what you'd focus on first and why."

What you're testing: Can they diagnose a funnel? Do they prioritize? Can they do basic math?

What good looks like: They ask clarifying questions ("What's your conversion rate from SQL to customer? What's your average deal size? How long is the sales cycle?"). Then they do basic math and find the biggest leak. ("Your biggest leak is MQL to SQL conversion—that's 30%. Everything else is reasonable. That's either a qualification problem or a sales bandwidth problem. Before I'd increase lead volume, I'd work with sales to understand why only 30% of MQLs are becoming SQLs. Is it quality or capacity?"). Then they suggest an immediate action: "I'd probably focus on improving lead quality from our current sources first, then test a new channel once we've proven we can convert at acceptable CAC."

#### Sales Alignment Questions

"Tell me about a time when sales was unhappy with the leads they were getting from marketing. What happened? How did you work through it?"

What you're testing: Have they been through sales-marketing conflict? Can they handle friction? Do they own the relationship or blame sales?

What good looks like: They acknowledge the problem without getting defensive. ("Yeah, we had that problem. We were sending a lot of leads that didn't fit the ICP, and reps were frustrated because they were spending time on bad leads. It was on us.") They explain what they learned and how they fixed it. ("We tightened up our lead scoring and our qualification criteria. We worked with sales to define what 'sales ready' actually meant. Conversion rates went down initially because we were being more selective, but deal velocity improved and reps were happier because they weren't chasing dead ends.") They show they can see it from sales' perspective, not just marketing's perspective.

"How would you structure the relationship between marketing and sales? Who owns what? How would you handle disagreement?"

What you're testing: Do they have a clear philosophy on this? Are they collaborative or territorial?

What good looks like: They have a framework that makes sense and they can defend it. ("I think marketing owns lead generation and lead quality standards. We set the target and we deliver against it. Sales owns qualification and conversion. Where it gets gray is the handoff. I'd have us work together to define SLAs: we commit to X leads of Y quality per month, you commit to following up within 24 hours and giving us feedback on quality weekly. We measure our performance against the SLA and we iterate together.") They're not trying to control everything and they're not abdicating responsibility.

"What's your view on the balance between inside sales and field sales? How does that change your marketing approach?"

What you're testing: Do they understand the go-to-market model? Can they adapt their approach to different sales motions?

What good looks like: They think about this strategically. ("If you're pure inside sales, you can do more volume and lower CAC. If you're field sales with a small team, you need to be more selective about lead volume and focus on fit. If you're hybrid, you're probably trying to feed both motions and it gets complicated. What's your model? Once I understand that, my approach to demand gen changes significantly.")

#### Strategic Thinking Questions

"What should our marketing budget be as a percentage of revenue? How did you arrive at that number?"

What you're testing: Do they understand unit economics? Can they think about marketing as an investment? Do they have a defensible methodology?

What good looks like: They don't answer off the cuff. They say something like: "That depends on a few things. What's your CAC today? What's your LTV? Are you trying to accelerate growth or optimize profitability? At 10M ARR scaling to 30M with a healthy unit economics, I'd probably recommend 7-10% of revenue on marketing, with the budget skewed toward demand gen early, then diversifying into brand as you grow. So that's roughly $700K to $1.2M right now, maybe $2.1M-$3M once you hit 30M ARR. But the real metric isn't the percentage—it's CAC payback and LTV/CAC. If we're hitting those targets, we could spend more. If we're not, we need to cut." They're tying it to metrics, not guessing.

"How big should our marketing team be in 12 months? What roles do you need?"

What you're testing: Can they think through team structure? Do they understand specialization?

What good looks like: They start with the work that needs to be done, not the budget. ("Let me think about this. You need someone running demand gen, someone doing content, someone running analytics, someone doing events or partnerships, probably a coordinator. That's 5-6 people with you as the leader. We could do it with 4 if we're really lean, but 5-6 is realistic. I'd hire demand gen first—that's the biggest lever—then content to feed demand gen, then analytics so we know what's working. Events and partnerships come later.") They're thinking about leverage and the sequence of hires, not just throwing numbers at it.

"Where should we focus: brand building or demand generation? How would you think about that tradeoff?"

What you're testing: Do they understand your stage? Do they prioritize correctly? Can they make tradeoffs?

What good looks like: "Right now, demand gen is the priority. You have to generate pipeline. But we can't completely ignore brand—a brand with no market presence makes demand gen harder. So I'd do 80% demand gen, 20% brand right now. That means we run heavy paid programs for lead gen, we build our content to feed the funnel and support demand gen, we show up on the podcasts and stages that your ICP listens to, but we're not doing brand for brand's sake. Once you hit 20M ARR and demand gen is more mature, we can shift to 60% demand gen, 40% brand."

#### Leadership & Team Building Questions

"How would you build this team from two people to seven? What's your sequence of hires?"

What you're testing: Do they think about team structure? What's their hiring philosophy? Can they prioritize?

What good looks like: They ask clarifying questions: "What does the current team do? What's broken that keeps you up at night? Once I understand that, I can talk about sequence." Then they give a thoughtful answer that prioritizes the biggest leverage: "I'd probably hire someone to run demand gen next—that's the bottleneck. Then someone for content. Then an analyst. We could do all this with 5 total people if we're smart about hiring people who can wear multiple hats."

"Tell me about your best hire and your worst hire. What made each one what they were?"

What you're testing: Can they evaluate talent? Do they learn from mistakes? Are they honest about failures?

What good looks like: They give a specific story about a good hire: "I hired someone who came in as a junior content marketer. They were curious, they asked good questions, they executed relentlessly. Within 18 months they were running half the content machine and becoming a leader on the team. What made them great was learning velocity and ownership." Then they give an honest answer about a bad hire: "I hired someone with a big name and amazing resume. They didn't work out because they were territorial, they didn't adapt to the startup environment, and they didn't take feedback well. That's on me—I was seduced by the resume instead of looking for cultural fit and coachability. I should have moved faster to make the change." They learn from it.

"Who would you bring with you if you joined? Why?"

What you're testing: Can they recruit? Do people want to follow them? Are they thinking about what the team needs?

What good looks like: They mention specific people they'd want to work with and specific roles they'd want them in. "I have a demand gen person I've worked with twice who I'd love to bring in. They're exceptional at funnel optimization. I'd also look for a great content person once we have some budget." The fact that people would follow them is a green flag. The fact that they're thinking about where those people fit is another green flag.

#### Culture & Fit Questions

"What type of company environment do you thrive in? What frustrates you?"

What you're testing: Do they align with your culture? Will they be happy here? Can they be honest?

What good looks like: They give honest answers that actually match your company. "I thrive in fast-moving environments where there's clear direction but room to figure out the how. I get frustrated with process for process's sake and with lack of clarity on what matters. I do my best work when I understand the strategy and the business model deeply."

"When was the last time you made a big mistake? What happened? What did you learn?"

What you're testing: Are they self-aware? Can they own failure? Do they learn?

What good looks like: They give a real story, not a humble-brag. "I launched a demand gen campaign once that completely missed the mark. I designed it without really understanding the buying process deeply enough. Spent $50K and got basically nothing. I realized I was operating on assumptions instead of asking sales. So now I always start with sales conversations and get clarity on the ICP and buying process before we build anything big. That mistake probably saved me $100K+ in avoided mistakes since."

### Step 5: The Topgrading / Chronological Deep-Dive

After the deep interview, conduct a "chronological interview" where you walk through their entire career, job by job, in order. This is based on the Topgrading methodology from Brad Smart. The goal is to understand their actual track record and capability over time, not just what they claim.

For each role, ask: "What were you hired to do? What did you accomplish? What were the low points? Why did you leave? What would your boss say about your strengths? What would they say about your weaknesses?"

This is revealing. You'll see patterns. Maybe they jump companies every 18 months—why? Maybe they talk about accomplishments but can't articulate their actual contribution vs. the team's contribution. Maybe they only want to talk about the positive moments and deflect on challenges.

Mark Roberge's adaptation of this (from his book "The Sales Acceleration Formula") adds a scoring dimension. For each role, score them on: coachability, curiosity, intelligence, track record, work ethic. Do they get better at coachability over time or worse? Does their track record show they actually deliver or do they fall short?

This takes 45-60 minutes and it's tedious, but it's worth it. You'll understand their actual capabilities, not their pitch.

### Step 6: Cross-Functional Interviews

Never hire a VP of Marketing based on your assessment alone. They're your closest hire to the rest of the business. They need to work with sales, with finance, with product. Get multiple perspectives.

Who should interview:
- You (the CEO) — overall fit, vision alignment, capability
- Your VP Sales or CRO — sales alignment, pipeline capability, mutual respect
- Your CFO or Finance lead — budget thinking, ROI mindset, financial acumen
- One or two people from the team they'll manage — will they want to follow this person?
- One board member or advisor with marketing expertise — external validation

Each interviewer has a specific charter. The VP Sales is looking for: "Can I trust them to deliver pipeline? Do they understand my world? Are they collaborative?" Finance is looking for: "Do they think about ROI? Can we work together on budgets?" The team is looking for: "Do they seem like a good leader? Will I learn? Is this someone I'd follow?"

Have evaluators score independently on your scorecard before you discuss. This prevents groupthink.

### Step 7: The Assignment (The Most Important Step)

The assignment is where most hiring processes are weak. Interviews test presentation skills, likability, and ability to tell a story. Assignments test actual thinking, judgment, and capability to do the work. This is where the talkers separate from the doers. The assignment section is so important that I've devoted the entire Part 3 to it.

## Part 3: The Assignment — How to Truly Evaluate a Marketing Leader

### Why Assignments Matter More Than Interviews

Interviews are theater. People are often better at interviewing than they are at actual work. They can tell a great story, reference impressive clients, drop names of companies they've worked with, and sound impressive. The person who interviews best isn't always the person who works best.

An assignment is different. An assignment requires them to do real thinking about your actual business. It shows you their judgment, their ability to prioritize, their analytical rigor (or lack thereof), how they think when the pressure is on, and what quality bar they have for their own work.

Some of the best founders (and recruiting partners like Sequoia and a16z) swear by executive assignments for hiring senior roles. Why? Because by the time someone reaches VP level, they've gotten good at interviews through repetition. An assignment shows their actual capability under time pressure and ambiguity.

The investment is worth it. You might spend 5-10 hours evaluating assignments if you have 2-3 finalists. You'll spend that 5-10 hours in interviews anyway. But the assignments will tell you way more than the interviews.

### Assignment Ethics: Pay for the Work

This is important. If you're asking a candidate for serious work (more than 2-3 hours), you should compensate them. This is both ethical best practice and practical. Compensation filters for seriousness—only people who are genuinely interested will take time to do the work. It also shows respect. These are experienced professionals. You wouldn't ask a lawyer for three hours of work without paying them.

Standard practice: $500-$2,000 depending on the scope of work and the level of seniority. This covers the time investment and shows you're serious. Make it clear upfront: "We'll pay you $1,000 to do this assignment. Here's why we do it: we want to see how you actually think, and we respect your time."

Use assignments only for your final 2-3 candidates. Earlier in the process, conversations and interviews are sufficient filtering.

### Assignment Type 1: The Marketing Audit & 90-Day Plan (Recommended)

This is the gold standard. It's the most informative for you and the most realistic for the candidate (they're actually doing a version of the work they'd do if hired).

Give the candidate access to your data. Create a sanitized data package that includes: current website analytics, funnel metrics (visitors, leads, MQLs, SQLs, customers, conversion rates at each stage), current marketing spend by channel, current team structure and headcount, competitive landscape (3-5 main competitors), current positioning/messaging (website copy, latest sales collateral), and any other context that would be helpful (product roadmap, sales process, ICP definition).

The ask: Based on this data, please provide:
1. A diagnosis of the top 3 problems and/or opportunities in our marketing function
2. A 30-60-90 day plan (what you'd do immediately, what you'd tackle in weeks 4-8, what you'd focus on in months 2-3)
3. A proposed team structure and headcount plan for 12 months
4. An estimated budget and how you'd allocate it
5. Key metrics and how you'd measure success

Deliverable: 7-10 slides or a written document (your choice). Presentation: 30 minutes + 30 minutes of Q&A.

Time limit: One week from the time they receive the brief.

What good looks like:

The diagnosis is grounded in data, not theory. They don't just quote best practices—they identify YOUR specific problems. "Your biggest opportunity is LinkedIn. You're running ads but the targeting is too broad. You should be ABM-ing your target accounts. Second, your funnel has a huge leak at lead-to-MQL conversion. That's your team's qualification process, not the volume problem." They've actually looked at your data.

The 90-day plan is specific and sequenced. They're not trying to do everything immediately. Day 1 is "meetings with everyone—sales, product, customers, the board, whoever's willing to talk to me." Week 1 is "analysis and diagnosis." Week 2 is "develop 30-60-90 plan and get feedback from the team." Week 3 is launching first initiatives—quick wins that don't take a long time but show progress. Month 2 is bigger structural changes. Month 3 is optimization and planning for the next phase. It's realistic about what can be accomplished.

The team structure is built from the work that needs doing, not from ego. "To accomplish this, I need someone running demand gen, someone running content, someone on analytics. That's 4 of us total (including me) to get started. By month 6-9, I'd want to add someone for events/partnerships. By month 12, maybe another content person or a junior coordinator."

The budget is realistic and tied to outcomes. Not just "I need $X"—it's "To hit our pipeline targets, we should spend roughly $X per month on paid ads, $Y on content and tools, $Z on events. That's roughly $50K/month or $600K/year. That should generate roughly $X in pipeline at $Y CAC." They're doing the math.

The communication is clear and concise. Not a novel. Not generic frameworks. It's specific, it's data-driven, it's realistic about constraints, and it shows they understand your business.

What bad looks like:

Generic advice that could apply to any company. "Every SaaS company should focus on brand awareness and thought leadership." Okay, but what does that mean for us specifically?

Over-focusing on brand at your stage. "First priority is positioning and messaging overhaul." You don't need that right now. You need pipeline.

Proposing a massive team or budget immediately. "I'd need a team of 8-10 and $200K/month budget." That's not realistic at $10M ARR and it signals they don't understand constraints.

Can't defend their reasoning in the Q&A. You ask "Why would you focus on LinkedIn before Google?" and they say "Just because" or "LinkedIn is hot right now." That's not strategic thinking.

Doesn't ask good questions during the presentation. Great candidates use the Q&A to clarify uncertainty: "I assumed your average deal size was $X, is that right? Because that changes my thinking on lead volume targets." Weak candidates just answer questions.

### Assignment Type 2: The Funnel Diagnostic Exercise

If the full 90-day plan feels like too much work for a candidate or if you want to evaluate them earlier in the process, use a funnel diagnostic. Present your actual funnel (visitors → leads → MQLs → SQLs → Opportunities → Closed Won) with real numbers. Ask them to:

1. Identify where the biggest problems/opportunities are
2. What they'd fix first and why
3. What they'd measure

This is live, done during a meeting. 45-60 minutes. Reveals how they think under pressure, whether they ask clarifying questions, how they prioritize.

What good looks like: They ask clarifying questions before jumping to conclusions. "What's the sales cycle? What's the average deal size? How many reps do you have?" Then they do math. "Your biggest leak is MQL to SQL at only 30% conversion. Here's what I'd focus on first..."

What bad looks like: They jump straight to ideas without understanding your context. "You should build more content. You should do more webinars." Sure, but why? How does that address your actual problem?

### Assignment Type 3: The GTM Strategy for a New Segment or Product

If you have a new product expansion or segment that you're considering, use it as an assignment. "Here's our new product X that we're launching to segment Y. Walk me through how you'd take it to market. Include: ICP definition, messaging, channels, timeline, budget, success metrics."

This is great because it's real work you'd be doing if they join, it shows their ability to think strategically about a new business problem, and it doesn't require them to have deep knowledge of your current operation.

### Assignment Type 4: The Competitive Positioning Exercise

Give them 3-5 competitor websites and your own. Ask: Where are we positioned? Where are we losing? How should we position against them? Draft new positioning and messaging for your homepage or a key landing page.

Tests: Product marketing chops, competitive awareness, writing ability, ability to think strategically about differentiation.

Time: Usually 3-4 hours. Can be 2-3 slide presentation or a written memo plus a draft homepage section.

### Assignment Type 5: The Content/Campaign Critique

Share 3-5 of your recent marketing pieces (recent emails, landing pages, ads, blog posts). Ask them to critique each: What's working? What's not? How would you improve the most impactful piece? Draft improved version if you have the time.

Tests: Creative thinking, ability to evaluate quality, understanding of what works in your category.

Time: Usually 2-3 hours. Quick and tactical.

### Evaluation Rubric for Any Assignment

Whatever assignment you choose, create a clear rubric before they start working. You need to know how you're going to evaluate them. Otherwise, you'll be subjective about it.

A sample rubric:

| Dimension | Score (1-5) | Notes |
|-----------|------------|-------|
| **Strategic Thinking** | | Did they diagnose the problem or just propose solutions? Do their recommendations flow from clear reasoning? |
| **Use of Data** | | Did they reference your actual numbers or speak in generalities? Did they do math to support their conclusions? |
| **Prioritization** | | Did they try to do everything or did they ruthlessly focus? Does the sequence make sense? |
| **Feasibility** | | Are their recommendations realistic given constraints? Or are they pie-in-the-sky? |
| **Communication Quality** | | Is this easy to understand? Or is it jargon-filled and unclear? |
| **Demand Gen Orientation** | | Does lead generation come through as the clear priority? Or is it lost among other initiatives? |
| **Cultural Fit Signals** | | From this work, do I see someone who'd thrive here? Are they collaborative, humble, curious? |

Have at least 3 people score each assignment independently before you discuss. This prevents one person's strong opinion from dominating. Then compare scores and discuss.

A great assignment will score 4+ across most dimensions. An okay one will be 3-3.5. A bad one will have variance (3 in thinking, 2 in data, 2 in feasibility) or lows across the board.

The person doesn't have to be perfect. But their thinking should be clear, grounded, and specific to your business. If they've given you a stock answer that could apply to any company, that's a red flag.

## Part 4: Reference Checks & Due Diligence

### The Reference Call Framework

Do 6-8 reference calls, not 3. The candidate will give you their three favorite references, and they'll all say nice things. That's not useful. You need a broader perspective.

References should include:
- 2 former managers (people who've directly managed them)
- 2 former peers at the same level (especially people from sales functions, since they're your closest partner)
- 2 former direct reports (people they've managed)
- 1-2 back-channel references (people they didn't provide, people you found through mutual connections)

For each reference call, spend 30-45 minutes. This is not a "check the box" exercise. This is where you'll learn the most about how someone actually works.

### The 10 Best Reference Questions

Have a consistent script. Ask every reference similar questions so you can compare. Here are the strongest questions, with what you're listening for:

**1. "What did you work together on? What was [candidate name] responsible for?"**

Listen for: Do they remember clearly or are they vague? Do they reference specific accomplishments or do they speak in generalities? This is a baseline check that they actually worked together and that the relationship was meaningful.

**2. "What were [candidate's] biggest strengths in that role?"**

Listen for: Do they mention demand gen, pipeline, or other things relevant to your role? Do they talk about execution or just strategy? Are they speaking from observation or are they reading from notes the candidate probably provided?

**3. "What were their weaknesses or areas where they could have been stronger?"**

Listen for: The honest answer. Good references will give you real weaknesses, not corporate spin. "She's so intense about execution that sometimes she doesn't take enough time to plan strategically." That's a real weakness. "She's a perfectionist" is corporate speak. Red flag if they can't name any weakness.

**4. "How big was the team they were managing? Did they hire well? Any particularly good or bad hires?"**

Listen for: Leadership capability. Could they recruit? Could they develop people? Or did they burn people out?

**5. "Tell me about a time [candidate] really frustrated you or you disagreed with them. How did that get resolved?"**

Listen for: Can they handle conflict? Do they get defensive or can they see the other person's perspective? What's their communication style when things are hard?

**6. "What energized [candidate] in that role? What frustrated them?"**

Listen for: Do these align with your company? If they got frustrated by ambiguity and your company has tons of ambiguity, that's important to know.

**7. "How did [candidate] work with sales?" (if applicable)**

Listen for: Any friction? How did they handle it? Are they territorial or collaborative? This is critical for your hire.

**8. "If you had the chance, would you hire/work with [candidate] again?"**

Listen for: The honest answer. Sometimes the reference is positive but has caveats. "Yeah, she's great, but she can be hard to work with." Or "Yeah, absolutely." That's different.

**9. "What would you advise [candidate] to work on for their next role?"**

Listen for: Self-awareness feedback. Are the weaknesses consistent across references? Is there a pattern?

**10. "Is there anything I should know that we haven't talked about?"**

Listen for: They might tell you something important that didn't fit neatly into earlier questions. "Oh, and she burns out. After 18 months she needs a break." That's useful.

### Back-Channeling: How to Do It Right

Before you do formal references, do informal back-channel checks. Find people who worked with them but weren't provided as references. Use LinkedIn to find colleagues from their previous roles. Ask mutual connections. This gives you a different perspective than formal references.

The back-channel conversation is more casual: "Hey, I'm hiring and [candidate name] came through. I see you worked together at [company]. How was that experience?" People are often more honest in informal conversations.

Also review their content. Do they have a blog? Do they post on LinkedIn? What are they saying? Are they thoughtful or are they just sharing generic motivational quotes? Do they show real thinking or just follow trends?

### Red Flags That Should Disqualify

Some things should be automatic disqualifiers:

**Can't articulate specific contributions.** They speak in "we" language but when pressed can't explain what specifically they did. "We grew revenue 50%" is great, but "I did X which led to Y which drove that growth" is better. If they can't be specific, they're either embellishing or they don't own anything.

**No pipeline commit in previous roles.** If they've never been accountable for lead generation or pipeline, they're not the right person for your stage. They're probably a strategist or a generalist, not a builder.

**Has never worked in a resource-constrained environment.** They've always had big budgets and big teams. They might not be able to adapt. Ask them directly: "Tell me about a time you had to do more with less." If they've never been there, flag it.

**Wants a massive team or budget immediately.** "I'll need 10 people and $300K a month." That's not realistic at $10M ARR and it signals they don't understand constraints.

**Badmouths previous employers or colleagues.** There's a difference between "I learned a lot but didn't think we were executing well enough" and "My last boss was incompetent." One shows reflection, the other shows blame orientation.

**Pattern of short tenures without good explanation.** One 18-month stint can happen for good reasons. Three of them in a row? That's a pattern. Why are they leaving so quickly?

**Focused on title and comp more than the problem to solve.** "What will my title be? How much equity?" before "Tell me more about the challenge." Wrong priorities.

**Can't articulate why they want to work for you specifically.** "I'm looking for a Series A opportunity" is different from "I've studied your market and I see you're solving X problem that I'm passionate about." The first says they'll leave for the next Series A. The second says they want to build this with you.

### Green Flags That Predict Success

Flip side—look for these things:

**Has built something from scratch before.** They understand what it takes to build a function from nothing. They're not expecting a well-oiled machine that they just scale.

**Can show clear before-and-after metrics.** "When I joined we had $X pipeline, when I left we had $Y pipeline and CAC was Z." They measure things and they drive them.

**Former colleagues would follow them.** This is powerful. If multiple people have worked with them and would happily work with them again, that says something about their leadership and their capability.

**Asks better questions than they answer.** In the interview, they keep asking you probing questions about the business, the market, the team. They're thinking, not just reacting.

**Gets excited about your specific problem.** Not generic enthusiasm ("I love SaaS!") but specific interest in the market, the problem, your ICP. They've done homework.

**Treats the interview as a two-way evaluation.** They're evaluating you too. They ask about management style, about the team, about what happens if things don't go well. They're thoughtful about fit, not just trying to close a job.

**Has strong opinions, loosely held.** They'll tell you exactly how they'd approach something, but when challenged they're willing to learn and adapt. Not dogmatic.

## Part 5: Making the Offer & Setting Up for Success

### Compensation Benchmarks

Based on market research from Betts Recruiting, TopStartups.io, Hunt Club, and Carta:

**Base Salary:** $200K-$300K (median $250K). This varies by geography—SF/NYC higher, Southeast lower. Also depends on the person's background and current comp.

**Equity:** 0.5-1.2% (non-founder level). This is probably higher than you're thinking. But you're competing with other Series A companies for the same talent, and the best people are choosing between multiple offers. 0.5-0.8% is standard for VP-level.

**Bonus:** 10-30% of base (median 20%). Typically tied to revenue targets or pipeline targets.

**Total Comp:** Usually $300K-$450K all-in, with the mix varying. Some candidates will want more base and less equity. Others will take lower base for higher equity. There's flexibility.

The mistake most founders make is anchoring too low on equity. You think 0.2% but the market is 0.5-0.8%. This creates negotiation friction and sometimes loses you the candidate.

Also, don't cheap out on base salary trying to save on equity. You want someone who can focus on the work, not stress about whether they're getting paid market rate. Underpay on base and you get resentment.

### The Sell: Closing a Great Candidate

Great candidates have options. By the time you're making an offer, they might have other offers or other opportunities. You need to make them want to choose you.

Here's what works:

**Sell the problem, not the company.** "This is an interesting market opportunity. You'd be solving X problem that affects thousands of companies." That's more compelling than "We're a Series A SaaS company."

**Sell the team.** Great people want to work with other great people. If your engineering team is impressive, say so. If your CEO is smart, demonstrate it. If your sales team is crushing it, mention it.

**Sell the trajectory.** $10M → $30M is exciting if you can show the path. "Here's how we get there" is more believable than "We think it's possible."

**Sell the relationship.** They're going to spend 60+ hours a week with you. Your working relationship matters. Show that you're someone they'd want to work for—you're collaborative, you're clear about expectations, you're coachable, you're ambitious but realistic.

**Demo the product.** Better than any pitch deck. Let them use it. Get them excited about what you've built.

**Show the equity potential.** Not with unrealistic scenarios ("you'll be a millionaire!") but with realistic math. "Here's what happened in these comparable companies at this stage. Here's a conservative scenario, a base case, and an upside case for equity value. You could do well here."

**Be honest about the challenges.** You're competing for pipeline, you're fundraising in tough markets, you have execution risks. Great candidates respect honesty more than hype.

### First 90 Days: What Good Looks Like

The first 90 days set the tone. A strong start compounds. A weak start creates problems that take months to overcome.

**Days 1-30: Listen and Learn**

The new VP spends time meeting everyone. Sales team, product, customer success, engineering, finance, board members, advisors. They're learning the business, the market, the team dynamics, the challenges. They're resisting big changes. They're auditing what exists. They're asking questions, not telling people what to do.

By end of month 1, they've created a picture of the business: where leads come from, what's working, what's broken. They've built relationships with the key people, especially sales.

**Days 30-60: Diagnose and Plan**

Month 2 is when they identify the top 3 priorities. They're not trying to fix everything. They're focused. "Here's what's working. Here's what needs fixing. Here are the three things we're going to tackle first."

They also start getting quick wins. Not big structural changes. Quick wins. "We're going to improve our landing page copy," or "We're going to test a new lead source," or "We're going to implement a new lead scoring model." Things that take 1-2 weeks and show results by the end of month 2.

They're building sales alignment. Sales should feel like they're getting support, not that marketing is taking over.

By end of month 2, there should be early pipeline impact or a clear plan for how to get it.

**Days 60-90: Plan and Execute**

Month 3, they present a comprehensive 12-month plan. What's the strategy? How does it connect to revenue targets? What's the team structure? What's the budget? How will we measure success?

They start executing harder. Some of the quick wins are showing results. They're hiring (or have hiring plans clear). They're building momentum.

By end of month 3, you should feel confident they have a plan and they're executing it. You should see early evidence of pipeline lift or at least a clear reason why not yet with a path to it.

### When to Pull the Plug

This is the hardest part, but it's critical. If by month 4-5 you realize you have the wrong person, you need to act.

Jason Lemkin at SaaStr has a rule: "If you wouldn't hire them again, it's time." That's the threshold. Not "are they perfect," but "would I rehire them?"

Also watch for these signals: Sales is losing faith (they're not responding to marketing, they're not treating leads as useful). You're not seeing pipeline impact and there's no credible explanation. They're focused on the wrong things (brand instead of demand gen, or vice versa). They're not coachable—they're defensive about feedback. They're not building a good team—people are leaving.

You'll know. And when you know, act. Most founders wait too long. You'll probably say, in retrospect, "I should have made a change 3-4 months earlier." That's normal. Most CEOs have this regret.

Be respectful when you do it. Sit down, be honest: "This isn't working for us or for you. Here's what we're seeing. I think you'd be happier somewhere else and we need someone who can drive pipeline immediately. Let's figure out a good transition." Often it becomes clear to them too and it's actually a relief.

| Strategic Thinking | Diagnosis grounded in your data. Reasoning clear. Not generic advice. |
| Use of Data | References your actual numbers. Does math. Doesn't speak in generalities. |
| Prioritization | Ruthlessly focused. Not trying to do everything. Sequence makes sense. |
| Feasibility | Realistic given your constraints. Not pie-in-the-sky. |
| Communication | Clear and concise. Easy to understand. No jargon. |
| Demand Gen Focus | Demand gen is clearly the priority. Pipeline is the focus. |
| Questions Asked | In Q&A, do they ask clarifying questions or just answer? |

### Reference Check Script

**Call Introduction:**

"Hi [Name]. Thanks for making time. I'm hiring for our VP of Marketing role and [Candidate Name] came through our process. I see you worked together at [Company] from [dates]. I'd like to learn more about that experience and about [Candidate] as a professional. Do you have about 30-45 minutes?"

---

**Question 1:** "What did you work together on? What was [Candidate] responsible for?"

*Listening for:* Real experience? Clarity on contributions? Or vague/rehearsed answers?

---

**Question 2:** "What were [Candidate's] biggest strengths in that role?"

*Listening for:* Specific strengths relevant to your hire? Demand gen? Pipeline? Execution? Leadership?

---

**Question 3:** "What were areas where they could have been stronger? What would you encourage them to work on?"

*Listening for:* Honest answer? Real weakness or corporate spin? Consistency across references?

---

**Question 4:** "Tell me about the team they were managing. Did they hire well? Any particularly great hires or cautionary tales?"

*Listening for:* Leadership capability? Judgment in hiring? Ability to recruit?

---

**Question 5:** "Can you think of a time when you disagreed with [Candidate] or they frustrated you? How did that get resolved?"

*Listening for:* How do they handle conflict? Defensive? Open? Collaborative?

---

**Question 6:** "What energized [Candidate] in that role? What frustrated them?"

*Listening for:* Do these align with your role/company? Is there a pattern of frustration?

---

**Question 7:** "How did [Candidate] work with the sales team?" [If applicable: If they managed sales relationships]

*Listening for:* Alignment? Friction? How did they resolve it? Any red flags?

---

**Question 8:** "If you had the chance to work together again, would you? Why or why not?"

*Listening for:* Honest answer. Sometimes there are caveats: "Yeah, she's great, but..." That's different from unequivocal yes.

---

**Question 9:** "What would you advise [Candidate] to be aware of as they step into their next role?"

*Listening for:* Self-awareness? What growth areas are consistent?

---

**Question 10:** "Is there anything I should know that we haven't covered?"

*Listening for:* They might volunteer something important here. Something they didn't fit into earlier answers.

---

**Call Close:**

"This was really helpful. Thank you for the time. Can I follow up if I have additional questions?"
