# Perception Score Methodology

## What this is

The Perception Score measures **what story AI tells about your brand**. Not whether you show up (that's Presence). Not what the market thinks (that's Reputation). This is the narrative layer. When a buyer asks ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Claude about your category, what do they actually say about you?

We score that narrative across six attributes. Each one is grounded in what B2B buyers actually care about, validated across 30,000+ survey respondents, four review platform scoring systems, five analyst frameworks, and 2.2 million real AI prompts.

Six attributes. One word each. Scored 0-10.

**Capability. Usability. Value. Trust. Support. Innovation.**

---

## Why these six

Every B2B vendor evaluation framework in existence collapses to a small number of dimensions. Gartner uses 15 criteria but two axes. Forrester uses three dimensions. G2 uses two. TrustRadius uses one composite score. The platforms that last are the ones that pick the right abstractions.

We tested our attributes against three filters:

1. **Does a CMO instantly understand it?** No jargon. No explanation needed. If you have to define the word, it's wrong.
2. **Can an LLM reliably score it from AI-generated text?** We're not surveying humans. We're classifying language. The attribute needs to map to detectable patterns in how AI engines talk about brands.
3. **Does it map to a distinct buyer concern with zero overlap?** Six attributes means zero waste. Each one must stand on its own.

Here's each attribute, what it absorbs, and the research that backs it.

---

## 1. Capability

**The question it answers:** Does AI describe your product as powerful and complete, or limited and narrow?

**What it absorbs:** Core features, functionality, scalability, AI/automation capabilities, integration and ecosystem fit, product depth and breadth.

**How we score it:** We classify every AI-generated mention of a brand for language related to product capabilities, feature completeness, scalability, integrations, and technical depth. Positive signals: "comprehensive," "feature-rich," "handles enterprise scale," "robust API," "integrates with." Negative signals: "limited," "basic," "lacks," "doesn't support," "standalone tool."

**Scale:**
- 0-3: AI describes the product as limited, basic, or narrow in scope
- 4-6: AI describes adequate functionality with noted gaps
- 7-8: AI describes strong, comprehensive capabilities
- 9-10: AI positions the product as best-in-class across multiple dimensions

### Why Capability is first

Every evaluation framework on the planet starts here. Gartner's Product/Service criterion is typically the heaviest-weighted factor in Magic Quadrant evaluations. Forrester's Current Offering axis spans 8-25+ sub-criteria, all rooted in what the product actually does. G2's "Meets Requirements" is one of only three HIGH-weight satisfaction dimensions.

The buyer data is unambiguous. 89% of B2B buyers in 2025 purchased solutions with AI features baked in (6sense, n=4,000). 81% rated AI functionality as important or very important (G2 2024). Feature/attribute scores on review platforms are valued highest by enterprise buyers at 22% when evaluating software reviews.

Capability absorbs several dimensions that might seem like they deserve their own slot:

**Scalability folds in here.** In our research across buyer surveys, scalability is rarely evaluated as a standalone dimension. It's a capability question: can this product handle my growth? When AI says "great for small teams but struggles at scale," that's a Capability signal, not a separate axis.

**Integration and ecosystem folds in here.** Modern B2B buyers don't buy tools. They buy pieces of a stack. When AI describes your integrations, API quality, and ecosystem partnerships, it's describing what your product can do in context. Transparent pricing with comprehensive integrations correlated with 3.2x more AI visibility (Goodie AI, 2.2M prompts analyzed).

**AI/automation capabilities fold in here.** G2 titled their 2025 report "AI Now Means Always Included." 55% of buyers said adding AI was the primary trigger for their most recent purchase. AI capabilities are no longer innovation. They're table-stakes product functionality.

### How review platforms measure this

| Platform | Dimension | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| G2 | Meets Requirements | HIGH |
| G2 | Feature Ratings | Varies by category |
| Capterra | Functionality | 50% of usability axis |
| Gartner PI | Product Capabilities | Required rating (1 of 3) |
| TrustRadius | Product capabilities | Core component of trScore |
| Forrester Wave | Current Offering | Primary axis (8-25+ sub-criteria) |
| Gartner MQ | Product/Service | Typically highest-weighted Execute criterion |

## 2. Usability

**The question it answers:** Does AI describe your product as easy to use and fast to implement, or complex and painful to adopt?

**What it absorbs:** Ease of use, implementation speed, onboarding complexity, time to value, learning curve, UX quality, ease of administration.

**How we score it:** We classify AI-generated text for language about user experience, setup complexity, learning curve, and time to value. Positive signals: "intuitive," "easy to set up," "minimal training," "quick onboarding," "user-friendly interface." Negative signals: "steep learning curve," "complex setup," "requires training," "difficult to configure," "clunky."

**Scale:**
- 0-3: AI describes a product that's hard to use, slow to implement, and frustrating
- 4-6: AI describes a product that's functional but requires effort to adopt
- 7-8: AI describes a product that's intuitive with reasonable onboarding
- 9-10: AI describes an exceptional user experience with near-instant time to value

### Why Usability stands alone

This is the most consistently high-weighted dimension across every review platform we studied.

G2 weights "Ease of Use" as HIGH in its satisfaction scoring. It's one of only three dimensions at that weight level. Capterra gives "Ease of Use" 50% weight on its entire usability axis. TrustRadius's Buyer's Choice award requires 75% of reviewers to select the product for best capabilities AND best relationship, and usability is the bridge between those two.

The buyer data adds urgency to this. 57% of B2B buyers expect ROI within three months of purchase. 11% expect it immediately (G2 2024). 58% of buyers report regretting purchases partly due to onboarding challenges. That means Usability isn't just about the daily experience. It's about how fast a buyer gets value.

This is why we fold time-to-value and implementation speed into Usability rather than treating them separately. From the buyer's perspective, "is this easy?" and "how fast do I get results?" are the same question asked at different moments.

Bain's B2B Elements of Value research provides the deepest validation. For IT infrastructure buyers, 7 of the top 10 most important value elements sit within the "ease of doing business" level, not the functional level. As products commoditize, usability-adjacent attributes (anxiety reduction, time savings, simplification) become increasingly decisive for loyalty and NPS.

### How review platforms measure this

| Platform | Dimension | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| G2 | Ease of Use | HIGH |
| G2 | Ease of Setup | MEDIUM |
| G2 | Ease of Admin | MEDIUM |
| Capterra | Ease of Use | 50% of usability axis |
| Gartner PI | Integration & Deployment | Additional sub-rating |
| TrustRadius | Implementation & onboarding sentiment | Component of trScore |
| Forrester Wave | Ease of implementation (sub-criterion) | Weighted within Current Offering |

## 3. Value

**The question it answers:** Does AI position you as worth the money, or as expensive relative to what you deliver?

**What it absorbs:** Pricing, affordability, ROI, total cost of ownership, pricing transparency, contract flexibility, cost efficiency.

**How we score it:** We classify AI-generated text for language about pricing, cost, ROI, and financial value. Positive signals: "affordable," "competitive pricing," "strong ROI," "transparent pricing," "good value," "free tier." Negative signals: "expensive," "overpriced," "hidden costs," "not worth it," "pricing complexity."

**Scale:**
- 0-3: AI describes the product as expensive with unclear value
- 4-6: AI describes adequate value with pricing caveats
- 7-8: AI describes strong value relative to capabilities
- 9-10: AI positions the product as best-in-class value in the category

### Why Value is the #1 purchase driver

This is not an opinion. It's the most cited factor in the largest buyer survey dataset we analyzed.

TrustRadius found that 66% of buyers selected their product because it "met our needs for the best price." That's the single most cited purchase driver across 2,164 respondents. Gartner Digital Markets corroborates: 49% name price a top consideration during search (n=2,499).

CFOs now hold final decision-making power in 79% of purchases. Variable and outcome-based pricing models are preferred by more than one-third of buyers. This means the financial narrative around your product isn't just a feature comparison. It's the story that gets you through the final approval gate.

AI engines surface pricing prominently. In the Goodie AI analysis of 2.2M prompts, products with transparent pricing achieved 3.2x more AI visibility than those without. When AI engines compare software, pricing and plans consistently rank as the second most frequently discussed dimension, right behind core features.

Value absorbs total cost of ownership. Buyers don't evaluate sticker price in isolation. They evaluate implementation costs, training, maintenance, customization, and migration costs. When AI says "Ramp is more affordable than Brex," that's a Value signal. When AI says "the setup costs can be significant," that's also a Value signal. Both matter.

### How review platforms measure this

| Platform | Dimension | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| G2 | Ease of Doing Business With | MEDIUM |
| Capterra | Value for Money | 25% of satisfaction axis |
| TrustRadius | Best Value for Price | 1 of 3 Buyer's Choice criteria |
| Gartner MQ | Sales Execution/Pricing | Ability to Execute criterion |
| Forrester Wave | Business Model | Strategy sub-criterion |

## 4. Trust

**The question it answers:** Does AI describe you as a safe, reliable, secure choice, or as a risk?

**What it absorbs:** Security, compliance, reliability, uptime, data protection, brand credibility, enterprise-readiness, vendor stability, breach history.

**How we score it:** We classify AI-generated text for language about security posture, compliance certifications, reliability, data handling, and enterprise-readiness. Positive signals: "SOC 2 compliant," "enterprise-grade security," "reliable," "trusted by," "99.9% uptime," "GDPR compliant." Negative signals: "security concerns," "data breach," "reliability issues," "downtime," "not enterprise-ready."

**Scale:**
- 0-3: AI raises security concerns or describes unreliable/risky product
- 4-6: AI describes adequate security without strong trust signals
- 7-8: AI references specific certifications and enterprise readiness
- 9-10: AI positions the product as the safe, trusted standard in the category

### Why Trust is the biggest collapse

Trust is the word that collapses the most sub-dimensions into one. Security, compliance, reliability, brand credibility, and vendor stability all answer the same underlying buyer question: can I bet my job on this?

The evidence for making it a single attribute is overwhelming.

Security alone is the #1 consideration across six major department types: accounting, engineering, R&D, purchasing, HR, and IT (G2 2024). Among enterprise buyers, 81% consider vendor breach history when evaluating solutions. 97% involve a security stakeholder at some point. This criterion eliminates vendors before they ever reach a shortlist.

But security alone isn't what Trust measures. Brand trust and prior experience function as a gatekeeper with even broader reach. 6sense found that 95% of the time, the winning vendor was on the buyer's Day One shortlist, and 77% purchased from their preliminary favorite before any vendor contact. TrustRadius confirms 78% of buyers shortlist products they've already heard of (86% among enterprise buyers).

Forrester's MaxDiff analysis of trust drivers reveals what actually builds it: competency (18.5%), consistency (17.0%), and dependability (16.8%) rank as the top three trust attributes.

The reason these collapse into one attribute is that buyers don't separate them. A buyer asking "is this safe to choose?" is asking about data security AND reliability AND vendor stability AND brand credibility simultaneously. In AI-generated text, these signals cluster together. When AI says "trusted by thousands of enterprises" in the same breath as "SOC 2 Type II compliant," those reinforce the same narrative dimension.

### How review platforms measure this

| Platform | Dimension | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| G2 | Quality of Support + Market Presence signals | Combined effect |
| TrustRadius | Best Customer Relationship | 1 of 3 Buyer's Choice criteria |
| Capterra | Profile completeness + verified reviews | Ranking signal |
| Gartner PI | Service & Support + review diversity | Required rating + methodology |
| Gartner MQ | Overall Viability | Ability to Execute criterion |
| Forrester Wave | Customer Feedback (dot size) | Third dimension |

## 5. Support

**The question it answers:** Does AI describe your support as responsive and helpful, or slow and frustrating?

**What it absorbs:** Customer support quality, responsiveness, documentation, self-service resources, success management, onboarding assistance.

**How we score it:** We classify AI-generated text for language about support quality, responsiveness, and customer success. Positive signals: "excellent support," "responsive team," "great documentation," "dedicated success manager," "24/7 support." Negative signals: "poor support," "slow response," "limited documentation," "no phone support," "long wait times."

**Scale:**
- 0-3: AI describes support as a significant weakness
- 4-6: AI describes adequate support with noted limitations
- 7-8: AI describes strong, responsive support
- 9-10: AI positions support as a competitive advantage and differentiator

### Why Support can't fold into anything else

We tried. It doesn't collapse cleanly.

Support isn't Trust (that's about whether you're safe to choose). Support isn't Usability (that's about whether you can figure it out yourself). Support is what happens when things break, when implementation stalls, when a buyer needs help.

Every single review platform measures it independently. Capterra gives Customer Support a staggering 50% weight on its entire satisfaction axis. G2 rates Quality of Support as one of only three HIGH-weight satisfaction dimensions. Gartner Peer Insights requires a Service & Support sub-rating as one of only three mandatory ratings. TrustRadius's Buyer's Choice award requires best customer relationship alongside best capabilities and best value.

The buyer journey data reinforces this. At the renewal stage, support quality is one of the strongest predictors of retention. Customers using 70%+ of core features are 2x more likely to stay, and a huge driver of feature adoption is how much help they got along the way. Best-in-class SaaS companies achieve 85-87% annual logo retention, and proactive customer success management is the common thread.

Support also absorbs customer success, which is increasingly what distinguishes top vendors. This isn't just reactive helpdesk. It's proactive guidance, strategic reviews, and outcome-driven engagement. When AI says "Ramp offers dedicated account management," that's a Support signal even though it's technically a success function.

|---|---|---|
| G2 | Quality of Support | HIGH |
| Capterra | Customer Support | 50% of satisfaction axis |
| Gartner PI | Service & Support | Required rating (1 of 3) |
| TrustRadius | Best Customer Relationship | 1 of 3 Buyer's Choice criteria |
| Forrester Wave | Support (sub-criterion) | Weighted within Current Offering |
| Gartner MQ | Customer Experience | Ability to Execute criterion |

## 6. Innovation

**The question it answers:** Does AI describe you as forward-thinking and evolving, or stagnant and falling behind?

**What it absorbs:** Product vision, roadmap, differentiation, competitive uniqueness, R&D investment, emerging capabilities, market leadership trajectory.

**How we score it:** We classify AI-generated text for language about product direction, uniqueness, and forward momentum. Positive signals: "rapidly improving," "recently launched," "innovative approach," "unique in that," "leading the way," "pioneering." Negative signals: "hasn't changed," "falling behind," "legacy," "stagnant," "no clear roadmap," "generic."

**Scale:**
- 0-3: AI describes a stagnant product with no differentiation
- 4-6: AI describes a product with some unique aspects but limited vision
- 7-8: AI describes an actively evolving product with clear differentiation
- 9-10: AI positions the product as a category-defining innovator

### Why Innovation absorbs Differentiation

Differentiation was in our original 10-attribute framework. We cut it. Here's why.

CEB research found that only 14% of companies offer benefits customers find truly unique. That means for 86% of B2B software, differentiation is aspirational rather than evaluable. An attribute that scores 86% of brands the same way isn't useful.

But the underlying signal matters. When AI says "Ramp is uniquely strong at real-time spend controls," that's meaningful. It's just that signal lives under Innovation, not a separate attribute. Innovation captures both "where are you going?" (vision, roadmap) and "what makes you different?" (uniqueness, competitive separation).

This maps directly to how analyst frameworks work. Gartner's Completeness of Vision axis evaluates market understanding, innovation investment, and offering strategy. Forrester's Strategy dimension covers roadmap, innovation commitment, and business model. Both combine forward-looking trajectory with competitive differentiation. We're doing the same.

Innovation is the hardest attribute to score from AI text. AI engines discuss product direction less frequently than features, pricing, or reviews. But when they do, it's highly differentiating. When ChatGPT says "Brand X is investing heavily in AI-native workflows while competitors are still bolting on basic automation," that's a strong Innovation signal that directly impacts buyer perception.

### How review platforms measure this

| Platform | Dimension | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| G2 | Product Direction (Satisfaction) | LOW (but published) |
| G2 | Momentum Grid | Separate report on trajectory |
| Gartner MQ | Completeness of Vision (full axis) | 50% of placement |
| Gartner MQ | Innovation | Vision sub-criterion |
| Forrester Wave | Strategy (full axis) | Primary dimension |
| IDC MarketScape | Strategies (full axis) | 3-5 year strategic alignment |
| Everest PEAK | Star Performer designation | Year-over-year movement |
| GigaOm Radar | Innovation criterion | Explicit, product-focused |

## How we calculate the Perception Score

### Input

Every AI-generated response about a brand, tracked across ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, Gemini, and Google AI Overviews. Thousands of responses per brand, collected across hundreds of prompts per category.

### Classification

Each response runs through a classification layer (our own LLM) that scores each relevant attribute on a 0-10 scale based on the language used. Not every response contains signals for every attribute. A response about pricing won't score on Innovation. A response about product roadmap won't score on Support. The classifier only scores what's present.

### Aggregation

For each attribute, we aggregate all classified scores across all responses:

- Weight by recency (recent responses count more than older ones)
- Weight by engine diversity (appearing positive across multiple engines is stronger than one)
- Normalize against category peers (a 7 in CRM means something different than a 7 in DevOps)

### Output

Six attribute scores (0-10 each) plus a composite Perception Score.

The composite Perception Score is the unweighted average of all six attributes. This is the public-facing number. In the dashboard, paying users can apply custom weights to match what matters most in their category (e.g., Trust might matter more in security software; Usability might matter more in design tools).

## The complete picture

The Perception Score is one of three scores in CheckThat's framework:

| Score | What it measures | Data source | Who else does this |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Presence** | Do you show up in AI? | CheckThat proprietary | Nobody |
| **Reputation** | What does the market think? | External platforms (G2, Gartner, etc.) | Everybody (fragmented) |
| **Perception** | What story does AI tell about you? | CheckThat proprietary | Nobody |

Presence tells you if you exist. Reputation tells you if you're credible. Perception tells you what narrative AI is building about your brand every day.

The combination gives CMOs a complete answer to the three questions that actually matter:

1. Do we show up in AI answers for the questions our buyers ask? **(Presence)**
2. How credible are we in the broader market? **(Reputation)**
3. What story does AI tell about us when it does mention us? **(Perception)**

## What we cut and why

We started with 10 attributes. We collapsed to 6. Here's what got absorbed and why.

**Scalability → Capability.** Scalability is a capability question. "Can it handle my growth?" is really "what can it do at my size?" Buyer surveys rarely evaluate scalability as a standalone dimension.

**Integration & Ecosystem → Capability.** Modern B2B buyers evaluate integrations as a product capability. "Does it connect to my stack?" is a feature question.

**AI/Automation → Capability.** AI features moved from differentiator to table stakes. 89% of purchases now include AI features. It's functionality, not innovation.

**Reliability → Trust.** Uptime, consistency, and dependability answer the same question as security and compliance: is this safe to choose?

**Market Position → cut entirely.** Market position is an outcome of the other scores, not an independent attribute. If Capability, Trust, and Innovation are strong, market position follows. And AI engines express market position through the other attributes anyway ("leading CRM" is a Capability + Innovation signal).

**Differentiation → Innovation.** Only 14% of companies offer benefits customers find truly unique. The signal that matters (what makes you stand out) maps naturally to forward-looking trajectory and vision.

## Research sources

This methodology is grounded in analysis across the following sources:

**Buyer surveys (30,000+ respondents):** TrustRadius 2024 B2B Buying Disconnect Report (n=2,164), G2 2024 and 2025 Buyer Behavior Reports (n=1,900 and n=1,169), Gartner Digital Markets 2024 Software Buying Survey (n=2,499), Forrester Buyers' Journey Surveys 2024-2025 (n=18,000+), 6sense Buyer Experience Reports 2024-2025 (n=4,000), McKinsey B2B Pulse Survey (n=3,942), HubSpot B2B Buyer Survey (n=422).

**Review platform scoring methodologies:** G2 Grid methodology (8 satisfaction dimensions, Market Presence formula), TrustRadius trScore algorithm (0-10 weighted average, bias correction), Capterra/Software Advice FrontRunners methodology (published weightings), Gartner Peer Insights Voice of the Customer v3.5 (June 2025).

**Analyst frameworks:** Gartner Magic Quadrant (15 criteria, two axes), Forrester Wave (2024 overhaul, three dimensions), IDC MarketScape (Capabilities vs Strategies), Everest Group PEAK Matrix (Star Performer designation), GigaOm Radar (product-focused, no market-share weighting).

**AI search behavior data:** Goodie AI analysis of 2.2M real prompts across 6 AI platforms (Jan-June 2025), Search Engine Journal cross-platform disagreement study (61.9% disagreement rate), TrustRadius "From Buzzword to Backbone" study (n=348, Aug 2025), 6sense AI adoption data (94% B2B buyer LLM usage).

**Buyer journey research:** Bain & Company B2B Elements of Value (40 elements, IT buyer analysis), CEB/Gartner buying jobs framework, Forrester purchase stall research (86% stall rate), LinkedIn/Edelman B2B buying committee study, Bain/Google Day One shortlist study (n=1,208).
