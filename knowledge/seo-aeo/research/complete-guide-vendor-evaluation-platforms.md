# How the B2B software world decides who wins: a complete guide to vendor evaluation platforms

**The B2B software buying landscape is shaped by roughly 30 influential evaluation platforms, but 10 dominate purchasing decisions.** These range from Gartner's $6.3B analyst empire — whose Magic Quadrant placement can make or break a vendor's pipeline — to crowd-sourced platforms like G2, where 2.9 million user reviews now rival analyst authority. Understanding how each platform scores, weights, and categorizes vendors is essential for any company seeking to build, participate in, or design evaluation methodologies. This guide dissects every major platform's methodology end-to-end, reveals the scoring criteria most buyers never see, and synthesizes cross-platform patterns into actionable intelligence.

---

## The landscape: 30 platforms that shape B2B buying decisions

Before selecting the top 10 for deep analysis, it's worth mapping the full ecosystem. These 30 platforms fall into five categories, each wielding different types of influence over enterprise purchasing.

**Traditional analyst firms** form the foundation. Gartner (Magic Quadrant, ~$6.3B revenue), Forrester (Wave, ~$404M revenue), and IDC (MarketScape, ~$500M revenue) constitute the "Big Three" or "FIGs" — every major enterprise consults at least one before a significant technology purchase. Below them sit ISG (Provider Lens, ~$280M revenue, strong in sourcing advisory), Everest Group (PEAK Matrix, strong in IT services), GigaOm (Radar, focused on disruptive infrastructure tech), Nucleus Research (Value Matrix, ROI-focused), Constellation Research (ShortList, digital transformation thought leadership), 451 Research/S&P Global (emerging tech analysis integrated with financial data), and HFS Research (Horizons, IT services with bold analyst-led opinions).

**Peer review platforms** have fundamentally disrupted analyst dominance since 2012. G2 (Grid, 2.9M+ reviews, $163M revenue) leads this category with the largest review database. Capterra/Gartner Digital Markets (Shortlist, 2.5M+ reviews across 100M+ annual visitors) dominates SMB discovery. TrustRadius (trScore, quality-focused with 400+ word average reviews) serves mid-market to enterprise buyers. Gartner Peer Insights (Voice of the Customer, 715,000+ reviews feeding directly into Magic Quadrants) bridges the analyst-peer divide. PeerSpot (enterprise-focused, 700+ categories) and Info-Tech/SoftwareReviews (Data Quadrant, 270+ categories with a unique analyst-plus-peer hybrid model) round out this segment.

**Market intelligence platforms** serve strategic rather than transactional buying. CB Insights (Mosaic Score, tracking 11M+ companies with ML-driven prediction) focuses on innovation intelligence and private-market analysis. PitchBook/Morningstar provides financial data for investment decisions. Omdia (Universe, formed from Ovum/IHS Markit merger) specializes in telecom, media, and technology. Frost & Sullivan (Frost Radar, growth consulting plus research) covers industrial and emerging tech markets.

**Industry-specific firms** command outsized influence within their verticals. KLAS Research is the definitive evaluator in healthcare IT — a "Best in KLAS" designation directly drives multi-million-dollar hospital purchasing. KuppingerCole Analysts dominate European identity and cybersecurity evaluation. Celent (Oliver Wyman group) is the go-to for financial services technology. Verdantix leads ESG and sustainability technology evaluation. NelsonHall (NEAT Assessment) specializes in BPO and IT services outsourcing.

**Emerging and developer-focused platforms** represent the frontier. AIMultiple provides data-driven benchmarks for AI, RPA, and automation categories. Aragon Research (Globe) covers AI and collaboration technology. RedMonk pioneered social-signal-driven evaluation, combining GitHub and Stack Overflow data to rank developer technologies. SourceForge, with 27.5M+ monthly visitors, has evolved from open-source hosting into one of the highest-traffic B2B software directories.

## Why these 10 made the cut

The top 10 were selected by scoring each platform across five dimensions: **market influence on actual B2B buying decisions**, **breadth of category coverage**, **reputation and trust among buyers and vendors**, **methodology rigor**, and **scale of data collection**. The final selection ensures representation across all platform types.

Gartner, Forrester, and IDC are indispensable — together they influence the vast majority of enterprise technology purchases exceeding $100K. G2 earns its spot through sheer scale: all Fortune 500 companies use it, and its quarterly Grid reports have become a standard marketing currency. TrustRadius brings the most rigorous verification process in peer reviews. Capterra dominates SMB software discovery with 100M+ annual visitors. Everest Group is the premier evaluator for IT services and outsourcing. GigaOm provides the purest technology-focused evaluation, deliberately excluding market share from its scoring. CB Insights brings unique algorithmic intelligence for emerging vendor evaluation. HFS Research rounds out the list with its practitioner-led, demand-side methodology and influential thought leadership in enterprise transformation.

## Deep dive 1: Gartner — the $6.3 billion kingmaker

### Overview and scale

Gartner is the world's most influential technology research and advisory firm, founded in 1979 by Gideon Gartner. Now publicly traded on the NYSE (ticker: IT) as an S&P 500 component, the company generated **$6.3B in revenue in 2024** and employs over 21,000 associates across 90+ countries. Its research operation fields approximately **2,500 analysts**, handles **500,000+ client interactions** annually, conducts **27,000+ vendor briefings** per year, and maintains **715,000+ vetted peer reviews** on its Peer Insights platform. Roughly 65% of Fortune 1000 and 80% of Global 500 companies are Gartner clients. The firm publishes **100+ Magic Quadrants per year** covering 300+ markets and analyzing nearly 1,000 vendors annually, alongside 90+ Hype Cycles and 100+ Market Guides.

### Core methodology: how the Magic Quadrant works end-to-end

The Magic Quadrant production process spans approximately **six months** from initiation to publication. In Phase 1, analysts select markets that are "established, distinct and viable" for comparative vendor analysis. Gartner now publishes a three-year market coverage outlook showing directional plans. In Phase 2, occurring 8–20 weeks before publication, analysts notify all vendors meeting potential inclusion criteria via email, sharing the market definition, evaluation criteria and weights, and timeline — regardless of whether the vendor is a Gartner client.

Phase 3 is data collection. Vendors receive a detailed questionnaire covering Completeness of Vision, Ability to Execute, and product capabilities, with typically 2–3 weeks to respond. Over **20 customers per vendor** complete lengthy online surveys as reference checks. Analysts supplement this with insights from briefings, client inquiries, Peer Insights reviews, and public sources. If a vendor declines to participate, analysts proceed with publicly available information and note the limitation.

In Phase 4, teams of analysts collaboratively evaluate and relatively rate each vendor against each weighted criterion, combining quantitative and qualitative elements. Phase 5 is rigorous internal peer review, with Gartner's Office of the Ombuds providing process oversight. In Phase 6, vendors review only the static MQ graphic for factual errors — they cannot influence opinions or positioning. Phase 7 is publication of the final report including market definition, evaluation criteria with weights, vendor strengths and cautions, and the MQ graphic.

The **Critical Capabilities** report serves as a companion to the MQ, scoring competing products on a **1–5 scale** against critical differentiators mapped to specific use cases. Off-cycle updates are issued when material product changes occur. Gartner Peer Insights reviews are integrated into the Interactive Magic Quadrant experience, with vendor dots color-coded by average Peer Insights rating.

### The 15 scoring criteria

Vendors are evaluated on **15 weighted criteria** across two axes. The **Ability to Execute** axis (Y-axis) comprises seven sub-criteria: Product/Service (core capabilities, quality, feature sets), Overall Viability (financial health and investment likelihood), Sales Execution/Pricing (deal management, presales, channel effectiveness), Market Responsiveness/Record (flexibility and competitive track record), Marketing Execution (brand programs, awareness, positioning), Customer Experience (support quality, satisfaction, account management), and Operations (organizational structure, skills, operational efficiency).

The **Completeness of Vision** axis (X-axis) comprises eight sub-criteria: Market Understanding (ability to translate buyer needs into products), Marketing Strategy (clarity and differentiation of messaging), Sales Strategy (channel breadth and market reach), Offering/Product Strategy (development approach emphasizing differentiation and future mapping), Business Model (logic of underlying business proposition), Vertical/Industry Strategy (segment-specific resource direction), Innovation (investment in related and complementary capabilities), and Geographic Strategy (international reach through partners, channels, or subsidiaries). Each Magic Quadrant may add market-specific sub-criteria beneath these standard 15.

### Weighting, scoring, and the opacity question

Each criterion is assigned a weight disclosed as High, Medium, or Low in published reports, with specific numerical weights varying by market. Gartner does **not publish specific numerical scores** because, as the firm states, "scores are based on not just quantitative elements, but qualitative as well, so they are not strictly mathematical calculations." The weighted composite scores on each axis determine a vendor's X,Y position on the quadrant, but exact formulas are proprietary. Clients can adjust criterion weightings in the Interactive MQ to generate customized views reflecting their priorities.

### Frameworks beyond the Magic Quadrant

The MQ plots vendors into four quadrants: **Leaders** (upper-right, high execution plus high vision), **Challengers** (upper-left, strong execution with limited vision), **Visionaries** (lower-right, innovative but unproven at scale), and **Niche Players** (lower-left, focused on specific segments). Beyond the MQ, Gartner publishes **Hype Cycles** (introduced 1995, tracking technology expectations across five phases from Innovation Trigger to Plateau of Productivity), **Market Guides** (for emerging markets where vendor positioning is premature), **Voice of the Customer** reports (aggregating Peer Insights data with its own quadrant methodology), and the pilot **Emerging Market Quadrant** (eMQ, launched 2024 for GenAI, refreshed every 2–4 weeks with a streamlined ~25-question questionnaire).

### Vendor participation and costs

Participation in MQ evaluation is **free** — there is no inclusion fee. Vendor briefings are free and can occur roughly four times per year. However, vendors cannot opt out: if they meet inclusion criteria, they are evaluated regardless. Advisory subscriptions range from **low five-figures to mid six-figures annually** ($10K–$500K+), and licensing MQ graphics for marketing (reprints) generates significant additional revenue. Client status provides substantial practical advantages: more frequent analyst access, better understanding of weighted criteria, and faster response capabilities. The estimated vendor effort to participate fully in an MQ process is **up to 150 person-hours** across briefings, questionnaire completion, and customer reference coordination.

### Known criticisms

The most persistent criticism is the **pay-to-play perception**. Gartner generates revenue from the same vendors it evaluates through subscriptions, events, and reprints, and does not disclose vendor-specific revenues. Multiple lawsuits have been filed, including ZL Technologies' $132M claim and NetScout Systems' suit alleging Gartner's model is "extortionate by its very nature." In its legal defense, Gartner characterized MQ reports as "pure opinion" — opinions not based on fact. Additional criticisms include **methodology opacity** (exact scoring formulas are proprietary), **bias toward large vendors** (inclusion criteria on revenue, customer count, and geographic presence inherently favor incumbents), **category rigidity** (annual cycles can't keep pace with technology convergence), and **simplicity of the 2×2 framework** (reducing complex landscapes to four categories, where being labeled a "Niche Player" can damage a vendor commercially regardless of actual product quality).

### Handling emerging categories

Gartner follows a defined progression: technologies first appear on **Hype Cycles**, then innovative small vendors are spotlighted in **Cool Vendors** reports, followed by **Market Guides** for emerging categories (no vendor positioning, only representative vendor descriptions), and finally **Magic Quadrants** once markets mature sufficiently. The 2024 pilot of the **Emerging Market Quadrant** addresses the annual-cycle limitation by refreshing every 2–4 weeks with lighter vendor burden, using Features and Future Potential as axes. MQs are typically updated annually, with the full production timeline spanning approximately six months.

## Deep dive 2: Forrester — transparency as competitive advantage

### Overview and scale

Forrester Research (NASDAQ: FORR), founded in 1983, is the second most influential analyst firm with **~$404M trailing revenue** (down ~8.7% YoY as of Q3 FY2025). The firm employs approximately 1,780 people including ~600 analysts, serves 61% of the Fortune 100, and surveys **700,000+ consumers, business leaders, and technology leaders** annually. Forrester publishes approximately 50–80 Wave reports per year across 225+ technology and services markets.

### Core methodology: the 18-week Wave process

The Forrester Wave follows a structured process lasting approximately **18 weeks**. During Weeks 1–2, the analyst defines scope, drafts inclusion criteria (e.g., $20M+ revenue, cloud-first offering), and invites qualifying vendors. Weeks 3–8 cover data gathering: vendors complete a detailed questionnaire over ~4 weeks, followed by executive strategy briefings and product demonstrations. As of 2024, live customer interviews replace the former survey-based reference approach. Weeks 9–14 involve scoring: analysts create scale explanations for each criterion, score vendors on a relative **0–5 scale**, apply weights, and share initial scores with vendors for limited feedback. Weeks 15–18 are finalization and publication, including a courtesy preview sent ~5 days before release.

**Major 2024 methodology changes** reshaped the Wave significantly. The framework now uses **three categories instead of four** (Leader, Strong Performer, Contender — the Challenger designation was eliminated). Market Presence was replaced by **Customer Feedback** as the third dimension, represented by halo markers instead of dot size. Non-participating vendors appear as gray dots. An interactive digital experience featuring a "Help me find a vendor" wizard was introduced.

### Scoring attributes in detail

The Wave evaluates vendors across **20–38+ criteria** organized into three categories. **Current Offering** (Y-axis) typically includes product capabilities specific to the category, ease of implementation, service and support quality, user experience, integration/interoperability, security and compliance, performance and scalability, analytics and reporting, and AI/ML capabilities. **Strategy** (X-axis) evaluates product vision and roadmap (features plus release dates over a 2-year horizon), market approach, innovation (including R&D budget and release cadence), execution history, partner ecosystem, pricing flexibility, and corporate strategy. The now-retired **Market Presence** dimension (replaced by Customer Feedback) evaluated company financials, installed base, average deal size, and market share.

### Scoring transparency: Forrester's key differentiator

Forrester is notably **more transparent than Gartner**: every criterion score for every vendor is published, all weights are disclosed, and a **downloadable macro-enabled Excel spreadsheet** allows buyers to adjust weights and create custom rankings. The scoring scale runs from 0 (not offered/won't disclose) through 3 (meets requirements) to 5 (exceeds requirements/best-in-class). Weights are determined by the analyst and research director based on market dynamics and buyer needs, but are not disclosed to vendors during the evaluation — only revealed post-publication. Current Offering and Strategy scores determine position on the grid; Market Presence (pre-2024) was represented only by dot size and didn't affect axis positioning.

### Vendor participation and the reprint economy

Participation is **free** — no fee to be included. However, reprint rights to distribute the report cost **$30,000–$45,000** per report, and Forrester membership subscriptions start at ~$75,000/year. Vendors cannot effectively opt out: Forrester reserves the right to evaluate products regardless of participation, using public information and independently sourced interviews. Vendors are limited to 5 factual-accuracy objections and cannot dispute criteria, scales, or weightings.

Criticisms mirror Gartner's but with distinct flavors: the **limited vendor inclusion** (9–18 vendors per Wave leaves out many legitimate players), **single analyst dependency** creating potential for individual bias, **reprint cost barriers** favoring well-funded vendors, and the **perception of pay-to-play** through Forrester Consulting's TEI studies commissioned by the same vendors being evaluated. The Wave updates every **2–3 years** per category, slower than Gartner's typical annual cycle.

## Deep dive 3: IDC — the data-driven third pillar

### Overview and scale

IDC (International Data Corporation), founded in 1964, is the "numbers house" of the Big Three — the industry's primary market-sizing and forecasting authority. Acquired by Blackstone in 2021 for **$1.3B enterprise value**, IDC employs **1,300+ analysts** across 110+ countries, surveys **300,000+ tech buyers annually**, and publishes approximately **700 Continuous Intelligence Services** and an estimated **100+ MarketScapes** per year. IDC has been named Analyst Firm of the Year by IIAR for three consecutive years and ranks #1 in business press mentions for over a decade.

### How the MarketScape differs

The IDC MarketScape is a **bubble chart** (not a quadrant grid) plotting vendors on **Capabilities** (Y-axis, measuring current "here and now" execution) versus **Strategies** (X-axis, measuring alignment with customer needs 3–5 years out), with **bubble size representing market share** from IDC's proprietary data — a third dimension unique among the Big Three. Vendors are classified as **Leaders**, **Major Players**, **Contenders**, or **Participants**, with a +/neutral/- symbol indicating year-over-year growth rate.

The Capabilities axis typically evaluates offering/product capability, delivery model, go-to-market execution, customer satisfaction, cost/pricing model, channel capability, financial viability, employee management, and revenue model. The Strategies axis assesses offering/product strategy, growth strategy, go-to-market strategy, innovation strategy, customer strategy, financial/funding strategy, marketing strategy, talent strategy, delivery model strategy, and alliance/partnership strategy. Weightings are **unique to each MarketScape study** — determined by user interviews, buyer surveys, and IDC expert input — and are not disclosed to vendors during the process.

IDC's key differentiator is its **data-driven foundation**: the proprietary market share data cannot be easily influenced by vendors, and the firm's deep market taxonomy lets it identify and define emerging niches before competitors. Category creation follows a progression from market data to MarketGlance to ProductScape to the full MarketScape. Reports are typically updated every **1–3 years** depending on market velocity, and IDC does not allow external usage of content from reports older than 18 months.

Criticisms of IDC focus on its **lower brand recognition** compared to Gartner (described as "the poor cousin at the analyst relations family gathering"), **lack of granular functional scoring** transparency compared to Forrester's downloadable spreadsheets, and a **bubble chart format** that can be harder to parse than Gartner's clean four-quadrant layout.

## Deep dive 4: G2 — the crowd-sourced disruptor

### Overview and scale

G2 (formerly G2 Crowd), founded in 2012 by former BigMachines executives including CEO Godard Abel, is the world's largest peer review marketplace for B2B software. The company achieved **unicorn status in 2021** at a $1.1B valuation, generated **$163M revenue in 2024** (up from $40M in 2019), and has collected **2.9M+ verified user reviews** across **180,000+ software products** in **2,100+ categories**. G2 attracts 60–100 million annual visitors and employs approximately 1,092 people. In a landmark move, G2 announced a formal agreement to **acquire Capterra, Software Advice, and GetApp from Gartner** in January 2026, pending regulatory approval.

### The Grid algorithm decoded

The G2 Grid places products along two axes: **Satisfaction** (Y-axis, based on review data) and **Market Presence** (X-axis, combining market data and review volume). Each component represents **50% of the total G2 Score**, normalized within each category. Products land in four quadrants: Leaders (high satisfaction, high market presence), High Performers (high satisfaction, lower market presence), Contenders (lower satisfaction, high market presence), and Niche (lower on both).

The Satisfaction score incorporates total number of reviews, review recency (full weight for 0–90 days, with exponential decay — a review loses ~30% of its value per year), likelihood to recommend scores, overall NPS, review quality and depth, feature-specific ratings, ease of use, meets requirements, quality of support, ease of admin, ease of setup, and ease of doing business with. Organic (non-incentivized) reviews receive algorithmic bonus weighting.

The Market Presence score draws from **15 metrics**, of which only 7 are publicly disclosed: number of reviews on G2, recent review volume, number of employees (from LinkedIn/Crunchbase), company revenue, online/web presence metrics, social media following, and employee growth velocity. A minimum of **10 reviews** is required for a product to appear on a Grid, and a category needs at least **6 products with 10+ reviews each** and **150+ total reviews** for Grid eligibility.

### Category creation and the G2 taxonomy

G2 adds approximately **300 new categories per year**, managed by a team of 50+ researchers. The process uses a "20 Feature Litmus Test" — each category gets a unique set of ~20 key features, and products must substantially match. A minimum of 3 competing products is required because "buyers of software, you at least want three choices." Vendors can request new categories by submitting evidence including a proposed name, eligibility criteria, and ~10 competitors.

### The business model and its tensions

Basic listing is free, but G2 Marketing Solutions generate the real revenue. Pricing ranges from $299/month for a Starter plan to $87,000+/year for full bundles including Buyer Intent data, review growth tools, and data licensing. Enterprise companies like Salesforce reportedly spend **over seven figures annually**. G2 states its scoring algorithm treats free and paid accounts identically — no scoring advantages for paid subscribers. However, criticisms include **incentivized review bias** ($10–$25 gift cards per review), **pay-to-play perception** from sponsored content resembling organic results, **Market Presence bias** heavily weighting company size (creating a David-vs-Goliath dynamic for startups), and **review rejection inconsistencies** from automated moderation systems.

Grid reports update **quarterly** (Winter, Spring, Summer, Fall), but the underlying scores change in **real-time** as new reviews arrive. The Winter 2026 reports alone produced **26,562 reports** across 1,300+ categories.

## Deep dive 5: TrustRadius — depth over volume

### Overview and methodology

TrustRadius, founded in 2012 in Austin, Texas, positions itself as "the most credible B2B technology decisioning platform." Acquired by HG Insights in June 2025, TrustRadius serves approximately **12 million annual B2B technology evaluators** with reviews averaging **400+ words** — significantly longer than competitors. The platform published 17,796 reviews in 2024, representing only **51.6% of submitted reviews** — a rejection rate of nearly 50% that reflects its rigorous verification process.

The proprietary **trScore** algorithm (0–10 scale) is a weighted average correcting for three factors: **recency** (more recent reviews weighted more heavily), **bias correction** (reviews from random/representative samples weighted more than vendor-invited "cherry-picked" reviews), and **depth** (in-depth reviews weighted more than single-click ratings). This bias correction is a distinctive feature — TrustRadius found that vendor-driven reviews can inflate simple averages by ~10%, and the trScore neutralizes this effect.

TrustRadius operates five recognition programs: **Customer Verified** (≥10 reviews from past 12 months, dynamic), **Top Rated Awards** (annual, requiring trScore ≥7.5 plus traffic thresholds), **Buyer's Choice Awards** (75% of reviewers must select the product for best capabilities, value, and relationship), **Trusted Seller Program** (verifying ethical review sourcing), and **TrustMaps** (scatter-plot charts measuring Product Fit versus Buyer Intent).

The key differentiator is that TrustRadius does **not sell paid placement or lead positioning** — rankings are entirely review-driven. Premium vendor pricing starts at ~$30,000/product/year. Weaknesses include smaller review volume than G2 or Capterra, English-only coverage, and expensive premium tiers for smaller vendors.

## Deep dive 6: Capterra — the SMB discovery engine

### Overview and the Gartner Digital Markets ecosystem

Capterra, founded in 1999 and acquired by Gartner in 2015 for $206.2M, operates as part of **Gartner Digital Markets** alongside GetApp and Software Advice. Together, these three brands list **100,000+ software solutions** across **900+ categories** with **2.5M+ verified reviews** and **100M+ annual visitors** across 70+ localized international sites. In January 2026, G2 announced a formal agreement to acquire all three brands, pending regulatory approval.

### Multi-brand, multi-methodology approach

Capterra/GDM operates six distinct evaluation frameworks across its three brands. The **Capterra Shortlist** plots products on Ratings (X-axis, 1–50 scale based on review scores normalized for recency and volume) versus Popularity (Y-axis, 1–50 scale combining search volume, SERP position, and review metrics). **GetApp Category Leaders** use a five-dimensional radar chart scoring Reviews, Integrations, Mobile, Functionality, and Security (each 0–20 points, 100-point maximum). **Software Advice FrontRunners** plot Usability versus Customer Satisfaction on a 1–5 scale. Annual **"Best of" Badges** recognize the top 25% of products scoring ≥4.5/5 in specific attributes.

Users rate products on a **5-star scale** across six dimensions: Overall Rating, Ease of Use, Customer Service, Features/Functionality, Value for Money, and Likelihood to Recommend (1–10 scale). The platform's primary revenue driver is **PPC (pay-per-click) advertising**: the default category page sort is "Sponsored," with vendors bidding from a $2/click minimum up to $20+/click in competitive categories. This means **paid placement dominates what buyers see first** — a fundamental tension with the platform's evaluation credibility, though the Shortlist, Category Leaders, and FrontRunners reports operate independently of advertising spend.

## Deep dive 7: Everest Group — the IT services evaluator

### Overview and the PEAK Matrix

Everest Group, founded in 1991 in Dallas, Texas, specializes in IT services, business process outsourcing, and engineering services evaluation. The firm employs an estimated **1,200–1,400 people** and operates from offices in six cities across four countries. Its flagship **PEAK Matrix® (Performance, Experiences, Ability, Knowledge)** evaluates providers on two dimensions: **Market Impact** (Y-axis, measuring revenue, client count, and growth) and **Vision & Capability** (X-axis, measuring service scope, innovation, delivery footprint, and buyer satisfaction). Providers are classified as **Leaders**, **Major Contenders**, or **Aspirants**, with top movers designated as **Star Performers**.

The end-to-end process takes approximately **5 months**: RFI distribution via online portal, data collection combining RFI responses with Everest's proprietary Transaction Intelligence database, provider briefings, client reference interviews, analysis and scoring, a 3–4 day pre-release preview (informational only), and publication. Even without vendor participation, Everest can assess providers using its TI database and public disclosures.

RFI participation is **free**, with revenue generated through licensing and distribution rights for PEAK Matrix positioning in vendor marketing. Everest produces four PEAK Matrix types: Services (evaluating service providers), Solutions (evaluating product-plus-service portfolios), Products (evaluating technology vendors), and Locations (evaluating delivery locations). Assessment criteria evolve to reflect current market realities. The primary criticism is that Everest's strength in IT/BPO services means **less coverage of pure SaaS/software products** compared to the Big Three.

## Deep dive 8: GigaOm — pure technology, no market share

### Overview and the Radar framework

GigaOm, originally founded as a tech blog by Om Malik in 2006, was relaunched as an analyst firm in 2015 after acquisition by Knowingly Corporation. With approximately **101 employees** and a network of **200+ independent analysts** (all practitioners with 20+ years of experience), GigaOm generates an estimated **$35M in revenue** covering 100+ technology categories across six domains: Cloud/Infrastructure, Data/Analytics/AI, DevOps, Network/Edge, People/Processes/Applications, and Security/Risk.

GigaOm's defining principle: **"Business considerations such as vendor market share, customer share, spend, recency or longevity in the market are not considered in our evaluations."** This makes it the purest technology-focused evaluator among major platforms.

### The three-report methodology

GigaOm's evaluation system uses three interconnected report types. The **Key Criteria Report** defines the evaluation framework, establishing Table Stakes (binary pass/fail inclusion criteria), Key Features (scored 0–5, highest weighting), Emerging Features (scored 0–5, lower weighting), and Business Criteria (scored 0–5, highest weighting alongside Key Features). The **Radar Report** applies this framework, producing a circular chart with concentric rings designating **Leaders** (inner), **Challengers** (middle), and **Entrants** (outer), with two axes creating four quadrants: Maturity versus Innovation (Y-axis) and Feature Play versus Platform Play (X-axis). Forward-looking arrows project vendor evolution over **12–18 months**. The **Sonar Report** serves as a "prequel to the Radar" for emerging technologies too immature for full evaluation.

The process takes approximately **17 weeks**: project planning, tailored questionnaire distribution, vendor briefings (within a 15 business day window), independent desk research, scoring, internal review (technical, peer, and quality), a 5 business day fact-check period, refinement, and publication with a 5-day courtesy preview. Participation is **free**, with revenue from reprint licensing and subscription services.

Key strengths include transparent methodology, a level playing field for smaller vendors, and practitioner-led analysis. Weaknesses include lower brand recognition than the Big Three, **no customer reference interviews** as a standard input, and narrower coverage focused primarily on infrastructure, cloud, and security technologies.

## Deep dive 9: CB Insights — algorithmic intelligence for emerging vendors

### Overview and the Mosaic Score

CB Insights, founded in 2008 in New York City (originally as "Chubbybrain"), is a market intelligence platform tracking **11M+ company profiles** across **1,600+ tech markets**. The company generated an estimated **$146M in 2024 revenue** from a premium platform priced at ~$30,000/year per seat. Its newsletter reaches **720,000+ subscribers**, and its market maps and rankings are cited by media 4,000+ times annually.

### The Mosaic scoring system

CB Insights' flagship metric, the **Mosaic Score** (0–1,000 scale), was developed with three NSF grants and is **4.7x more predictive than top-decile venture capitalists** at identifying future unicorns. The score comprises four weighted components: **Momentum (50%)** tracks company traction through hiring patterns, web traffic, product launches, media mentions, and partnership activity. **Money (40%)** evaluates financing history, burn rate, investor quality, and funding rounds. **Market (5%)** assesses the health of the company's industry. **Management (5%)** scores leadership team track record including previous exits, funding history, and network strength.

The **ESP Matrix** (Execution, Strength, Positioning) provides quadrant-based vendor ranking for specific markets, classifying companies as Leaders, Highfliers, Outperformers, or Challengers. CB Insights also produces annual curated lists (AI 100, Fintech 250), market maps, State Of reports (quarterly investment analysis), and Future Of reports (technology predictions).

Data collection is ML-driven, mining patents, earnings calls, news, websites, regulatory filings, and social media with automated consistency checks, outlier detection, and cross-source verification. Vendor participation is **explicitly free** with "none of the pay-to-play/shakedown tactics" — a positioning CB Insights actively markets. Mosaic Scores update **daily or in real time**.

The primary limitation is scope: Mosaic applies only to **private technology companies** with sufficient data, and the platform's depth weakens for SMEs and non-tech companies. Premium pricing (~$30K/year) limits accessibility. Employee count declined 32% YoY as of late 2023, suggesting business headwinds.

## Deep dive 10: HFS Research — the practitioner's analyst

### Overview and the Horizons framework

HFS Research, formalized in 2010 from Phil Fersht's "Horses for Sources" blog, is a lean (~57 employees) but influential analyst firm focused on enterprise transformation. With an estimated **$7.5M in revenue** and a community of **100,000+ professionals**, HFS punches above its weight through bold, opinionated research authored by former practitioners. Fersht, a 3x IIAR Analyst of the Year, coined industry terms including "OneOffice" (2016), "Generative Enterprise" (2023), and wrote the first analyst report on RPA (2012).

### The Horizons methodology

The **HFS Horizons** framework evaluates service providers across four equally weighted dimensions at **25% each**: Value Proposition ("The Why?"), Execution and Innovation Capabilities ("The What?"), Go-to-Market Strategy ("The How?"), and Market Impact ("The So What?"). Providers are placed into three tiers — **Horizon 1 (Disruptors)** focusing on functional transformation, **Horizon 2 (Enterprise Innovators)** adding cross-functional integration, and **Horizon 3 (Market Leaders)** requiring ecosystem-level innovation. Within each tier, providers are listed **alphabetically** — there is no internal ranking within Horizons.

HFS's data collection is demand-side heavy: **large-scale Pulse surveys** (up to 801 enterprises), **multi-stakeholder reference checks** (one study used 71 clients, 75 partners, and 130 employees), deep-dive interviews with 70+ enterprise leaders, and vendor briefings with supporting documentation. The firm also operates the **Hot Vendors/Hot Tech** quarterly designation for emerging players, with designations valid for one year. Previous Hot Vendors have subsequently received major funding or been acquired, validating the selection methodology.

Most HFS research is freely accessible except Horizons reports, which are paywalled. Vendor engagement programs (PoV licenses, webinars, sponsorships) create revenue but also potential perceived conflicts. Horizons reports update **annually** per topic area.

### Seven scoring dimensions that appear across nearly every platform

Despite vastly different methodologies, a remarkable convergence emerges in what these platforms evaluate. **Product/service capability** — the core functionality, feature set, and technical quality of the offering — is universal across all 10 platforms. **Innovation and vision** appears in 9 of 10, assessing R&D investment, product roadmap, and forward-looking strategy. **Customer satisfaction** is measured by all 10, though methods range from Gartner's structured reference calls to G2's crowd-sourced NPS-style ratings. **Market execution and go-to-market** — encompassing sales effectiveness, channel reach, and commercial model — is evaluated by all analyst-led platforms and approximated by peer platforms through review volume and market presence metrics.

**Ease of use and user experience** appears explicitly in 7 of 10 platforms, most prominently in peer review platforms where it's a primary scoring dimension. **Financial health and viability** is evaluated by 8 of 10, though GigaOm deliberately excludes it. **Support and customer success** is scored by all 10, reflecting buyers' concern about post-purchase experience.

### The fundamental methodological divide

These 10 platforms represent three fundamentally different epistemological approaches to vendor evaluation, each with distinct strengths and blindspots.

**Analyst-led evaluation** (Gartner, Forrester, IDC, Everest Group, HFS Research) relies on expert judgment informed by structured data collection. Analysts bring deep domain knowledge and can assess strategic vision, but introduce individual bias and create potential conflicts through the simultaneous advisory-and-evaluation business model. These firms excel at evaluating complex enterprise purchases where buyer needs are nuanced, but their annual cycles lag fast-moving markets and their inclusion criteria systematically exclude smaller innovators.

**Peer-driven evaluation** (G2, TrustRadius, Capterra) aggregates user experience data to generate scores algorithmically. This approach captures real-world product performance at scale and updates continuously, but is vulnerable to review manipulation, incentivized-review bias, and a structural advantage for larger vendors who can mobilize more reviewers. Peer platforms excel at evaluating widely-adopted products with large user bases but struggle with niche, enterprise, or emerging categories where review volume is insufficient.

**Data-driven evaluation** (CB Insights, GigaOm) uses algorithms, ML, or structured technical analysis to minimize human bias. CB Insights' Mosaic Score is demonstrably predictive (4.7x more accurate than top VCs for unicorn prediction), and GigaOm's exclusion of market share data creates the most level competitive field for smaller vendors. However, purely data-driven approaches can miss qualitative factors like product vision or customer relationship quality that experienced analysts detect.

### What a new AI-era evaluation methodology should learn

For anyone building a new evaluation methodology — particularly for AI visibility — these 10 platforms collectively point toward several design principles.

**Transparency drives credibility**. Forrester's downloadable scoring spreadsheets and GigaOm's published criteria represent the direction of trust. Gartner's scoring opacity is its most persistent vulnerability, and newer entrants should default to maximum transparency in how scores are calculated and weighted.

**Market share should be separable from capability**. The most contentious methodological choice across all platforms is how to handle market share. G2 weights it at 50% of its total score; GigaOm excludes it entirely. Forrester removed it in 2024. The optimal approach may be IDC's: include market share as a visible third dimension (bubble size) but separate it from capability scoring, letting buyers decide how much weight to assign to install base versus innovation.

**Update frequency must match market velocity**. Annual cycles were designed for enterprise software markets that evolved slowly. In AI, where competitive positions shift weekly, Gartner's eMQ pilot (refreshing every 2–4 weeks) and G2's real-time scoring represent the new standard. Any new methodology should be designed for continuous or at minimum quarterly updates.

**Multi-source data beats single-source data**. No single data source — analyst opinion, user reviews, or algorithmic signals — captures the full picture. The most credible next-generation methodology would integrate verified user reviews (TrustRadius model), technical product evaluation (GigaOm model), financial and momentum signals (CB Insights model), and structured buyer interviews (HFS model) into a composite framework. The challenge is weighting these sources appropriately and transparently.

**Emerging categories need purpose-built frameworks**. Every platform struggles with new markets. The most effective approaches — GigaOm's Sonar, Gartner's Market Guide/eMQ progression, G2's 300-new-categories-per-year pace, CB Insights' dynamic market detection — share a common thread: they provide value before the market is mature enough for definitive rankings, using lighter-weight formats that acknowledge uncertainty. A new methodology should build in explicit maturity indicators that shift evaluation criteria as a category evolves from nascent to mainstream.

## The unspoken truth about vendor evaluation

Across all 10 platforms, a fundamental tension persists: **the companies being evaluated are also the primary revenue source**. Gartner charges vendors for advisory subscriptions and report reprints. Forrester sells TEI studies to the vendors it evaluates in Waves. G2 generates $163M from vendor marketing solutions. Even "free to participate" platforms monetize through licensing, reprints, advertising, or intent data that flows back to vendors. This doesn't invalidate the evaluations — rigorous methodology and editorial firewalls do exist — but any buyer relying on these frameworks should understand that every platform has a financial relationship with the vendors it rates.

The most valuable takeaway from studying all 10 methodologies is that **no single evaluation captures the full truth** about a vendor. A Gartner Leader may score as a G2 Contender if its enterprise users don't leave reviews. A GigaOm Leader may be an Everest Aspirant if it lacks services revenue. A CB Insights top Mosaic score tells you a startup is healthy and well-funded — not that its product works well in production. The sophisticated buyer — and the sophisticated methodology builder — synthesizes across these perspectives rather than treating any single one as authoritative.
