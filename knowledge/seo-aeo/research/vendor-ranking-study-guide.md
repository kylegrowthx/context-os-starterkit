## 1. Why This Matters

Every B2B technology purchase worth six figures or more starts with a ranking. Gartner says you're a Leader, Forrester says you're a Strong Performer, G2 says you're #3 in your category. These labels move billions in enterprise software spend every year.

But here's the thing nobody talks about: each of these platforms uses a completely different methodology. Different axes. Different weights. Different data sources. A vendor can be a Gartner Leader and a Forrester Contender at the same time, and both can be right, because they're measuring different things.

Understanding how these rankings actually work is critical for three reasons:

- **If you're a buyer**, you need to know what each ranking actually tells you (and what it doesn't).
- **If you're a vendor**, you need to know which levers you can pull to improve your positioning across platforms.
- **If you're building a ranking system** (like CheckThat), you need to understand what works, what's broken, and where the opportunity is.

This guide breaks down the 10 most influential vendor ranking platforms in B2B technology. For each one, we cover the methodology, the scoring criteria, the evaluation process, and the strategic implications. No fluff, no filler, just how it actually works.

## 2. The Landscape at a Glance

Vendor ranking platforms fall into three categories, each with a fundamentally different approach to evaluating technology providers.

### Analyst Firms: Expert Opinion at Scale

These firms employ hundreds of analysts who spend their careers studying specific markets. Their rankings blend analyst judgment with vendor briefings, customer references, and market data. The output carries enormous weight because enterprise buyers trust analyst independence, even though vendors pay for inclusion in some cases.

- **Gartner Magic Quadrant:** The most influential. Two axes (Ability to Execute + Completeness of Vision), four quadrants, annual cycle, 15 weighted criteria.
- **Forrester Wave:** Three dimensions (Current Offering + Strategy + Customer Feedback), transparent scoring on a 0-5 scale, 18-week evaluation.
- **IDC MarketScape:** Two axes (Capabilities + Strategies), bubble size shows market share, strong emphasis on quantitative rigor and market sizing.
- **Everest Group PEAK Matrix:** Two axes (Market Impact + Vision & Capability), three categories (Leader, Major Contender, Aspirant), plus Star Performers for year-over-year improvement.
- **ISG Provider Lens:** Two axes (Portfolio Attractiveness + Competitive Strength), four quadrants, combines empirical research with ISG advisory team experience.

### Peer Review Platforms: Democratic Voice of Users

These platforms aggregate real user reviews to rank vendors. They're algorithm-driven and update more frequently than analyst reports. Their credibility comes from volume and verification, not expert opinion.

- **G2 Grid:** Dominant B2B review platform. G2 Score = (Satisfaction + Market Presence) / 2. Quarterly updates, 3M+ monthly visitors, 70%+ traffic from Google.
- **TrustRadius:** Weighted average algorithm (trScore) on 0-10 scale. Prioritizes recent reviews and review quality over volume. 400+ word average review length.
- **Capterra Shortlist:** Two dimensions (Ratings + Popularity), scores scaled 1-50 each. Owned by Gartner Digital Markets. 2.5M+ verified reviews.
- **Gartner Peer Insights Voice of the Customer:** Two axes (Overall Experience + User Interest & Adoption), four quadrants. Reviews feed into Magic Quadrant research.

### Vendor Research Platforms: Algorithmic Intelligence

These platforms use algorithms and proprietary data to score and rank companies, primarily for investors and corporate strategy teams rather than software buyers.

- **CB Insights Mosaic:** Four-pillar scoring model (Momentum 50%, Financial Strength 40%, Industry Health 5%, Management 5%) on a 0-1000 scale. NSF-backed, 4.7x more predictive than top VCs for identifying future unicorns.

### 3.1 Gartner Magic Quadrant

**The bottom line:** The most influential vendor evaluation in enterprise tech. A Leader position can accelerate an entire sales pipeline. Exclusion can kill it.

#### How It Works

Gartner plots vendors on a 2D matrix. The Y-axis measures **Ability to Execute** (can you deliver today?) and the X-axis measures **Completeness of Vision** (are you building for tomorrow?). This creates four quadrants: Leaders (top-right), Challengers (top-left), Visionaries (bottom-right), and Niche Players (bottom-left).

Each axis rolls up multiple weighted criteria.

**Ability to Execute includes 8 criteria:**

- Product/Service: core offerings, capabilities, quality, feature sets (native or OEM)
- Overall Viability: financial health, business unit success, likelihood of continued investment
- Sales Execution/Pricing: sales capabilities, pricing models, negotiation
- Market Responsiveness: ability to respond to market changes, customer needs
- Marketing Execution: marketing programs, brand awareness, demand generation
- Customer Experience: quality of customer support, services, programs
- Operations: organizational execution, delivery capabilities
- Additional criteria vary by market

**Completeness of Vision includes 7 criteria:**

- Market Understanding: ability to understand buyer needs and translate into products
- Marketing Strategy: clear, differentiated messaging and go-to-market approach
- Sales Strategy: sales approach aligned with market needs
- Offering (Product) Strategy: product development roadmap and innovation
- Business Model: soundness of business model and revenue approach
- Vertical/Industry Strategy: strategy for specific industries or verticals
- Innovation: investment in R&D, new technologies, market leadership
- Geographic Strategy: international presence and expansion plans

Criteria are weighted as high, medium, low, or no rating depending on the specific market being evaluated. The exact weightings are proprietary and change by category, which is one of the persistent criticisms of the methodology.

#### The Process

The evaluation cycle runs approximately 12 months. Gartner initiates the process by defining market boundaries and inclusion criteria (revenue thresholds, customer base, geographic presence, product maturity). Vendors cannot opt in or out. Those meeting inclusion criteria receive a detailed RFI questionnaire. Gartner also collects 20+ customer reference surveys per vendor, conducts vendor briefings and strategy sessions, then analysts score against weighted criteria.

Vendors get a limited feedback window to review scores, then a 5-day courtesy preview before publication. No new information is accepted during preview.

#### What Each Quadrant Means

- **Leaders:** High execution + high vision. Established market share, credibility, innovation. Drive market acceptance of new technologies. Being here is the gold standard.
- **Challengers:** High execution + lower vision. Strong products and market position but lack the forward-thinking strategy of Leaders. Often large incumbents. Serious threat to Leaders.
- **Visionaries:** High vision + lower execution. Innovative products addressing important problems, but haven't captured market share or sustained profitability. Often acquisition targets.
- **Niche Players:** Lower scores on both axes. May focus on specific segments or verticals, be newer entrants, or struggle with relevance. Not inherently bad; can be excellent for specific use cases.

**Typical progression:** Niche Player to Visionary to Challenger to Leader (the "backwards Z" pattern). Entering directly as a Leader is extremely rare.

#### Companion Research

The Magic Quadrant doesn't exist in isolation:

- **Critical Capabilities:** Deeper product-level analysis by use case, scoring vendors 0-5 on key differentiators.
- **Market Guides:** For emerging or very mature markets where a Magic Quadrant isn't appropriate.
- **Hype Cycles:** Technology maturity and adoption predictions.
- **Cool Vendor Reports:** Emerging players in categories.

#### Key Criticisms

- Methodology and component scores are not transparent. You see the quadrant position but not the underlying math.
- Potential conflict of interest: vendors pay Gartner for various services, though Gartner maintains editorial independence.
- Simplicity can be reductive: complex vendor capabilities compressed into a single dot on a 2x2 grid.
- Open source vendors have historically been underrepresented.
- ZL Technologies sued Gartner in 2009 over its MQ position; Gartner defended by claiming First Amendment protection as "pure opinion."

### 3.2 Forrester Wave

**The bottom line:** More transparent than Gartner, more customer-centric, and now built around three dimensions instead of two. The transparent scorecard is its biggest differentiator.

#### How It Works

Forrester evaluates vendors across three primary dimensions:

- **X-axis: Current Offering** — Product features, usability, integration, security, scalability, enterprise readiness.
- **Y-axis: Strategy** — Product roadmap, innovation commitment, market approach, ecosystem, business model sustainability.
- **Dot size: Customer Feedback** — Real customer satisfaction and outcomes. This replaced Market Presence in 2024, reflecting Forrester's "customer obsessed" philosophy. Top performers receive "Customer Obsessed" halos on the graphic.

Scoring uses a **0-5 scale with detailed explanations** for each criterion, published transparently. This is the single biggest difference from Gartner: you can see exactly why a vendor got its score.

#### The Process

The 18-week cycle:

1. **Preliminary Research (Weeks 1-2):** Develop vendor list, define inclusion criteria.
2. **Vendor Questionnaire (4 weeks to complete):** Detailed product capabilities, strategy, company info.
3. **Scenario-Based Demos (2 weeks after questionnaire):** Vendors demonstrate against Forrester-defined use cases, not their own pitch decks.
4. **Customer Reference Interviews:** Forrester contacts references directly.
5. **Scoring (6-7 weeks after questionnaire):** Analyst creates scale explanations, scores each criterion, applies weightings.
6. **Vendor Review:** Vendors receive scorecards for factual corrections.
7. **Courtesy Preview:** 5 days before publication (no new information accepted).

#### Vendor Categories (Updated July 2024)

- **Leaders:** High scores in Current Offering and Strategy. Comprehensive, mature solutions.
- **Strong Performers:** Solid scores across dimensions. Competitive offerings.
- **Contenders:** Lower scores but viable options for specific use cases.

The Challenger and Visionary categories were **removed in 2024** to streamline focus on top players.

#### Key Differentiators from Gartner

- **Three dimensions vs. two:** adds customer feedback as an independent axis.
- **Transparent scoring:** published criteria and weightings, unlike Gartner's proprietary approach.
- **Scenario-based demos:** vendors demonstrate against Forrester-defined use cases, not marketing presentations.
- **Business-centric:** emphasizes outcomes and customer value over pure technology.
- **Freeze date policy:** major changes during evaluation (acquisitions, product launches) are not incorporated, ensuring fair point-in-time comparison.

**Companion research:** Forrester Landscape Reports outline market categories, Planning Guides provide implementation guidance, and Total Economic Impact (TEI) studies calculate ROI and cost-benefit.

### 3.3 IDC MarketScape

**The bottom line:** The most data-driven of the analyst evaluations. IDC's heritage as the industry's leading market sizing and forecast provider gives its MarketScape evaluations a quantitative rigor that Gartner and Forrester lack.

#### How It Works

The IDC MarketScape plots vendors on two axes:

- **X-axis: Capabilities** — Product quality, go-to-market execution, business execution in the short term.
- **Y-axis: Strategies** — Alignment of vendor strategies with customer requirements over a 3-5 year timeframe.
- **Bubble size:** Vendor market share.
- **Plus/Neutral/Minus symbol:** Year-over-year growth rate relative to the market.

Vendors are categorized into four segments: **Leaders, Major Players, Contenders, and Participants.** The dividing lines between segments can shift based on how the two axes are weighted for a specific market.

#### The Process

IDC analysts tailor the standard evaluation criteria through structured discussions, surveys, and interviews with market leaders, participants, and end users. Market weightings are based on user interviews, buyer surveys, and IDC expert input. Vendor scores are based on detailed vendor surveys and interviews, publicly available information, and end-user experiences. IDC has over 1,300 analysts worldwide contributing local expertise across 110+ countries.

#### Key Differentiators

- **Market share data:** IDC is the premier source for market sizing, so bubble size carries real meaning.
- **Growth rate indicators:** the plus/minus symbol shows momentum, not just current position.
- **Quantitative rigor:** scoring methodology blends qualitative and quantitative criteria with documented methodology.
- **Customer validation:** buyer perception is a component of the evaluation, not just analyst opinion.
- **Future-focused strategy axis:** explicitly evaluates 3-5 year strategic alignment with customer needs.

### 3.4 Everest Group PEAK Matrix

**The bottom line:** The go-to framework for evaluating IT services and outsourcing providers. PEAK stands for Performance, Experiences, Ability, and Knowledge. Its Star Performer designation for year-over-year improvement is unique in the industry.

#### How It Works

Each provider is assessed on two dimensions:

- **Y-axis: Market Impact** — Revenue, number of clients, year-over-year growth.
- **X-axis: Vision & Capability** — Scale of operations, scope, technology and innovation, delivery footprint, and buyer satisfaction.

Providers are categorized as **Leaders, Major Contenders, or Aspirants.**

The **Star Performer** designation is awarded to providers showing the strongest upward movement between successive evaluations. This recognizes momentum rather than absolute position, meaning an Aspirant moving rapidly can earn Star Performer status even though it isn't a Leader.

#### The Process

Evaluations are based on annual RFI processes, direct interactions with service providers, client reference checks (a step some competitors exclude), and ongoing market analysis. Everest Group uses its proprietary Transaction Intelligence database, public disclosures, and enterprise interactions. The assessment criteria are realigned regularly to reflect current market reality and future enterprise expectations.

Not all providers make the PEAK Matrix. Being represented is itself a favorable recognition, as the framework highlights only best-in-class providers.

#### Key Differentiators

- **Star Performers:** unique year-over-year momentum designation that no other framework offers.
- **Customer references included:** direct client interviews are a required step.
- **Services-focused:** particularly strong in IT services, BPO, engineering services, and outsourcing.
- **Four assessment types:** Service PEAK Matrix, Solutions PEAK Matrix, Products PEAK Matrix, and Pricing analytics.
- **Broad coverage:** 50+ studies producing 800+ unique reports and 15,600+ distinct quadrants.

### 3.5 ISG Provider Lens

**The bottom line:** The only evaluation that combines empirical data-driven research with real-world advisory experience from ISG's global consulting team. Particularly strong for sourcing and managed services decisions.

#### How It Works

ISG evaluates providers using an evaluation matrix with two axes:

- **X-axis: Portfolio Attractiveness** — Quality and breadth of service offerings.
- **Y-axis: Competitive Strength** — Market presence and execution capability.

This produces four quadrants:

- **Leaders:** Strong market presence, comprehensive offerings, competitive stability.
- **Product Challengers:** Strong portfolio but limited competitive strength.
- **Market Challengers:** Competitive strength but weaker portfolio.
- **Contenders:** Need improvement on both dimensions.

**Rising Stars** are providers ISG identifies as having strong potential to move into the Leader quadrant based on promising portfolios, market experience, roadmaps, and focus on key market trends.

#### Key Differentiators

- **Advisory integration:** ISG advisors use Provider Lens data directly in client sourcing engagements, creating a direct link between research and purchasing decisions.
- **Regional granularity:** evaluations span specific geographies (not just global), with 50+ studies creating close to 800 unique reports.
- **Rising Star designation:** identifies providers with strong upward trajectory.
- **Maximum 25 providers per quadrant**, with exceptions for particularly competitive markets.
- **Voice of Customer data:** integrates survey data and advisory awareness alongside empirical research.

### 3.6 G2 Grid

**The bottom line:** The dominant B2B software review platform. With 3M+ monthly visitors, 1M+ reviews, and 70%+ of traffic from Google, G2 has become the de facto standard for user-driven software evaluation. Its methodology is more transparent than Gartner's but still contains proprietary elements.

#### How It Works

The G2 Score formula is simple:

> **G2 Score = (Satisfaction Score + Market Presence Score) / 2**

Each component makes up 50% of the total.

**Satisfaction Score (Y-axis, 50%)**

This aggregates user review data across multiple dimensions: likelihood to recommend (1-10 scale), user satisfaction ratings, admin satisfaction, feature ratings, quality of support, ease of use, ease of setup, ease of administration, ease of doing business with, meets requirements, and overall NPS.

Review quality factors include: statistical significance (minimum 10 reviews for Grid inclusion), review quality and thoroughness (longer reviews weighted more heavily), review age (Grid Decay means older reviews lose influence), and verified users (business email required, personal emails ineligible).

**Market Presence Score (X-axis, 50%)**

This combines 15 metrics, 7 known and 8 proprietary. The known metrics include: number of employees (from LinkedIn, Crunchbase), number of reviews, SEO traffic (Moz, SEMrush), social followers, employee growth velocity, company age, and Glassdoor ratings. Product-specific metrics are weighted more heavily than general company metrics.

**Critical insight:** Employee count is a dominant factor. Analysis shows spots 11-15 had only 10% fewer reviews than spots 5-10, but 50% fewer employees.

#### Quadrants and Reports

The four quadrants: **Leaders** (high satisfaction + high market presence), **High Performers** (high satisfaction + low market presence, often innovative challengers), **Contenders** (low satisfaction + high market presence, often legacy vendors), and **Niche** (lower on both axes, specialized or newer entrants). Scores are normalized within each category.

Beyond the standard Grid, G2 publishes:

- **Momentum Grids:** Growth trajectory over past year based on satisfaction changes, employee growth, digital presence growth.
- **Index Reports:** Focused on Implementation, Usability, Results, or Relationship with unique algorithms per index.
- **Best Software Awards (annual):** Top 100 Companies, Top 100 Products, Best by Function.

**Market segment breakdowns:** Small Business (<50 employees), Mid-Market (51-1,000), Enterprise (1,000+).

#### Key Considerations

- **Review incentives are allowed:** vendors can run paid campaigns with gift cards, though incentives must be for the act of reviewing, not positive sentiment.
- **Quarterly updates** (January, April, July, October) vs. annual analyst reports.
- **Free for buyers** (analyst reports require expensive subscriptions).
- **Larger vendors are structurally advantaged** through employee count weighting.
- **Ranking tie-breaker:** furthest right (Market Presence) typically wins over highest up (Satisfaction).

### 3.7 TrustRadius

**The bottom line:** Positions itself as the most credible B2B review platform by prioritizing review depth and verification over volume. Reviews average 400+ words, every reviewer is verified, and the trScore algorithm is designed to prevent vendor manipulation.

#### How It Works

The **trScore** is a proprietary algorithm that calculates a product score on a **0-10 scale** based on a weighted average of reviews and ratings, rather than a simple average. This was a deliberate design choice: simple averages are easily gamed by vendors who invite only known promoters, and they don't reflect current product reality when a product has many old reviews.

The algorithm places more weight on newer reviews (recent sentiment matters more than historical scores), prioritizes review quality and depth, factors in reviewer verification status, and incorporates sentiment analysis and engagement metrics. Vendors with trScores of 80% or above see a 12% higher conversion rate from profile visitors to leads.

#### Category Rankings

Products within categories are sorted by a proprietary algorithm that considers four factors: trScore and Top Rated status (product quality), profile completeness (including pricing, demos, recent reviews), Top Rated award status, and optimization for buyer engagement. This means vendors can only improve rankings by providing buyers with better information, not by paying.

#### Awards and Recognition

- **Top Rated Awards (annual):** Based entirely on customer feedback using trScore plus product use sentiment. 442 products won across 562 categories in 2025.
- **Buyer's Choice Awards:** Products where 75%+ of reviewers select them for best capabilities, best value, and best relationship.
- **Customer Verified Badge:** Products with 10+ reviews from the past 12 months.
- **Trusted Seller Program:** Verifies vendors following ethical review sourcing and providing accurate product information.

#### Key Differentiators

- **Review depth:** 400+ word average review length, dramatically higher than G2 or Capterra.
- **100% verified reviewers:** requires work email or LinkedIn authentication.
- **No pay-to-play:** rankings are algorithm-driven and independent of vendor relationship.
- **TrustMaps:** visual plots across Product Fit and Buyer Intent axes (requires 5+ verified reviews with 4.0+ average).

### 3.8 Capterra Shortlist

**The bottom line:** Owned by Gartner Digital Markets (alongside GetApp and Software Advice), Capterra provides the most accessible entry point to B2B software discovery. The Shortlist methodology is straightforward, transparent, and review-driven.

#### How It Works

Capterra Shortlist scores products on two dimensions:

- **Ratings (X-axis):** Based on overall average user ratings from the past 24 months.
- **Popularity (Y-axis):** Based on search volume, web presence, and other market signals.

Each score is weighted and scaled to a value between **1-50**. Products are placed on a grid or ranked in a stack-ranked list, completely independent of vendor influence.

#### Inclusion Criteria

- Minimum 20 unique product reviews published on Capterra within 24 months.
- Must demonstrate U.S. market presence (as of January 2025, based on U.S.-based reviews and publicly available information).
- Must be relevant to software buyers across industries (no niche-only solutions).
- Must achieve minimum normalized overall rating after recency weighting.
- Must achieve minimum popularity score.
- Each Shortlist includes 10-25 products depending on qualifying products in the category.

#### Key Differentiators

- **2.5M+ verified reviews** with human moderators checking reviewer identity and using AI to detect plagiarism and generative AI.
- **Monetization via sponsored profiles** (link-out icons to vendor websites), not ranking manipulation.
- **Capterra lists all providers**, not just paying customers.
- **Proprietary data science algorithms** identify trends across the full review corpus.

### 3.9 Gartner Peer Insights Voice of the Customer

**The bottom line:** Gartner's answer to G2 and TrustRadius, but with a crucial difference: Peer Insights reviews feed directly into Gartner's Magic Quadrant research. This makes it both a standalone review platform and a pipeline into the most influential analyst framework in the industry.

#### How It Works

The Voice of the Customer report categorizes vendors into four quadrants along two axes:

- **Y-axis: Overall Experience** — Product capabilities, support/delivery quality, general satisfaction.
- **X-axis: User Interest and Adoption** — Three equally-weighted factors:
  1. **Review and consideration score:** volume of eligible reviews plus how often a vendor is mentioned as a competitor considered.
  2. **User willingness to recommend:** NPS-style metric.
  3. **Review market coverage:** diversity across industries, company sizes, and regions.

Reviews from the first 6 months of the 18-month evaluation period receive **50% weight**; reviews from the most recent 12 months receive **full weight**. This ensures current sentiment is prioritized.

#### Inclusion and Quality Criteria

- Vendors need a minimum number of eligible reviews (typically 20+, with 15+ ratings for Capabilities and Support/Delivery sub-ratings).
- Maximum 25 vendors per Voice of the Customer report. If more qualify, the 25 with the highest eligible review count are included.
- Reviews must demonstrate variance in ratings (uniform perfect scores are flagged), logical consistency between overall and sub-ratings, and experience with the product.
- Vendor employees cannot review their own products, competitor products, or products in any market where their company is active.
- Supported in 10 languages as of late 2025.

#### Customers' Choice Designation

A maximum of **7 vendors per market** can be named Customers' Choice. To qualify, a vendor must have its overall rating at or above the market mean, meet review coverage requirements (minimum review volume plus at least 20% of reviews or 25 reviews from outside the primary industry, region, and company size), and if more than 7 qualify, the **PARC (Product Adoption Review Coverage) score** is the tiebreaker, with a maximum PARC of 12 points.

#### Connection to Magic Quadrant

Gartner has stated that Peer Insights will eventually replace vendor reference surveys as a data input to analyst research. Currently, reviews serve as one source of customer information considered alongside analyst research, vendor briefings, and other inputs. Individual quotes are not used in published research, but aggregate sentiment analysis informs analyst evaluation.

### 3.10 CB Insights Mosaic Score

**The bottom line:** Not a product evaluation framework. The Mosaic Score is a predictive health score for private tech companies, essentially a FICO score for startups. It's designed to tell investors and corporate strategy teams which companies are most likely to succeed, not which products are best.

#### How It Works

The Mosaic Score is an algorithmically-driven index from **0-1000** calculated across four forward-looking dimensions, each scored independently on the same 0-1000 scale:

- **Momentum (50% weight):** Measures company traction using signals from social media, news sentiment, mobile and web traffic, and more. This is the dominant factor.
- **Money / Financial Strength (40% weight):** Assesses financial viability based on financing history, burn rate, investor quality, and related signals.
- **Market / Industry Health (5% weight):** Quantifies the health of the industry the company operates in, based on funding activity, deal volume, industry sentiment, and exit activity.
- **Management (5% weight):** Data-driven assessment of founding and management team quality, including prior exits, funding rounds led, industry experience, education, and professional network. Scored out of 1000 independently.

#### Predictive Power

In a retrospective study of 150,000+ private companies, companies in the top 10% of Mosaic scores were **8.06x more likely to become unicorns** than the general population. Non-unicorns in the top 100 Mosaic bracket were 155x more likely to become unicorns within two years. The score was 4.7x more predictive than top-decile VC investments as a signal of future success.

#### Additional Frameworks

Beyond the Mosaic Score, CB Insights offers the **ESP (Execution, Strength, and Positioning) ranking**, which uses a quadrant methodology similar to analyst firms. This divides companies into Leaders, High Potentials, Contenders, and Emerging Players within specific market categories.

#### Key Differentiators

- **NSF-backed:** developed with three National Science Foundation grants, lending academic credibility.
- **Private company focus:** scores only private tech companies with sufficient public information, filling a gap that analyst firms don't cover.
- **Historical tracking:** scores are calculated dynamically and tracked over time, enabling trend analysis.
- **Management Mosaic:** the only algorithmic assessment of founding team quality, reducing the subjectivity of traditional team evaluation.

## 4. Cross-Platform Comparison

| Platform | Type | Primary Axes | Categories | Update Cycle | Transparency | Data Source | Access |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Gartner MQ** | Analyst | Execute + Vision | 4 quadrants | Annual | Low | Analyst + Vendor + Customer | Paid |
| **Forrester Wave** | Analyst | Offering + Strategy + Customer | 3 categories | Varies | High | Analyst + Demos + Customer | Paid |
| **IDC MarketScape** | Analyst | Capabilities + Strategies | 4 segments | Annual | Medium | Analyst + Buyer + Vendor | Paid |
| **Everest PEAK** | Analyst | Impact + Vision | 3 categories | Annual | Medium | RFI + Client refs + Market | Paid |
| **ISG Provider Lens** | Analyst | Portfolio + Competitive | 4 quadrants | Annual | Medium | Research + Advisory data | Paid |
| **G2 Grid** | Review | Satisfaction + Market | 4 quadrants | Quarterly | Medium | User reviews + Public data | Free |
| **TrustRadius** | Review | trScore (weighted avg) | Ranked list | Continuous | Medium | Verified reviews | Free |
| **Capterra** | Review | Ratings + Popularity | Grid + List | Periodic | High | Verified reviews + Public | Free |
| **Gartner PI** | Review | Experience + Adoption | 4 quadrants | 18 months | High | Verified enterprise reviews | Free |
| **CB Insights** | Algorithm | 4M scoring model | Ranked 0-1000 | Dynamic | Medium | Proprietary data + AI | Paid |

### Key Patterns Across Platforms

**1. The Two-Axis Problem.** Every platform reduces vendor evaluation to two primary dimensions. The labels change (Execute vs Vision, Capabilities vs Strategies, Satisfaction vs Market Presence) but the structure is the same: current state on one axis, future potential or adoption on the other. This creates a fundamental tension: nuanced vendor capabilities get compressed into a single dot on a 2D plane.

**2. The Transparency Spectrum.** Forrester, Capterra, and Gartner Peer Insights publish detailed scoring criteria and weights. Gartner Magic Quadrant keeps them proprietary. G2 reveals some Market Presence factors but keeps others hidden. CB Insights explains the four pillars but not the exact signals. The less transparent the methodology, the more vendors complain about unfairness, and the more mystique the framework carries.

**3. The Volume-Quality Tradeoff.** G2 and Capterra optimize for review volume (which drives SEO traffic and market coverage). TrustRadius optimizes for review depth (400+ word averages). Gartner Peer Insights optimizes for review diversity (coverage across industries, company sizes, regions). Each approach creates different blind spots.

**4. The Incumbency Advantage.** Every framework structurally favors established vendors. Analyst reports require minimum revenue thresholds. G2 weights employee count heavily. TrustRadius needs review volume over time. CB Insights weights financial history. New entrants face a systematic cold-start problem across all platforms.

**5. Reviews Are Becoming Table Stakes.** The line between "analyst firm" and "review platform" is blurring. Gartner now operates Peer Insights alongside the Magic Quadrant. Forrester replaced Market Presence with Customer Feedback. Every analyst firm increasingly incorporates user sentiment. The question isn't whether user reviews matter; it's how much weight they carry.

## 5. What This Means for CheckThat

These 10 platforms collectively shape how B2B technology buyers discover, evaluate, and select vendors. Each one has carved out its niche, but they all share a common limitation: they measure how the market sees a vendor today. None of them measure how AI sees a vendor today.

That's the gap CheckThat fills.

### The Structural Opportunity

**Every platform above is backward-looking.** Analyst reports evaluate what vendors did last year. Review platforms aggregate what users experienced in the past. CB Insights scores historical momentum. None of them answer the question that's becoming most important: when a buyer asks AI for a recommendation, does your brand show up?

AI engines are creating a new ranking layer that sits on top of all existing rankings. When ChatGPT recommends an expense management tool, it's synthesizing signals from G2, Gartner, vendor websites, Reddit, and dozens of other sources into a single answer. That synthesis is the new ranking. And nobody is systematically tracking it.

### Design Principles from the Research

Several patterns from these platforms should inform how CheckThat builds its own ranking methodology:

- **Transparency wins trust.** Forrester's published scorecard is its biggest competitive advantage over Gartner. CheckThat should publish exactly how AI visibility scores are calculated, what signals feed them, and how weights are determined.

- **Historical data creates moats.** Every platform above is defensible because of accumulated data that can't be replicated. CheckThat's historical AI response tracking creates the same dynamic: once you've tracked how AI answers a question for six months, no competitor can backfill that history.

- **Dual-axis frameworks are universal.** Every successful evaluation framework uses two primary dimensions. For CheckThat, the natural axes are Visibility (how often does AI mention you?) and Perception (how does AI describe you when it does?).

- **Category-level context matters more than brand-level data.** The most useful rankings (Gartner MQ, Forrester Wave, G2 Grid) all evaluate vendors within defined market categories. CheckThat's category-first approach to AI visibility mirrors this pattern.

- **User-generated signals are replacing expert opinion.** The trajectory across all platforms is toward more user input, less pure analyst judgment. CheckThat's open index, where anyone can see AI visibility data, follows this same democratic trend.
