## Overview

**The Elements of Style** (commonly known as "Strunk & White") is the most influential English writing style guide in American history. Originally written by William Strunk Jr. in 1918 for his students at Cornell University, it was later revised and expanded by E.B. White in 1959, becoming a publishing phenomenon that has sold over 10 million copies.

Time magazine recognized it in 2011 as one of the hundred best and most influential non-fiction books in English since 1923. The Open Syllabus Project (2016) listed it as the most frequently assigned text in US academic syllabuses.

---

## Book Structure

The book is organized into five main sections:

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| **I. Elementary Rules of Usage** | 8 rules of grammar and punctuation |
| **II. Elementary Principles of Composition** | 11 principles for effective writing |
| **III. A Few Matters of Form** | Formatting guidelines |
| **IV. Words and Expressions Commonly Misused** | 49 common errors to avoid |
| **V. An Approach to Style** | 21 reminders for developing writing style |

## Part I: Elementary Rules of Usage

### The 8 Rules

1. **Form the possessive singular of nouns by adding 's**
   - Charles's friend, Burns's poems, the witch's malice
   - Exceptions: ancient proper names ending in -es or -is (Jesus', Moses')

2. **In a series of three or more terms, use a comma after each term except the last**
   - Red, white, and blue
   - He opened the letter, read it, and made a note of its contents

3. **Enclose parenthetic expressions between commas**
   - The best way to see a country, unless you are pressed for time, is to travel on foot
   - Note: Restrictive clauses are NOT set off by commas

4. **Place a comma before a conjunction introducing an independent clause**
   - The early records of the city have disappeared, and the story of its first years can no longer be reconstructed

5. **Do not join independent clauses with a comma (comma splice)**
   - Wrong: It is nearly half past five, we cannot reach town before dark
   - Correct: It is nearly half past five. We cannot reach town before dark
   - Or use a semicolon: It is nearly half past five; we cannot reach town before dark

6. **Do not break sentences in two**
   - Wrong: I met them on a Cunard liner several years ago. Coming home from Liverpool to New York.
   - Correct: I met them on a Cunard liner several years ago, coming home from Liverpool to New York.

7. **A participial phrase at the beginning must refer to the grammatical subject**
   - Wrong: Walking slowly down the road, he saw a woman accompanied by two children.
   - (This suggests the woman was walking, not the observer)

8. **Divide words at line-ends in accordance with their formation and pronunciation**
   - Divide according to syllables and word structure

## Part II: Elementary Principles of Composition

### The 11 Principles

1. **Make the paragraph the unit of composition**
   - Each paragraph should develop one topic or idea
   - Begin each paragraph with a topic sentence that sets expectations

2. **Use the active voice**
   - Active: "John threw the ball"
   - Passive: "The ball was thrown by John"
   - Active voice is more direct, vigorous, and concise

3. **Put statements in positive form**
   - Instead of: "He was not very often on time"
   - Write: "He usually came late"
   - Avoid: not honest → dishonest; did not remember → forgot

4. **Use definite, specific, concrete language**
   - Instead of: "A period of unfavorable weather set in"
   - Write: "It rained every day for a week"

5. **Omit needless words**
   > "Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts."

   Common phrases to eliminate:
   | Wordy | Concise |
   |-------|---------|
   | the question as to whether | whether |
   | there is no doubt but that | no doubt / doubtless |
   | the fact that | (often can be deleted entirely) |
   | in a hasty manner | hastily |
   | this is a subject that | this subject |
   | owing to the fact that | since / because |

6. **Avoid a succession of loose sentences**
   - Don't string together clause after clause with "and" or "but"
   - Vary sentence structure and length

7. **Express coordinate ideas in similar form (parallelism)**
   - Correct: "She likes reading, writing, and painting"
   - Wrong: "She likes reading, writing, and to paint"
   - The likeness of form helps readers recognize likeness of content

8. **Keep related words together**
   - Subject and verb should not be separated by long phrases
   - Relative pronouns should immediately follow their antecedents

9. **In summaries, keep to one tense**
   - Use present tense when summarizing dramatic works
   - Be consistent throughout

10. **Place the emphatic words of a sentence at the end**
    - The end position is the most prominent
    - Build toward the most important word or idea

11. **Choose a suitable design and follow it**
    - Plan your composition before writing
    - The outline should be a working document, not a straitjacket

## Part III: A Few Matters of Form

### Key Formatting Guidelines

- **Headings**: Leave a blank line after a title or heading
- **Numerals**: Spell out numbers under 100, except dates and addresses
- **Parentheses**: Punctuate as if the parenthetical were absent
- **Quotations**: Introduce formal quotations with a colon
- **References**: Use standard bibliographic format
- **Titles**: Italicize titles of books and periodicals; use quotation marks for articles and poems

## Part IV: Words and Expressions Commonly Misused

### Essential Distinctions

| Confused Pair | Correct Usage |
|---------------|---------------|
| **Affect/Effect** | Affect (verb): to influence; Effect (noun): result |
| **Less/Fewer** | Less: quantity; Fewer: number ("fewer items," "less water") |
| **That/Which** | That: restrictive clauses; Which: non-restrictive (with comma) |
| **Who/Whom** | Who: subject; Whom: object |
| **Comprise/Compose** | The whole comprises the parts; the parts compose the whole |
| **Imply/Infer** | Writer implies; reader infers |

### Words to Avoid or Use Carefully

- **Aggravate**: Means "to make worse," not "to irritate"
- **Hopefully**: Often misused as a sentence modifier; use "I hope" instead
- **Literally**: Do not use to support exaggeration
- **Very**: Use sparingly; choose stronger words instead
- **Kind of/Sort of**: Not substitutes for "rather" or "somewhat"
- **Currently/Presently**: "Presently" means "soon," not "now"
- **Nauseous/Nauseated**: Nauseous means "causing nausea"; nauseated means "feeling sick"
- **Data/Media/Criteria**: Technically plural (though usage is evolving)

### Expressions to Eliminate

| Avoid | Use Instead |
|-------|-------------|
| At the present time | Now |
| Due to the fact that | Because |
| In terms of | (Rephrase to be specific) |
| The fact that | (Delete or rephrase) |
| As to whether | Whether |
| The question as to whether | Whether |
| In order to | To |
| He is a man who | He |
| The reason is because | The reason is that / Because |

## Part V: An Approach to Style (21 Reminders)

E.B. White's contribution to the book, this chapter offers guidance on developing your own voice and style.

### The 21 Reminders

1. **Place yourself in the background**
   - Don't draw attention to yourself; serve the reader and subject

2. **Write in a way that comes naturally**
   - Use words and phrases that come readily to you
   - Don't adopt an artificial style

3. **Work from a suitable design**
   - Have a plan, but be flexible
   - Design is essential but should not constrain

4. **Write with nouns and verbs**
   - Nouns and verbs are the workhorses of language
   - Adjectives and adverbs are qualifiers, not substitutes

5. **Revise and rewrite**
   - Writing is rewriting
   - Don't be afraid to throw away weak passages

6. **Do not overwrite**
   - Avoid ornate prose
   - Rich, ornamental writing can obscure meaning

7. **Do not overstate**
   - Resist the temptation to exaggerate
   - Let facts speak for themselves

8. **Avoid the use of qualifiers**
   - Rather, very, little, pretty—these diminish force
   - Choose precise words that don't need qualification

9. **Do not affect a breezy manner**
   - Forced casualness is transparent and off-putting

10. **Use orthodox spelling**
    - Don't use nite for night, thru for through
    - Respect conventions

11. **Do not explain too much**
    - Trust your reader
    - Let dialogue and action reveal meaning

12. **Do not construct awkward adverbs**
    - Avoid overly and tiredly
    - Find a better construction

13. **Make sure the reader knows who is speaking**
    - Clear attribution in dialogue
    - Avoid confusion about point of view

14. **Avoid fancy words**
    - Anglo-Saxon words are often stronger than Latinate ones
    - Simple language has more power

15. **Do not use dialect unless your ear is good**
    - Bad dialect is worse than none
    - If you use it, be consistent

16. **Be clear**
    - Clarity is the paramount virtue
    - Every reader must understand your meaning

17. **Do not inject opinion**
    - Present facts, not judgments (unless that's your purpose)
    - Let readers form their own opinions

18. **Use figures of speech sparingly**
    - One good metaphor is better than five
    - Avoid mixed metaphors

19. **Do not take shortcuts at the cost of clarity**
    - Don't sacrifice understanding for brevity

20. **Avoid foreign languages**
    - Don't use French, Latin, etc. to seem sophisticated
    - If there's an English equivalent, use it

21. **Prefer the standard to the offbeat**
    - Unconventional style can distract from content
    - Master the rules before breaking them

## Core Philosophy: The Strunk & White Approach

### Brevity and Clarity
The book's central tenet is that good writing is concise and clear. Every word should earn its place.

### The Active Voice
Active voice creates direct, vigorous prose. Passive voice has its uses but should not be the default.

### Positive Form
State what something IS rather than what it is NOT. Positive statements are more powerful.

### Parallel Construction
Ideas of equal importance should have equal grammatical form.

### The Paragraph as Unit
Each paragraph should focus on one idea, introduced by a topic sentence.

## Practice Exercises

### Exercise 1: Omit Needless Words
Revise these sentences:
1. "He is a man who is very careful about details."
2. "The question as to whether we should proceed is under consideration."
3. "Owing to the fact that the weather was bad, the game was postponed."

**Suggested answers:**
1. "He is careful about details." or "He attends to details."
2. "Whether we should proceed is under consideration."
3. "Because of bad weather, the game was postponed." or "Bad weather postponed the game."

### Exercise 2: Active Voice
Convert to active voice:
1. "The ball was thrown by the pitcher."
2. "The proposal was rejected by the committee."
3. "A good time was had by all."

**Suggested answers:**
1. "The pitcher threw the ball."
2. "The committee rejected the proposal."
3. "Everyone had a good time."

### Exercise 3: Parallelism
Fix the parallel structure:
1. "She likes swimming, hiking, and to read."
2. "The course teaches writing clearly, thinking critically, and research skills."

**Suggested answers:**
1. "She likes swimming, hiking, and reading."
2. "The course teaches clear writing, critical thinking, and research skills."

### Exercise 4: Positive Form
Revise to positive form:
1. "He did not think studying was important."
2. "She was not present at the meeting."
3. "The report was not inaccurate."

**Suggested answers:**
1. "He considered studying unimportant." or "He dismissed studying."
2. "She was absent from the meeting." or "She missed the meeting."
3. "The report was accurate."

- English professor at Cornell University for 46 years
- Ph.D. from Cornell (1896)
- Known for teaching Shakespeare and style

**E.B. White (1899-1985)**
- Strunk's student at Cornell
- Legendary essayist and writer for The New Yorker
- Author of Charlotte's Web and Stuart Little
- Wrote the famous "Introduction" and Chapter V

## Criticisms and Debates

While enormously influential, the book has faced criticism:

### Linguistic Criticism
Geoffrey Pullum and other linguists have criticized:
- Misunderstanding of the passive voice
- Proscription of established English usages
- Over-prescription that ignores how language actually works
- Pullum famously called it "the book that ate America's brain"

### Dated Examples
Some find the examples antiquated and the pronouns outdated (the fourth edition addressed the generic "he" issue).

### Counter-Arguments
Supporters argue:
- The principles remain sound even if examples are dated
- It provides a foundation that writers can build upon
- Mastering rules precedes breaking them meaningfully

## Conclusion

The Elements of Style endures because its core message is timeless: clear thinking leads to clear writing. The book teaches writers to respect their readers by being concise, direct, and precise. While some specific advice may be debated, the underlying philosophy—that every word should serve a purpose—remains the foundation of effective communication.

As E.B. White wrote in his introduction:
> "Will Strunk loved the clear, the brief, the bold, and his book is clear, brief, bold."
