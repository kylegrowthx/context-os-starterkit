# The operator's handbook for scaling from $12M to $36M ARR

A 100-person hybrid tech+services company tripling revenue in 12 months faces a fundamental operating challenge: you must build organizational systems fast enough to support growth without bureaucracy slowing execution. This synthesis distills battle-tested frameworks from operators who've navigated this exact scaling journey—Andy Grove's leverage principles, Amazon's operating mechanisms, EOS's execution discipline, and modern remote-first playbooks from companies like Zapier and GitLab.

The core insight threading through all successful operators: **execution discipline beats strategy elegance every time**. Frank Slootman captured it bluntly: "High-growth enterprises are not easy places to live. The pressure is relentless." Your job is building an operating system that channels that pressure into productive output.

## Part 1: The foundation—operating philosophy and mental models

### Andy Grove's leverage equation changes how you spend time

Grove's fundamental insight from *High Output Management* remains the most important mental model for scaling operators: **a manager's output equals the output of their organization plus the output of neighboring organizations under their influence**.

This reframes every calendar decision. Training one person for an hour creates leverage across thousands of future hours of their work. A decision that unblocks ten engineers amplifies your output tenfold. Conversely, waffling on decisions—Grove's observation that "no green light is a red light"—destroys organizational throughput.

**High-leverage activities to prioritize:**
- Teaching and training (amplifies across many people over time)
- Decision-making that unblocks others
- Customer complaint deep-dives (reveals systemic issues)
- Planning work done in advance

**Task-Relevant Maturity (TRM)** guides how to manage each person differently based on their experience with specific tasks, not their overall seniority:

| TRM Level | Employee Profile | Management Approach |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Low** | New to this specific task | Structured direction, frequent check-ins |
| **Medium** | Building experience | Coaching, explain the "why" not just "what" |
| **High** | Proven track record here | Set goals, minimal intervention |

A senior engineer tackling a new domain needs closer management than a junior person working in their sweet spot. Match your management intensity to task maturity, not titles.

### Ben Horowitz's wartime vs peacetime CEO framework

Scaling **3x in 12 months puts you in wartime**. Horowitz's framework from *The Hard Thing About Hard Things* distinguishes two fundamentally different leadership modes:

| Dimension | Peacetime CEO | Wartime CEO |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| Protocol | Follows proper protocol | Violates protocol to win |
| Detail focus | Empowers detailed decisions | Obsesses over small details affecting mission |
| Deviation tolerance | Tolerates deviation with creativity | Completely intolerant of deviation |
| Conflict approach | Minimizes conflict | Heightens contradictions |
| Buy-in | Strives for broad consensus | Neither indulges nor tolerates disagreement |

**You're in wartime when:** cash runway is tight, existential competitive threats emerge, major product/market shifts are required, or you're attempting aggressive scaling targets. The 3x growth target qualifies.

The danger is **management debt**—expedient short-term decisions with expensive long-term consequences. Skipping 1:1s when busy, putting two people in a box with shared responsibility, overcompensating key employees to retain them—these compound like technical debt. Track them consciously.

### Frank Slootman's "Amp It Up" operating intensity

Slootman's framework from his time scaling Snowflake and ServiceNow demands uncomfortable intensity:

**1. Raise your standards:** Don't ask "what do you think?" Ask "**are you THRILLED with this?**" If the answer is "it's okay" or "not bad"—rework until excitement emerges.

**2. Align your people around drivers vs passengers:**

| Drivers | Passengers |
|---------|------------|
| Make things happen | Along for the ride |
| Strong ownership | Avoid accountability |
| Take clear positions | Hedge positions |
| Say "why not?" | Say "that's impossible" |
| Exude energy and urgency | Blend in |

Slootman's advice: ruthlessly identify and promote drivers. Exit passengers. "Passengers are mostly dead weight."

**3. Sharpen your focus:** If leadership can't explain priorities in 30 seconds, they're too broad. Say NO to good things to do great things.

**4. Pick up the pace:** Shorten timelines on everything. "Hope is not a strategy."

### Matt Mochary's Zone of Genius and energy audit

From *The Great CEO Within*, two frameworks help leaders sustain performance:

**Zone of Genius framework:**
| Zone | Definition | Action |
|------|------------|--------|
| Incompetence | Others do better | Delegate immediately |
| Competence | You're fine, others equally good | Delegate if no joy |
| Excellence | Better than others but don't love it | **DANGER ZONE**—delegate even this |
| Genius | Uniquely good AND love it | Maximize (target 75-80%) |

The trap is the **Zone of Excellence**. You're good at it, so people keep assigning it to you, but it drains you. Restructure your role until 75-80% of time is in your Zone of Genius.

**Monthly energy audit process:**
1. Print calendar for past two weeks
2. Mark each activity green (energizing) or red (draining)—no neutrals
3. Look for patterns
4. Create plan to outsource/eliminate red activities
5. Repeat monthly until 75%+ green

Mochary calls energy audits "the single most powerful tool I know for creating joy and engagement."

## Part 2: The EOS execution system—rocks, scorecard, and L10 meetings

The **Entrepreneurial Operating System (EOS)** from Gino Wickman's *Traction* provides the most adopted execution framework for companies at your scale. Its power lies in simplicity and consistency.

### The Vision/Traction Organizer (V/TO)

This two-page strategic document aligns the entire company:

**Page 1 (Vision):**
- Core Values: 3-7 guiding principles
- Core Focus: Purpose + Niche
- 10-Year Target: Your BHAG
- 3-Year Picture: Revenue, profit, key measures

**Page 2 (Traction):**
- 1-Year Plan: Revenue goal, profit goal, 3-7 company Rocks
- Quarterly Rocks: 90-day priorities with owners
- Issues List: Obstacles to address

### Rocks: The 90-day priority system

**Rocks** are the 3-7 most important things that must be accomplished in the next 90 days. The metaphor comes from Stephen Covey—put big rocks in the jar first, then sand fills around them.

**Rock requirements (SMART+Owner):**
- Specific outcome: Clear deliverable
- Measurable success criteria
- Single owner: One person accountable
- Realistic timeline: Achievable in 90 days
- Clear connection to company goals

**Example Rock:** "Document and implement 5-step sales methodology—Owner: VP Sales—Due: March 31—Success Criteria: 80% team adoption, documented playbook complete"

**Tracking:** Weekly during L10 meetings. Status is simply On Track / Off Track / Done. Target completion rate: 80%+ of Rocks done each quarter. Off-track Rocks immediately go to the Issues List.

### The Scorecard: 5-15 numbers that tell you if you're winning

Every function needs numbers that create accountability. The Scorecard contains **5-15 weekly metrics** tracking company health:

**Design principles:**
- Actionable: Team can influence it
- Owned: Single person accountable for each number
- Consistent: Same metrics weekly for pattern recognition
- Leading mix: Include predictive metrics, not just lagging outcomes

**For your tech+services company:**

| Metric | Owner | Type |
|--------|-------|------|
| ARR | CEO | Lagging |
| New qualified leads | Marketing | Leading |
| Pipeline coverage ratio | Sales | Leading |
| Win rate | Sales | Lagging |
| NRR (Net Revenue Retention) | Customer Success | Lagging |
| Product usage score | Product | Leading |
| Billable utilization | Delivery | Leading |
| Customer health score | CS | Leading |
| Services gross margin | Finance | Lagging |
| Open headcount vs plan | HR | Leading |

### Level 10 (L10) meetings: The weekly rhythm

The L10 is a structured **90-minute weekly leadership meeting** designed to rate a "10" on effectiveness:

| Section | Time | Purpose |
|---------|------|---------|
| Segue/Check-in | 5 min | Personal good news, transition into meeting |
| Scorecard Review | 5 min | Off-track numbers only |
| Rock Review | 5 min | On/off track status |
| Customer/Employee Headlines | 5 min | Notable updates |
| To-Do List Review | 5 min | Last week's 7-day commitments: Done/Not Done |
| **IDS (Issues)** | **60 min** | Identify, Discuss, Solve top issues |
| Conclude | 5 min | Recap to-dos, rate meeting 1-10 |

**IDS (Identify, Discuss, Solve) is the heart of the meeting:**
1. **Identify:** What's the real issue? Dig to root cause, not symptoms
2. **Discuss:** Open dialogue until fully understood (cut tangents)
3. **Solve:** Determine action items with owners and 7-day due dates

**L10 rules:**
- Same day, same time, every week—no exceptions
- Start and end on time, always
- 6-7 people optimal; larger teams need departmental L10s
- No explanations for missed to-dos—just "done" or "not done"
- Update Issues List throughout week asynchronously
- Rate meeting 1-10 at end; discuss low scores

**Remote adaptations:** Use threaded comments for async pre-discussion, visible timer on screen, video-on expectation.

## Part 3: Amazon operating mechanisms—six-pagers, WBR, and decision systems

Amazon's operating mechanisms scale from two-pizza teams to 1.5 million employees. The core principle: **force clarity through writing and create consistent rhythms for accountability**.

### The six-pager: Narrative memos replace slide decks

Bezos banned PowerPoint because "bullet points hide weak thinking." The six-pager forces complete thought:

**Structure (6 sections, narrative prose):**
1. **Introduction:** Context, problem statement, why this matters
2. **Goals:** SMART objectives, success metrics
3. **Tenets:** Guiding principles for decision-making
4. **State of the Business:** Current situation with relevant data
5. **Lessons Learned:** What worked/didn't in past efforts (brutally honest)
6. **Strategic Priorities:** Recommendations, asks
7. **Appendix:** Supporting data (no page limit)

**Meeting format:**
- Distribute memo at start of meeting (not before)
- First **20-30 minutes in silence**: Everyone reads, takes margin notes
- Discussion follows, driven by questions that arose during reading
- Bezos: "The best memos might take 2 weeks to write"

**When to use:** Strategic decisions, annual planning, significant investments, new initiatives. For smaller proposals, use the **PR/FAQ (Working Backwards)** format—a fictional press release announcing the product, followed by customer and internal FAQs.

### Weekly Business Review (WBR): Exception-based operational rhythm

Amazon reviews **400-500 metrics weekly** but focuses only on exceptions. The format builds "fingertip feel" over time:

**The 6-12 Graph (Amazon's signature visual):**
- Left side: Trailing 6 weeks (short-term trends)
- Right side: Trailing 12 months (long-term patterns)
- MTD, QTD, YTD comparisons
- Color-coded RAG against targets

**Meeting principles:**
- Same format every week creates pattern recognition
- Focus on **variance and exceptions**—if on track, move quickly
- Metric owners explain anomalies or say "I don't know, investigating"
- Tactical meeting—NO strategy discussions allowed
- Duration: 60-90 minutes, but well-run teams finish in 15 minutes

**Input vs output metrics:** Prioritize **input metrics** (actions you control: calls made, features shipped) over output metrics (revenue, retention). Input metrics are leading indicators you can actually influence.

### One-way door vs two-way door decisions

This simple framework determines appropriate decision speed:

| Type | Definition | Process |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Type 1 (One-Way Door)** | Irreversible or costly to reverse | Methodical, consultative, slow |
| **Type 2 (Two-Way Door)** | Easy to reverse, limited consequences | Fast, delegate to closest team |

**Critical insight:** Organizations default to treating everything as Type 1, creating unnecessary slowdown. **Assume Type 2 unless proven Type 1.** Ask: "What would it cost to reverse this?"

For Type 2 decisions, Bezos recommends deciding with **~70% of information** you wish you had. Waiting for 90% certainty means moving too slowly.

### Disagree and commit

When teams reach impasse on reversible decisions: "I know we disagree. Will you gamble with me? **Disagree and commit?**"

The process:
1. Voice disagreement clearly with data
2. Make the ask for commitment
3. Commit genuinely—not passive-aggressive
4. No "I told you so" regardless of outcome
5. Document the decision and dissenting views for retrospective learning

Bezos example: "I disagree and commit and hope it becomes the most-watched thing we've ever made."

## Part 4: Decision-making frameworks—DACI, RAPID, and informed captain

Decision paralysis kills scaling companies. These frameworks clarify who decides what.

### DACI (Atlassian's framework)

Four roles for every significant decision:

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|----------------|
| **D - Driver** | Corrals stakeholders, ensures decision gets made by deadline |
| **A - Approver** | Makes final decision (ONE person) |
| **C - Contributors** | Provide expertise; voice but no vote |
| **I - Informed** | Need to know outcome; no input |

**DACI document template:**
```
DACI: [Question we're trying to answer?]
Status: [Draft/In Progress/Decided]
Due Date: [Date]

ROLES:
Driver: [Name]
Approver: [Name]  
Contributors: [Names]
Informed: [Names]

BACKGROUND: [Context]
OPTIONS: [A, B, C with pros/cons]
RECOMMENDATION: [Preferred option]
DECISION: [Final choice + rationale]
```

**When to use:** Complex decisions with multiple stakeholders, high-stakes choices, cross-functional projects.

### RAPID (Bain/Coinbase implementation)

Five roles for organization-wide decisions:

| Role | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **R - Recommend** | Gathers input, proposes solution |
| **A - Agree** | Must approve (sparingly—Legal, Finance compliance) |
| **P - Perform** | Implements decision |
| **I - Input** | Provides expertise, data |
| **D - Decide** | Makes final call (ONE person) |

**Best practices:**
- Limit Agree roles (avoid consensus trap)
- One Recommender, one Decider
- Pre-wire with stakeholders before formal decision

### Netflix's informed captain model

One person "owns" each significant decision. No committees:

1. Identify the **Informed Captain** (person closest to problem with relevant expertise)
2. **Farm for dissent**: Captain must actively solicit opposing views
3. **Captain decides**—no consensus required
4. Others commit regardless of personal view
5. Reflect after to learn from outcome

The critical principle: **context, not control**. Managers provide context (strategy, constraints, resources) but don't approve decisions. The informed captain is the decision-maker, not the boss.

### Framework selection guide

| Use Case | Recommended Framework |
|----------|----------------------|
| Cross-functional projects | DACI |
| Org-wide strategic decisions | RAPID |
| Product/technical decisions | Informed Captain |
| Breaking team deadlocks | Disagree and Commit |
| Categorizing decision speed | One-Way/Two-Way Doors |

## Part 5: Planning and goal-setting—OKRs, V2MOM, and annual cycles

### OKRs: The standard for strategic alignment

**Objectives** are significant, inspiring destinations. **Key Results** are quantitative measures of progress:

**Writing good OKRs:**
- Objectives: Answer "Where do I want to get?" Should NOT be "business as usual"
- Key Results: Formula is "I will _______ as measured by _________"
- 3-5 Key Results per Objective
- Must be binary: "Did I do that or not?"

**Example:**
```
Objective: Become the market leader in our segment

Key Results:
- Achieve $36M ARR (from $12M)
- Reach 120% net revenue retention
- Win 3 enterprise logos from top competitor
```

**Scoring:** 0.0 to 1.0 scale. For aspirational OKRs, **70% achievement is target**—100% means goals were too easy. For committed OKRs (delivery promises), target 100%.

**Cadence:** Quarterly team OKRs nested within annual company OKRs. Skip individual OKRs at your scale—they become task lists.

**Anti-patterns to avoid:**
- Too many OKRs (stick to 3-5 per team)
- Treating OKRs as task lists
- Tying OKRs to compensation (kills risk-taking)
- 100% completion rate (goals too easy)

### V2MOM: Salesforce's strategic alignment tool

Marc Benioff created V2MOM for company-wide cascading:

| Component | Question |
|-----------|----------|
| **Vision** | What do we want to achieve? |
| **Values** | What's important about it? (ranked) |
| **Methods** | How do we get it? (ranked) |
| **Obstacles** | What might stand in the way? |
| **Measures** | How do we know we've succeeded? |

**Use case:** Annual strategic planning. Every employee creates aligned V2MOM visible company-wide.

### Rocks + OKRs integration for scaling

**Recommended stack for $12M→$36M:**

| Layer | Framework | Cadence |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| Vision/Strategy | V2MOM or 3-Year Picture | Annual |
| Annual Goals | Company OKRs | Annual with quarterly refresh |
| Quarterly Execution | Rocks (EOS) | 90-day cycles |
| Weekly Operations | Scorecard + L10 | Weekly |
| Financial Planning | Annual budget + rolling forecast | Quarterly reforecast |

This gives strategic clarity through OKRs while maintaining execution focus through Rocks.

## Part 6: Communication systems for remote-first companies

### Channel architecture: What goes where

| Channel | Purpose | Response Time |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| Wiki/Handbook | Permanent decisions, policies, processes | Reference only |
| Long-form posts | Strategic discussions, important decisions | 24-48 hours |
| Email | External communication, formal records | 24 hours |
| Slack/Chat | Quick coordination, social, urgent | Same day |
| Video calls | Relationship building, complex discussions | Scheduled |
| All-hands | Company updates, culture | Monthly |

**GitLab's principle:** Optimize for **speed of knowledge retrieval** rather than speed of knowledge transfer. Document everything in a single source of truth.

**Basecamp's key insight:** "Speaking only helps who's in the room, writing helps everyone."

### CEO communication cadence

| Cadence | Format | Content | Length |
|---------|--------|---------|--------|
| Weekly | Written post | Top-of-mind, company health, wins | 500-800 words |
| Monthly | Business update | Financials, metrics, progress vs goals | 1,000-1,500 words |
| Quarterly | All-hands (recorded) | Strategy, Q review, recognition | 45-60 min |
| Annually | Planning communications | Vision, annual priorities, retrospective | Multi-format |

**Weekly CEO update structure:**
1. What I'm thinking about (1-3 topics with "why it matters")
2. How we're doing (company metrics, honest assessment)
3. Wins and highlights (team accomplishments)
4. Ask (question to drive engagement)

Send Monday morning. Prepare Friday afternoon. Always end with a question.

### Transparency tiers

| Tier | Access | Examples |
|------|--------|----------|
| Public | Anyone | Culture, values, general policies |
| Company-wide | All employees | Financials, strategy, OKRs, salaries |
| Team-level | Relevant teams | Project details, team metrics |
| Need-to-know | Specific individuals | Personnel matters, legal, M&A |
| Confidential | Leadership only | Pre-announcement items, board |

Buffer's experience: 50% increase in job applications after publishing salaries publicly. Transparency builds trust—but must include bad news, not just good.

### Async-first practices from Zapier

Zapier's 500+ employees span all time zones. Their principles:

- **Ask complete questions:** Avoid "quick question" followed by hours of back-and-forth
- **Record video calls:** Share for those who couldn't attend
- **Default to transparency:** Almost all Slack channels public
- **Written follow-ups:** Summarize every meeting in writing
- **Pair calls:** Random weekly pairing (via Donut) for connection

**Channel naming convention:** Prefix channels with purpose: `fun-`, `feed-`, `city-`, `team-`, `proj-`

## Part 7: People operations at scale

### Performance review system

**Recommended cadence for 100-person company:**

| Component | Frequency | Format |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Real-time feedback | Ongoing | Informal, specific |
| Manager 1:1s | Weekly | 30-min development focus |
| Formal reviews | Semi-annual | Written + calibration |
| 360 feedback | Annual | Peer/manager/self |
| Goal check-ins | Quarterly | OKR/Rock reviews |

### Calibration meetings ensure consistency

**Three-tier process:**
1. Direct manager level: Calibrate IC ratings within teams
2. Manager of managers: Calibrate manager ratings, spot-check ICs
3. Executive level: Review trends, final sign-off

**Meeting format (2-3 hours):**
- Managers submit draft ratings with evidence
- HR prepares distribution analysis
- Focus discussion on outliers and borderline cases
- Document justifications for adjustments

**Rating scale (5-point):**
| Rating | Definition | Expected % |
|--------|------------|------------|
| 5 - Exceptional | Far exceeds all | ~5% |
| 4 - Exceeds | Exceeds most | ~15-20% |
| 3 - Meets | Meets all | ~60-70% |
| 2 - Developing | Meets some | ~10-15% |
| 1 - Below | Does not meet | ~5% |

### Hiring with Bar Raisers

Amazon's Bar Raiser concept adapted for your scale:

**What Bar Raisers do:**
- Trained interviewer from outside hiring team
- Ensures candidates are "better than 50% of current employees in similar roles"
- Brings objective, cross-functional perspective
- Has veto power (rarely used)

**Implementation:**
- Select 3-5 trained interviewers across departments
- 3-6 months training, shadowing experienced interviewers
- Participate in all leadership/senior hires
- Lead debrief meetings, drive to data-driven decisions

**Debrief format:**
1. Each interviewer shares assessment independently first
2. Discuss strengths/concerns with evidence
3. Bar Raiser facilitates consensus
4. Hiring manager + Bar Raiser make final call
5. Document decision rationale

### 30-60-90 day onboarding

**Days 1-30 (Learn):**
- Complete technical/tools onboarding
- Understand company history, mission, values
- Shadow team members
- 5 virtual coffee chats with cross-functional peers
- Assign peer buddy (not manager)

**Days 31-60 (Contribute):**
- Take on small, low-risk projects
- Receive and act on feedback
- Set goals with manager

**Days 61-90 (Execute):**
- Own deliverables independently
- Complete 90-day performance check-in
- Create longer-term development plan

## Part 8: Operating a hybrid tech+services business

With 60 of 100 employees in client delivery, services operations require distinct frameworks.

### Utilization targets by role

| Role | Target Billable % | Non-Billable Allocation |
|------|-------------------|------------------------|
| Junior Consultant | 80-85% | Training, admin |
| Consultant | 75-80% | Training, internal projects |
| Senior Consultant | 70-75% | Mentoring, BD, methodology |
| Principal/Partner | 50-60% | Sales, thought leadership |
| Practice Lead | 40-50% | Management, strategy, hiring |

**Warning signs:**
- Utilization >85%: Burnout risk
- Utilization <65%: Profitability risk
- Bench time >2 weeks: Forecasting/sales issue

### Services margin management

**Key metrics (SPI Research 2025 benchmarks):**

| Metric | Formula | Target |
|--------|---------|--------|
| Billable utilization | Billable Hours / Available Hours | 70-75% |
| Realization rate | Billed Hours / Billable Hours | 90%+ |
| Revenue per consultant | Total Revenue / Delivery Headcount | $150K-$250K |
| Gross margin | (Revenue - Direct Costs) / Revenue | 40-50% |
| Client concentration | Largest client % of revenue | <20% |

### Project governance framework

| Project Size | Governance Level | Review Cadence |
|--------------|------------------|----------------|
| <$50K | Project Lead + Manager | Weekly internal |
| $50K-$150K | Steering Committee | Bi-weekly |
| $150K+ | Executive Sponsor | Monthly + Steering |

**Weekly status template:**
- Project health (RAG status with evidence)
- Key accomplishments this week
- Planned activities next week
- Risks and issues with mitigations
- Budget/timeline tracking
- Client satisfaction pulse

### Service-to-product transition

**Identify productization opportunities:**
- Tasks performed frequently with little variation
- Repeatable methodologies and frameworks
- Areas where automation can replace human time

**Approaches:**
| Approach | Example |
|----------|---------|
| Template/Tool | Assessment frameworks, playbooks |
| Foot-in-Door Product | Diagnostic, workshop, audit |
| Methodology License | Training certification |
| Platform | Client portal, automation tools |
| Hybrid | Platform + advisory services |

## Part 9: Reporting and accountability systems

### Exception-based reporting

Don't review every metric—focus on outliers:

| Threshold | Deviation | Action |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Tier 1 (Alert) | 1 std dev | Monitor |
| Tier 2 (Review) | 2 std dev | Investigate |
| Tier 3 (Escalate) | 3 std dev | Immediate action |

### Avoiding watermelon metrics (green outside, red inside)

**Causes:**
- Aggregate metrics hiding subset problems
- Blame culture discouraging honesty
- Subjective status assessment
- Fear of reporting bad news

**Prevention:**
- Tie RAG colors to data thresholds, not judgment
- Multiple data sources per metric
- Psychological safety: red = support needed, not failure
- Segmented analysis: look at outliers within aggregates

### RAG template with evidence

```
Metric: Customer Satisfaction (NPS)
Status: 🟡 YELLOW
Current: 42 | Target: 50 | Prior: 45

Evidence:
- Overall NPS down 3 points
- Enterprise segment: 58 (strong)
- SMB segment: 31 (declining - RED)
- Root cause: Support response time up 40%

Action Plan:
- Hire 2 additional support reps by [date]
- Implement ticket prioritization by [date]
- Owner: VP Customer Success
- ETA to Green: 6 weeks
```

### Leading vs lagging indicators for tech+services

**Balance:** Dashboards should be 60% leading, 40% lagging. Team scorecards: 70% leading (more actionable).

| Function | Leading | Lagging |
|----------|---------|---------|
| **Sales** | Pipeline coverage, qualified leads, activities | Closed-won, win rate, cycle length |
| **Product** | Feature adoption, time to value, support tickets | MAU, churn, NPS |
| **Customer Success** | Health score, usage engagement, QBR completion | NRR, gross churn, expansion |
| **Services** | Utilization forecast, milestone completion, scope changes | Realized margin, CSAT, repeat business |
