# AI Visibility & AEO: Buyer Evaluation Prompts — Study Guide

> **For:** B2B marketing and growth teams (content, SEO, demand gen) who need to show up when buyers use AI during vendor evaluation and shortlisting.  
> **Goal:** Learn how to *think about* creating and prioritizing the right prompts to track so you can measure and improve AI visibility in the buyer evaluation stage.  
> **Time Investment:** 4–6 hours (read + apply to one category).  
> **Last Updated:** 2025  

---

## How to Use This Guide

This guide teaches **methodology** — how to decide *which* prompts to track and why — not tool setup or content optimization. Use it when:

- You’re starting AI visibility / AEO tracking and don’t know which prompts to monitor.
- You have a long list of ideas and need a clear way to prioritize.
- You want to align prompt tracking with the buyer evaluation stage (shortlisting, comparing, choosing).

**Order of use:** Read Part 1 (Foundations) and Part 2 (Frameworks) first. Use Part 3 and the appendices when building or refining your prompt list.

## Part 1: Foundations

### What Is AEO in the Context of Buyer Evaluation?

**AEO (Answer Engine Optimization)** — also called **GEO (Generative Engine Optimization)** — is the practice of making your content and brand **discoverable, interpretable, and citable** by AI answer engines (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, Claude, etc.) when users ask questions.

In B2B, the highest-leverage moment is the **evaluation stage**: when buyers compare options, build shortlists, and narrow to a few vendors. If an AI doesn’t mention or recommend you in those answers, you’re effectively invisible before sales ever gets involved.

- **95%** of B2B buyers plan to use generative AI in at least one area of a future purchase (Forrester).
- **~90%** of B2B buyers use gen AI during their purchasing process; **~50%** start vendor research in AI chatbots instead of Google (multiple 2025 studies).
- **95%** of the time, the winning vendor is already on the buyer’s Day One shortlist; **80%** of deals are won by the “pre-contact favorite” (6sense).

So: **visibility in AI answers during evaluation = visibility in the shortlist.**

### Why the Unit of Measurement Is the “Prompt”

In traditional SEO you optimize for **keywords** and measure **rankings** and **clicks**. In AI search there are no public query logs or a “Search Console” for ChatGPT. Users ask **full questions** (prompts), and engines return **synthesized answers** that cite or omit brands. You can’t see what everyone asked; you can only **define the prompts you care about** and then **measure whether you’re cited** for those prompts.

So the **prompt** is the unit you track: each prompt is a concrete question you run (or automate) against one or more AI engines to see if your brand appears, how it’s described, and who else is mentioned.

- **Topic** = the category or theme you care about (e.g., “CRM for mid-market”).
- **Prompt** = a specific question under that topic (e.g., “What’s the best CRM for a 50-person SaaS company?”).

You choose **topics** that map to business goals, then **define prompts** under each topic that reflect how buyers actually ask. Your tracking setup is only as good as the prompts you select.

### The Evaluation-Stage Mental Model

Not every prompt is an “evaluation” prompt. Many are informational (what is X?, how does Y work?). Those matter for awareness and consideration, but they don’t directly drive shortlisting.

**Evaluation-stage prompts** are ones where the AI is **weighing options, comparing vendors, or recommending solutions**. In those moments, if your brand isn’t present, it doesn’t factor into the decision.

A useful filter (Semrush, Conductor, others):  
*“In AI SEO, visibility only matters when AI is evaluating choices — when it weighs alternatives, applies constraints, and points someone toward a solution.”*

So when you create prompts to track, **bias toward**:

- Comparison and recommendation intent (best, alternatives, vs, compare).
- Use-case and constraint (“for healthcare,” “for 50-person team,” “that integrates with X”).
- Shortlist-building language (top 5, shortlist, options for).

**De-prioritize** (for evaluation-stage tracking) pure definitions, how-to steps, or generic explanations unless they’re gatekeepers to a buying decision.

### Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|---------------|--------|
| Prompt tracking is like keyword research (volume, difficulty). | There’s no public “volume” for prompts. Value comes from **intent** (evaluation vs informational) and **commercial impact** (mapped to pipeline). |
| You need to track hundreds of prompts from day one. | Start with **topics that matter** and 1–3 prompts per topic; expand from data and iteration. |
| Any question that mentions your category is worth tracking. | Focus on questions where the **output** is a comparison, recommendation, or shortlist — not just an explanation. |
| AI visibility is “nice to have” alongside SEO. | For many B2B buyers, AI is the **first** place they build a shortlist; if you’re not there, you’re not in the deal. |

## Part 2: Frameworks & Process

### Two Ways to Discover What to Track

You don’t have to invent prompts from scratch. Two standard approaches (Conductor, others):

**Approach 1: Start with topics that matter**

- List business-critical themes: your category, your strongest use cases, your key segments (industry, company size, role).
- For each theme, ask: “What questions would a buyer in evaluation mode ask that lead to a shortlist or recommendation?”
- Turn those into concrete prompts (see Part 3 and the six categories below).

**Approach 2: Start from data you already have**

- **Sales and success:** Discovery call questions, evaluation-stage objections, post-sale questions that mirror pre-sale evaluation.
- **Support and reviews:** G2/Capterra/trustpilot language — how buyers describe you and competitors.
- **Search and content:** Queries that already drive traffic; “People also ask” and related questions.
- **Win/loss:** Reasons buyers chose you or a competitor; language they use about alternatives.

Use one or both: topics give you coverage of the map; data grounds you in real buyer language.

### Buyer Stage → Prompt Type

Rough mapping:

| Stage | What buyers do | Prompt types to track |
|-------|----------------|------------------------|
| **Awareness** | Learn about the problem or category | “What is X?”, “Why do I need Y?” |
| **Consideration** | Understand options and how they work | “How does X work?”, “What are the types of Y?” |
| **Evaluation** | Shortlist, compare, choose | “Best X for [constraint]”, “X vs Y”, “Alternatives to Z”, “Top 5 X” |

For **buyer evaluation stage** visibility, concentrate on the **evaluation** row. Add awareness/consideration prompts only if they’re proven to lead into evaluation (e.g., “What to look for in X” that leads to “Best X for…”).

### The Six Prompt Categories (Evaluation Stage)

Every evaluation-stage prompt you track will tend to fall into one of these buckets. Your list should include at least a few from each that matter to your business.

1. **Direct comparison** — “[Brand A] vs [Brand B]”, “Compare X and Y for [use case]”.
2. **Alternatives to** — “Alternatives to [Competitor]”, “Best [Competitor] replacement”, “What can I use instead of [Competitor]?”
3. **Category + use case** — “Best [category] for [industry/size/role]”, “Top [category] that integrate with [platform]”.
4. **Review & reputation** — “[Brand] reviews”, “Is [Brand] good for [use case]?”, “[Brand] pros and cons”.
5. **Pricing & cost** — “[Brand] pricing”, “[Brand] vs [Competitor] pricing”, “Best [category] under $X”.
6. **Feature & integration** — “Does [Brand] have [feature]?”, “[Brand] integrations”, “Which [category] has [capability]?”

When you **create** prompts, use these as a checklist so you don’t over-index on one type (e.g., only “best of” and miss “alternatives to [competitor]”).

### How to Prioritize Prompts

Not all prompts are equal. Prioritize by:

1. **Commercial impact** — Does this map to a real buying decision? Would being cited here influence pipeline?
2. **Gap vs competitors** — Are competitors cited and you’re not? Those are fast priorities.
3. **Existing content** — Do you already have content that could answer this (quick win) or do you need to create it (strategic bet)?
4. **Balance** — Mix of branded (your name) and unbranded (category, use case, alternatives); both matter for evaluation.

A simple 2×2 works: **Business value** (high/low) × **Content effort** (high/low). Quick wins = high value, low effort; strategic bets = high value, high effort. Deprioritize low value, high effort.

## Part 3: How to Think About Creating the Right Prompts

### Topics First, Then Prompts

1. **Define topics** = categories you want to “own” in AI answers (e.g., “e-signature for enterprise”, “contract lifecycle for mid-market”).
2. **For each topic**, write 3–5 **prompts** that a buyer in evaluation would actually ask. Use the six categories: comparison, alternatives, best-for, reviews, pricing, features.
3. **Validate** with sales/support/reviews: would our best prospects ask this? Does the AI answer look like a recommendation/shortlist?

This keeps you from either tracking random questions or only branded queries.

### Use a Simple Matrix

Organize prompts along dimensions so you don’t miss big gaps:

| Dimension | Examples |
|-----------|----------|
| **Prompt category** | Comparison, Alternatives, Best-of, Reviews, Pricing, Features |
| **Buyer persona** | VP Ops, IT Director, Founder, Procurement |
| **Industry / use case** | Healthcare, SaaS, Manufacturing, FinTech |

Each cell (e.g., “Best-of × VP Ops × Healthcare”) suggests specific prompts. You don’t need to fill every cell; focus on high-value segments first.

### Source Prompts From Real Behavior

Don’t rely only on intuition. Mine:

- **Sales call transcripts** — Questions prospects ask when comparing you to others.
- **Customer success / support** — Post-sale questions that mirror pre-sale evaluation.
- **G2, Capterra, Gartner** — How buyers describe you, competitors, and “best for” use cases.
- **LinkedIn, Reddit, communities** — How practitioners frame “best tool for…” and “alternatives to X”.
- **Search data** — Queries that already bring evaluators to your site; expand into natural-language prompts.

Prompts that mirror real language are more likely to match what buyers type into ChatGPT or Perplexity.

### Intent: Comparative vs Informational vs Diagnostic

When you write or select a prompt, label intent (e.g., Alex Genovese / AEO content frameworks):

- **Informational** — Definition, steps, examples (often top-of-funnel).
- **Comparative** — “X vs Y”, “best”, “alternatives” (**evaluation**; prioritize these).
- **Diagnostic** — “Why isn’t…”, “how to fix…” (support/troubleshooting; track if it affects renewal or expansion).

For **buyer evaluation stage** tracking, **comparative** intent should dominate your list.

## Part 4: Skills & Practice

### What Distinguishes Good Prompt Lists

- **Tied to evaluation** — Focus on prompts that produce comparisons, recommendations, or shortlists, not just explanations.
- **Grounded in data** — Sourced from sales, support, reviews, search; not only brainstormed.
- **Structured** — Topics → prompts; categories × persona × use case so you can spot gaps.
- **Prioritized** — Value vs effort; competitor gaps and commercial impact first.
- **Iterative** — Start small (e.g., 20–30 prompts), see who gets cited, then add or refine.

### How to Validate Your List

- **Run a sample** — Manually ask 5–10 of your prompts in ChatGPT/Perplexity. Do the answers look like “evaluation” (options, comparison, recommendation)? If not, refine the prompt or move it to a lower priority.
- **Compare to sales language** — Do your prompts mirror the way your best prospects talk about “best tool for…” or “alternatives to…”?
- **Track over time** — When you create or optimize content for a prompt, does your citation or position in the answer change? That confirms the prompt is measurable and actionable.

## Appendix B: Templates & Tools

### Prompt discovery checklist

- [ ] **Topics** listed (business-critical categories, use cases, segments).
- [ ] **Prompts** under each topic (3–5 per topic to start).
- [ ] **Six categories** represented where relevant: comparison, alternatives, best-for, reviews, pricing, features.
- [ ] **Matrix** used (category × persona × use case) to spot gaps.
- [ ] **Sources** used: sales, support, reviews, search, communities.
- [ ] **Intent** labeled: comparative (evaluation) vs informational vs diagnostic.
- [ ] **Prioritized** by value vs effort and competitor gaps.

### Minimal prompt list (per topic)

For one topic, aim for at least:

- 1–2 **direct comparison** (you vs competitor; competitor A vs B).
- 1–2 **alternatives to** (alternatives to [competitor]).
- 2–3 **category + use case** (best [category] for [constraint]).
- 1 **review/reputation** ([your brand] reviews / good for [use case]?).
- 1 **pricing** ([brand] pricing or best under $X).
- 1 **feature/integration** (does [brand] have X? / integrate with Y?).

Adjust by what actually drives your pipeline.

## Appendix C: Learning Path

1. **Read** Part 1 and Part 2 (≈1 hour).
2. **Pick one topic** (e.g., your core category + one use case).
3. **Draft 10–15 prompts** using the six categories and the matrix (≈1 hour).
4. **Source** 3–5 more from sales or reviews; add to the list (≈30 min).
5. **Prioritize** with value/effort; choose 5 “quick wins” and 3 “strategic bets” (≈30 min).
6. **Run** 5 prompts manually in ChatGPT and Perplexity; note who’s cited and how (≈30 min).
7. **Document** your topic + prompt list and iterate monthly (e.g., in your AEO playbook or tracking sheet).
