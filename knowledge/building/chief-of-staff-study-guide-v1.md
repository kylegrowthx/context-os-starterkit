## About This Document

The Chief of Staff is the most misunderstood role in business. It's not an executive assistant with a better title. It's not a shadow CEO accumulating power. It's the person who makes the CEO's time worth 10x more by ensuring the right things happen, the right information flows, and the right decisions get made.

This guide synthesizes lessons from the best Chiefs of Staff in history (James Baker, Rahm Emanuel), the operators who translated the role into tech (Kevin Fishner at HashiCorp, Claire Hughes Johnson at Stripe), and the frameworks that make the role work at companies like GrowthX — hybrid tech+services businesses scaling fast with a founder who thinks in systems.

**When to use it:**
- Evaluating whether GrowthX needs a Chief of Staff
- Defining the role for the first time
- Onboarding someone into the CoS role
- Self-assessing if you're the right fit
- Understanding what "great" looks like versus "busy"

**Core insight that threads through everything:** The best Chiefs of Staff are force multipliers, not power accumulators. They make the CEO more effective by building systems, not by becoming gatekeepers. James Baker said it best: focus on "of staff," not "chief."

## Part 1: What a Chief of Staff Actually Is

### The role in one sentence

A Chief of Staff exists to multiply the CEO's impact by managing the operating system of the company — the flow of information, decisions, priorities, and accountability.

### What it is NOT

Most people confuse the CoS with adjacent roles. The distinctions matter:

| Role | Focus | Key Difference from CoS |
|------|-------|-------------------------|
| **Executive Assistant** | Calendar, logistics, travel | Manages time. CoS manages leverage. |
| **COO** | Ongoing operational ownership | Owns functions permanently. CoS owns the gaps. |
| **VP of Operations** | Specific operational domain | Runs a department. CoS works across all of them. |
| **Project Manager** | Specific deliverables | Manages projects. CoS manages the portfolio of priorities. |
| **Shadow CEO** | Making decisions for the CEO | This is a failure mode, not a role description. |

Tyler Parris, author of *Chief of Staff: The Strategic Partner Who Will Revolutionize Your Organization*, puts it clearly: "The Chief of Staff is the CEO's strategic partner — someone who extends the CEO's reach without replacing the CEO's judgment."

### The three fundamental jobs

Every great CoS does three things, regardless of company size or industry:

**1. Align:** Ensure all activities map to CEO priorities. If the CEO has three things that matter most this quarter, the CoS makes sure every team, meeting, and initiative connects to those three things — or gets questioned.

**2. Execute:** Drive strategic projects that don't have a natural owner. These are the cross-functional initiatives, the "someone needs to own this" projects, the things that fall between org chart lines.

**3. Amplify:** Scale the CEO's impact through systems. The CEO shouldn't have to say the same thing forty times. The CoS builds the mechanisms — documents, processes, communication cadences — so the CEO's intent propagates without the CEO in the room.

This is the Vannin Impact Model, and it's the simplest way to evaluate whether a CoS is earning their seat.

## Part 2: The Masters — Chiefs of Staff Who Defined the Role

### James Baker (White House, Reagan and Bush)

Baker is the gold standard. He served as Chief of Staff to two presidents and is widely considered the most effective person to ever hold the role.

**What made Baker exceptional:**

Baker's operating philosophy: "Prior preparation prevents poor performance." He was obsessive about preparation — knowing the issues, the stakeholders, the likely objections, and the political dynamics before any meeting happened.

**His core principles:**

- **"Of staff" not "chief":** Baker never confused his role with the president's. He managed the building, the flow of information, and the staff — not the country. His power came from being indispensable to the principal, not from accumulating authority.

- **Control the paper flow:** Baker insisted that everything going to the president went through him. Not to gatekeep — to ensure quality, context, and prioritization. Bad information and poorly framed decisions kill principals.

- **Honest broker:** Baker was known for presenting all sides of an issue to the president, not just the side he agreed with. This built extraordinary trust. The principal must believe the CoS is filtering for quality, not for ideology.

- **Protect the principal's time:** Every interaction with the president was evaluated against scarcity. Is this the best use of the most constrained resource in the organization?

**Why this matters for GrowthX:** Marcel has said "flying blind is not an option." Baker's paper flow discipline — ensuring the right information reaches the principal in the right format at the right time — maps directly to Marcel's preference for written context before meetings and data-driven decisions.

### Rahm Emanuel (White House, Obama)

Emanuel brought a different energy — intensity, accountability, and relentless follow-through.

**What made Emanuel exceptional:**

- **Pocket notecard discipline:** Emanuel carried a list of the administration's top priorities in his pocket. He checked items off obsessively. Every conversation, every meeting, every decision got filtered through: "Does this advance the list?"

- **Written reports over verbal updates:** Emanuel required weekly written status reports from senior staff. Writing forces clarity. Verbal updates allow ambiguity and revision. If you can't write it down clearly, you haven't thought it through.

- **Prevented "dissension and drama":** Emanuel was ruthless about alignment. Once a decision was made, you committed. Relitigating in hallways, leaking to press, or passive-aggressive noncompliance were career-ending offenses.

- **Created urgency without panic:** Emanuel operated at high intensity but channeled it productively. The team moved fast because he moved fast. The pace was the message.

**Why this matters for GrowthX:** Marcel values people who "know their numbers cold." Emanuel's written-reports discipline and notecard accountability map to GrowthX's scorecard culture and EOS-style L10 meetings. The CoS at GrowthX should be the person who always knows the numbers — and always knows when they're off track.

### Denis McDonough (White House, Obama's second term)

McDonough represented a different archetype — the CoS as institutional steward and process builder.

**What made McDonough exceptional:**

- **Process integrity:** McDonough believed the quality of decisions was a function of the quality of the process that produced them. He obsessed over who was in the room, what information they had, and whether dissenting views were heard.

- **"Walk and talk":** McDonough was famous for walking the halls of the West Wing constantly. He didn't wait for information to come to him — he went to it. This gave him an unmatched feel for what was really happening versus what was being reported.

- **Bridge builder:** Where Emanuel was a battering ram, McDonough was a bridge. He connected people and ideas across silos, found common ground, and built coalitions.

**Why this matters for GrowthX:** At a hybrid company with delivery teams, product/engineering, and sales all operating with different rhythms, the CoS needs McDonough's ability to "walk the halls" — to have a feel for what's really happening across functions, not just what shows up in dashboards.

## Part 3: The Modern Operators — CoS in Tech and Startups

### Kevin Fishner (HashiCorp)

Fishner is the most-cited example of a Chief of Staff in tech, and his thinking directly shapes the GrowthX CoS role file.

**His breakthrough insight:** "My product is the company, where employees are the user."

This reframe changes everything. The CoS isn't serving the CEO — they're serving the company through the CEO. The company is the product. Employees are the users. Your job is to make the product (company) work better for the users (employees).

**Fishner's core framework: Focus on systems, not hires**

"Focus on your first 10 systems, not just your first 10 hires."

Most scaling companies think their problem is people. Fishner argues the problem is almost always systems. You can hire great people into a bad system and get bad outcomes. You can hire good people into a great system and get great outcomes. Systems are higher leverage than individuals.

**What Fishner built at HashiCorp:**

- Structured the company's operating cadence (planning cycles, review cadences, communication rhythms)
- Created the decision-making frameworks the company used to scale
- Built the internal communication systems
- Designed the org structure transitions as the company grew
- Owned the "no man's land" — initiatives too important to ignore but that didn't belong to any function

**Why this matters for GrowthX:** GrowthX is building "the GitHub + Cursor + Replit for knowledge work." The company literally thinks in systems. A CoS who thinks about the company as a product — with users, feedback loops, iteration cycles, and version control — speaks GrowthX's language natively.

### Claire Hughes Johnson (Stripe, Google)

Johnson scaled Stripe from 200 to 7,000+ employees. While her title was COO, her early work at Stripe was pure Chief of Staff — operationalizing a brilliant but unstructured founder.

**Her core contribution:** Codifying the operating system of a scaling company.

Johnson's book *Scaling People* is the definitive guide to building the organizational infrastructure that lets a company grow without collapsing. Her key principles:

- **"The vast majority of issues are a result of poor or infrequent communication."** The CoS exists to ensure communication happens — the right information reaches the right people at the right time.

- **Operating principles over rules:** Johnson didn't create rigid processes. She created principles that guided decisions across thousands of situations. This scales. Rules break.

- **The "Working with Me" document:** Johnson pioneered the executive user manual concept. She understood that organizational friction often comes from people not understanding how their leaders think and operate. Transparency reduces guesswork.

**Why this matters for GrowthX:** Marcel already has a detailed user manual. A CoS at GrowthX should treat that document as their operating specification — internalize how Marcel processes information, makes decisions, and operates under stress. Then build systems that feed that operating style rather than fighting it.

### Tyler Parris (CoS researcher and practitioner)

Parris literally wrote the book on the modern CoS role.

**His most important framework: The CoS Lifecycle**

| Phase | Duration | Focus |
|-------|----------|-------|
| **Listening** | First 30-60 days | Understand the principal, the team, the culture, the real problems |
| **Building** | Months 2-6 | Create systems, establish cadences, build relationships |
| **Operating** | Months 6-18 | Run the operating system, drive initiatives, multiply the principal |
| **Transitioning** | After 18-24 months | Hand off systems, move into a line role, or evolve the CoS scope |

**Critical insight:** The CoS role should evolve. If you're doing the same thing in month 18 that you were doing in month 6, either the company stopped growing or you stopped growing. Great Chiefs of Staff work themselves out of specific responsibilities by systematizing them.

## Part 4: The Core Competencies — What Separates Great from Good

### The five competencies that matter most

Research across political, military, and corporate CoS roles reveals five consistent competencies:

**1. Pattern recognition across domains**

The CEO sees the whole board. The CoS must see it too — or at least see enough of it to know when pieces aren't connecting. This means understanding finance well enough to spot margin issues, understanding delivery well enough to recognize quality problems, understanding product well enough to evaluate technical trade-offs.

You don't need to be an expert in each domain. You need to know enough to ask the right questions and recognize when answers don't add up.

**2. Translation**

The CEO thinks at one altitude. Department heads think at another. Individual contributors at another. The CoS translates between altitudes — turning the CEO's strategic intent into actionable direction, and turning ground-level reality into strategic insight for the CEO.

This is harder than it sounds. It requires understanding not just what the CEO said, but what they meant, and how that meaning needs to be repackaged for different audiences.

**3. Disciplined follow-through**

Ideas are cheap. Execution is everything. The CoS is the person who remembers that we decided X three weeks ago and tracks whether X actually happened. They close loops. They follow up on action items. They prevent the organizational amnesia that kills scaling companies.

Emanuel's pocket notecard. Baker's paper flow. Fishner's systems. Different mechanisms, same principle: relentless follow-through on what matters.

**4. Earned trust without positional authority**

The CoS has no direct reports (or very few). They have no P&L. They have no functional expertise to fall back on. Their only power is trust — trust from the CEO that they're representing the CEO's intent accurately, and trust from the team that they're acting in the company's interest, not their own.

This trust is earned daily through consistency, honesty, and visible service to the mission rather than to personal advancement.

**5. Ego management (their own)**

The best Chiefs of Staff have strong egos that they keep on a very tight leash. You need enough ego to operate at the CEO's level — to push back, to challenge, to hold people accountable. But you need enough humility to remember that the wins belong to others and the credit belongs to the principal.

Baker understood this. His power came from his indispensability, not from his visibility. The moment a CoS starts taking credit or accumulating visible power, the role breaks.

## Part 5: Frameworks and Mental Models

### Principal Enablement, Not Replacement

The single most important mental model: **every action the CoS takes should pass the test "Does this multiply my principal's impact?"**

If the answer is "no, it multiplies my own impact," the CoS is drifting into shadow CEO territory. If the answer is "no, it just keeps the lights on," the CoS is drifting into EA territory. The sweet spot is always leverage — actions that make the principal's time, attention, and decisions more effective.

### Air Traffic Control

Think of the CoS as air traffic control for the CEO's workflow. ATC doesn't fly the planes. ATC doesn't decide where the planes are going. ATC manages the flow — sequencing, spacing, prioritizing, preventing collisions, and ensuring safe landings.

The CEO decides the destinations (strategy). The team flies the planes (execution). The CoS manages the airspace (operating system).

### Systems Over Heroics

Fishner's principle: build repeatable systems rather than solving problems one-off. Every time the CoS personally saves the day, they should ask: "What system would prevent me from needing to save the day next time?"

This is especially relevant at GrowthX, where "master the process first" is a core value. The CoS should be the embodiment of this value — building the operating infrastructure that makes heroics unnecessary.

### CEO Time as Scarce Resource

The CEO's calendar is the company's most constrained resource. Every meeting, every decision, every interaction should be evaluated against this scarcity:

- Is this the highest-leverage use of Marcel's time right now?
- Could someone else handle this with 80% of the quality?
- Is this a decision only Marcel can make, or has he already given the team enough context to decide?

The CoS is the guardian of this resource. Not a gatekeeper who blocks access — a steward who ensures access is earned and productive.

### Work Yourself Out of Jobs

Great Chiefs of Staff work themselves out of specific responsibilities by building processes, hiring the right people, and creating systems. The goal isn't to own things permanently — it's to build the infrastructure and hand it off.

This creates a natural evolution: the CoS picks up the messiest, most ambiguous problems, structures them into repeatable processes, hands them to the right owner, and moves to the next mess.

### The Information Asymmetry Advantage

The CoS sits at the intersection of all information flows. They see what sales is doing, what delivery is struggling with, what product is building, what finance is worried about. No one else in the company — except the CEO — has this cross-functional visibility.

This asymmetry is a tool, not a weapon. Use it to connect dots others can't see, to spot conflicts before they escalate, and to ensure decisions in one function don't create problems in another.

## Part 6: The Operating Rhythm — Cadences, Systems, and Accountability

### The CoS operating cadence

| Cadence | Duration | Purpose | CoS Role |
|---------|----------|---------|----------|
| **Daily** | 15 min | Priority alignment with CEO | Surface what needs attention today |
| **Weekly** | 45-60 min | L10-style leadership review | Prepare scorecard, track Rocks, drive IDS |
| **Bi-weekly** | 30 min | Cross-functional check-in | Identify dependencies, prevent collisions |
| **Monthly** | 60-90 min | Business review | Synthesize performance, surface trends |
| **Quarterly** | Half-day | Strategic review and Rock setting | Facilitate planning, ensure alignment |

### What the CoS prepares for each cadence

**Before the daily sync:**
- Overnight developments that change priorities
- Blockers from yesterday that weren't resolved
- Decisions needed today with deadlines

**Before the weekly L10:**
- Updated scorecard with off-track items flagged
- Rock status from each owner (on track / off track)
- Prioritized issues list for IDS discussion
- Action items from last week with done/not done status

**Before the monthly review:**
- Performance narrative: what happened, why, and what we're doing about it
- Financial summary with variance analysis
- Customer health snapshot
- Pipeline and forecast vs. plan
- Key decisions needed from leadership

**Before the quarterly planning:**
- Retrospective on outgoing quarter: Rock completion rate, scorecard trends, wins, misses
- Strategic context: market changes, competitive moves, customer feedback themes
- Draft Rocks for next quarter based on CEO priorities
- Resource allocation review: where are we over/under-invested?

### Decision documentation system

Every significant decision should be captured:

```
DECISION LOG
Date: [Date]
Decision: [What we decided]
Context: [Why we were deciding]
Alternatives considered: [What else we evaluated]
Rationale: [Why we chose this option]
Owner: [Who's accountable for execution]
Expected outcome: [What success looks like]
Review date: [When we check if it worked]
```

The CoS maintains this log. Not because it's bureaucratic — because organizational memory prevents relitigating decisions and enables learning from outcomes.

## Part 7: The GrowthX Context — What a CoS Means Here

### Why GrowthX specifically needs this role

GrowthX is in a particular moment that makes the CoS role critical:

**1. Scaling velocity:** Approaching $7M ARR in under 12 months, with a forecast to $22M+. At this pace, the organizational systems that worked at $3M break at $7M and collapse at $15M. Someone needs to be rebuilding the plane while it's flying.

**2. Hybrid complexity:** GrowthX runs services (60+ delivery people) and product (CheckThat) simultaneously. These businesses have different rhythms, different metrics, different cultures, and different economics. The CEO is the primary bridge between them. A CoS can distribute that bridging function.

**3. Founder-dependent knowledge:** Marcel has said "clients are buying Marcel." His pattern recognition, strategic frameworks, and client relationships are the company's most valuable — and least scalable — asset. The CoS's job is to codify what Marcel does instinctively so the organization can do it systematically.

**4. AI-native ambition:** GrowthX is building an AI-native company where AI is embedded in everything. The CoS should model this — using AI to enhance their own productivity and building AI-augmented systems for the operating rhythm.

### What the CoS at GrowthX specifically owns

Based on GrowthX's context, values, and Marcel's operating style:

**Strategic alignment:**
- Ensure every team's priorities connect to the company's top 3 goals
- Flag when work is diverging from strategy
- Facilitate quarterly Rock-setting and ensure cross-functional alignment

**Information flow:**
- Marcel prefers written context before meetings. The CoS ensures this happens.
- Synthesize cross-functional updates into a format Marcel can consume quickly
- Build dashboards and reporting that give Marcel "the picture" without requiring a dozen conversations

**Execution tracking:**
- Own the scorecard. Know every number. Flag anomalies before they become crises.
- Track Rock completion and escalate off-track items early
- Close loops on decisions — ensure what we decided actually happens

**Cross-functional coordination:**
- Bridge delivery and product teams
- Ensure sales isn't selling things delivery can't deliver
- Connect customer feedback from delivery to product roadmap decisions

**Meeting effectiveness:**
- Prepare agendas and pre-reads for leadership meetings
- Drive L10 structure and IDS discussions
- Capture and distribute decisions and action items
- Follow up on action items between meetings

**The "no man's land" projects:**
- Initiatives too important to ignore but that don't fit neatly into any function
- Building new systems (compensation frameworks, planning processes, communication cadences)
- One-time strategic projects (board prep, fundraising support, organizational design)

### How to work with Marcel as his CoS

Drawing directly from Marcel's user manual:

**Do:**
- Send written context before every meeting
- Come with recommendations, not open questions — Marcel edits faster than he builds from scratch
- Know the numbers cold. If Marcel asks about the scorecard, you should have the answer without looking it up.
- Escalate early and specifically. "I need a decision by Thursday on X" not "I'm kind of blocked."
- Filter information for quality, not for comfort. Marcel wants the truth, even when it's hard.
- Protect his family time (4:30-7:30pm). Don't schedule calls in this window.

**Don't:**
- Assume his silence means approval — follow up
- Bring him problems you haven't thought through
- Say "it'll be fine" without confirmation
- Add to the chaos during stress — bring structure, facts, and options
- Try to own things permanently — build the system and hand it off

**When Marcel is stressed:**
His stress manifests as catastrophic extrapolation — projecting current problems to worst-case scenarios. This is him processing urgency out loud. The CoS's job is not to dismiss the concern or match the intensity, but to bring facts, options, and structure. "Here's what's actually happening. Here are our options. Here's what I recommend."

## Part 8: Common Failure Modes — How Chiefs of Staff Go Wrong

### Failure Mode 1: The Gatekeeper

The CoS starts controlling access to the CEO instead of managing information flow. People can't get decisions made. The CoS becomes a bottleneck rather than an accelerant.

**The fix:** Default to transparency. The CoS's job is to ensure the CEO gets the right information at the right time — not to decide who gets to talk to the CEO.

### Failure Mode 2: The Shadow CEO

The CoS starts making decisions the CEO should make, or is perceived as speaking for the CEO without authorization. The team doesn't know whether directives come from the CEO or the CoS.

**The fix:** Be explicit about authority. "Marcel asked me to..." vs "I think we should..." The CoS should amplify the CEO's decisions, not make their own.

### Failure Mode 3: The Super EA

The CoS gets pulled into calendar management, travel booking, and administrative tasks. These are important, but they're not the CoS role. The CoS should be working on leverage — systems, strategy, and cross-functional alignment.

**The fix:** Clear role definition from day one. If the company needs an EA, hire an EA.

### Failure Mode 4: The Permanent Owner

The CoS takes on a function or project and never lets go. They become a mini-COO, owning operational areas indefinitely instead of building systems and handing off.

**The fix:** Every responsibility the CoS picks up should have a transition plan. "I'll own this for 90 days, build the process, and hand it to [person/team]."

### Failure Mode 5: The Politician

The CoS uses their proximity to the CEO to accumulate political capital — playing favorites, withholding information selectively, or positioning themselves as the "real power."

**The fix:** Radical honesty. The CoS must be a trusted broker — presenting all sides, not just their preferred side. The moment the team suspects the CoS has an agenda, the role's effectiveness collapses.

### Failure Mode 6: The Yes-Person

The CoS agrees with everything the CEO says to maintain the relationship. They stop challenging, stop questioning, and become an echo chamber.

**The fix:** The CoS must be the person who tells the CEO what they need to hear, not what they want to hear. Marcel explicitly values this: "Tell me if you disagree, but bring data."

## Part 9: The Assessment — Is Someone Right for This Role?

### Non-negotiable traits

These aren't nice-to-haves. Without them, the role fails:

**Extreme ownership:** The CoS can't wait for permission, can't blame others, and can't hide behind ambiguity. If something falls through the cracks, the CoS owns it — even if it wasn't technically their responsibility.

**Written communication excellence:** GrowthX values "communicate with clarity" as a core principle. The CoS writes constantly — memos, decision logs, meeting notes, strategy documents. If they can't write clearly and concisely, they can't do the job.

**Emotional regulation under pressure:** The CoS absorbs stress from the CEO and from the organization. They can't be the person who amplifies anxiety. They need to stay calm, stay factual, and stay solution-oriented when things get chaotic.

**Intellectual curiosity without ego:** The CoS must be genuinely interested in every part of the business — finance, product, delivery, sales, people — without needing to be the expert. They ask questions to learn, not to show off.

**Discretion:** The CoS hears everything. They know the financials, the personnel issues, the strategic concerns, the board dynamics. This information is a trust. Leaking it — even casually — is a fireable offense.

### Strong indicators

| Signal | Why It Matters |
|--------|---------------|
| Track record of building systems | Shows the "systems over heroics" mindset |
| Cross-functional experience | Able to translate between domains |
| Experience managing up | Can work productively with a founder who's intense, direct, and data-driven |
| Comfort with ambiguity | The CoS role is inherently undefined and constantly evolving |
| Previous startup or scaling experience | Understands the chaos and pace of a company growing 3x |
| Knows when to ask vs. when to decide | Calibrated judgment on when to involve the CEO |

### Red flags

| Signal | Why It's Dangerous |
|--------|-------------------|
| Needs clear job description to function | The role is defined by the gaps, and gaps shift |
| Wants credit and visibility | The CoS role is backstage by design |
| Uncomfortable pushing back on authority | Will become a yes-person |
| Prefers depth over breadth | The role requires constant context switching across domains |
| "I'll figure it out" without follow-through | This role is 90% follow-through |
| Can't write clearly and quickly | Bottleneck on the most critical CoS output |

## Part 10: Quick Reference and Recommended Sources

### The CoS operating cheat sheet

**Every morning, ask:**
- What are the CEO's top 3 priorities today?
- What decisions are blocked waiting on the CEO?
- What information does the CEO need that they don't have?

**Every week, ask:**
- Are Rocks on track?
- What's the scorecard saying? What's off and why?
- What fell through the cracks this week?
- What's coming next week that needs preparation?

**Every month, ask:**
- Are we still working on the right things?
- What patterns am I seeing across functions?
- What systemic issues keep recurring?
- What does the CEO not see that they should?

**Every quarter, ask:**
- Did we accomplish what we set out to accomplish?
- What did we learn?
- What should the next 90-day priorities be?
- What systems need to be built or rebuilt?

### The "great CoS" test

A Chief of Staff is doing their job well when:
- The CEO has more focused time for high-leverage work
- Decisions are made faster and with better information
- Cross-functional friction is decreasing
- Action items actually get completed
- The team feels aligned on priorities
- Problems get surfaced before they become crises
- The CEO says "I don't know how I operated without this role"

## Summary

The Chief of Staff is a leverage role. Not a title upgrade. Not a stepping stone. Not a holding pattern.

The best Chiefs of Staff share five qualities:
1. **They think in systems.** Every problem solved once should never need solving again.
2. **They serve the mission, not themselves.** The credit goes to others. The wins belong to the team.
3. **They close loops.** In a world of ideas and good intentions, they're the person who makes sure things actually happen.
4. **They tell the truth.** Especially when it's uncomfortable. The CEO needs one person who will never sugarcoat.
5. **They evolve.** The role changes as the company changes. What mattered in month 3 isn't what matters in month 18.

At GrowthX, the CoS has a specific opportunity: to be the person who translates Marcel's systems thinking into organizational infrastructure. To take a founder who thinks in feedback loops, compounding value, and first principles — and build the operating system that lets those instincts scale beyond one person.

The question isn't whether GrowthX will need this. It's whether the person in the role will be a force multiplier or a bottleneck.

Baker chose "of staff." Fishner chose "systems." Johnson chose "principles." The best CoS at GrowthX will choose all three.
