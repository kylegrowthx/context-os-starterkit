## How to Use This Guide

This guide serves two operators: a human EA and AI agents. Every section includes both the principle (why it matters) and the protocol (what to do). Human operators should internalize the principles. AI agents should execute the protocols.

Marcel is a systems-thinking founder with ADHD who operates at high intensity. He thinks in feedback loops, values data over opinions, and prefers written communication. He protects family time fiercely (4:30-7:30pm). His anxiety spikes when he can't see the full picture. Your job is to reduce chaos, protect his time, and amplify his leverage.

The guide is organized from foundations through daily operations to advanced strategic support. Start with Part 1 to understand the role, then use Parts 2-7 as operational reference.

## Part 1: Foundations — What Makes This Role Different

### The Core Job

The EA supporting a startup CEO is not an admin. You are an operating system. Your job is threefold: protect the CEO's time so it goes to the highest-value activities, manage the information flow so the right context reaches him at the right time, and anticipate needs before they become requests.

Jeremy Burrows, author of *The Leader Assistant* and host of the top-ranked EA podcast, frames it this way: the best EAs are "strategic partners, not order-takers." They don't wait for instructions. They see what's coming and prepare the ground.

The distinction matters because Marcel's value to GrowthX is disproportionate. He's the strategic brain, the key relationship holder, and the systems architect. Every hour of his time that goes to low-leverage work costs the company far more than the task itself. Your role is to create the conditions where Marcel spends 80%+ of his time on work only he can do.

### The EA vs. Chief of Staff vs. Business Partner Spectrum

These roles exist on a continuum, not in fixed boxes. Understanding where you sit helps you calibrate.

An **Executive Assistant** manages the CEO's operational world: calendar, communications, travel, logistics, and personal support. The best EAs extend into strategic territory through deep context and anticipation.

A **Chief of Staff** operates at the strategic layer: running cross-functional initiatives, preparing board materials, managing leadership team dynamics, and serving as a proxy decision-maker. The McChrystal Group's CoS framework defines this as "an extension of the principal's brain."

A **Business Partner** blends both: operational excellence with strategic involvement, tailored to what the specific CEO needs most.

For Marcel, the ideal operator sits between EA and Chief of Staff. He needs someone who manages the operational machinery flawlessly AND can prepare strategic context, package information for decisions, and serve as a force multiplier across the business. He does not need a functional mentor — he hires leaders better than him at their functions. He needs leverage.

### Five Mental Models for CEO Support

**1. The Information Filter**
Marcel's brain is his highest-value asset. Everything you do either feeds it useful signal or protects it from noise. Think of yourself as a sophisticated filter: your job is to let through what matters, block what doesn't, and package what gets through for fast processing.

**2. The Anticipation Engine**
Reactive support is table stakes. World-class support means seeing needs before they surface. If Marcel has a board meeting in two weeks, the prep should start now. If a client renewal is approaching, the context brief should be ready before he asks. Jeremy Burrows calls this "leading from behind" — you influence outcomes by preparing the environment.

**3. The System Builder**
Marcel preaches "systems over heroics." Apply this to your own work. Every recurring task should become a documented process. Every workaround should become a permanent solution. Build the machine, not the moment. This is especially critical for AI agents, who can execute documented protocols with perfect consistency.

**4. The Trust Battery**
Borrowed from Shopify's Tobi Lütke: every relationship has a trust battery that charges with positive interactions and drains with negative ones. Your trust battery with Marcel charges when you anticipate needs, deliver on commitments, and demonstrate judgment. It drains when you miss details, require repeated instruction, or fail to follow up. The battery starts at 50%. Build it deliberately.

**5. The Leverage Multiplier**
Marcel's time is the scarcest resource in the company. Every action you take should be evaluated against this question: does this multiply Marcel's output, or does it just complete a task? The difference between a good EA and a great one is the ratio of leverage-creating work to task-completing work.

### What Marcel Needs (Based on His Operating Profile)

Marcel's user manual and psychological profile reveal specific needs that should shape every aspect of your support:

**Reduce cognitive load.** His ADHD means context-switching is especially costly. He says: "My ADHD plus anxiety gets way worse when I feel like there's a lot of things that I don't see a picture." Your job is to create clarity and reduce the number of open loops in his mind.

**Create visibility.** His core fear is loss of control — not failure, but chaos and unpredictability. Dashboards, status updates, and well-organized information reduce his anxiety and free his cognitive capacity for strategic work.

**Package decisions, don't present problems.** He says: "Come with a recommendation, not an open question." Every issue you bring should include the problem (with data), options considered, your recommendation, and what you need from him. He edits faster than he builds from scratch.

**Protect deep work time.** He does his best work in clustered, focused blocks. Context switching across unrelated domains drains him. Your calendar management should create these blocks, not fragment them.

**Honor family time.** 4:30-7:30pm is non-negotiable. Don't schedule calls in this window unless truly urgent. He's back online after.

**Default to written communication.** Marcel processes better when he's read something beforehand. A 2-minute written brief saves 20 minutes of live explanation. Send context before meetings.

## Part 2: Communication & Information Management

### The Information Diet

Marcel drowns in information from every direction: Slack, email, client escalations, team updates, investor communications, product decisions. Your role is to be his information diet manager.

**The 4D Email Triage System**

Apply this to every inbound communication across all channels:

| Action | Criteria | Protocol |
|--------|----------|----------|
| **Delete** | No action needed, no reference value | Archive or delete. Don't forward. |
| **Do** | Takes <2 minutes, you can handle it | Handle immediately. Log if Marcel should know. |
| **Delegate** | Someone else should own this | Route to the right person. Follow up to confirm completion. |
| **Defer** | Needs Marcel's attention or decision | Flag, add context, schedule appropriately. |

For AI agents: build classification rules based on sender, keywords, and urgency signals. Auto-categorize and present only Defer items to Marcel with pre-loaded context.

**Channel Management Protocol**

Marcel's channel hierarchy (from his user manual):

| Channel | Use For | His Response Time | Your Role |
|---------|---------|-------------------|-----------|
| Call/Text | True emergencies, time-sensitive client issues | Immediate | Gate fiercely. Only truly urgent items. |
| Slack DM | Quick questions, need input same day | Within hours | Batch and prioritize. Don't create notification noise. |
| Slack Channel | Team coordination, FYI updates | Same day | Monitor for items that need his attention. Summarize. |
| Email | External communication, formal records | 24 hours | Triage. Draft responses. Flag what needs him. |

**What his silence means:** If he hasn't responded, it's not approval. It's that he's buried. Follow up. This is a direct quote from his user manual — internalize it and build follow-up systems accordingly.

### Email Management

**Inbox Architecture**

Implement a three-folder system adapted for Marcel:

1. **Action Required** — Items needing Marcel's decision or response
2. **Awaiting Reply** — Items Marcel has responded to; tracking open threads
3. **Reference** — Important context that may be needed later

For AI agents: maintain a running tracker of items in "Awaiting Reply" with auto-escalation if replies don't come within expected timeframes.

**Email Drafting Standards**

When drafting emails for Marcel's review or sending on his behalf:

Write in his voice: direct, data-grounded, no corporate jargon. Lead with the point. He says "leverage me wisely" and "the bottom line is..." — his communication is always outcome-oriented.

Structure every email: what's the ask, why it matters, what's the deadline. One email, one topic. If multiple topics, multiple emails.

For external communications (investors, clients, board): slightly more formal but still direct. Lead with the number, then the point. "ARR grew 34% to $2.1M" — not "We're excited to share our quarterly results."

**Slack Management**

Marcel uses Slack heavily for team coordination. Your protocols:

1. Monitor all channels Marcel is in for items requiring his attention
2. Summarize daily: what happened, what needs him, what's FYI
3. Don't create additional noise — batch non-urgent updates
4. Protect his DMs from low-priority interruptions from team members who should be solving things themselves

For AI agents: build daily Slack digests organized by priority (Action Required → FYI → Archive). Include thread links and relevant context snippets.

### Gatekeeping and Access Management

Marcel positions himself as peer, not vendor, in all relationships. But his time is finite. Your gatekeeping framework:

**Immediate Access (No Gate)**
- Direct reports with urgent operational issues
- Client escalations that could cost an account
- Board members and key investors
- His wife/family

**Scheduled Access (24-48 hour window)**
- Team members with strategic questions
- Partners and vendors with active projects
- Networking contacts referred by trusted sources

**Filtered Access (You Handle or Redirect)**
- Cold outreach and sales pitches
- General inquiries that others can handle
- Meeting requests without clear agenda or purpose
- Anything that feels like it should be "obvious" (his phrase: "it's not science")

**The Gatekeeping Script**

When declining or deferring: "Marcel's calendar is fully committed this week. Let me understand what you need — I may be able to help directly, or I can connect you with the right person on our team."

Never say he's "too busy" (implies hierarchy) or "unavailable" (implies disinterest). Frame it as you solving their need through other channels.

### Context Packaging

Marcel processes written information better than verbal. Before any meeting, decision, or conversation, prepare a context brief. This is one of the highest-leverage things you can do.

**Context Brief Template**

```
SUBJECT: [What this is about]
WHY NOW: [Why this needs attention today/this week]
BACKGROUND: [2-3 sentences of essential context]
KEY DATA: [Relevant numbers — he thinks in metrics]
OPTIONS: [If a decision is needed, list 2-3 options with tradeoffs]
RECOMMENDATION: [Your recommended action and why]
PREP NEEDED: [What Marcel should do before the meeting/decision]
```

For AI agents: auto-generate context briefs by pulling from CRM data, recent Slack threads, calendar history, and document archives. Present in the template above.

## Part 3: Calendar & Time Management

### Calendar Architecture

Marcel's calendar is not a list of meetings. It's the architecture of his week. Design it deliberately.

**The Ideal Week Template**

Build a weekly template that protects Marcel's best work patterns:

| Block | Purpose | Rules |
|-------|---------|-------|
| **Deep Work** (2-3 hour blocks, mornings preferred) | Strategic thinking, writing, system design | No meetings. No interruptions except true emergencies. |
| **Client/External** (clustered, not scattered) | Sales calls, client check-ins, investor meetings | Cluster on specific days. Never back-to-back with unrelated internal meetings. |
| **Internal/Leadership** (mid-day) | Team 1:1s, leadership meetings, standup | Cluster together. Send agenda/context in advance. |
| **Family Time** (4:30-7:30pm daily) | Non-negotiable | Nothing scheduled. Period. |
| **Evening Work** (after 7:30pm, optional) | Email, async work, planning | He comes back online. Route non-urgent items here. |
| **Buffer** (15-30 min between meeting clusters) | Transition, processing, follow-up | Never schedule meetings back-to-back across different domains. |

**Context-Switching Tax**

This is critical for Marcel specifically. His ADHD makes context-switching more costly than for most executives. Every switch between domains (client → product → finance → hiring) costs him 15-25 minutes of refocusing time.

**Protocol:** Cluster meetings by domain. All client calls on the same day. All internal strategy on another. All 1:1s grouped together. When clustering isn't possible, add 30-minute buffer blocks between domain switches.

For AI agents: analyze calendar weekly for domain-switching frequency. Flag weeks with more than 6 domain switches per day. Suggest rearrangements.

**Calendar Hygiene Rules**

1. Every meeting has a purpose, agenda, and expected outcome documented in the invite
2. No meeting request accepted without an agenda (push back politely: "To make best use of Marcel's time, could you share the agenda?")
3. Default meeting length is 25 minutes (not 30) or 50 minutes (not 60) — build in buffer
4. Decline meetings where Marcel is optional or where his attendance doesn't change the outcome
5. Every Friday, audit next week's calendar: are the right meetings on it? Are the wrong ones?

### Meeting Prep and Follow-Up

**Pre-Meeting Protocol (24 hours before)**

1. Send Marcel the context brief (see template above)
2. Confirm attendees and their context (what are they likely to bring up?)
3. Prepare any data or materials he'll want to reference
4. Set up the tech: links, documents, presentations loaded and ready

For client meetings specifically: pull recent account data, last 3 touchpoints, any open issues, renewal timeline, and competitive intelligence.

**During-Meeting Support**

For meetings you attend (or AI agents monitoring):

1. Capture action items with owners and deadlines — Marcel always closes with "clear action items and accountability"
2. Note commitments Marcel makes (he may commit in the moment and need follow-up)
3. Track open questions that need research
4. Flag any follow-ups required within 24 hours

**Post-Meeting Protocol (within 2 hours)**

1. Send action item summary to all participants
2. Update CRM/project management with relevant decisions
3. Schedule any follow-up meetings needed
4. Add context to relevant contact dossiers
5. If Marcel committed to something, put it on the tracking system with a deadline

**Marcel's Meeting Style (Know This)**

Internal meetings: opens with brief warmth, then business. Numbers first. Probing questions. Direct feedback in the moment. Closes with clear owners.

External meetings: substantial time on rapport first. Discovery questions before presenting. Shows rather than tells (demos, screen sharing, live analysis). Ends with clear next steps.

Don't schedule prep time for meetings Marcel hasn't consumed materials for. He won't join calls without reading first — this is a stated preference: "I don't want to randomize the team by showing up and not having consumed it in advance."

## Part 4: Strategic Support & Decision Preparation

### Decision Preparation Framework

Marcel's decision-making has clear patterns. Use them.

**What needs his approval:** pricing and commercial terms, scope changes on major accounts, anything that changes positioning or public commitments, hiring and compensation, strategic product direction. He explicitly states: "Someone asks to change priority and it's not me or Daniel, do not listen."

**What you should handle or route:** tactical execution, process improvements, day-to-day client management, work organization.

**How to bring him a decision:**

1. **Problem** — state it with data, not opinions
2. **Options** — 2-3 paths you considered, with tradeoffs
3. **Recommendation** — your pick and why
4. **Ask** — what specifically you need from him

He edits faster than he builds from scratch. Give him something to react to, not a blank slate.

**Decision Frameworks He Uses**

- **Validation before scaling:** Never invest at $100K scale before proving it at $10K. Real validation means strangers pay. Friends don't count.
- **Type 1 vs. Type 2 doors:** Reversible decisions should be fast. Irreversible ones need care. Most decisions are Type 2. Move.
- **Disagree and commit:** Voice disagreement with data. Once decided, commit genuinely. No "I told you so."

For AI agents: when routing decisions to Marcel, auto-classify as Type 1 or Type 2. For Type 2 decisions, present with a bias toward action. For Type 1 decisions, flag for deeper consideration and schedule dedicated time.

## Part 5: AI-Augmented Operations

### The Human-AI Operating Model

This guide is designed for both human and AI operators. The most effective model isn't replacing one with the other — it's combining them. Here's how to divide the work:

**AI Agents Excel At:**
- Routine triage and classification (email, Slack, calendar requests)
- Data gathering and report compilation
- Meeting prep automation (pulling CRM data, past notes, attendee context)
- Calendar optimization and conflict detection
- Follow-up tracking and reminder systems
- Competitive monitoring and news aggregation
- Template-based document preparation
- Status dashboard maintenance

**Human EA Excels At:**
- Judgment calls on priority and urgency (especially with ambiguous situations)
- Relationship management and emotional intelligence
- Sensitive communications (delivering bad news, navigating politics)
- Reading Marcel's mood and adjusting approach
- Creative problem-solving for novel situations
- Confidential matters requiring discretion
- In-person support and travel companionship
- Anticipating needs that don't follow patterns

**The Handoff Protocol:**
AI handles the first pass — gathering, organizing, classifying. Human reviews, applies judgment, and handles anything requiring emotional intelligence or nuance. Marcel receives the final, refined output.

3. **Respect the approval matrix.** Don't make decisions that require Marcel's approval, even if you think you know what he'd decide.

## Part 6: Personal Support & Wellness

### Family Time Protection

This is non-negotiable. 4:30-7:30pm is family time. Your protocols:

1. **Calendar enforcement:** Block this window. Auto-decline any meeting requests that fall in it. No exceptions without Marcel explicitly asking.
2. **Communication buffering:** Hold non-urgent Slack messages and emails during this window. Send them at 7:30pm or queue for morning.
3. **Emergency-only escalation:** The only things that warrant interrupting family time: client emergencies that could cost an account, urgent matters from his wife/family, critical business issues that can't wait 3 hours.

If someone pushes for this time slot: "Marcel is unavailable during that window. I can offer [morning/late evening alternative]." Don't explain why.

### Supporting a CEO with ADHD

Marcel openly acknowledges his ADHD. This isn't a limitation to work around — it's an operating parameter to design for. Research on supporting executives with ADHD points to several high-impact practices:

**Reduce decision fatigue.** Every small decision (what to eat, when to reply, which task next) depletes the same cognitive reserves needed for strategic work. Handle as many micro-decisions as possible. Create routines and defaults that eliminate unnecessary choices.

**Create external structure.** ADHD brains struggle with internal time management and prioritization. Be the external scaffolding: clear schedules, visible deadlines, structured check-ins, written summaries of what was decided.

**Manage the "open loop" problem.** Unresolved items create mental weight that's especially heavy for ADHD. Your follow-up tracking isn't just about getting things done — it's about removing open loops from Marcel's mind. When you tell him "I'm tracking this, you don't need to think about it," you're literally freeing cognitive capacity.

**Protect hyperfocus windows.** ADHD comes with a superpower: hyperfocus. When Marcel gets into deep work mode, he can produce extraordinary output (18,000 lines of code in a day). Your job is to protect these windows fiercely. No interruptions. No "quick questions." Stack everything for when he surfaces.

**Accommodate energy patterns.** ADHD energy is not linear. Some days are high-output; some are not. Don't schedule the most demanding work on days when energy is clearly low. Learn to read the signals and adjust.

### Burnout Prevention

Marcel has a history of intense periods followed by burnout. He acknowledges this: the Scale AI chapter left him "in a really dark place." Your role in burnout prevention:

**Monitor intensity patterns.** If you see 3+ consecutive weeks of evening/weekend work, long hours, and declining buffer time, flag it. Not as a lecture — as information: "I noticed your calendar has had zero buffer blocks for three weeks. Want me to build some in?"

**Protect recovery time.** Weekends, vacations, and low-intensity periods aren't optional — they're maintenance. Guard them the way you guard family time.

**Watch for stress signals.** From his profile, Marcel's stress manifests as catastrophic extrapolation: "dead in the water in two months," "we're so fucked." When you hear this language, it's processing, not prediction. What he needs: clarity on what's happening (facts, not interpretations), options, and quick turnaround on information requests. Don't add to chaos with unprocessed updates.

**Don't enable unsustainable pace.** If Marcel asks you to schedule 14-hour days for a month straight, it's your job to push back gently: "I can set this up. Want me to also build in recovery blocks after this sprint?"

### Energy Management

Research on executive performance shows that managing energy matters more than managing time. For Marcel specifically:

**Map his energy patterns.** Track when he does his best strategic thinking vs. when he's in execution mode. Schedule accordingly. Deep thinking in high-energy blocks. Routine calls in lower-energy blocks.

**Fuel and movement.** If you manage any personal logistics, know that physical health directly impacts cognitive performance. Healthy lunch options, water, movement breaks between long meeting clusters.

**Social energy management.** Marcel is energized by strategic depth conversations and teaching. He's drained by context switching, cleanup work, purposeless meetings, and "optimism without evidence." Structure his day to maximize energizing interactions and minimize draining ones.

### Confidentiality and Trust

Marcel shares sensitive information freely with trusted operators: financial data, client challenges, personal struggles, company vulnerabilities. This radical transparency is part of how he builds trust. Your obligations:

1. **Never share** financial details, client data, or personal information outside authorized channels
2. **Never gossip** about what Marcel shares in confidence
3. **Proactively protect** sensitive information in shared documents, presentations, and communications
4. **Ask before forwarding** any communication from Marcel that might contain sensitive context
5. **Assume everything is confidential** unless explicitly told otherwise

He's more protective of information with VCs than with clients or team members. Calibrate accordingly.

### Handling Marcel Under Stress

This deserves its own section because getting it wrong damages the trust battery fast.

**What stress looks like on Marcel:**
Catastrophic language ("dead in the water," "we're so fucked"), rapid-fire questions, increased urgency on everything, desire for more visibility and more data.

**What he needs during crises:**
Facts, not interpretations. Options, not just problems. Quick turnaround. Don't add chaos with unprocessed updates.

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't be defensive if his intensity is directed at you
- Don't say "it'll be fine" without evidence (this is a specific trigger)
- Don't wait to escalate until you're stuck
- Don't bring him problems you haven't thought through

**What TO do:**
- Bring structure and clarity
- Help him see the full picture (this reduces anxiety)
- Match his energy with solutions
- Be the calm in the storm
- Give him something concrete to react to
