## How to Use This Guide

Most scheduling advice starts with time. This guide starts with energy. Time is fixed — you get 16 waking hours. Energy is variable — and it's the actual currency of performance.

The research is clear: a well-timed 2-hour block of strategic work during a cognitive peak produces more value than 6 hours of scattered effort during an energy trough. For a CEO with ADHD, this gap is even wider. Your brain has specific windows where it's capable of extraordinary output and other windows where it's fighting its own neurochemistry.

This guide gives you the science, the frameworks, and the practical architecture to build a calendar that works with your biology instead of against it. Read Part 1 to understand the science. Use Parts 2-6 as operating reference. Appendices give you templates, tools, and a reading list.

## Part 1: The Science of CEO Energy

### Why Energy Management Beats Time Management

Tony Schwartz and Jim Loehr's research at the Human Performance Institute (originally built for elite athletes, later adapted for executives) established the foundational insight: **energy, not time, is the fundamental currency of high performance.** Their book *The Power of Full Engagement* documents how corporate leaders who managed energy — not hours — outperformed those who simply worked longer.

The key distinction: time is finite and non-renewable. You can't make more of it. Energy is renewable — it depletes and recovers in predictable cycles. The science of scheduling is really the science of managing these cycles.

This matters more for you than for most CEOs. ADHD brains have less consistent baseline dopamine, which means your energy is more variable, your peaks are higher, your troughs are lower, and the cost of working against your natural rhythms is steeper.

### The 90-Minute Cycle: Your Brain's Operating Rhythm

In the 1950s, sleep researcher Nathaniel Kleitman discovered that human sleep moves through 90-minute cycles. His later research revealed something more consequential: **these 90-minute cycles continue during waking hours.** He called it the Basic Rest-Activity Cycle (BRAC).

Peretz Lavie's research confirmed it through pupillometric studies: within each 90-120 minute cycle, the first 60 minutes show elevated alertness, followed by a natural decline in performance between minutes 75-90. Your brain is literally signaling that it needs a break.

What this means for your calendar: **the fundamental unit of deep work is not the hour — it's the 90-minute cycle.** Schedule focus blocks in 75-90 minute chunks, followed by genuine 15-20 minute recovery breaks. Not "email breaks" — actual recovery: movement, nature, conversation, food.

**The ADHD adjustment:** Research suggests ADHD brains may operate on shorter cycles — closer to 60-75 minutes before attention naturally degrades. Monitor your own patterns. If you find yourself breaking focus consistently at 60 minutes, that's not failure — that's data. Build your blocks around your actual cycle, not the textbook one.

### Chronotype: When Your Brain Peaks

Daniel Pink's research in *When: The Scientific Secrets of Perfect Timing* synthesized decades of chronobiology data. The core finding: your cognitive peak depends on your chronotype (whether you're a lark, owl, or third bird), and the gap between peak and trough performance is enormous.

For most people, analytic thinking peaks in the morning, creative thinking peaks in the late afternoon, and the early-to-mid afternoon is a cognitive trough for virtually everyone.

**The critical ADHD finding:** Research published in *Frontiers in Psychiatry* shows that up to 80% of adults with ADHD have delayed circadian rhythms. Melatonin release is delayed (11pm vs. 9:30pm typical), sleep onset pushes to 1-2am, and morning alertness is genuinely impaired — not from laziness, but from biology. This means **standard "early bird" CEO advice is actively harmful for most ADHD executives.**

Your natural cognitive peak is likely **10:30am-3:00pm** — not 6-8am like standard advice suggests. The implication is significant: schedule your highest-stakes cognitive work (strategic decisions, client presentations, creative problem-solving) in this window. Don't waste it on status update meetings.

### Decision Fatigue: Your Judgment Degrades Predictably

The famous Danziger study of Israeli judges found that favorable decisions dropped from 65% at the start of a session to near 0% by the end — then jumped back to 65% after a break. Columbia Business School research shows executive decision quality degrades approximately 23% after 4pm.

Roy Baumeister's ego depletion research (updated by more recent models emphasizing motivation over willpower) demonstrates that each decision draws from a limited daily pool. The practical implication: **every low-value decision you make (what to eat, which email to answer first, whether to take a meeting) depletes the same cognitive resources you need for the decisions that actually matter.**

This is why Steve Jobs wore the same outfit every day. This is why you need an EA handling micro-decisions. And this is why your highest-stakes decisions should be scheduled before 2pm, never after 4pm.

### Context Switching: The Hidden Tax on ADHD Brains

Gloria Mark's research at UC Irvine quantified what you already feel: the average knowledge worker switches tasks every 3 minutes. Each switch costs approximately **23 minutes** to return to the same level of deep focus. Sophie Leroy's research on "attention residue" showed that even after switching, cognitive fragments of the previous task linger, reducing performance on the new one.

For neurotypical executives, this is bad. For ADHD executives, it's devastating. Research shows that ADHD brains have "rusty on/off switches" — making transitions between tasks abnormally expensive in terms of cognitive energy. Task-switching costs up to 40% of productive time.

**Your user manual confirms this:** "In one single day I'm being hit from loans, legal board, product design, CheckThat launch, growth, marketing, sales, delivery, churn, risk... I just can't operate at all."

The prescription is clear: **minimize transitions.** Fewer, longer blocks of related work. Cluster meetings by domain. Never scatter unrelated meetings across the day.

### The Four Energy Dimensions

Schwartz and Loehr identified four energy dimensions that all need management:

**Physical:** Sleep, exercise, nutrition, hydration. This is the foundation. Without it, nothing else works. Matthew Walker's sleep research is unambiguous: below 7 hours, executive function degrades rapidly. For ADHD brains with already-impaired executive function, sleep debt is catastrophic.

**Emotional:** The quality of your emotional state. Positive emotions expand cognitive capacity (Barbara Fredrickson's broaden-and-build theory). Negative emotions narrow it. Your psychological profile notes catastrophic extrapolation under stress — this is an emotional energy drain that narrows your cognitive field precisely when you need it wide.

**Mental:** Focus, concentration, and the ability to sustain attention. This is where ADHD has the most direct impact. Your mental energy is more variable and more dependent on interest, novelty, and challenge (the ADHD interest-based nervous system).

**Spiritual:** Purpose, meaning, and alignment. You've said "this is my life's work" — that spiritual energy is a genuine performance asset. Purpose-driven work generates its own energy. Purposeless meetings drain it.

The scheduling implication: your calendar should optimize across all four dimensions, not just mental focus. Exercise isn't "nice to have" — it's energy infrastructure. Family time isn't "time off" — it's emotional recovery. Strategic conversations aren't just business — they're what energizes you spiritually.

## Part 2: The Calendar Architecture

### Your Ideal Week Template

Based on the research and your specific operating profile, here's the architecture:

**Design Principles:**
1. Match cognitive demand to energy level
2. Cluster by domain to minimize context switching
3. Protect family time (4:30-7:30pm) as non-negotiable
4. Build recovery into the structure, not around it
5. Create predictability — ADHD brains thrive on external structure

**The Template:**

| Time Block | Purpose | Energy Level | Rules |
|-----------|---------|-------------|-------|
| **7:00-8:30** | Morning runway | Rising | Light processing: read overnight updates, review day's plan, physical movement. NOT deep decisions. |
| **8:30-10:00** | Warm-up block | Building | Medium-demand work: email triage, async responses, prep for deep work. |
| **10:00-12:00** | **Peak Performance Block** | **Peak** | **Deep strategic work, high-stakes decisions, creative problem-solving. NO meetings except mission-critical.** |
| **12:00-1:00** | Midday reset | Recovering | Lunch, movement, social connection. Genuine break, not a working lunch. |
| **1:00-2:30** | Afternoon power block | High | Second-best cognitive window. Strategic meetings, client calls, important 1:1s. |
| **2:30-4:30** | Execution block | Moderate-declining | Meetings clustered by domain, operational syncs, team coordination. |
| **4:30-7:30** | **Family time** | **Protected** | **Non-negotiable. No work except true emergencies.** |
| **7:30-9:00** | Evening processing | Variable | Optional async work: email, Slack catch-up, planning tomorrow. Low-stakes only. |

**The math:** This gives you a 2-hour protected peak block every day. That's 10 hours per week of your best cognitive output — more strategic thinking time than most CEOs get in a month of scattered scheduling.

### Theme Days vs. Task Clustering

Jack Dorsey famously managed Twitter and Square simultaneously by assigning each day a theme: Monday was management, Tuesday was product, Wednesday was marketing, Thursday was partnerships, Friday was culture. The core idea: eliminate within-day context switching by dedicating entire days to a single domain.

For a Series A CEO, pure theme days are often impractical — you can't ignore clients all week until "Client Thursday." But the principle of clustering applies.

**Recommended hybrid approach:**

| Day | Theme | Cluster Focus |
|-----|-------|---------------|
| **Monday** | Planning & Strategy | Weekly planning, strategic work, no external meetings |
| **Tuesday** | Client & Revenue | Client calls, sales meetings, pipeline review |
| **Wednesday** | Team & Internal | 1:1s, leadership sync, product review, hiring |
| **Thursday** | Client & External | Client work, partner meetings, investor touchpoints |
| **Friday** | Review & Deep Work | Weekly review, deep work overflow, learning, planning ahead |

The clustering doesn't need to be absolute — it's a bias, not a law. But even a 70% adherence rate dramatically reduces context switching.

### Dan Sullivan's Focus/Buffer/Free Day System

Dan Sullivan's Strategic Coach framework splits all days into three types:

**Focus Days:** Doing the work that creates the most value. For you: strategic thinking, key client conversations, teaching frameworks, building systems. These are revenue-generating, leverage-creating days.

**Buffer Days:** Preparing for and recovering from Focus Days. EA work, meeting prep, administrative catch-up, planning. For you, much of this should be delegated to your EA/AI agents.

**Free Days:** Complete disengagement from work. No email, no Slack, no "just checking." Genuine recovery.

The research is clear: Free Days aren't optional for sustained performance. Jim Loehr's research on elite athletes showed that recovery is when adaptation happens — training breaks the body down, rest builds it back stronger. The same principle applies to cognitive work. Your burnout after Scale AI is evidence of what happens when Free Days disappear.

**Target:** 2-3 Focus Days per week, 2-3 Buffer Days, 2 Free Days (weekends). Adjust based on the season — fundraising sprints might require more Focus Days, but recovery must follow.

### Paul Graham's Maker vs. Manager Schedule

Paul Graham's influential essay identified the fundamental tension CEOs face: **managers operate on 1-hour blocks (meetings, check-ins, decisions), while makers operate on 4-hour blocks (building, writing, designing).**

You're both. As a Series A CEO, you need manager time (team leadership, client relationships, operational decisions) AND maker time (strategic thinking, content creation, system design). The mistake most CEOs make is letting manager time devour maker time.

**Protocol:** Protect at least one 3-4 hour maker block per week in addition to your daily Peak Performance Block. This is where you do the thinking that only you can do — connecting dots, building frameworks, designing systems. Schedule it like a meeting with your most important client, because it is.

### Buffer Architecture

Research on cognitive transition shows that moving between unrelated domains requires genuine reset time. The standard calendar mistake is scheduling meetings back-to-back with zero transition.

**Buffer rules:**

| Transition Type | Minimum Buffer | What Happens |
|----------------|---------------|--------------|
| Within same domain (e.g., two client calls) | 10 minutes | Quick notes, mental prep |
| Between domains (e.g., client call → product review) | 25-30 minutes | Full cognitive reset: movement, water, review context for next block |
| After high-intensity (e.g., board meeting, crisis) | 45-60 minutes | Genuine recovery: walk, decompress, process |

For AI agents managing Marcel's calendar: **auto-add buffers.** Never schedule meetings in adjacent slots without buffer. Reject meeting requests that would create back-to-back cross-domain meetings.

## Part 3: Meeting Architecture

### The Meeting Audit

Harvard Business School's landmark CEO study (Raffaella Sadun, Michael Porter, Oriana Bandiera — tracking 1,100+ CEOs, 60,000+ hours) found that **CEOs spend 72% of their time in meetings.** Microsoft's study of 31,000 workers found a 192% increase in meeting time since 2020.

The question isn't whether you have too many meetings. You do. The question is which ones to cut.

**The Meeting Audit Framework:**

For every recurring meeting, ask:

1. **What decision or outcome does this meeting produce?** If you can't name one, the meeting probably shouldn't exist.
2. **Could this be async?** Status updates, FYI sharing, and information distribution are almost always better as written updates.
3. **Does this need me specifically?** If someone else could represent you, delegate.
4. **What's the cost?** Your fully-loaded hourly rate plus context-switching tax. If a meeting costs $500+/hour of your time, it better produce $500+/hour of value.

**Likely eliminations for a Series A CEO:**
- Status update meetings (replace with async written updates)
- "Alignment" meetings where no decision is made (replace with a shared doc)
- Meetings where you're an observer, not a contributor (get a summary instead)
- Any meeting without an agenda (reject or ask for one)

Shopify eliminated 322,000 hours of meetings annually using a systematic audit. Asana's No Meeting Wednesdays reduced meeting time by 26%.

### No-Meeting Blocks

The research is consistent: protected no-meeting blocks increase productivity by 30-70%.

**Recommended no-meeting zones:**

1. **Daily:** 10:00am-12:00pm (your Peak Performance Block). Non-negotiable.
2. **Weekly:** Monday mornings (planning) and Friday afternoons (review/deep work).
3. **Monthly:** One full "deep work day" per month — zero meetings, zero interruptions.

For AI agents: block these in the calendar and auto-decline requests. The only override is Marcel explicitly approving a specific meeting in one of these blocks.

### Meeting Length Defaults

Research shows meetings expand to fill their allotted time (Parkinson's Law). Shorter default lengths force efficiency.

**New defaults:**

| Meeting Type | Default Length | Notes |
|-------------|--------------|-------|
| 1:1 with direct report | 25 minutes | Agenda required. They own it. |
| Internal operational | 25 minutes | Standing if possible. |
| Client/external | 50 minutes | Front-load discovery and decisions. |
| Strategic/planning | 90 minutes | Matches one BRAC cycle. Hard stop. |
| All-hands | 30 minutes | Shorter is better for morale. |

The 25-minute default (instead of 30) and 50-minute default (instead of 60) automatically builds in 5-10 minute buffers between meetings.

### Async-First Protocol

Companies like GitLab (2,000+ employees, fully remote), Basecamp, and Doist have proven that most communication doesn't need a meeting.

**The async hierarchy:**

1. **Document** — If it's information sharing, write it down. A 2-minute read replaces a 30-minute meeting.
2. **Slack thread** — If it needs discussion, start a thread. Let people respond on their own time.
3. **Loom/video** — If it needs visual explanation, record a 5-minute video.
4. **Meeting** — Only if real-time interaction is genuinely needed (negotiation, brainstorming, relationship building, complex decision with multiple stakeholders).

**Rule of thumb:** If only one person talks for more than 60% of a meeting, that meeting should have been a document.

### The CEO 1:1 Structure

Your user manual says: "You own the agenda. Bring what you're working on, where you need input, issues or blockers, what you need from me."

Research on CEO 1:1s (from Matt Mochary's coaching practice and *The Great CEO Within*) recommends:

**Structure:**
- 5 minutes: quick personal check-in (builds trust battery)
- 10 minutes: their top 2-3 items (they drive)
- 5 minutes: your items (if any)
- 5 minutes: action items and next steps

**Cadence:** Weekly for direct reports during Series A. Can shift to biweekly as the company matures and trust batteries are charged.

**Key rule:** Never cancel 1:1s. Rescheduling is fine. Canceling signals that the person doesn't matter. This is especially important because your direct reports need to feel they have reliable access to you — it reduces the unscheduled interruptions that kill your focus.

## Part 4: ADHD-Specific Calendar Strategies

### Understanding Your Interest-Based Nervous System

Dr. William Dodson's concept of the ADHD "interest-based nervous system" is foundational. While neurotypical brains activate on importance ("I should do this"), ADHD brains activate on **PINCH: Passion/Play, Interest, Novelty, Competition/Challenge, Hurry/Urgency.**

This is why you can write 18,000 lines of code on a Sunday (high interest + challenge + hyperfocus) but struggle to review a routine report (low novelty, no urgency).

**Calendar implications:**

1. **Front-load boring tasks** into your peak energy window when you have the most executive function to override the interest gap
2. **Add novelty** to recurring tasks — change the location, pair with a coffee, gamify the metric
3. **Create artificial deadlines** for tasks without natural urgency — ADHD brains respond to deadlines because they create the "hurry" component of PINCH
4. **Pair uninteresting tasks with a body double** — the presence of another person (or virtual body double) provides enough external accountability to activate execution

### Protecting Hyperfocus Windows

Research shows 68% of ADHD adults experience frequent hyperfocus episodes — deep, immersive engagement that can last hours and produce extraordinary output. This is your superpower. But it needs protection.

**Hyperfocus patterns to track:**
- Most common trigger: mid-morning (10am-12pm) when medication peaks align with circadian rhythm
- Second window: late evening (after 8pm) when external demands decrease
- Typical frequency: 2-4 productive hyperfocus episodes per week

**Protection protocol:**
1. Track your hyperfocus patterns for 2 weeks — when do they happen? What triggers them?
2. Schedule your Peak Performance Block around your most common hyperfocus window
3. When you're in hyperfocus, **everything else waits.** Your EA should know: if Marcel is deep in something, hold all non-emergencies.
4. Don't schedule anything immediately after a hyperfocus block — you'll need decompression time

**Danger:** Hyperfocus doesn't respect boundaries. Set alarms for hard stops (family time, critical meetings). And protect basic needs — it's easy to skip lunch and water during hyperfocus, which crashes the next energy cycle.

### Transition Rituals

For ADHD brains, the hardest part of any task is usually starting it — not doing it. This is "task inertia," and it's neurological, not motivational. Your dopamine system needs a bridge between tasks.

**Build these transition rituals between calendar blocks:**

1. **Close the previous task:** Close all tabs/apps from the prior task. Write one sentence summarizing where you left off (reduces attention residue).
2. **Physical reset:** Stand up, stretch, drink water, walk to a window. 60-90 seconds of movement.
3. **Contextual prime:** Spend 2 minutes reviewing the brief or agenda for the next block. This "loads" the relevant mental context.
4. **Start trigger:** Have a specific action that signals "go" — opening a specific app, reading the first line of a document, pressing play on focus music.

Total transition time: 5-7 minutes. This is not wasted time — it's the bridge that makes the next block productive instead of scattered.

For AI agents: send Marcel a transition prompt 5 minutes before each block change: "Next up: [Meeting/Task]. Key context: [1-2 lines]. Start by: [specific first action]."

### Medication Timing and Calendar Design

This section provides general framework — specifics depend on Marcel's medication and should be discussed with his prescriber.

**General stimulant medication patterns:**
- Immediate-release: peaks at 1-3 hours, fades at 3-6 hours
- Extended-release: peaks at 2-4 hours, lasts 6-16 hours depending on formulation
- Afternoon booster (if used): covers 2:30-6pm gap

**Calendar alignment principle:** Your highest-demand cognitive work should coincide with peak medication effectiveness. For most extended-release formulations taken at 7-8am, this means **10am-2pm is your pharmaceutical + circadian sweet spot.** This is scientific validation for protecting your Peak Performance Block.

**The afternoon crash:** Between 2-4pm, most ADHD adults experience a natural circadian dip compounded by medication wear-off. This is the worst possible time for high-stakes decisions. Schedule operational meetings, routine tasks, or better yet — a movement break.

### Notification Management

ADHD brains have lower baseline dopamine, which makes notification pings disproportionately rewarding (each ping triggers a small dopamine hit). This creates a genuine neurological pull toward distraction.

**Protocol:**

| Mode | When | What's On | What's Off |
|------|------|-----------|-----------|
| **Deep Focus** | Peak Performance Block | Nothing. Phone on airplane mode. | Everything. |
| **Available** | Operational blocks, meeting clusters | Slack DMs, calendar alerts | Social media, email notifications, channel notifications |
| **Recovery** | Breaks, family time | Phone calls only (emergency) | Everything else |

For AI agents: auto-activate Focus Mode on Marcel's devices during Peak Performance Block. Queue all non-urgent notifications for the next Available window.

### The Wall of Awful and Task Avoidance

ADHD coach Brendan Mahan's "Wall of Awful" concept explains why certain tasks feel impossible to start: accumulated negative experiences (past failures, harsh criticism, overwhelm) build an emotional barrier around specific tasks.

**Calendar strategy for avoidance-prone tasks:**

1. **Never schedule dreaded tasks at the end of the day** when willpower is depleted
2. **Pair with accountability:** Schedule a 5-minute check-in call with your EA before and after the task
3. **Break it down:** A "review Q1 financials" block should be decomposed into "open spreadsheet and look at revenue line" as the first micro-step
4. **Use the 5-minute rule:** Commit to just 5 minutes. Momentum usually carries you further, but even if it doesn't, you've made progress
5. **Reward completion:** Pair dreaded tasks with something you enjoy afterward

## Part 5: The Series A CEO's Time Allocation

### Where Your Time Should Go

The Harvard/London Business School CEO time-use study (tracking 1,100+ CEOs across 60,000 hours) found that the most effective CEOs spent their time intentionally across specific categories. Combined with Series A-specific research from YC, First Round Review, and a16z:

**Recommended Series A allocation:**

| Category | % of Time | Weekly Hours (~50hr week) | What's Included |
|----------|-----------|--------------------------|----------------|
| **Strategy & Product** | 25-30% | 12-15 hrs | Strategic thinking, product direction, competitive analysis, system design |
| **Revenue & Clients** | 20-25% | 10-12 hrs | Sales calls, client relationships, pipeline, renewals |
| **Team & Hiring** | 20-25% | 10-12 hrs | 1:1s, leadership, culture, interviewing |
| **External & Investors** | 10-15% | 5-7 hrs | Board prep, investor updates, partnerships, networking |
| **Admin & Operations** | 5-10% | 3-5 hrs | Should be minimized aggressively |

**The key shift at Series A:** You're moving from "do everything" (seed stage) to "do the highest-leverage things and hire/delegate the rest." Sam Altman's guidance: "The CEO has five jobs: set the vision, raise money, hire and manage the team, build culture and values, and make sure the company doesn't die." Everything else should be delegated.

**Brian Chesky's "Founder Mode"** (the concept Paul Graham wrote about in 2024) argues that Series A CEOs should stay deeply involved in product and culture rather than purely delegating to professional managers. The balance: stay in the details on 2-3 things that matter most, fully delegate everything else.

For GrowthX, the 2-3 things that likely need your hands: strategic client relationships (you're the product), content/positioning strategy (it's your competitive advantage), and CheckThat product direction (it's your strategic bet).

### The Hiring Time Question

Research from First Round Review and YC consistently recommends that Series A CEOs spend **25-40% of their time on hiring.** This feels excessive until you do the math: at 20-50 employees, every hire dramatically shifts the company's capability. A bad hire costs 6-12 months of team velocity.

**Practical approach:** Cluster hiring activities into 2-3 dedicated blocks per week rather than scattering interviews across every day. Review all final candidates personally, but have your team own sourcing and first-round screening.

### The Time Trap Audit

Research identifies these as the most common Series A CEO time traps:

1. **Staying in execution mode.** You built this company by doing the work. Now your job is building the team that does the work. Every hour you spend on tasks someone else could do is an hour stolen from the work only you can do.

2. **Meeting inflation.** Every new hire adds meetings. Without active pruning, you'll be at 80%+ meeting time within 6 months of Series A.

3. **The "helpful CEO" trap.** Being too available means everyone comes to you first. Build escalation paths: team → lead → you. Not team → you.

4. **Perfection on non-critical tasks.** Your quality standards are a strength. But applying $500/hour attention to $50/hour tasks is a misallocation. Decide what needs to be excellent (client work, positioning, key hires) and what needs to be "good enough" (internal processes, admin, formatting).

5. **Investor time creep.** Monthly investor updates are enough between board meetings. Don't let ad hoc investor requests consume your weeks.

## Part 6: Recovery Architecture

### Why Recovery Is Not Optional

Michael Freeman's UCSF research on founder mental health found: 72% of entrepreneurs have mental health concerns (vs. general population baseline), with depression at 30% and ADHD at 29%. A separate survey found 53% of founders experienced burnout in the past year, with 49% considering quitting.

You've lived this. The Scale AI chapter. The burnout. The dark place when personal identity was too tightly coupled to work performance.

The research on sustainable performance is unambiguous: recovery is not the absence of work. It's a specific cognitive and physiological process that requires deliberate design.

### Micro-Recovery: Built Into Every Day

Sabine Sonnentag's research at the University of Mannheim demonstrated that **psychological detachment** (mentally disengaging from work) during off-hours is the strongest predictor of next-day energy and performance. But you don't have to wait until evening. Micro-recovery throughout the day compounds.

**Micro-recovery protocol (build into buffer blocks):**

| Duration | Activity | Energy Impact |
|----------|----------|---------------|
| 2 minutes | Standing, stretching, deep breaths | Physical reset |
| 5 minutes | Walk to window or outside, look at distance | Attention restoration (nature exposure) |
| 10 minutes | Walk (preferably outdoors) | Full physiological reset |
| 20 minutes | Power nap | Complete cognitive refresh (NASA study: 26-minute nap improved performance 34%) |

**The ADHD amplifier:** Research from the University of Illinois (Kuo & Faber Taylor) found that a **20-minute nature walk is as effective as methylphenidate** for temporarily improving concentration in ADHD. This isn't metaphorical — it's attention restoration theory in action. Nature engages "involuntary attention" (fascination), which rests the "directed attention" that your ADHD brain exhausts faster than neurotypical ones.

Schedule a 10-20 minute outdoor walk into your midday reset. It's not a luxury — it's pharmaceutical-grade intervention.

### Sprint/Recovery Cycles

Elite athletes don't train at peak intensity year-round. They periodize: intense training blocks followed by recovery periods where adaptation happens. The same principle applies to cognitive work.

**Quarterly periodization for a Series A CEO:**

| Phase | Duration | Intensity | Focus |
|-------|----------|-----------|-------|
| **Sprint** | 4-6 weeks | High (50-55 hour weeks) | Board prep, fundraising, product launches, hiring pushes |
| **Sustain** | 6-8 weeks | Moderate (45-50 hour weeks) | Normal operations, team development, strategic thinking |
| **Recovery** | 1-2 weeks | Low (35-40 hour weeks) | Reduced meetings, more free time, learning, reflection |

**The annual rhythm:** Plan 3-4 sprint cycles per year (tied to board meetings, product launches, or seasonal patterns). Each sprint should be followed by at least a sustain phase, and the recovery phase should happen at least quarterly.

### Sleep Architecture

Matthew Walker's research is categorical: executive function is the first cognitive ability to degrade with sleep loss. For an ADHD brain that already has executive function challenges, sleep debt is a force multiplier for dysfunction.

**The ADHD-specific sleep challenge:** 75% of ADHD adults report racing thoughts at sleep onset. Circadian delay means natural sleep onset is 1-2am, not 11pm.

**Sleep protocol:**
1. **Non-negotiable 7-hour minimum.** Track it. Below 7 hours, cognitive performance drops measurably.
2. **Consistent sleep/wake times** (even weekends — shifting by more than 1 hour disrupts circadian rhythm).
3. **Wind-down ritual starting 60 minutes before target sleep:** dim lights, no screens or blue-blocking glasses, cool room, no caffeine after 2pm.
4. **Morning light exposure** within 30 minutes of waking (Andrew Huberman's research: 10-15 minutes of bright light advances circadian phase in delayed sleepers).

### Exercise as Energy Infrastructure

Research published in *The Lancet Psychiatry* found that aerobic exercise is the single most effective intervention for improving executive function in ADHD — more effective than any behavioral intervention except medication. A UC Davis study confirmed that fidgeting and movement improve ADHD cognitive performance by activating underactive cerebellar and somatosensory regions.

**Minimum effective dose:** 20-30 minutes of moderate aerobic exercise, 4-5 days per week. Morning exercise is ideal (raises dopamine for the day, advances circadian rhythm, improves morning alertness).

Build it into the calendar as infrastructure, not an optional extra. A 7am workout is not "taking time from work" — it's investing in 10+ hours of better cognitive performance.

### Circuit Breakers

Build automatic rules into your calendar that prevent unsustainable patterns:

1. **Max meeting hours:** No more than 5 hours of meetings per day, 20 per week
2. **Forced buffers:** If 3+ meetings are back-to-back, auto-insert a 30-minute break
3. **Weekly audit:** Every Friday, EA reviews next week's calendar against the ideal week template. Flag deviations.
4. **Intensity monitor:** If 3+ consecutive weeks show evening/weekend work beyond 2 hours, flag for recovery phase
5. **Vacation minimums:** At least one full week off per quarter, plus long weekends monthly

For AI agents: monitor these circuit breakers automatically. Generate weekly reports on adherence. Flag violations before they become patterns.

4. Set up buffer architecture in calendar

1. Read *ADHD 2.0* by Hallowell & Ratey

## Final Note

The goal isn't a perfect calendar. It's a calendar that works with your biology instead of against it.

You have a brain that's capable of extraordinary output when the conditions are right — and that degrades faster than average when they're wrong. Most CEO scheduling advice is designed for neurotypical brains with linear energy patterns. Yours isn't linear. It has peaks that can produce more in 2 hours than most people produce in a week, and troughs where forcing output creates debt you'll pay in burnout.

The architecture in this guide isn't about discipline. It's about design. Design the system, then let the system run. That's how you think about everything else in the business — apply it to your own calendar.

Start with one change: protect the Peak Performance Block. Two hours a day of your best work, in your best window, with zero interruptions. That single change will create more leverage than any meeting you'll ever attend.
