## Summary

This 95-minute podcast episode features Marcel Santilli, CEO of GrowthX, interviewing Kristin Fracchia, Head of Marketing at Gamma, about her unconventional career path, approach to marketing, and the explosive growth of Gamma as an AI-native company. The conversation explores Kristin's journey from academia (PhD in theater and critical theory) through education technology to leading marketing at a 70M+ user company with just 70 employees. Marcel and Kristin dive deep into hiring philosophy—particularly the importance of intellectual curiosity and the ability to think on one's feet rather than lean on frameworks—and debate the role of writing and taste-making in the age of AI. The discussion culminates in a detailed breakdown of Gamma's wildly successful Series B campaign, which included inflatable unicorn office stunts, a 10M-view Rainn Wilson video, Las Vegas Sphere advertising, and an upcoming global world tour across South Korea, London, and São Paulo in March 2026. Throughout, both leaders emphasize the tension between using AI as a learning accelerant versus a thinking shortcut, and how Gamma is expanding its product to support team collaboration and deeper integrations (including a Claude MCP connector).

## Key Topics Discussed

### 1. Kristin's Career Journey: Unlikely Path to Tech

**Context:** Kristin traces a non-linear arc from academia to ed tech to AI-native startups, which Marcel frames as unusual but increasingly valuable in the AI era.

**What was discussed:**
- Kristin started as a high school English teacher at 21, teaching AP Literature to 18-year-olds
- Pursued a PhD in theater and critical theory (7 years total: 2-year master's, 5-year PhD program) with the goal of becoming a college professor
- Discovered academia doesn't value teaching; research is what's prioritized institutionally
- Built a tutoring side business during grad school (SAT, GRE, GMAT prep) for financial survival and practical problem-solving
- Pivoted to ed tech startup Magoosh (SAT/ACT test prep) at age 30, drawn to the mission of scaling education
- Progressed through Magoosh wearing multiple hats: content → product → marketing (solved the user acquisition problem by switching teams)
- Held various roles at high-growth startups, then a public company (~2,000 people), before joining Gamma in 2025
- The timing of joining a public company "about a week before ChatGPT came out" crystallized the need to be at the frontier of AI

**Notable quotes:**
- "Everything not to do" is the way she reflects on her career journey, yet paradoxically "it's somehow worked out to be everything you should do for the current age we're set up in."
- "I would have to do tests where I would have to read three hundred books in the summer. You can't read three hundred books in the summer. This is pre AI, man."
- "I took a job at a public company about a week before ChatGPT came out, and then it felt like the whole world changed."

**Key insight:** Diverse, generalist backgrounds—especially those grounded in learning and intellectual problem-solving—are increasingly valuable in AI-native companies.

### 2. Hiring Philosophy: Intellectual Curiosity Over Frameworks

**Context:** Marcel raises the difficulty of finding truly intellectually curious hires, and Kristin articulates a philosophy centered on thinking on one's feet and embracing ambiguity.

**What was discussed:**
- Most people with great tech résumés exhibit "attachment to how things have been done before" that doesn't fit hyper-growth environments
- Gamma has 70M+ users with ~70 employees—a 1:1M ratio. This requires enormous ownership and huge strategic swings; you can't run small experiments
- Two hiring profiles Kristin targets:
  1. **The "Done with the Old Way"**: Senior talent from larger companies who are fully bought in on a new way of building, often backed up by side projects, AI tinkering, or building personal tools
  2. **The "Transition" Profile**: People from non-tech backgrounds who have solved problems creatively and think on their feet
- Kristin deliberately "throws people off their game" in interviews—changing the entire playing field to see how people react to the unknown
- The "unicorn whiteboard test": One interview involved a prompt like "Unicorns are buying your product" to see if candidates light up with curiosity or crumble under unexpected change
- Many people show up over-prepared and confident, but the best hires admit "I never thought of that" and engage genuinely with new problems
- Confidence in the face of certainty is increasingly dangerous; what matters is comfort with the unknown and genuine enthusiasm for change

**Notable quotes:**
- "I don't think I mean in interviews, but I just throw people off their game like all the time... I just want them to be like, I never thought of that before. Like that is so wild. Like that's crazy. Okay, let me think about it." — Kristin
- "The people that you know I think tend to be really successful, they're like Alright, let's do it. Unicorns. Hmm. Alright. Well, how would I think through that?" — Kristin
- (Marcel) "LLMs already do that. I don't need a person next to me that will do that as well."
- "Part of me doesn't blame I I feel like we've been giving bad interview advice a long time. It's like come in prepared, like be confident, you know, show up. Um and uh it's actually a little bit off putting, as you mentioned, sometimes right now I'm not confident. Like things are gonna change tomorrow." — Kristin

**Key insight:** Interviewing should prize adaptability, intellectual curiosity, and honest engagement with ambiguity—not preparation and false confidence.

### 3. Writing as a Foundation for Clear Thinking

**Context:** Marcel emphasizes that many marketers dismiss writing as unimportant, yet writing is foundational to organizing thought and being effective in the AI era.

**What was discussed:**
- Kristin spent hundreds of hours writing during her PhD—a discipline that taught information synthesis and finding through-lines
- Writing forces you to organize your thinking; "lazy thinking" happens when you skip the writing step
- Marcel discovered that writing is not about being "a great writer" but a tool for personal cognition: "if I write and organize my thoughts, then my thoughts will be better"
- Engineers and marketers who write are dramatically more effective than those who don't
- There's a correlation between writing discipline and job effectiveness across disciplines
- In the age of AI, writing is becoming both more critical (to preserve taste and avoid delegation of thinking) and more difficult (as models get better, it's tempting to just prompt them)
- Kristin has started to worry about her own writing atrophy—models are so good now that copy-pasting is tempting, even if she's intellectually against it
- Marcel highlights a critical distinction: Using AI to accelerate learning vs. using AI to skip learning entirely (the latter produces lazy marketers)

**Notable quotes:**
- (Marcel) "If you write, they're gonna see that I'm not a great writer, you know? But then over time I've gotten more comfortable with just like writing is just my personal tool to organize my thoughts, you know?"
- (Marcel) "If I can write and organize my thoughts, then my thoughts will be better, you know. And and I feel like for for marketers at times, how like I've heard this so many times that people, oh, I'm not good at writing, I don't write. But then it's like, how could you possibly like like you you have lazy thinking if you've never read anything or you're you're not capable of writing anything, you know?"
- (Kristin) "I worry a lot about early career people and like how are they preserving taste."

**Key insight:** Writing is not optional; it's the primary mechanism for developing clear thinking, taste, and creativity in the age of AI.

### 4. Marketing to AI vs. Marketing to Humans

**Context:** Kristin describes a bifurcated marketing challenge: optimizing for LLM discoverability while also creating visceral human experiences.

**What was discussed:**
- Marketing today requires both AI-scale thinking (AEO, marketing to LLMs, AI-native distribution) and human-scale experiences (events, community, taste-making)
- Everyone on Kristin's marketing team spikes or gravitates toward one pole but understands both
- Example: One team member uses AI for everything but maintains a side business in video—a creative discipline that can't be outsourced to LLMs without degradation
- The best marketing leaders are "good enough" in both the technical and creative domains, even if they're not top-tier in either
- Kristin's PhD in humanities (processing information, synthesizing, finding through-lines) paired with her interest in the visceral human experience of theater makes her suited to this moment
- Marcel raises the problem: AI can now generate reasonable-sounding analysis, but without internalization and taste, marketers become mere delegators

**Notable quotes:**
- (Kristin) "Everything about being a good marketing leader right now, I think is like both being able to like see the technical side of things and then see like the art and craft, right? So everything for me, I have been obsessed with the idea of like how do I market to AI and how do I market to humans?"
- (Marcel) "There's two types of people. There are the people that are using AI to accelerate the crap out of their learning and and how they learn. And then the people are using AI just skip so that they don't have to learn."

**Key insight:** The marketing leaders who will thrive combine AI-native thinking with human-centered taste and creativity.

- The product was designed differently from other AI presentation tools: nested cards, expandable, mobile-responsive, interactive—not just a PowerPoint clone
- Core AI advantage: Gamma uses 20+ AI models with sophisticated prompting, generating interactive charts and visualizations that competitors can't match
- Timeline compression:
  - **2020–2023**: Pre-product market fit, building core user base of enthusiasts who preferred Gamma to PowerPoint

**Notable quotes:**

**Key insight:** Being founded at the right moment (problem-first, before AI hype) and building a profitable, product-first culture provides both stability and leverage.

### 6. Marketing Philosophy: Zone Defense, Pick Your Bet

**Context:** Kristin describes her marketing approach as "zone defense" rather than "man-to-man" coverage, leaning hard into Gamma's unique strengths.

**What was discussed:**
- Three zones in modern marketing:
  1. **Brand**: Breaking through the noise, differentiation
  2. **Product Marketing**: Clarity on what you do and why it matters
  3. **Growth**: User acquisition, reach, scale
- Gamma was a "team of designers and dreamers" when Kristin joined; they had just done a rebrand (visually distinctive)
- Rather than hire traditional marketers, Kristin leaned into existing strengths: creative director, video marketer, and the brand itself
- Out-of-home/sphere advertising emerged from leveraging the brand advantage, not from prior experience
- Key principle: Pick one thing to be top 1% at; you can't be good at everything right now
- Many job candidates claim they'll do "all the strategies and all the channels" but should instead pick their spike and scale it
- Gamma does 300–350 creators/influencers every month as an always-on machine; product launches dial this up
- The Series B campaign was built entirely from existing playbooks, scaled for the moment
- Lesson: Build depth in growth and product marketing where the foundation was weaker; don't build breadth everywhere

**Notable quotes:**
- "Zone defense vs man-to-man defense. Your areas, your virtuals are like brand. Uh so are you breaking to the noise? Uh product like marketing, like do you know how to like can people understand like what you do? And then like growth, right? Like do you know how to reach people?"
- "There's like fifteen to twenty AI startups founded a day or something right now in the world... There's so much noise."
- "Pick your one thing. Like that is going to you're not eighty ninety percent of your impact is gonna come from like the one thing that only you can do really well right now."

**Key insight:** Effective marketing in high-noise environments requires brutal focus on competitive advantages, not broad execution.

### 7. Series B Campaign: Four Bets in Three Weeks

**Context:** Gamma announced a Series B in fall 2025, just three weeks after a major product launch (Gamma 3.0). Kristin reveals how they created a memorable announcement amid the noise of constant startup fundraising announcements.

**What was discussed:**

#### The Setup
- Three weeks to plan after the Gamma 3.0 launch
- Key insight: Can't create a truly good moment in three weeks unless built on always-on work
- Gamma had spent an entire year refining playbooks: influencer partnerships, community activation, video content, experiential events
- The Series B brainstorm categorized ideas as "sure wins" vs. "reach for the moon" bets
- Four major bets emerged, ranging from easy to ambitious

#### Bet 1: Employee Lens-Signing Video
- Original idea: Camera lens signing moment with all employees
- Deferred to team's creative expertise; wasn't the direction they were most excited about
- Pivoted into something better (Bet 2)

#### Bet 2: Inflatable Unicorn Costumes (Office Takeover)
- Entire office (~30 people in-office) dressed in inflatable unicorn costumes
- Founders brought to the roof for photo opportunity; unicorns "taking over" the office
- Short-form video content: unicorns making coffee, working at desks, having meetings, using Gamma
- One of the most successful videos on LinkedIn of all time
- Amusing sidebar: They almost tried to bring an actual horse into the office; building management nixed it ("you can't bring a horse through an elevator")

#### Bet 3: Rainn Wilson Celebrity Video
- Cold-emailed Rainn Wilson's agent with a Gamma pitch
- Didn't expect it to go anywhere
- "Rainn Wilson felt like the right celebrity for us to be working with" (connection to "The Office")
- Unexpected lesson: Used ChatGPT to draft a contract with talent agent on an evening—normally would hire a brand or talent agency
- Negotiated with SAG-AFTRA union official representation
- Filmed video: Made Rainn Wilson "CEO of Gamma"
- Result: 10 million views; proved resourcefulness and hustle can replace traditional agency infrastructure
- Marcel's takeaway: "There's like a moment where I was like, I can do things with like my own brain and like AI right now."

#### Bet 4: Las Vegas Sphere Advertising
- Brought up in "every single brainstorm" but dismissed as too expensive
- CEO Grant is "chief enabling officer"; loves big swings
- Got on a call about Sphere pricing; learned about conference package templates that brought cost down
- Most brands just use templates with brand colors; doesn't look good
- Gamma's motion designer and brand made custom motion design for Sphere (motion design wasn't something other brands typically asked for)
- "Hacked it" with motion design to make it visually stunning
- Tied a team trip to Vegas; combined company holiday party with campaign execution

#### The Broader Lesson
- Always-on infrastructure (creators, video production, community, events) enables quick mobilization for major moments
- "Ramping up" plays you already run well beats trying new channels for a launch
- Marketing brainstorms every two weeks; ideas circulate for months before being deployed
- When a three-week timeline hits, you're not starting from scratch—you're dialing up existing plays

**Notable quotes:**
- "The only reason we were able to do that [in three weeks] was because of like all our always-on strategies that we were doing all the time and like the learnings we had gotten in the year." — Kristin
- "You have so many accumulated learnings along the way that when you are getting to a major launch moment or a major announcement moment, like you uh are in a much better position in order to be able to make news out of that." — Kristin
- (On the Sphere) "But it was like me in an evening with like chat GPT back and forth, like drafting something that was like good enough to get me to like the next stage." — Kristin
- "So we like hacked it basically um to make it look really, really amazing." — Kristin

**Key insight:** Major campaigns are built on months of consistent execution, not three-week bursts of creativity.

### 10. Product Expansion: Team/Business Plans, Claude MCP, Remixing

**Context:** Gamma is expanding beyond individual "pro streamer" users toward team collaboration and deeper tool integrations.

**What was discussed:**

#### Team & Business Plans (Launched Fall 2025)
- Gamma was primarily a creator product; individuals used it (consultants, teachers, small agencies)
- Team and business plans enable on-brand, reusable presentations for sales, customer follow-ups, internal comms
- Real use case: Sales teams remix decks with updated info; RFP responses now generated in Gamma in minutes rather than hours
- Internal Gamma usage: Everything communicated via Gamma presentations, not docs or emails
- Use case example: Take call notes from a meeting → feed to Claude → generate a Gamma deck → send visual follow-up instead of email recap
- Competitive advantage: Visual communication where it wouldn't have happened otherwise (no one would manually format a deck recap)

#### Claude MCP Integration
- "MCP is like a huge unlock for us," Kristin says
- Launching spring 2026; Gamma is coming to where users work (versus forcing them to context-switch into Gamma)
- Kristin sits in Claude and says "I'm trying to communicate this idea" → Claude offers to generate a Gamma deck → instant presentation
- Use case: Instead of writing a long email, generate a presentation first; much more impactful
- Not about generating decks for their own sake; it's about enabling communication where it wouldn't happen

#### Remix / Templates
- Called "remix" internally; branded as "create from template"
- Once you design a template (e.g., "2025 retreat deck"), you can generate variations
- Example: 2025 deck → need 2026 version → remix auto-repopulates with new info and visual themes
- Dramatically speeds up content creation for teams with high-volume presentation needs
- Competes with PowerPoint templates but with AI at the core

**Notable quotes:**
- "It's not like you should just create decks for the sake of creating decks, but like there's actually a lot of opportunity to like creating good communication." — Kristin
- (On Claude MCP) "And so you know, it it sounds like... now I'm like sitting in my Claude and I'm like, okay, I'm like trying to communicate this idea, and then Claude's like, You want me to make a gamma deck of that? Like, yeah, oh, there it is, great, let me send that, you know, instead of the email I was like about to send." — Kristin
- "Default workspace templates, like we have this amazing I it's like we we call it like remix internally but it's like create from template where you just like you generate from a template." — Kristin

**Key insight:** The future of Gamma is not about creation for creation's sake, but about enabling better communication wherever teams already work.

### 11. AI-Native Education and Curriculum

**Context:** Marcel raises a critical problem: There's no AI university for the hundreds of millions of knowledge workers retraining themselves, and this is a massive gap.

**What was discussed:**
- ~300 million knowledge workers in North America need to retool themselves with AI, but there's no curriculum
- Online AI education is fragmented, low-quality, and fast-moving—by the time something is written, it's outdated
- Educational institutions can't move fast enough; updating a curriculum every 2 years is unrealistic when AI changes weekly
- Kristin is tackling this at Gamma:
  - Creating interactive, constantly-updated educational experiences rather than static content libraries
  - Example: Persona doc for ICP + a Claude project with 20 raw user interview transcripts → team can chat with the "synthetic audience" rather than read a persona description
  - As interviews and concepts change, update the training data; content stays fresh
  - Contrast with traditional AI educational content: Structured curricula become stale before the first class is taught
- The analog for education: Can't plan a semester of AI curriculum when it's changing daily
- Marcel is giving away "maybe a little bit too much" about how Gamma works internally to help others learn—he sees it as an obligation given how fast things are moving

**Notable quotes:**
- (Marcel) "There's what 300 million knowledge workers or so, like in you know, in like North America, don't quote me on the numbers, but like there's hundreds of millions of knowledge workers. And there's no AI university for knowledge workers. And we're expecting hundreds of millions of people to completely retool themselves in the right way with a brand new technology that's gonna impact every single person in the future. And there's no like curriculum for this."
- (Kristin) "Educational institutions don't move anywhere close to fast enough... it's like that content is gonna be stale um before you even finish teaching that first class, much less the winter class or the spring class."
- (Marcel) "So like that's why like I like I'm so passionate about like community education, doing workshops, giving away maybe a little bit too much that that I should, that gives away a little bit of how like our you know what makes what we do special."

**Key insight:** The solution to AI literacy won't be traditional education; it will be interactive, constantly-updated experiences that evolve with the technology.

## Insights & Takeaways

- **Intellectual curiosity trumps experience.** The best hires are those who ask "I never thought of that—tell me more" rather than defaulting to frameworks they know. Throw people off their game in interviews to see how they think, not what they've memorized.

- **Writing is the foundation of thinking.** Kristin and Marcel agree that in the AI age, writing discipline is even more critical. It's not about being a "good writer"; it's about using writing as a tool to organize and clarify your thinking. Without it, you're prone to lazy thinking and over-reliance on AI outputs.

- **Preserving taste is a discipline.** As AI models get better, it's tempting to delegate more work to them. The risk: You lose the ability to evaluate whether outputs are good. Side projects, creative pursuits, and deliberate practice in craft (writing, video, art) are non-negotiable for leaders.

- **Marketing to AI and humans is dual expertise.** The leaders who will thrive are those who understand both AI-native channels (AEO, LLM optimization) and human-centered experiences (events, visceral brand moments). Most marketers will specialize in one; the best will be "good enough" in both.

- **Profitability changes the game.** Gamma being profitable is unusual among AI startups. It allows the company to invest in brand, take bigger creative swings, and avoid the "burn everything" mentality. Profitability is a strategic advantage, not a constraint.

- **Build on always-on infrastructure.** Gamma didn't create a Series B campaign from scratch in three weeks. It ramped up plays it had been running all year (influencer partnerships, video production, community events). The lesson: Invest continuously in core competencies, then scale them for big moments.

- **Pick your 1%, not your 80%.** Most marketers claim they can do everything. Reality: You'll get 80–90% of your impact from one thing. Pick it. Lean into it. Let other areas stay scrappy.

- **Global user bases are rare and should be leveraged.** 80% of Gamma's users are outside the US. Rather than treating this as a metric, Gamma is using it as a strategic imperative: physical world tours, regional product thinking, diverse hiring. Few startups actually show up globally.

- **AI-native education doesn't exist yet.** Hundreds of millions of workers are retraining themselves on AI with no curriculum, no institution backing them. This is a massive gap. The solution won't be traditional education; it will be interactive, constantly-updated experiences (like Claude projects with interview data).

- **Three weeks isn't enough time unless you've been planning for a year.** The Series B campaign felt like spontaneous fun, but it was built on 12 months of accumulated learnings, relationships, and playbooks. Startups that want "quick wins" need to think in years, not weeks.

- **GrowthX partnership opportunity.** Kristin is thinking about how Gamma and GrowthX might collaborate (e.g., lightning sessions, template development). The connection is natural: both focused on clear communication, both think deeply about AI, both value intellectual honesty.
