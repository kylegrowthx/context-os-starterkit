### Deepgram Tax Situation
The exercise will trigger Section 498 ordinary income recognition and potential short-term capital gains upon sale. The same-day exercise and sale creates complex tax implications with an estimated 55% total tax rate across combined gains. Marcel is seeking strategies to mitigate this massive tax burden.

QSBS rollover was discussed but deemed unlikely because Deepgram is not currently QSBS qualified (raised over $75M, which exceeds the threshold). Even if it were qualified, the same-day exercise and sale creates timing issues. The only pathway for QSBS rollover would be if gains were put into another QSBS or into Marcel's own company.

### Charitable Contributions
Christopher Wong explained upcoming 2026 tax law changes that will reduce benefits from charitable contributions:
- 2026 baseline of 0.5% to all charitable contributions (unless above 0.5% of AGI, no deduction)
- Itemized deduction benefits reduced to 35% (from 37%)

This timing makes charitable giving less effective in 2026 compared to 2025. However, charitable contributions remain an option:
- Cash donations: deductible up to 60% of AGI
- Appreciated stock donations: limited to 30% of AGI
- Private foundation gifts: limited to 20% of AGI

Marcel's salary of approximately $300,000 defines his maximum deductible amounts. His father's nonprofit offers a potential vehicle for charitable giving this year, though total donation size will be moderate.

### Qualified Opportunity Funds
Qualified Opportunity Funds can defer gains if invested within 180 days of a gain recognition event. However, this is less beneficial than previously because the first round of QOF zones ends December 2026, deferring gains for only one year at most. Additionally, QOF investments are illiquid and must be held long-term. By end of 2026, tax payment is still required. Therefore, QOF is not recommended as a primary strategy.

### Retirement Contributions Strategy
Retirement account contributions emerged as a short-term offset option:
- **SEP IRA**: Contribution limit of lesser of 25% of net self-employment income or $70,000 per person
- **Solo 401(k)**: Can be set up by year-end to qualify for 2025 contributions; allows employee and employer contributions

Marcel's LLC had significant revenue in early 2025, providing basis for meaningful contributions. His wife's consulting income also qualifies her for contributions. Daughter cannot contribute due to employment requirements (requires self-employment income).

If Marcel can generate ~$280,000 in self-employment income (25% x $70k limit), he and his wife could each contribute up to $70,000, totaling $140,000 for 2025. Whether SEP IRA and Solo 401(k) can be used together requires verification.

Self-directed 401(k) plans were discussed as potentially allowing shares to be moved post-exercise, but tax liabilities remain with Marcel regardless of transfer—the exercise itself triggers recognition.

### Real Estate Investment Strategy
Real estate emerged as the primary viable substantial tax offset strategy. Marcel's wife qualifies as an active real estate professional due to her construction background, making passive real estate losses immediately usable rather than suspended.

Real estate vehicles can generate substantial upfront depreciation losses to offset gains, with later dividend income (~50% return/year) from the investment. The key requirement: must qualify as "real estate professional" with 750+ hours per year in real estate and more than 50% of personal activity in that work.

Christopher Wong and Hazel agreed to provide documentation on real estate professional qualification tests. This pathway is particularly viable given Marcel's wife's construction background and ability to qualify.

### Stock Ownership Compliance and 83(b) Elections
Marcel confirmed he owns shares outright in his company without vesting restrictions. 83(b) elections apply only to restricted stock with substantial risk of forfeiture (vesting conditions). Marcel's founder shares do not have company clawback provisions—the only restrictions are founder departure provisions under Series A, not forfeiture by the company.

Hazel explained that if 83(b) was required but not filed, there's no late filing option—the deadline is 30 days from grant. However, Marcel stated that three different lawyers previously told him he did not need to file 83(b) given his share structure. The team agreed to review his original stock grant documents to confirm.
