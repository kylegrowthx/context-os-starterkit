GrowthX is a B2B content marketing services firm ($200k+/year engagements) with a newer "sprint" model intended to deliver rapid content discovery and strategy work. The sprint was designed to compress weeks of discovery work into a focused week-long engagement. However, the model is failing operationally. The current sprint conversion rate (leads that close into ongoing GrowthX relationships) sits at 40%, which is unsustainable and worse than the pre-sprint baseline. This creates non-reproducible outputs and cascading process failures. The team recognized they can't scale without embedding strategic expertise into the entire delivery system.

## Key Topics

### The Problem: Inconsistent Execution & Unsustainable Sprint Conversion

  - Current execution is inconsistent, relying on individual "heroes" (Marcel, Ada, George) instead of a scalable, documented system.
  - The sprint conversion rate is a critical failure at 40%, making the business model unsustainable and worse than the original pre-sprint model.
  - The root cause is a process breakdown in how the team executes sprints:
      - **Ada's workaround:** Because the formal sprint process is unclear or slow, Ada has taken to using Claude to rapidly generate strategy work, bypassing the documented OS and creating non-reproducible outputs.
      - **Misaligned delegation:** Ada then delegates this non-standard work to editors, engagement managers, and others downstream, creating a cascade of process failure where work quality becomes unpredictable.
      - **Time pressure:** Engagement managers don't have enough time to do quality discovery, strategy, and calibration work themselves — the process is vague and slow — so they've internalized that "Ada will fix it in week 2."

### The Solution: A Standardized Sprint Process & Strategic Mindset

  - **Standardized Sprint Process:** Codify the best-practice discovery and strategy approach into a repeatable, teachable system. The process has 8 steps that engagement managers and editors should follow:
    1.  **Deep Discovery:** Uncover client goals, bottlenecks, market context, and the "why" behind the engagement. Don't assume you know what matters.
    2.  **Stakeholder Mapping:** Identify all key decision-makers and influencers. Understand who drives purchasing decisions, who cares about SEO/traffic, who has sign-off authority.
    3.  **Input Validation:** Play back your understanding of the discovery to the client for confirmation and sign-off. Make sure you actually understand their business.
    4.  **Context Sign-off:** Create a big-picture strategy document that articulates the opportunity, the market segments, the buyer journey, and the content strategy. Get client approval before you move to production.
    5.  **Persona Focus:** Select one primary persona or segment for initial calibration. Don't try to solve for everyone.
    6.  **Calibration Loop:** Use a "first shot" artifact (a small content piece or outline) to find failure modes. If it doesn't resonate, adjust your inputs (context, instructions, persona focus) and try again.
    7.  **Bottleneck Identification:** Flag client-side blockers that slow the project (review approval processes, misaligned stakeholders, budget constraints) and help resolve them collaboratively.
    8.  **Roadmap Communication:** Frame the strategy around the large-scale opportunity and multi-year roadmap to create a sense of momentum and opportunity (rather than a one-off content project).
  - **Strategic Mindset Shift:** The team must change how they think about client relationships:
      - **From Output → Engine:** Stop thinking about delivering a content strategy doc. Start building a sustainable, repeating system that drives compounding growth for the client (and recurring revenue for GrowthX).
      - **From Tactical → Strategic:** Act as a C-level partner, not a task executor. Understand the client's business, competitive landscape, and long-term goals. Proactively flag risks.
      - **From Positive-Only → Transparent:** Honestly discuss what's working and what's not. Build trust by saying "this approach isn't working for your ICP" instead of spinning everything as a win.
      - **Active Curiosity:** This is the key. Ask probing questions. Understand pain points. Test assumptions. Don't assume every engagement is a good fit — be willing to say "based on what we learned, here's my honest assessment."

### Implementation: Enablement Program & Checkpoints

  - **Enablement Program:** Marisol will lead the program, starting with sprint team training (both engagement managers and editors).
      - **Phase 1 (Dec–Jan):** Organize and consolidate existing training materials: George's onboarding doc (end-to-end thinking), Andi's Figma swim lanes (role responsibilities), the Lovable case study (best practice example), and churn analysis retro (what went wrong).
      - **Phase 2 (Jan):** Structure a curriculum around the 8-step standardized sprint process. Link each step to the customer journey. Make it clear what success looks like at each gate.
      - **Phase 3 (Jan–Feb):** Create new assets: a case study around Lovable (a sprint that worked), a churn analysis case (what didn't work and why), decision trees for engagement managers (when to escalate, when to pivot).
      - **Timeline:** Marisol will deliver a draft plan by tomorrow (Dec 19). The team reviews on Dec 30.
  - **Mandatory Checkpoints:** All client-facing deliverables (content strategy presentations, roadmap documents, etc.) must be reviewed by George or Kyle via Loom *before* being shared with the client.
      - **Requirement:** Engagement managers/editors create a quick Loom walkthrough of the deliverable (2–5 min). George or Kyle review and sign off. Only then does it go to the client.
      - **Rationale:** This catches 90% of quality issues (strategy misaligned with discovery, missing stakeholder mapping, tone issues, etc.) before the client sees it. It also creates accountability and reinforces the standard.
      - **Enforcement:** George and Kyle will be the gate-keepers. They'll block shipment on substandard work.
