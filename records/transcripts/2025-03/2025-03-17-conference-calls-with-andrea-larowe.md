## Summary

GrowthX's Bridget McGillivray and Daniel Lopes consulted with Andrea LaRowe, Head of People at 37signals, on how to scale remote hiring and HR infrastructure. Key takeaways included 37signals' intentional approach to compensation (location-agnostic pay at the 90th percentile of San Francisco rates), their flat organizational structure with no traditional managers, and their profit-sharing model (10% of annual profits distributed to employees with 2+ years tenure). Andrea emphasized that policies are hardest to remove once implemented, so GrowthX should be deliberate about building foundational HR systems now while relatively small—particularly around employee vs. contractor classification, international hiring compliance, and severance policies.

---

## Context

Daniel Lopes and Bridget McGillivray co-founded GrowthX, a B2B content marketing agency that pairs human writers with AI automation for research, ideation, and analysis. The company recently secured funding after bootstrapping to a point where they now have ~30 people on the services team and are planning to build a 10-20 person product team by end of year. They also plan to open a physical office in San Francisco for events and team collaboration, similar to the model 37signals previously operated. Daniel knew Andrea LaRowe from his time at 37signals and reached out as GrowthX is now facing the HR and scaling challenges that 37signals navigated years ago.

Andrea LaRowe is Head of People at 37signals (formerly Basecamp), where she manages HR, operations, and people strategy for the 61-62 person company. 37signals is a fully remote, profitable company known for its contrarian approach to management and compensation—no traditional managers, no performance-based pay, standardized location-agnostic salaries, and a profit-sharing model. The call was a peer consultation on how GrowthX can design foundational policies that will scale with their growth.

---

## Relevance

**To GrowthX Operations & Scaling:**
- GrowthX must distinguish now between employees (W2 in US) vs. contractors (particularly international staff) with different legal and tax requirements; delaying this becomes increasingly difficult as headcount grows. Consider limiting contractor hours to 30/week or less to strengthen legal position, especially for low-wage agency roles ($1,500-3,000/month).
- Severance policies should be designed and documented now in writing before needed; 37signals learned this in 2021-2022 when policy changes triggered 20 resignations and required complex separation agreements. Build intentionality now rather than reactively.
- Location-agnostic compensation benchmarking (37signals uses Radford data at 90th percentile of SF market) eliminates internal equity tensions and is particularly valuable for retaining international talent; current practice of $1,500-3,000/month contractor pay is well below this but may be intentional for agency model.
- Professional contractor setup (invoicing through proper business entities, health insurance reimbursement for international staff, computer reimbursement policies) protects both company and employee; risk from disgruntled departing contractors reporting violations to foreign labor authorities is real.

**To GrowthX Organizational Design:**
- 37signals' "no managers" model relies on extreme selectivity in hiring (2,000 applications for one Rails engineer role, no hire made) and assumes "managers of one"—autonomous, self-directed people who don't need oversight; GrowthX's larger contractor base and mixed seniority may require different structure.
- Daily transparent communication (37signals uses Basecamp check-ins where people post what they did each day) is the primary mechanism for visibility and catching quiet quitters in remote environments; GrowthX should implement structured async communication early.
- Performance reviews (37signals: 3-month, 6-month, 12-month for new hires, then annual) with clear expectations make it possible to exit underperformers decisively; the 12-month mark is a critical decision point, not a formality.

## Overview

- 37signals uses a flat organizational structure with no traditional managers, emphasizing individual autonomy and responsibility
- Compensation is standardized by role/level, location-agnostic, and not tied to performance reviews
- International contractors are treated similarly to full-time employees, with some legal distinctions
- Careful, intentional policy-making is crucial when scaling a remote company to avoid future complications

---

## Key Topics

### Company Structure and Management

  - 61-62 employees total, including international contractors
  - Flat structure with few managers; team leads act as mentors rather than traditional managers
  - Emphasis on "managers of one" - autonomous, self-directed employees
  - No engineering managers, product managers, or project managers
  - Performance reviews conducted at 3, 6, and 12 months for new hires, then annually

### Compensation and Benefits

  - Location-agnostic pay based on 90th percentile of San Francisco market rates
  - Standardized pay by role/level, no individual negotiations
  - Annual salary benchmarking using Radford data
  - 10% profit-sharing distributed based on tenure (2+ years eligibility)
  - Health insurance reimbursement for international contractors
  - $200/month coworking space stipend

### Remote Work Practices

  - Daily check-ins and transparent communication via Basecamp
  - Two week-long company meetups per year (one in North America, one in Europe)
  - No regular team meetups, but conference attendance encouraged

### International Hiring and Compliance

  - International team members hired as contractors for legal/tax reasons
  - Contractors invoice the company through their own business entities
  - Company helps contractors set up proper business structures if needed

### Office Space and Events

  - Previously had an office in Chicago, now fully remote
  - Hosted external events in the office space, managed informally

### Hiring and Retention

  - Extremely selective hiring process (2,000 applications for recent role, no hire made)
  - Low turnover due to competitive compensation and work environment
  - Some attrition due to limited career advancement opportunities

### Performance Management

  - Performance not tied to compensation, but impacts promotions
  - Clear expectations set for first-year performance
  - Willing to let go of underperforming employees at 12-month mark

## Action Items

- **Bridget McGillivray (GrowthX):** Define clear distinction between W2 employees and contractors (including hour limits for contractor roles) to ensure legal compliance before scaling; consult with employment law firm on contractor classification rules for each country where GrowthX operates.
- **Daniel Lopes & Bridget McGillivray (GrowthX):** Write severance policy with separation agreements and document in handbook before first terminations occur; include legal review of confidentiality language (FLSA restrictions).
- **Bridget McGillivray (GrowthX):** Decide on compensation philosophy for agency contractors vs. core team; determine if location-agnostic benchmarking (like 37signals) is feasible or if separate comp bands are needed given $1,500-3,000/month vs. core team salary difference.
- **Daniel Lopes & Bridget McGillivray (GrowthX):** Implement structured daily check-ins (async communication in Basecamp or similar) to establish visibility into work and culture early, before hitting larger scale.
- **Bridget McGillivray (GrowthX):** Design performance review cadence (consider 37signals model: 3-month, 6-month, 12-month for new hires, then annual) and document expectations for first-year performance and 12-month evaluation criteria.
- **Daniel Lopes & Bridget McGillivray (GrowthX):** Model out profit-sharing structure and determine if feasible given current equity pool constraints; decide whether to offer as part of offer letter or as separate benefits discussion.
