## Key Topics

### The Training Problem: Scale vs. Effectiveness

The core issue: scalable training (content, e-learning) doesn't work. Effective training (human role-play, one-on-one coaching) is hard to scale. Most CFOs and HR leaders wish people were better trained but don't do much about it because traditional modalities force that choice. The gold standard is fields where training works: F-16 pilots, Olympic athletes, CIA operatives. Why? Their practice environment is indistinguishable from their performance environment, and they get fast feedback. Exec's insight: AI can now bring that model to business conversations.

### What Exec Does Differently

Exec targets the highest-value conversations in business: sales discovery/demo, renewals/customer success, and management coaching. Not broad training, but the conversations that drive revenue or protect it. The platform lets you create custom role-play scenarios in 90 seconds (vs. hours/days before). Setup is simple: pick a scenario type, add context, define rubrics (what you want to evaluate), and go. The AI plays "Alice," a realistic prospect/customer who pushes back, raises concerns, and responds to what you say. After the role-play, you get feedback from an AI coach based on how you did against the rubrics.

### Product Design and Features

The scenario setup is fast, but the key is customization. Rubrics are user-editable; AI generates them, but you can make them specific to your company's values or sales methodology (e.g., "Demonstrate Linear's speed and developer ergonomics philosophy" vs. generic "show product benefits"). The platform includes screen sharing, so you can show your actual product during the role-play. There's a voice-based AI feedback coach that explains what you did well or poorly. Scenario creation is flexible: use a template, add content, or use a voice-setup process where you talk to the coach and it builds the scenario for you. Managers can listen to recordings and evaluate whether reps are practicing the right things.

### Why Exec Wins vs. Competitors

Two categories of competitors: (1) Embedded AI role-play (MindTickle, Udemy, etc.) checks the box but the scenarios feel lazy and thin. (2) Standalone AI role-play products try hard but scenarios still feel generic and the creation process is cumbersome. Exec's advantage is "taste" — the AI generates better scenarios (probably because of training data, prompts, and refinement), the customization is easier, features work better (screen sharing, voice coach), and the overall product design feels thoughtfully made. Nick cited a 100% win rate in head-to-head comparisons. The scenarios "feel designed" because Exec spent effort on them. This is hard to communicate upfront because competitors could theoretically use better prompts, but they don't — and that's where taste and design discipline matter.

### Customer Concerns and Responses

The biggest question: Will it take a lot of effort to build good scenarios? Exec's answer: No. They have a services team that moves fast. The process: analyze a good call to reverse-engineer the rubric, or use an interview with the customer to define what "good" looks like, or leverage off-the-shelf methodologies (SBI for feedback, MEDDIC for sales). Once you have the rubric, you're done — AI handles scenario generation.

### Integration Roadmap

Native integrations: call recorders (in progress). LMS integration is possible and quick. The approach: customer asks, Exec's tech team figures it out. No pre-built Salesforce integration because nobody has asked yet. But the team will build whatever integration customers need.

### The Coaching Product

Exec has a separate coaching product (not AI role-play, but one-on-one coaching) used by Zoom Info, LinkedIn, and other enterprises. When selling to HR leaders, Nick pitches both. But for fundraising and primary SaaS focus, AI role-play is the priority.
