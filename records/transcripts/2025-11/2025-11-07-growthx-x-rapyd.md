## Summary

Mark Stiltner from Rapyd explained the decision to non-renew GrowthX's contract, driven by a CEO-level pivot to allocate all marketing budget to physical events (trade shows, proprietary events) where Rapyd is seeing measurable ROI while digital marketing efforts show minimal return. Beyond budget reallocation, the core operational issue was content review bottlenecks: Rapyd's team lacked subject matter expertise to validate the accuracy of GrowthX's long, complex blog posts, making the review process more resource-intensive than Rapyd's own successful internal strategy of publishing short, hyper-focused posts. GrowthX is still prioritizing delivery of country and versus posts through December, though Mark signaled potential future interest in a low-cost, self-serve platform model (circa $1,000/month) rather than full-service agency work.

## Context

Rapyd is a fintech/payment processing company that has been working with GrowthX for content marketing and blog strategy. The relationship involved GrowthX producing long-form, in-depth blog posts on payment-related topics, with Rapyd's internal team responsible for review and publication. Mark Stiltner is the decision-maker on the GrowthX engagement and collaborated with Mara Leighton (GrowthX VP of Delivery) and Vivek Shankar (GrowthX) on account strategy. The non-renewal decision came from Rapyd's CEO as part of a broader 2025 budget reallocation exercise; Mark had delayed sending the formal notice to explore options but ultimately had to communicate the decision. This exit meeting gave GrowthX a chance to understand what went wrong and capture learnings for future client engagements.

## Key Topics

### Primary Driver: Budget Reallocation

  - The non-renewal was a CEO-level decision to reallocate all marketing budget from digital to physical events.
  - **Rationale:** Events are the only source of new sales, while digital marketing shows no measurable ROI.
  - **Outcome:** Mark could not justify the \~$120k annual contract against the CEO's directive to fund a major European trade show.

### Secondary Driver: Content Strategy Mismatch

  - The core issue was a review bottleneck caused by the length and complexity of GrowthX's blog posts.
  - **Problem:** Rapyd's team lacked the subject matter expertise to validate the accuracy of complex content, making the review process slower and more resource-intensive than writing their own posts.
  - **Contrast:** Rapyd's own successful strategy involves publishing short, hyper-focused posts (e.g., "MCC3799," "Travel Agency Services").
      - **Why it works:** Easy to write, quick to review, and highly effective for targeted traffic.
      - **Opportunity:** GrowthX could beat competitors (e.g., Adyen) by adding simple data points to short posts, making them more valuable and easier to review.

### Platform & Process Issues

  - **Content Quality Decline:** Content quality noticeably declined after the Atlas platform launch.
      - **Cause:** Misconfigured personas (e.g., marketplace-focused) led to a hyper-focus on irrelevant topics.
      - **Recovery:** Quality has since improved to pre-Atlas levels.
  - **Process Bottleneck:** Rapyd lacked the internal capacity to manage the GrowthX process effectively.
      - **Requirement:** Effective use of tools like Scrunch and detailed persona work requires significant time investment.
      - **Constraint:** This time investment was not possible without additional headcount.

### Future Partnership Opportunity

  - **Current Need:** Rapyd's need has evolved from blogging to broad SEO management (site schema, keyword mapping, off-site optimization).
  - **Ideal Solution:** A full-time, in-house SEO specialist.
  - **Platform Opportunity:** A low-cost, self-serve platform could be a fit for Mark's personal budget authority (e.g., $100–$1,000/mo).
      - **Why it works:** This model shifts the cost to a different budget category, making it a viable option.
