## Summary

GrowthX's director team aligned on LLM.txt rollout progress and addressed critical workforce capacity challenges. Dave Capone reported that the LLM.txt workflow is complete and ready for production, with rollouts completed for Monograph, Engine, and Smith, and planned completion for Galileo, Yurko, and Town this week—validating the direction as major SEO tools (Ahrefs, SEMrush) have begun including LLM.txt in audits. The team discussed broader delivery challenges: broken workflows causing team members to work 50-70 hour weeks, job security fears among content makers and marketing engineers creating burnout, and fragmented communication between engineering and delivery that leaves tickets in backlog limbo without resolution updates. Matthew Panzarino committed to conducting one-on-ones with all content makers to surface pain points and pipeline breakdowns, with a planned sprint on workflow fixes after the agented workflow shift rolls out—and the team agreed that clearer account strategies across all pods are essential to give work purpose and meaning.

---

## Context

This was a weekly director standup among GrowthX's delivery and product leadership (directors managing content makers, marketing engineers, and strategy work). The meeting had an unusual split focus: the first half was a scheduled geo experiments discussion led by Dave Capone and Jason Gong, but the second half pivoted sharply to address systemic staffing and morale issues that have been compounding over weeks. Andi Bailey and Kyle Gaudreau explicitly flagged that team members are experiencing fear about job security and sustainability, working unsustainable hours, and losing confidence in the company's ability to fix broken delivery systems. This meeting represents an inflection point where leaders began naming the problem openly in a group setting rather than as individual complaints. Matthew Panzarino and Darrell Etherington both acknowledged that the underlying issue is not individual performance but systems—broken pipelines, unclear account strategies, and poor interfaces between delivery and engineering teams that prevent visibility and urgency around problem tickets.
