## Key Topics

### Finance Operations Hire: Scope, Concerns, and Resolution

Bridget pitched the candidate as an operational finance specialist who would handle invoicing, bill pay, payroll, customer collections, tax compliance, bookkeeping firm management, and HR support (expense approvals, contract administration). Marcel initially raised a cost objection: at ~$200k/year plus employment overhead, the company is paying near-CFO rates for someone who lacks formal finance training and doesn't do strategic finance work. Bridget clarified the candidate is strong systems thinker and executor — he handled complex billing infrastructure at AirBite (custom usage-based billing, integration with product team) and is now migrating GrowthX to Rippling. He's not expected to grow into leadership; his strength is taking directives and executing flawlessly. Marcel accepted this model once he understood Bridget would pair the hire with fractional CFO advisory for strategic guidance. Consensus: hire the operations specialist, layer in fractional CFO separately.

### Growth Strategy and Burn Rate

With $10 million in the bank, GrowthX is planning to burn $4-5 million before returning to profitability. Bridget noted this spending level felt "unnatural" — she prefers a steady-growth model. Marcel agreed the aggressive burn was a departure from their usual thinking but accepted it as necessary to scale. The conversation implied confidence in the business model but acknowledgment that scaling at GrowthX speed requires significant upfront investment.

### International Hiring, Currency Exposure, and Contractor Classification

The team discussed FX risk across multiple countries: Canada (CAD weakness), Brazil (favorable to GrowthX but volatile), Germany (labor law constraints make EOR necessary for new hires like Eric). Using Employer of Record (EOR) services (Deel) exposes the company to monthly FX fluctuation — invoices come in local currency, and conversion rates vary month-to-month (Marcel cited examples where one contractor's cost ranged $1,300–$1,700 month-to-month). Both prefer USD contracts where possible for predictability. Bridget emphasized the complexity: EOR requires local currency contracts, but the company's cost ultimately depends on FX rates set when invoices are approved. The team also discussed contractor vs. employee classification risk: keeping client-facing staff as contractors is easier to defend than classifying internal/operations staff as contractors (even Bridget acknowledged she's "clearly not a contractor"). Germany hiring carries additional complexity due to employment protection laws.

### Headcount Planning and Hiring Sequencing

Marcel and Bridget agreed to create a headcount plan mapping 2–3 "really high leverage" roles (outside the finance hire). The discussion revealed a hiring philosophy tension: Marcel noted that hiring at manager level first sometimes backfires because managers hire their own teams. Bridget acknowledged the risk and suggested a structured headcount approach: identify highest-impact roles, decide if the new finance hire fills gaps, then identify remaining critical gaps. Agreed to have Marcel sketch a quick org chart and Bridget flesh out the full headcount plan before their next alignment.
