Marcel Santilli: The way to think about it is the mechanics of this company and the unit economics of this company only work if strategy sprints are successful. Let that sink in.

The hardest thing has been us figuring out how to set customers up for success. Getting people to want to buy what we offer—getting people to sign contracts—that's honestly been the easiest part of running this company. The hard part is execution.

For those newer to this, about a year ago—Aida, I think you were about a month in—our thinking was straightforward: you sign a new customer, you hire new people to help run that account, so you put new customers with new people. And then over time, you had people like Aida doing really well, and the question became: do you pull those people away from their good accounts to put them on new accounts, or do you just keep putting new people on new accounts? We chose the latter.

And right around April, beginning of May, it was as close to a shit show as you can get. Most clients weren't converting to long-term contracts. We had a 90-day pilot period, which was also a huge mistake. So in May, we paused sales. Then beginning of June, we took a lot of people who had proven themselves, knew what they were doing, and had the business context, and put them all into strategy sprints. I was heavily involved. For the first 22 of those kickoffs, we converted 100%.

Since then there's been drift. We added more and more people to both sprints and ongoing accounts. We're not yet at a place where sprints are working so well that we can go from two to three to four kickoffs a week. And at the same time, conversions have fallen quite a bit.


Marcel Santilli: We had some positive conversions in the last few weeks, which helped a little bit. We're sitting at around 75% for Q4, which is a couple of percentage points higher than Q3.

To understand how conversion rates work: in a time period like Q4, we take every sprint that ended and look at what percentage converted—went into 12-month contracts—divided by total sprints. That gives you the 75%.

Another way to think about it: in the last quarter, we had about 20 kickoffs total. If you're losing five of those, that's a million dollars in ARR walking out. We know some things are outside our control, but those logos don't come back.


Marcel Santilli: The Sprint is so critical. If we're not at 85%—which is how we built our entire business projections—then you need to do more kickoffs to make up for that loss. That means way more work and the business becomes way less predictable. It becomes harder to staff everything else. The Sprint makes or breaks the entire company.

If the Sprint's not working, fixing everything else still wouldn't help overall because the Sprint is just too leaky if we don't fix it.

The good news is we have as much predictability as you can possibly have. Very few tech companies have signed contracts six weeks out. So we know what's coming way ahead. We don't need to start work an hour before the kickoff. We've made this fairly repeatable. We had 75 kickoffs last year. This year we need to do at least 150 kickoffs while maintaining an 85% conversion rate. If we do, we can go from 13 million in ARR to 45 million in ARR. That's straightforward and a known target.

What works is we have so much opportunity in the first couple of weeks to build trust and impress them. We have processes, we know they work. The thing that impresses them is coming in with the mentality of "I'm not just going through the motions. I'm trying to become an expert in them, their product, their market, their personas." Within the first few weeks, you reach a point where they're like, "Wow, this team is so much more productive than my entire marketing team combined." That's what we got from folks like Monograph. Robert said to me, "I've never worked with any team or vendor as productive as your team in the first few weeks. The amount of outputs, the quality, the level of care—it's insane."

When that happens, they want to upsell by the end of the sprint. They're like, "Can I buy more from you? Can I have more of your team capacity?"

But there are failure modes we can fall into. We listen to the client wanting something that's not how we operate, and we drift toward it. We don't map the organization. They say it's fine, and we move on. No one reviews it. Things get stuck. These patterns are predictable, but we need to overcome them.

Here's what matters: there's so much we can do that's not just checking boxes. We can go deeper into the company as if we were hired there. Reputation is critical. Take CRV—they're a VC. VCs talk. They have tons of portfolio companies. It would be easy to just run the company context generator, add personas, document it, run the meeting, ask questions, and move on. But you can do tons of research on all the companies, the history, what publications startup founders read, all the market research. There are tons of tools. You don't need to give it all on day one, but it's really helpful for learning, understanding, coming up with strategy, connecting dots.

Think of the two weeks prior to the kickoff as the most critical period to internalize those companies. We're trying to make sure you have breathing room in those weeks to do amazing work—not feel like you're jumping around with your head cut off. But we never let slop through. That's the most important thing. If trust is broken in the first two to three weeks, that's it. Then it's six months of them checking our work, checking every line. So you go the extra mile, be a perfectionist, don't let shit through, so you have the breathing room to operate and drive results for them.

It's an investment and an extension of sales. There's nothing more important than figuring this out. Getting conversions right—once we do, the whole business runs better and we can fund this whole thing.
