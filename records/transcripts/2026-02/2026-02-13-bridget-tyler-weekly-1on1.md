## Key topics

### Contract status and redline process
Knox came back with very light redlines — Tyler accepted them live during the call. Otter is signing the same day with zero redlines (only asked for a written opt-in for annual renewal). Vercel is in process (Cody expected to respond that day). Binti is negotiating pricing with Marcel directly, likely landing between $10k–$12k.

The contrast between Josh's legal reviews and Andrea's was a central discussion. Josh (external counsel, startup B2B SaaS specialist) delivers efficient, minimal redlines — exactly what's needed for a Series A company closing fast. Andrea's reviews have been far more thorough than necessary — adding multiple new contract sections, detailed clauses — which slows deals and creates confusion. Bridget met with Andrea for an hour and gave her clear direction: don't touch contracts already in circulation. Focus on template improvements and building a legal knowledge base.

### Legal knowledge base and concession playbook
Bridget's solution to get Andrea more autonomous: build a contract concession playbook in Notion. For every key contract section, define: (1) default position in the template, (2) first acceptable concession, (3) final acceptable concession. Example: renewal terms — default is auto-renew with opt-out; first concession is auto-renew with opt-in required; final concession is no auto-renewal. Same logic for logo rights, liability caps, etc.

The goal is to eventually let Tyler and others resolve most redlines without involving Bridget or Josh. For complex indemnification/liability language — still route to Josh for fast review (usually same-day).

### Pricing strategy
Bridget confirmed: don't offer $15k/year discounts proactively. Stay at $18k/month. If clients push on price, offer quarterly payment terms. If they want a discount, tie it to paying upfront annually (discount for cash up front, not for reducing ARR). This preserves reported ARR while giving clients a path to reduce monthly cash burden.

### Pipeline strategy
Tyler reviewed the current pipeline (Knox, Otter, Binti, Nerdio, Coralogix, Atria, ABK, Vercel, St. Jude's). Bridget challenged Tyler's habit of categorizing some deals as "backups." Her argument: if a deal is a good fit but slow-moving, go full court press and push them to book a kickoff date weeks out. This builds a buffer — instead of scrambling for 2 deals per week at crunch time, you have a backlog of booked clients. Run Layer was explicitly removed from active consideration — delivery risk, bad relationship dynamics, Marcel had already been seen downselling them.

### Notion cleanup and AI tooling
Bridget flagged the Notion sales workspace as full of stale content — a problem as Marcel builds AI agents that query it. She asked Tyler to spend 1–2 hours archiving old materials, verifying key docs, and updating them using AI tools (Claude, Whisper Flow). Marcel is working on a proper knowledge base outside of Notion that AI agents can query reliably — only referencing verified content.

Marcel demonstrated a use case live: using Cursor + Claude with HubSpot, Fireflies, and Slack MCPs connected, he queried "what's happening with the HubSpot opportunity?" — the system found 2 sales calls, cross-referenced Fireflies transcripts and Slack, and surfaced a summary in minutes. Tyler expressed strong interest in using similar tooling for CRM updates and closed-lost analysis. Company-wide AI training planned over the next couple of months.

### Nigel and third AE hiring
Tyler flagged difficulty building scalable sales process while managing 2+ $250k deals per week in close. He proposed having Nigel take on some services sales to free Tyler up for process-building. Bridget agreed to evaluate after seeing Nigel's calendar performance from a Check That strategy session outreach campaign (150 people hit up). She confirmed they're actively hiring a third AE focused on services sales. In the interim, Tyler to identify 2–3 lower-priority calls per week for Nigel to run, particularly Check That pitches — to build his reps and rhythm.

### New contract templates — platform and IP
Bridget explained why linking to Terms of Service alone isn't sufficient for enterprise clients buying CheckThat/platform. When selling a platform product (vs. pure services), GrowthX needs clients to explicitly sign off on: data retention, IP ownership of custom prompts, and AI training permissions. GrowthX intends to build a shared prompt library from client inputs — so they need to own client-generated prompts contractually. By default, AI training is on; enterprise clients can negotiate it off (similar to how Claude's training can be toggled). Three new contract templates (Services only, CheckThat only, Services + CheckThat) to be in Panda by next week. In the interim, use Andrea's new version (with ToS link) for contracts going out today.

Bridget acknowledged new platform sales contracts will add at least a month to the sales cycle — accepted as unavoidable when selling technology with IP implications.
