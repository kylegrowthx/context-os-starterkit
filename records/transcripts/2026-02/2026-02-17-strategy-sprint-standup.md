## Key Topics

### First Principles for Technical Audiences

Marcel's main coaching point: when writing for technical, expert, skeptical audiences, you cannot start with advanced or nuanced topics. You must build from first principles — establish the foundational layer first, then reference it as you go deeper. This serves two purposes: it educates the market and it gives you credible "back catalog" to cite later. The Viscom client example: the CEO was dissatisfied because existing content was oversimplified and didn't respect the audience's expertise. Information density matters. Authoritative references matter. Depth signals credibility.

The analogy Marcel used: build the content like a study guide. Start with what the reader needs to know before they can understand the more complex topics. This is especially important for clients with no existing content — they're starting from zero and the sequence of what gets published first shapes how the audience perceives the brand.

### Topic Selection: Broad vs. Narrow

Marcel clarified what "broad" means in this context — it's not about topic size, it's about actionability and specificity. A broad topic is one that doesn't give the reader something specific to do or think about. A narrow topic is concrete, has a clear scope, and delivers real value. For technical audiences, concrete and narrow is almost always better. The group discussed this in the context of AI agents content — many common topic ideas are too vague to be useful.

### Content Strategy Presentation Structure

William walked through a content strategy presentation for a client. Marcel's feedback: the current structure spends too much time on competitor and keyword research before delivering the core recommendations. This "buries the lead." The fix: lead with the core content clusters — what you're going to build and why it matters — then bring in the supporting analysis as justification. Clients engage better when they see the destination first. The detailed research is reassuring backup, not the opening argument.

William acknowledged the tension: detailed research builds trust with clients who are evaluating whether GrowthX knows their space. Marcel's counterpoint: trust comes from showing you understand their problem, not from the volume of slides. Clusters first, research second.

### Spendesk: Audit-First, Refresh-Heavy Strategy

Spendesk's situation: significant existing content library, organic traffic in free fall, goal to increase organic leads from ~600 to ~800/month within six months. Marcel's guidance:

1. **Get Google Search Console access immediately** — you cannot do a proper audit without URL-level traffic data. GSC shows what's dropping, how much, and why.
2. **Measure loss by section** — identify the top pages driving traffic decline. A few key pages usually account for most of the drop.
3. **Prioritize refreshes over net-new** — for a site with lots of existing content in a competitive space, refreshing high-value pages that have decayed is more impactful than creating new surface area.
4. **Qualitative review** — don't just look at the numbers. Open the pages. Assess quality, relevance, and alignment with current market expectations.

William planned to follow up with Spendesk to secure full GSC access and then rebalance the strategy accordingly.

### Balancing Deep Technical Content with Volume

For clients targeting technical audiences, Marcel advised against the false choice between "deep technical content" and "volume." You can produce both — the key is that even the volume pieces (listicles, roundups, guides) must be genuinely curated and information-dense. He cited Tech Beacon's conference listicles as a model: they were highly curated, methodology-transparent, and genuinely useful — not generic link dumps. Quality listicles build the same credibility as deep technical articles, just through different means.

The editorial pipeline is the differentiator. If your pipeline can't produce curated, high-quality content at volume, you produce fluff. Fluff destroys credibility with expert audiences faster than no content at all.

### Live Blog Post Review

Marcel reviewed an actual client blog post live on the call (Black Soul's blog). Key critique points:
- The opening paragraph doesn't state the problem clearly — reader doesn't know within the first few sentences what they're going to learn or why it matters
- Too verbose — ideas buried in long sentences
- Tone doesn't respect the reader's intelligence
- Lacks "bottom line up front" structure

Marcel's standard: if he can look at a piece of content and within five seconds not know what he's reading, it fails. The opening must state the problem, establish relevance, and deliver value fast.
