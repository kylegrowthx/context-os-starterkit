## Key Topics

### Competitive landscape and content strategy

Tyler walked through GrowthX's analysis of Nerdio's competitive landscape. Key observations: Citrix dominates non-branded keywords, but Nerdio has opportunities in long-tail and comparison content. Liquidware and ControlUp are more MSP-focused, with traffic Nerdio could partially capture. Competitors haven't invested heavily in AEO.

Ashley clarified a nuance: Nerdio doesn't compete with Citrix directly. The competitive motion is helping organizations migrate *off* Citrix and other legacy VDI platforms *onto* Microsoft cloud. Microsoft itself also wants customers off legacy VDI but won't lead a competitive campaign against Citrix — Nerdio fills that gap.

Brian flagged that Microsoft is pushing Windows 365 hard as their hero SKU, and search volume for "Windows 365" is close to overall virtual desktop search volume. Tyler agreed this is a priority content area to capture. (Note: Windows 365 is distinct from Microsoft 365 — both are relevant to Nerdio but Windows 365 is more specific to the MSP/enterprise desktop virtualization space.)

Tyler proposed a content model covering the full cloud desktop lifecycle for IT leaders: evaluating → migrating → deploying → optimizing → expanding — filling gaps in Microsoft documentation and competitor content at each stage.

### Microsoft partnership positioning

Ashley made a point Tyler found compelling: Nerdio should be positioned *alongside* Microsoft, not just against Microsoft's competitors. People are going to think of Microsoft first in this space, and Nerdio needs to be thought of alongside them — it's their strongest partner. Tyler agreed this is a unique differentiator that most competitors don't have.

### Pricing and contract terms

Tyler was transparent about pricing without being prompted: $40k for the 8-week strategy sprint, then $18k/month for growth execution. The strategy sprint gives Nerdio the ability to opt out before the 12-month term kicks in.

Brian asked about what determines whether it's $18k or more/less. Tyler explained the "work stream" model: one work stream = 5 editorial articles/week. Adding programmatic or custom work streams (like SentinelOne's vulnerability database) runs $12k–$18k each. GrowthX can also switch between editorial and programmatic within the same cost structure.

Brian noted SentinelOne had three work streams, which affected pricing. Tyler invited Nerdio to propose redlines and discounts when reviewing the contract, and committed to discussing with Marcel.

The 8-week sprint replaces the old 3-month opt-out window from previous contracts. After the sprint, the 12-month term only terminates for breach — not convenience. Tyler explained this is tied to how GrowthX is measured on software metrics for VC purposes.

### Workflow and bottlenecks

Brian flagged two issues from the SentinelOne engagement that caused friction: (1) product marketing wanted to review everything in detail and was slow, and (2) the client team was manually posting all content rather than giving GrowthX CMS access.

Lane confirmed Nerdio is setting up CMS permissions to give GrowthX direct publishing access. Ashley noted their current content consultant Chris is closely involved and may need to be brought into the strategy process to build confidence. Her hope is that once traction is established, the review process can be lightened.

Brian suggested aligning with Chris on a quality threshold model — where content above a certain score threshold doesn't need full review. Tyler confirmed that's how GrowthX thinks about it internally: articles are evaluated at each step in the workflow, a human editor reviews and signs off, and GrowthX does 2–3 rounds of review early, then drops to 1 round after about 10 published articles.

Tyler clarified that scores are used internally to decide if each workflow step is complete — they don't share numeric scores externally. The human editor's judgment is the final gate before articles go to the client.

### AI and content quality

Veronica expressed cautious optimism about AI-generated content for a technical space like Nerdio's. She acknowledged that Nerdio has deep SMEs in the space, and general AI hasn't fully mastered the nuance. Tyler acknowledged this directly: "We're not going to write like your subject matter experts on day one." But the team's strength is calibrating fast, using the client's best existing content to train workflows.

Veronica noted Nerdio actually has a lot of strong SME content, which makes training the AI workflows easier. Tyler also mentioned that GrowthX can handle the Microsoft partnership content they currently receive (glossary articles, referral articles) — either as standard scope or published directly in the CMS alongside organic content.
