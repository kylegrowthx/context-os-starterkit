## Key Topics

### Content Bandwidth and Strategic Decisions

The RealReal made a deliberate choice to prioritize Substack over their internal blog because of limited editorial capacity. Kristen noted that their blog now relies on third-party content from a freelance company, which appears on a blog subdomain and gets decent traffic but lacks The RealReal's brand voice and editorial standard. Meanwhile, their internal blog still surfaces outdated content prominently. The core opportunity is enabling the team to serve both channels without hiring additional writers.

### Overcoming Internal Resistance to AI

Kristen mentioned pushing back against her editorial team's (especially Sarah's) skepticism about AI in content. Her team cited the Sports Illustrated AI writer scandal as a cautionary tale, but Kristen reframed the conversation: AI as augmentation, not replacement. She's currently using AI in her graduate school work and saw firsthand how it enabled her to start from 25% instead of zero, freeing up time for higher-level thinking. Her goal is to educate internally that AI can be an empowerment tool while maintaining authenticity and brand standards.

### GrowthX's Augmentation and Calibration Framework

Marcel outlined the core GrowthX approach: don't automate editorial, amplify expert thinking. The idea is to interview Sarah and her team about their research process, source selection, filtering logic, and trend identification — then build workflows that replicate that expertise at scale (10x the output, not 1000x automation). This requires three phases of artifacts: defining the publication's voice and brand, creating audience personas and editorial "archetypes," and identifying data sources (APIs, forums, Substack, Reddit, etc.). Derek Banas (former Shopify columns head, now GrowthX managing director) validates outputs, iteratively improving quality toward 80%+ while flagging what needs adjustment.

### Existing Content Audit and "Brand Police"

Kristen asked if GrowthX could audit The RealReal's existing blog content and recommend improvements without full rewrites. Marcel showed a "fact-checker" workflow that flags inaccurate statements and rewrites with citations. The same model could become a "brand police" system that checks for on-brand tone, relevance, and structure. Marcel also noted that analyzing 100 pieces of content with a 30% improvement recommendation could save The RealReal's team hundreds of hours and identify which older posts (like the five-year-old Telfar article that keeps trending) should be refreshed or removed.

### Research Sourcing and Platform Constraints

Kristen asked how GrowthX crawls "walled gardens" — can you pull from TikTok, Instagram, Substack, Reddit? Marcel explained the pragmatic approach: if there's an API, yes. If a platform is completely closed (no scraping, no API access, like TikTok currently), it's harder and lower priority. For Substack, most content is accessible; paywalled content can be purchased if needed. Kristen identified fashion Substacks as a key trend vector (e.g., Serial Aisle by Leandra Cohen) and offered to compile a list to share. This led to a discussion about monitoring influencer content as a way to identify and validate trends early.

### Visibility and SEO as Justification

Kristen raised an important internal question: does investing in blog content actually drive visibility and SEO value for The RealReal? She's concerned about having to justify the project internally, especially if she needs to allocate resources to Sarah's editorial team. Kyle brought up another angle: synthesizing The RealReal's own first-party data (buying patterns, search patterns, latest trends) into editorial insights could make content more relevant and tie blog output to business impact. This is a synth workflow that can be optimized with AI but requires The RealReal's data and editorial judgment.
