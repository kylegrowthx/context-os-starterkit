## Summary

Darrell Etherington (GrowthX) conducted a discovery interview with Victor Ronin, who is a software architect at Aembit, a company focused on application authentication and identity management for Fortune 500 enterprises. Victor shared his 20+ year security background, Aembit's evolution from a rough initial concept to a refined product staying close to its original vision, and identified a critical go-to-market gap: while the company effectively targets security teams (CISOs), it needs to build mindshare with engineers and DevOps practitioners who are the actual implementers and gatekeepers. The conversation revealed that successful engineer-focused content requires technical depth, novelty, conciseness, and clarity — qualities that differ fundamentally from traditional marketing, and Victor suggested exploring open-source offerings as a path to engineer adoption (following the HashiCorp model).

## Key Topics

### Victor's Background and Role at Aembit

  - 20+ year security career starting with mobile security in Ukraine, contracting for Trust Digital where he met Aembit co-founders Kevin Saab and David Cory
  - Moved to US and worked with Saab and Cory across 7-8 companies through acquisitions and multiple ventures
  - Joined Aembit from day one as a developer; as the team grew, became manager and team lead
  - About 9 months ago stepped back from management to focus on software architecture and development
  - Now serves as self-appointed liaison between engineering and marketing, writes blog articles (for 10-15 years, English-only for past 5 years with Grammarly and AI assistance)
  - Title: Software Architect; day-to-day involves architecture, development, close partnership with marketing on content

### Aembit's Evolution and Market Fit

  - Initial concept was rough; Victor took a month-long sabbatical before joining, came back restless, asked Kevin and David if they were planning something, and signed on before the full idea was formed
  - The founding vision was narrow: focus on application authentication and identity, but everything else was "three slides of a pitch deck without clear vision"
  - Over 4 years, the product refined significantly while staying close to the original statement, with Victor heavily involved in groundwork, prototyping, and closing technical gaps
  - Victor works "in the trenches" solving specific customer problems: customers struggle to articulate needs around API key management and secret rotation; many default to Vault without understanding its limitations
  - Aembit's education process: (1) help customers articulate the problem, (2) show why existing solutions (Vault, etc.) are incomplete, (3) explain Aembit's advantages in A, B, C ways, (4) close gaps to fit into the customer's specific environment

### Client Environments and Integration Challenges

  - Most customers are Fortune 500 enterprises with heterogeneous "everything" environments: VMs, on-prem, cloud, weird legacy stuff nobody bothers to rip out
  - Repeating customer requests: Kubernetes (including OpenShift variants), AWS Lambda, Windows support, VM support
  - Example: customer says "we need OpenShift Kubernetes support," but Aembit initially only supported generic Kubernetes. Victor and the team researched OpenShift-specific config, turned a few knobs, changed code slightly, and added OpenShift support
  - Team prioritizes features based on hearing the same request from multiple prospects; Kevin (Product) and David Walker (Technical Leadership) track these patterns
  - As a startup, Aembit must fit into customer environments rather than asking customers to adjust around them — the power dynamic doesn't allow Aembit to dictate terms yet (unlike Okta at billion-dollar valuation)

### Marketing and Communication Strategies

  - Current focus on security professionals (CISOs) is effective; Aembit has found a way to talk to them using threat modeling, risk reduction language
  - Critical gap: engineers and DevOps practitioners are the actual implementers and gatekeepers who can block adoption, yet are largely ignored by current marketing
  - Why engineers matter: when a CISO approves a tool, they ask "Who should work on this?" The team of engineers becomes the implementation gatekeeper. Engineers can easily say "we're too busy, come back in 3.5 weeks" and indefinitely delay adoption
  - Engineer-focused content needs different characteristics: deeply technical (not marketing fluff), novelty, conciseness, and clarity — "almost antithesis of marketing"
  - Examples of bad engineer content: jargon-heavy, overly florid, implying context that should be explicit, designed for people already 3 years deep in the topic
  - Example of good engineer content (per Victor): his recent AI authentication/authorization article, written for someone with no background, explaining basic components, how they talk, adding MTP servers as new components, showing how they interact — progressive knowledge-building
  - Challenge: marketing teams struggle to produce this material; it must come from technical people (engineers, architects, founders)
  - Open-source strategy: Exploring open-source offerings to build engineer mindshare (following HashiCorp model: free tools build familiarity and adoption, which converts to enterprise paid adoption)

### Victor's Passion Projects

  - Most exciting part of job: solving complex problems compactly. Example: a project that felt like a one-dev-year endeavor became 3x more compact through whiteboard architecture (no coding, just thinking). Instead of 6 people for 9 months, solved with 2 people in 1.5 months.
  - Loves writing technical blog articles to share knowledge in easily consumable ways, especially when learning something new
  - Recent AI authentication/authorization article written for backend engineers without 3 years of AI experience, progressively building knowledge through basic components, interactions, new additions (MTP servers), and emergent behaviors — goal was to compress 3 weeks of learning into 1 hour of reading
  - Approach to technical writing: novelty, conciseness, clarity — three qualities that are almost the opposite of marketing
  - Passion: building knowledge-progressive content that helps "average persons" (not experts) understand deeply technical topics
