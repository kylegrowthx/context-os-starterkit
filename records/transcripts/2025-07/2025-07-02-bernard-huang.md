## Summary

Bernard Huang (CEO of Clearscope) and Jason Gong (GrowthX) discussed Clearscope's 40% revenue decline from its peak, driven by AI commoditization of SEO visibility tracking, and the company's strategy to compete with Surfer by matching their $99 price point, bundling AI drafting features, launching topic clustering, and rolling out an affiliate program. Both founders reflected on the existential challenge facing content-focused businesses: creating content for AI to consume, raising questions about sustainable business models as publishers struggle and AI training data becomes scarce. Jason shared GrowthX's bet on productized services rather than pure software, while Bernard grappled with depression tied to nine years of trying to scale Clearscope through market shifts.

---

## Context

Bernard Huang founded Clearscope nine years ago as a bootstrapped SEO tool company focused on content visibility tracking. The company achieved strong product-market fit but failed to scale through organizational and leadership challenges. Jason Gong worked on AI products (including Kite, an AI code assistant acquired by Google) and is now CEO of GrowthX, a B2B content marketing and productized services firm. The two have an existing relationship from the San Francisco tech community and catch up periodically on their respective company challenges. This conversation occurred during a period when both were grappling with how AI disruption is reshaping their industries: for Bernard, AI has commoditized the core SEO visibility metrics Clearscope was built to offer; for Jason, it's forcing GrowthX to shift strategy from selling software tools to offering done-for-you services.

## Key Topics

### Personal Updates

  - Jason now lives in Oakland with his Canadian wife (who has a Chinese passport) and 2-year-old son (the only American in the family). Jason is Canadian and considers himself "safe" from deportation concerns.
  - Bernard moved to Austin nine years ago with co-founder for tax benefits (no state income tax vs. California) and because the founding team all grew up in Texas and attended UT Austin
  - Bernard openly discusses struggling with depression and burnout tied to nine years of trying to scale Clearscope; he questions whether he's been a liability to the company despite building it from scratch

### Company Updates

  - **Clearscope:** Facing 40% revenue decline from peak due to AI commoditization and missing the GPT/AI adoption wave when the market shifted. Company has achieved organizational stability with a new COO hired by Bernard's co-founder.
  - **Clearscope's Recovery Plan:** Launch marketing campaign and feature blitz including AI drafting wizard (integrated into editor), topic clustering, LLM visibility tracking, price match Surfer at $99/month, and affiliate program with higher commissions than Surfer to poach affiliates
  - **Clearscope's Positioning Challenge:** Core customer base (SEOs, content creators) targets same audience as Surfer. Bernard acknowledges Surfer has "more bells and whistles" but questions whether new features are actually useful. Both he and Jason see this as race to the bottom in a shrinking market.
  - **GrowthX.AI:** Pursuing productized services model (GrowthX does content work for clients rather than selling software) and finding success in delivering value. Jason notes their team still uses Surfer internally despite building automation tools, showing the gap between what customers want and what's theoretically more efficient.

### Industry Trends and Challenges

  - **SEO Decline:** SEO is becoming a "ghost channel" with people getting "much traffic at all." The industry is in a feedback loop where AI writes content for AI to summarize back to end users (retrieval augmented generation), making the original content less discoverable or valuable.
  - **Content Commoditization:** Current model of AI writing content for AI to consume is "circular" and "feels rather meaningless." Bernard sees the publisher model as unsustainable — either publishers collapse or AI labs (OpenAI, etc.) buy them for training data and editorial integrity.
  - **Cloudflare HTTP 402 Status Code:** Bernard discussed Cloudflare's recent pay-per-crawl proposal using HTTP 402 (payment required), but noted it's a prisoner's dilemma: sites won't block AI crawlers individually because they fear losing citation/mention benefits, so collective action is needed.
  - **Labor Market and Societal Risk:** Bernard is pessimistic about AI's net societal impact. Workers aged 30-50+ without AI fluency will face job displacement. Self-driving vehicles will eliminate delivery/rideshare jobs. Widening gap between technocrats and have-nots will increase potential for "riots and things burned down." Geopolitical instability could accelerate as countries resort to external conflict to manage internal economic unrest.

### Austin vs. San Francisco

  - **Austin's Appeal:** Bernard rates Austin "7 out of 10" across most categories — food (7/10), affordability (7/10), weather (7/10), nature (5/10). It's consistent and solid across categories rather than exceptional in any one area. Summer 2025 was cooler than typical due to freak thunderstorms and hailstorms.
  - **San Francisco's Recovery:** Jason reports SF is "getting back there" post-COVID — significantly cleaner, fewer tents everywhere, shops reopening, people returning to Union Square (which was "dystopian tent land" during peak COVID). Jason lives on the east side of South Park, near Blue Bottle Coffee, between Second and Fourth streets.
  - **Cost/Lifestyle Trade-offs:** Bernard would consider moving back to SF only for venture-backed YC-eligible startups requiring Silicon Valley access. Otherwise the cost ($1000+ rent for shoebox apartments) and California taxes make it prohibitive. Bernard personally enjoys walking everywhere and the density of "brilliant, hardworking, ambitious people" and quality conversation in SF, but prioritizes tax efficiency and life stability in Austin.
  - **Geopolitical Undertones:** Jason is Canadian (safe status) and his Chinese-passport-holding wife is "hopefully also safe from being deported," reflecting subtle anxiety about geopolitical tensions and immigration policy uncertainty affecting tech workers.
