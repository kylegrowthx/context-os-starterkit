## Key Topics

### Auth0 Background and Pushp's Journey

  - Pushp discovered Auth0 in 2014 as a customer at PwC, reached out to founder Eugenio, and helped sell Auth0 into PwC before joining as a solution engineer in May 2016
  - Early years (2016-2017): handled all customer types from smallest to largest due to small SE team at early-stage startup
  - 2017-2018 evolution: shifted to larger enterprise customers, six-figure deals, diverse use cases including fintech
  - Worked across many customer segments with sales leaders Rachel, TIFF, and others on technical and architectural sides
  - Current role: oversight of specialist teams, internal team support, high-level customer engagement—more strategic and high-level than in-the-weeds technical work

### Sales Process and Onboarding

  - Typical sales cycle: inbound or outbound lead → ADR qualification → AE qualification → discovery meetings → demo or POC → Q&A technical questions → technical win decision → procurement → contract signature
  - Post-sale handoff depends on deal size: larger deals often include professional services transition, smaller deals go directly to TAM (Technical Account Manager) for ongoing support
  - B2C onboarding: self-service sign-up, self-service password recovery
  - B2B/SaaS onboarding: varies widely—often invitation-based or uses federation (SSO), already configured at setup
  - Example: Jersey Mike's loyalty program uses Auth0 with simple sign-up flow to Auth0 login page

### Product Positioning and Challenges

  - Biggest misconception: Auth0 is only a developer platform, lacking enterprise-readiness. Pushp says Auth0 needs better perception management and actual feature work to address this gap
  - Key missing features customers request: delegated administration for organizations (especially B2B SaaS), post-identifier hooks (technical), audit logging capabilities
  - Improvement priorities: audit logging, delegated administration, vertical-specific (industry-tailored) solutions, stronger enterprise positioning story
  - Opportunity: Auth0 can remain developer-friendly while expanding enterprise coverage—but needs both perception work and actual product improvements
  - Value proposition: "We secure login for most brands you interact with daily"—concrete examples: IKEA, Dick's Sporting Goods, Peloton, utility companies, AI platforms

### Competition and Market Dynamics

  - B2B SaaS: WorkOS is the primary competitor
  - B2C and broader enterprise: Okta SIAM, Ping, ForgeRock are major competitors; Azure B2C increasingly relevant
  - No single dominant competitor—competitive landscape varies significantly by use case, customer segment, and buying patterns
  - Customers come from: dissatisfied legacy vendor implementations, contract renewals, in-house solutions being modernized, specific new functionality needs, SIAM platform searches

### Future Investments and AI Integration

  - Auth0 is already investing in improving SaaS features; needs to double down on B2C capabilities and increase overall platform depth
  - Major future opportunity: AI integration and AI agents. Pushp expects significant uptake of AI agents; Auth0 needs to define what makes sense in identity/auth space
  - R&D team actively researches emerging trends and develops solutions; product team then takes these and manages go-to-market. Field then learns via features, documentation, and experimentation
  - Structure at Auth0: R&D discovers trends → Product builds solutions → Marketing/Sales creates presentations and documentation → Field learns and shares with customers
  - Pushp's recommendation: GrowthX should sign up for Auth0 trial to better understand product mechanics before creating enable content; it's deeply developer-focused and requires hands-on experience
