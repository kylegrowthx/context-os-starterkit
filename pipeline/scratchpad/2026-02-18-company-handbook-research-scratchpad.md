## Frameworks Discovered

### 1. Handbook-First Approach (GitLab)
**Source:** GitLab Handbook
- Document solutions BEFORE announcing via Slack/email
- Single source of truth prevents duplication
- Changes made via merge requests, then announced
- Public by default, sensitive info separate

### 2. Principles Over Policies
**Source:** Pebb.io, multiple
- Policies tell you a rule; principles tell you HOW TO THINK
- Gives employees autonomy when no specific rule exists
- Example: Instead of "No personal calls during work hours" → "We trust you to manage your time effectively"

### 3. Human-First vs Legal-First
**Source:** Pebb.io
- Traditional: Written by lawyers for lawyers, feels like a threat
- Modern: Written for new employees on day one (hopeful but nervous)
- Shift from defensive compliance to culture-building trust

### 4. 6-Week Cycles (Basecamp)
**Source:** Basecamp Handbook
- 6 weeks of focused work
- 2-week cooldown for bugs, docs, planning
- Prevents project bloat
- Creates sustainable urgency

### 5. Departmental Organization (GitLab)
**Source:** GitLab Handbook
- Company → People → Product → Engineering → Security → Marketing → Sales → Finance
- Each department owns its section
- Works for 2000+ pages

### 6. Living Document Philosophy
**Source:** Multiple
- Regular updates, not annual rewrites
- Editable by employees (Valve, Sourcegraph)
- Version control via Git
- Announce changes with diffs

## Patterns Observed

### What Great Handbooks Have in Common

1. **Publicly available** — Transparency as a value, recruiting tool
2. **Editable by employees** — Not HR-owned, living document
3. **Honest tone** — Real practices, not idealistic prescriptions
4. **Principles > rules** — Trust over control
5. **Version controlled** — Git/GitHub for tracking changes
6. **Culture + operations** — Not just policies, also "how we work"
7. **Onboarding-focused** — Written for day-one employees
8. **Updated frequently** — Living document, not annual refresh
9. **Department-owned sections** — Engineering writes engineering, etc.
10. **Search-optimized** — Easy to find what you need

### What Makes Handbooks Fail

1. Written by lawyers for lawyers
2. Too long, no one reads it
3. Outdated (last updated 3 years ago)
4. Jargon and acronyms new employees don't know
5. Rigid policies that box you in
6. Creating accidental employment contracts
7. Overly formal/cold tone
8. No maintenance process
9. Hidden in a PDF no one can find
10. Tries to cover every possible scenario
