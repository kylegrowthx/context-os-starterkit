## The shift

AI is the biggest single shift in how buyers behave and make decisions. Potentially as big as the Internet itself.

When someone researches a product or vendor today, they increasingly interact with AI agents that form opinions on their behalf and fetch information to support those opinions. The mechanism of discovery has changed.

Brands used to optimize for one audience. Now there are three:

1. **Buyers.** Getting harder to reach. Competing with more noise than ever. Increasingly trusting what AI tells them before they visit a single website.
2. **AI agents.** Acting on behalf of buyers, forming recommendations, synthesizing hundreds of sources into a single answer. If you're not in that answer, you don't exist.
3. **Training bots.** Crawling your site continuously. Learning what your brand stands for. Encoding that into model behavior that shapes future recommendations.

This shift is inevitable. Every company on the planet is affected. If you build a company around an inevitable shift, that's a good place to start.

## What every company wants

Strip it back. Every company wants two things: growth and leverage. Grow faster. Do it with less. More output, fewer people, lower cost per result.

That hasn't changed. What's changed is what you have to do to get there.

## Why the website

The website is the biggest single driver of growth for B2B companies right now. Not the most important channel. The most strategic asset.

Here's why:

- It influences buyers who come through the front door.
- It's where AI agents fetch information about you.
- It's the one thing you can control. You can change every page on your site tomorrow.
- Most B2B transactions still flow through it.
- If done right, it compounds.

B2B companies have treated websites like a publishing channel for years. E-commerce figured this out long ago. Amazon doesn't see pages as pages. It sees a catalog of assets. Billions of dollars poured into enriching, de-duplicating, and optimizing that catalog because it drives discoverability, conversion, and loyalty. The Amazon flywheel is built on product pages.

B2B is catching up fast. But the demands are accelerating faster.

### More pages, faster decay

The number of pages required to be visible is exploding. An AI search result doesn't return 10 blue links. It pulls from 20 to 200 to 500 sources. To show up in those answers, you need more surface area. More pages, more content, covering more of the long tail than was ever necessary before.

And those pages are decaying faster. AI systems always look for fresh, high-quality sources. The half-life of a stale page is getting shorter. Leave a page alone and it fades.

The website went from important to existential. The job of keeping it healthy is no longer something a small team can manage manually.

### What companies face today

Most B2B companies can't manage their websites well. CMSs are a mess. Analytics are broken or buried in noise. There's no one with both the strategic clarity to know what to build and the operational capacity to build it at scale. They're adding AI tools that create more confusion, not less. They don't know what's actually driving results.

This is nearly universal. It's true for startups and it's true for billion-dollar companies. It's what we see across every customer we serve.

## Three jobs

Every website comes down to the same three jobs:

**Manage pages.** Keep them organized, current, healthy, and performing. Know what's working, what's broken, and what needs attention before it decays. This is the job most companies fail at first. They don't know what they have, let alone whether it's working.

**Create pages.** Expand the surface area of influence for buyers, AI agents, and training data. More answers for more questions. More citations. More signals teaching the models what your brand stands for. The long tail is getting longer and the brands creating the most surface area are winning.

**Optimize pages.** Every page, all the time. Fresh, relevant, high quality, converting. Not once. Not on a schedule. Continuously. A page you published six months ago and never touched is working against you.

These three jobs are the work. Everything we build and everything we deliver ladders up to doing them better, faster, and at a scale that would be impossible without technology.

## Why AI isn't optional

There is no way to do manage, create, and optimize at scale without AI as a force multiplier.

The long tail is getting longer. The pace of decay is accelerating. The number of pages required to compete is growing faster than any team can produce manually. Our best customers already prove this. Lovable went from 5 guides per week to 25. That's only possible with an AI-powered workflow. Without it, you either don't publish or you sacrifice quality.

The promised land isn't robots replacing people. It's a better division of labor:

- AI handles production, optimization, and learning at scale.
- Humans guide strategy, quality, and judgment where it matters.
- The system gets smarter with every page, every week.
- Growth compounds across all three audiences: buyers, agents, and bots.

That's what we're building toward.

## What we built

We built four layers. Each solves a different part of the problem. Together, they compound.

### Infrastructure

The operational primitives that make scale possible. Output AI is our agentic workflow framework. It powers execution across every piece of work we produce, for every customer. It's what makes it possible to publish 25 guides a week for one client while running 70+ others simultaneously. We're open-sourcing it because the framework is genuinely good and the developer community needs it.

### Data

The visibility layer. CheckThat is the first AI visibility index for B2B. It measures how AI engines see, mention, and recommend brands across categories. This data is some of the most important available right now because AI agents are the audience that shapes buyer decisions before a human ever gets involved. CheckThat is both a product and a lead-generation engine for everything else we do.

### Platform

The operating system for website-driven growth. The OS (formerly Atlas, now being rewritten) is where the three jobs come together: manage, create, optimize, and continuously learn and improve. When the platform is in a client's hands, they feel the compounding. They see what's working, what's decaying, and what to do next. When it's not, they see five Google Docs a week and wonder why they're paying.

The platform now decouples from the service. Some companies want platform-only. Others want full managed service with a pod, an engagement manager, and a team keeping them honest. Most land somewhere in between. The spectrum is open.

### Services

The strategy and forcing function layer. This is the human expertise that guides the system. Strategy sprints that set direction. Engagement managers who keep customers honest. Domain experts who catch what AI misses. The service layer is what turns a platform into outcomes.

Not every company needs the same amount. The platform decoupling means we can serve both: companies that want a self-serve tool and companies that want everything done for them. The value of the service is the judgment, the forcing function, and the strategic clarity that technology alone can't provide.

### Distribution

Above all four layers: the community. AI-led growth education and demand generation at scale. This is the distribution engine that feeds awareness, builds trust, and drives demand into CheckThat (which feeds the OS, which feeds services). The flywheel starts here.

## Why we win

The most powerful business models build compounding advantages that grow stronger over time. We have four moats, and they reinforce each other.

### Brand moat

Trust compounds. Results speak for themselves.

When we do great work, customers tell everyone. Word of mouth has driven nearly all our growth. That's not an accident. It's the byproduct of being the best hire our customers ever made. Pedro from Tavern on the Team sending a WhatsApp saying "I don't know what to do with so much traffic" and referring his fiance's startup. That's the brand moat at work.

### Scale moat

Unit economics improve over time.

Each project teaches our workflows something new. Each client makes our systems smarter. The marginal cost of serving the next customer drops while quality stays high or improves. This is already visible: Lovable at 25 guides per week, Augment Code at $50K per month, both running on the same infrastructure that serves every other client.

### Embedding moat

Switching costs increase over time.

As the platform adapts to a customer's voice, context, and domain, leaving becomes expensive. They'd lose the accumulated intelligence, the compounding learning, the context that makes every output better than the last. The faster pages decay, the more valuable a system that continuously learns and improves becomes. That's a moat that deepens with every week of service.

### Network moat

Knowledge compounds across clients.

What we learn from one engagement improves outcomes for all engagements. Cross-client patterns feed our AI. Our AI attracts better experts. Better experts produce better insights. The dataset we're building on how AI sees brands across hundreds of categories can't be recreated by a competitor starting today.

## Strategic priorities

Three priorities for 2026:

**1. Make the platform the OS for website-driven growth.**

The platform should enable manage, create, optimize for any company, regardless of how much service they need on top of it. That's the product. That's the moat. When a client can log in, see what's working, see what's decaying, and know exactly what to do next, they're never leaving.

**2. Build the best dataset on how AI sees brands.**

AI agents are a new audience and we have more data on how they perceive B2B companies than anyone. That data compounds. It informs the platform. It informs every piece of client work. And it's going to matter more, not less, over time. CheckThat is the product. The dataset is the moat.

**3. Make Output AI the best framework for developers building agentic workflows.**

The infrastructure we built to power our own work is genuinely good. Opening it to the developer community extends our reach and builds a different kind of defensibility. When external developers build on Output AI, our infrastructure improves, our recruiting pipeline deepens, and our technical credibility grows.

We're not burning money to get there. We're building infrastructure, data, platform, and community while staying efficient on burn. We own our own destiny. Minimal dilution. We've raised very little money to get here because we're building a generational company, not a fundraising story.
