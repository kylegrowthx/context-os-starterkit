# Account Health Scoring Rubric

Andi Bailey's rubric, embedded verbatim. Apply exactly as written.

---

## Independence Rules — CRITICAL

**The Problem:** EMs allow one bad dimension to drag down ALL scores. If a client relationship is rocky, suddenly Content Quality, Performance, and Strategy all get scored lower — even when content quality is actually fine and performance data is trending up.

**The Rule:** Each dimension measures ONE specific thing. Score it based ONLY on the criteria for that dimension. A bad relationship does NOT mean bad content quality.

**The Test:** Before submitting scores, ask yourself: "If I cover up all other scores, does THIS score still make sense based ONLY on THIS dimension's criteria?"

## Scoring Thresholds

| Score | What It Means |
|-------|---------------|
| **1 — Critical** | Active risk. Immediate director intervention needed. Churn is likely without action. |
| **2 — Concerning** | Below expectations. Needs attention this week. Trending toward risk. |
| **3 — Functional** | Meeting minimum bar. Not a crisis, but not strong. Room for improvement. |
| **4 — Strong** | Exceeding baseline. Healthy in this dimension. Minor improvements possible. |
| **5 — Exceptional** | Best-in-class for this dimension. A model for what great looks like. |

## Low Scores → Action

- **Any score <= 2:** Director ticket gets created next cycle. Include your "why" note and any Red Flag indicators present.
- **Any score drop >= 1 point:** Flag in weekly update with explanation. Director reviews trend.
- **Red Flag present:** Document in notes regardless of score. Red Flags are early warning signals — they may not have impacted the score YET, but they signal incoming risk.

## 1. RELATIONSHIP

**What It Measures:** Strength of our relationship with key stakeholders — trust, communication quality, and partnership dynamic.

**Why It Matters:** Relationship strength is the leading indicator of retention. When clients trust us as partners (not vendors), they give us grace during rough patches, share strategic context proactively, and advocate for renewals internally. Weak relationships mean every stumble becomes a churn risk.

### Scoring Rubric

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1 — Critical** | Bad or no relationship. They don't trust us. Actively adversarial or disengaged. High churn risk. Communication has broken down. |
| **2 — Concerning** | Strained relationship. Client is polite but frustrated. Trust is eroding. They're questioning our value or going silent between syncs. |
| **3 — Functional** | Functional but transactional. Polite meetings, predictable conversations. No real partnership energy. They see us as a vendor, not a partner. |
| **4 — Strong** | Good trust and rapport. Client is open, shares context, and engages collaboratively. They see us as valuable. Minor friction is handled constructively. |
| **5 — Exceptional** | Full trust. They proactively share ideas, backchannel with us, seek our opinion on strategic decisions. We're partners. They'd advocate for us internally. |

**Self-Check:** Is there anything we can do to improve the relationship? Does this person trust me like a friend? Would they recommend us to a peer?

### Red Flags
- Decision Maker stops attending calls
- New Decision-maker hired, or key teammates transition out
- We find out other agencies are involved (not previously disclosed)
- Client goes silent between syncs — no Slack, no async communication
- Client starts CC'ing leadership on routine emails (escalation signal)
- Tone shifts from collaborative to directive or combative

### Green Flags
- Decision Maker consistently attends and is engaged on calls
- Client proactively shares company news, roadmap changes, or strategic context
- Client introduces us to new stakeholders as "our content partners"
- Async communication (Slack) is active and friendly between syncs
- Client seeks our opinion on decisions beyond content scope
- Client provides referrals or agrees to case studies / testimonials

## 2. QUANTITY / CONTRACTUAL FULFILLMENT

**What It Measures:** Are we delivering what we contractually promised — on time, at the agreed volume?

**Why It Matters:** This is our baseline obligation. Under-delivering on contracted volume is the fastest way to lose trust and give clients a concrete reason to churn. It's also the most objective dimension — either we hit the number or we didn't. Score this based purely on delivery math, NOT on how the client feels about it.

### Scoring Rubric

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1 — Critical** | Significantly under-delivering. Missing contracted quantities by 25%+. Pieces are late or undelivered. We are in breach territory. THIS SHOULD BE A ONE EVEN IF THE CLIENT IS THE BLOCKER. |
| **2 — Concerning** | Under-delivering. Missing 10-25% of contracted volume. Some pieces are delayed. We're falling behind and struggling to catch up — EVEN IF THE CLIENT IS THE BLOCKER. |
| **3 — Functional** | Meeting most deliverables with some inconsistency. Occasional delays but generally on track. No major gaps. |
| **4 — Strong** | Consistently meeting all contracted deliverables on time. Reliable cadence. Client never has to ask "where's my content?" |
| **5 — Exceptional** | Meeting everything AND proactively proposing additional ideas. Ahead of schedule. Client is excited about the pipeline. |

**Self-Check:** If I look at the contract and count deliverables this month, are we on pace? Would the client say we're meeting the deal we made?

### Red Flags
- Publishing is blocked for any reason (CMS issues, approval bottlenecks, resource gaps)
- Site Migration (blocks publishing, causes delays, diverts client attention)
- Approvals start to expand to new teams (PM/PMM) — slowing delivery velocity
- Writer bench issues — can't staff enough quality writers for the account
- Client's internal bandwidth disappears (no reviews returned, no SME access)
- We're consistently delivering pieces late relative to editorial calendar

### Green Flags
- All contracted deliverables on track or ahead of schedule
- Editorial calendar is current and client-approved
- Publishing pipeline flows smoothly — no bottlenecks
- Client's review turnaround is fast (2-5 business days)
- Proactive buffer built into production schedule
- Client expresses interest in increasing volume

## 3. CONTENT QUALITY

**What It Measures:** Is the client happy with the quality of what we produce? How much friction exists in the content review cycle?

**Why It Matters:** Content quality drives client confidence in our expertise. When quality is high, reviews are fast, trust builds, and the engagement runs smoothly. When quality is low, every piece becomes a battle — revision cycles expand, client frustration grows, and the team gets demoralized. Score this based on feedback patterns and review cycle friction, NOT on whether the client likes you personally.

### Scoring Rubric

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1 — Critical** | Very unhappy with quality. Excessive feedback on every piece. Quality meetings needed. Client may be rewriting our work. Trust in our expertise is gone. |
| **2 — Concerning** | Frequent quality concerns. Multiple rounds of heavy edits. Client is losing patience with revision cycles. Calibration feels stuck. |
| **3 — Functional** | Acceptable quality. Some feedback per piece but manageable. Calibration is progressing. Client isn't thrilled but isn't escalating. |
| **4 — Strong** | Good quality with minor, constructive feedback. Reviews are efficient (1-2 light rounds). Client trusts our editorial judgment on most decisions. |
| **5 — Exceptional** | Full trust in quality. Streamlined reviews, minimal feedback, consistent praise. Client approves with little to no changes. We've nailed their voice. |

**Self-Check:** How many rounds of edits does each piece take? Is the client's feedback getting lighter or heavier over time? Are we past calibration?

### Red Flags
- Still calibrating on content after 8+ weeks — lots of edits and feedback with no convergence
- Approvals start to expand to new teams (PM/PMM) — adding reviewers signals dissatisfaction
- Client starts rewriting our drafts instead of providing feedback
- Feedback is vague ("this doesn't feel right") with no actionable direction
- Client requests a quality review meeting or escalates concerns to leadership
- Volume of redline edits is increasing, not decreasing, over time

### Green Flags
- Calibration phase completed — feedback is light and consistent
- Client praises specific pieces or shares them internally/externally
- Review cycles are short (1 round, minimal edits)
- Client trusts us to publish with minimal oversight
- Feedback shifts from corrective to additive (suggestions, not fixes)
- Client references our content positively in calls or Slack

## 4. PERFORMANCE / ROI

**What It Measures:** Are we driving results the client cares about? Are expectations aligned to where we are in the engagement lifecycle?

**Why It Matters:** Performance is ultimately why clients pay us. But performance expectations must be calibrated to engagement stage — a 2-month-old account shouldn't be judged on ROI the same way a 9-month account is. Score this based on whether results match reasonable expectations for the engagement stage, NOT on whether the client is happy with you or the content quality.

**Stage Note:**
- **NEW CLIENTS (0-3 months GE):** Score based on foundation-building — strategy set, publishing regular, early metrics okay, expectations aligned.
- **ESTABLISHED CLIENTS (3+ months GE):** Score based on outcomes — hitting ROI goals, driving business impact, trending in right direction.

### Scoring Rubric

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1 — Critical** | No results to show. Expectations completely misaligned. Client is questioning the investment. We don't know their target KPIs. |
| **2 — Concerning** | Below expected trajectory. Some signals but not enough. Client is getting impatient. We may not know their specific ROI definition. |
| **3 — Functional** | Making progress but not hitting targets yet. Trending in the right direction. Client understands timeline but wants to see acceleration. |
| **4 — Strong** | On track to meet or approaching ROI goals. Clear data showing momentum. Client sees the trajectory and is encouraged. |
| **5 — Exceptional** | Exceeding ROI goals. Driving real business impact (leads, pipeline, revenue). Client attributes measurable outcomes to our work. |

**Self-Check:** Do we know their ROI definition and target number? Are we on track to hit it? If this is a new client, have we set realistic expectations from the start?

### Red Flags
- Client is asking about immediate results or moving the goalpost on timeline expectations
- Product Pivot, change in audience, or rebrand (invalidates current strategy and performance trajectory)
- We don't know the client's target KPI or ROI definition
- Client compares our organic results unfavorably to paid channels
- Client mentions board/leadership pressure on marketing ROI
- Performance metrics are flat or declining after 6+ months of engagement

### Green Flags
- Clear, shared ROI definition and target KPIs documented
- Performance trending up consistently month-over-month
- Client cites our work in their internal reporting or board updates
- Client understands organic timeline and is patient with the ramp
- Content is ranking, driving traffic, and generating measurable leads/pipeline
- Client proactively discusses expanding engagement based on results

## 5. STRATEGY / CONFIDENCE

**What It Measures:** How confident are we (and they) in the 3-6 month plan? Do our actions clearly connect to their desired outcomes?

**Why It Matters:** Strategy confidence determines whether we're proactively driving the engagement or reactively responding to whatever comes up. When strategy is strong, everyone knows the plan, believes in it, and can articulate the connection between our work and their goals. When strategy is weak, we're publishing content without direction and hoping something works. Score this based on plan clarity and alignment, NOT on current results or relationship quality.

### Scoring Rubric

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1 — Critical** | No clear strategy. Publishing but directionless. We can't articulate how our work connects to their goals. Flying blind. |
| **2 — Concerning** | Strategy exists on paper but feels reactive. Not clearly tied to outcomes. Client or team lacks confidence in the plan. Frequent pivots. |
| **3 — Functional** | Strategy exists and is generally agreed upon, but lacks proactivity or excitement. Actions aren't clearly tied to their outcomes. Autopilot mode. |
| **4 — Strong** | Clear strategy with strong alignment. Both sides can articulate the plan and how it connects to goals. Data supports the direction. Proactive adjustments. |
| **5 — Exceptional** | Exceptional clarity and conviction. Everyone is aligned and excited. Clear line of sight from our actions to their desired outcomes. We're driving, not following. |

**Self-Check:** Can I clearly articulate how our current work connects to their business goals in 2 sentences? Does the client share that clarity? Would they describe our strategy the same way?

### Red Flags
- Product Pivot, change in audience, or rebrand (forces strategy reset)
- Acquisition (new ownership may change goals, strategy, or stakeholders entirely)
- Client is asking about immediate results / moving the goalpost (signals strategy misalignment)
- We find out other agencies are involved (strategy may be fragmented across vendors)
- New Decision-maker hired, or key teammates transition (may not share existing strategic alignment)
- Strategy hasn't been revisited or updated in 60+ days despite changing conditions

### Green Flags
- Clear, documented 3-6 month content strategy that both sides reference
- Client proactively asks "what should we do next?" (trusts our strategic lead)
- Strategy meetings are forward-looking, not just status updates
- Data is used to inform strategic pivots — not gut feel or panic
- Client and EM can both articulate the same strategic narrative
- Strategy adapts to new information while maintaining core direction

## Cross-Dimension Red Flag Mapping

Some red flags map to multiple dimensions. Score them in ALL mapped dimensions:

| Red Flag | Mapped To | Rationale |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Decision Maker stops attending calls | Relationship | Direct signal of relationship disengagement |
| New Decision-maker hired / teammates transition | Relationship + Strategy | Affects trust continuity and strategic alignment |
| Approvals expand to new teams (PM/PMM) | Quantity + Content Quality | Slows delivery velocity and signals quality concerns |
| Publishing is blocked for any reason | Quantity | Direct impact on contractual delivery |
| Product Pivot / audience change / rebrand | Performance/ROI + Strategy | Invalidates current trajectory and strategic plan |
| Site Migration | Quantity | Blocks publishing and diverts client attention |
| Client asking about immediate results (goalpost moving) | Performance/ROI + Strategy | Signals expectation misalignment on both dimensions |
| Other agencies involved (not previously disclosed) | Relationship + Strategy | Trust gap and potential strategic fragmentation |
| Still calibrating — lots of edits & feedback | Content Quality | Direct measure of content review friction |
| Acquisition | Strategy | Forces complete strategy reassessment with new ownership |
