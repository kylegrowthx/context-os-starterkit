# Copywriting

Write copy that can't be copied. Copywriting is the number one skill in marketing — it's the wrapper that determines whether a product lives or dies. Snickers and Fuse tasted the same. One is the bestselling chocolate bar in the world. The other was discontinued in 2006. The difference is the copy.

## The Three Rules

Apply these three tests to every sentence. Three nos — you've written rubbish. Three yeses — you're onto something.

### 1. Can I visualize it?

If you can't see it, it's not there yet. People remember concrete images and forget abstract language.

**The memory test:** Read these six words — *seamless transition, charging pitbull, muscly Irishman, better way, leg of lamb, charging*. You'll remember the pitbull, the Irishman, and the leg of lamb. You'll forget the rest. That's the difference between concrete and abstract. Abstract is intangible — you can't drop "a better way" on your foot. You can drop a muscly Irishman on your foot, and it hurts.

**How to go from abstract to concrete — the zoom-in method:**
1. Take a sheet of paper. Write the abstract word at the top.
2. Ask: what do I actually mean by this?
3. Rewrite it. Ask again. Keep going until you land on a concrete object.

Example: "Regain fitness" → "Getting people off the couch" → "Running" → "Running 5K" → **"Couch to 5K"** — the most downloaded fitness program of all time, made by a Boston TV producer, not Nike or Adidas. It stuck because the name is concrete.

**Metonymy** — substituting a literal term for a visual one. "1,000 songs in your media player" is literal. "1,000 songs in your pocket" is metonymy. It doesn't literally make sense, but it's more visual, more surprising, more punchy. Everyone had an iPod. That's why copywriting matters.

### 2. Can I falsify it?

A falsifiable claim is one that can be proved true or false. When you write one, you're putting your head on the chopping block. It makes people sit up. Ears prick.

Galileo got sentenced to house arrest for saying the Earth spins around the Sun — that's falsifiable, that's dangerous. If he'd said "the Earth has a harmonious connection with a celestial object," they'd have told him to go to the pub.

**The "don't talk, only point" technique:**
- Imagine setting up a friend on a blind date.
- Version 1 (subjective): "He's good-looking, intelligent, funny." → Nobody believes you.
- Version 2 (falsifiable): "6'2, looks like Ryan Gosling, reads on the tube." → Now they're interested.

You're pointing at specific, verifiable things instead of trailing adjectives. To sell gold, don't say "it's a great investment" — point at the 50-year price chart. Point at Warren Buffett's portfolio. Let the facts do the work.

### 3. Can nobody else say this?

If your competitor could have written the sentence, cut it. This rule forces you to look deeper at what you're actually selling.

**Volkswagen ad:** "Your car has five numbers on the speedometer. Volvo has six. One could get the impression that the people who made your car lack a little confidence." Only Volkswagen can say this — you have to get in the Volvo and count the dials. It's visual, falsifiable, and nobody else can claim it.

**Corvette ad:** "They don't write songs about Volvos." Six words. The only brand that can say this — Post Malone and country singers write songs about Corvettes, not Volvos.

## The Writing Process

Start with: what am I trying to do? "The current attitude of the consumer is the starting point. The desired attitude is the finish line. You can't start a race in the middle." Picture two telephone poles — that's point A and point B. Your job is to string the wire between them.

### Piece 1: Know who you're talking to

Form follows function. If you don't know who you're writing for, you'll waste the message.

- **Snapchat** spent $7M on a Super Bowl ad saying "More Snapchat, less social media." Average Super Bowl viewer age: 39. Nobody at 39 is switching to Snapchat. They didn't think about who they were talking to.
- **Two cafes in St Ives.** Cafe one had a blown-up menu with 17 different drinks and prices. Cafe two: "Coffee and pastry = £5." Everyone queued at cafe two. Why? The audience is tourists walking to the beach, glancing out of the corner of their eye. They don't need the Instagram handle — they need to know there's coffee.

### Piece 2: Have something to say

You need to be building things for more than money. You need a belief, a vision, something you think is wrong with the world. That makes copy infinitely easier to write.

Marketing Examples exists because Harry was fed up trying to learn marketing from two pages of theory. He wanted examples. Write of Passage exists because there was no podcast about *how* people wrote, only about *what* they wrote. Find your "why this shouldn't exist the way it does" and write from there.

### Piece 3: Say it well

This is where craft lives. All the techniques below serve this piece.

## The One Mississippi Test

Show your ad to someone. Count: one Mississippi, two Mississippi. Did they get it? If it takes longer than two seconds, rework it. Sometimes you know more in 2 seconds than you know in 2 hours.

## Write Where Readers Read

Don't write copy in a Google Doc and then drag it into the real environment. Write directly in the medium where people will see it — ConvertKit for newsletters, Figma for ads. You need to see it as the reader sees it. A line that looks great in a doc might spill to three lines on mobile.

Ogilvy's version of this: if you're making a billboard, don't evaluate it alone in Photoshop. Pull it into a photo with seven other ads around it and people walking by. Does it still grab attention? Put things in reality.

## Facts, Facts, Facts

Lead with concrete, verifiable information. Facts ground your writing and provide credibility that adjectives never can.

**Tesla Cybertruck ad:** "Tougher than an F-150. Faster than a Porsche 911." They're instantly communicating what it is by showing what it isn't. Two comparisons. Two competitors. No adjectives. You believe it because it's falsifiable — either it's tougher or it isn't.

**The job of a sales page:** Make a bold claim at the top. Spend the rest of the page backing it up with a ridiculous amount of proof.

## First Line, Second Line

The only job of the first line is to get someone to read the second line. The only job of the second line is to get the third line read. Chain the reader forward, line by line.

## Kaplan's Law of Words

**Any word that isn't working for you is working against you.** There are no neutral words. Every word either earns its spot or drags the sentence down. A great sentence is a good sentence made shorter.

Extended to ideas: **The strength of an idea is inversely proportional to its scope.** Narrow beats broad. "Couch to 5K" beats "regain fitness." "1,000 songs in your pocket" beats "portable digital music player."

## The Burrito Principle

A good paragraph is like a burrito — you should be able to throw it and it shouldn't come apart in the air. Every sentence in the paragraph contributes to the whole. Remove one and the entire thing collapses, like a Jenga tower. If you can pull a sentence out and nothing changes, pull it out.

**Rule: keep paragraphs to two lines maximum.** Short paragraphs are scannable on mobile, where most people read. Harry hasn't written a paragraph longer than two lines since March 2021.

## Structure Is Wildly Underrated

How you organize ideas matters as much as the ideas themselves.

**For newsletters and long-form content:**
- Start with time, place, and context — ground the reader in what's happening right now
- Each section should build on the previous one while standing alone
- End with a line that creates forward momentum and intrigue
- Use parallelism to show relationships between different concepts

## Conflict Makes Copy Compelling

Every great piece of copy contains conflict — tension between opposing ideas or desires. Before/after. Problem/solution. What people think vs. what's actually true.

**The Economist ad:** "I've never read The Economist. — Management trainee, aged 42." The conflict is between "never read The Economist" and the implication that you should have. It tells a story in two lines.

**The diamond ad:** "How can you make two months' salary last forever?" The juxtaposition of "two months" (finite, small) and "forever" (infinite) shouldn't work, but the contrast creates desire.

**How to create conflict without using "but":** Draw a line down the middle of a page. Put opposing ideas on each side. Find the tension. That's your copy.

## Why AI Can't Replace Copywriting

AI can produce competent text, but it can't create genuine conflict or make taste-driven judgment calls. The critical gap is that AI doesn't have experience to draw from — it hasn't sat in St Ives choosing between two cafes, it hasn't watched Dave Kitson score no goals. Great copy comes from noticing things in the real world and pointing at them. AI is a tool that amplifies, but it cannot replace, human taste and judgment.

**Your standards are your work.** The quality of what you produce reflects the standards you hold yourself to. Develop taste by studying great ads, newsletters, and copy across industries. Collect examples. Analyze why they work. Surround yourself with excellence.

## Copy Creation Process

A step-by-step workflow for writing copy of any kind — ads, headlines, taglines, landing pages, emails, newsletters, sales pages. Designed so AI can iterate in a loop at each phase, using the evaluation rubric and personas below to judge quality before advancing.

**The rule: don't move to the next step until the current step clears a 4/5 average score.**

### Step 1: Define the Wire

Before writing a single word, define two telephone poles and the wire between them:

1. **Who you're talking to** — Be specific. A tourist glancing at a cafe sign? A 6-month user who hasn't upgraded? Someone skimming their inbox at 7am? The context determines everything. If Snapchat had asked this, they wouldn't have spent $7M advertising to 39-year-olds.
2. **Current attitude** — What does this person currently believe, feel, or do?
3. **Desired attitude** — What should they believe, feel, or do after reading your copy?

The gap between current and desired attitude is your job. You can't start a race in the middle.

**Iterate:** Write 3+ audience/attitude definitions. Score each for specificity and clarity. Only advance with one that scores 4/5+.

### Step 2: Core Message

Define the one thing you're saying. Not three things. One. Then pressure-test it against the three rules:

- **Can I visualize it?** If not, run the zoom-in method. Rewrite the abstract word until you land on a concrete object. "Regain fitness" → "Couch to 5K."
- **Can I falsify it?** If not, you're trailing adjectives. Point, don't talk. Replace "great investment" with the 50-year gold chart.
- **Can nobody else say this?** If your competitor could sign this sentence, cut it. Dig deeper into what's actually unique about what you're selling.

**The screenshot test:** If someone saw only the core message with zero context — no brand, no design — would they still understand and care? If not, the message isn't strong enough.

**Iterate:** Generate 5+ core message variations. Score each against the three rules. Only advance with one that gets three yeses.

### Step 3: Say It Well

Craft the actual copy. This is where technique lives. Apply every relevant principle:

- **Kaplan's Law** — Read every word. Is it working for you? If not, it's working against you. Cut it. A great sentence is a good sentence made shorter.
- **The Burrito Principle** — Every sentence in a paragraph must contribute to the whole. If you can remove a sentence and nothing changes, remove it.
- **Conflict** — Find the tension. Before/after. What people think vs. what's true. Finite vs. infinite. Draw a line down the middle of a page if you need to.
- **Metonymy** — Can you substitute a literal term for a more visual one? "Media player" → "pocket."
- **First line, second line** — Does each line pull the reader into the next? Chain them forward.
- **Two lines max per paragraph.** If it's longer, break it.

**Iterate:** Draft the copy. Score it. Rewrite until it clears 4/5. Read it aloud — if it doesn't flow when spoken, it won't flow when read.

### Step 4: The Reality Test

Put the copy in context. This is where most people skip and shouldn't.

- **One Mississippi test** — Show it cold. One Mississippi, two Mississippi. Did they get it? If not, rework.
- **Environment test** — Place the copy where it will actually live. If it's a billboard, pull it into a photo with seven other ads and people walking past. If it's an email subject line, put it in a fake inbox between 20 other subject lines. If it's a landing page headline, view it on mobile. Does it still grab attention when it's competing against everything else?
- **The outsider test** — Show it to someone who isn't the target audience. They don't need to "get it" — but did it get their attention? If you don't get it but you talked about it, the copy is working.

**Iterate:** Test in context. Score. Rework until 4/5.

### Step 5: Final Review

Score the complete copy against all 6 rubric dimensions from all 5 personas. Must clear 4/5 average across all personas to ship.

If it fails, identify the weakest dimension, loop back to the relevant step (weak message → Step 2, weak craft → Step 3, fails in context → Step 4), fix it, and re-evaluate.

## Evaluation Rubric

Score each dimension 1-5. Minimum 4/5 average across all dimensions to pass.

| Dimension | 1 (Fail) | 3 (Mediocre) | 5 (Ship it) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Visualization** | Abstract. Can't picture anything. | Some imagery but generic. | Concrete object in your mind instantly. |
| **Falsifiability** | Vague adjectives. "Great," "innovative." | Mostly specific but some hand-waving. | Every claim is pointable — true or false, no hiding. |
| **Uniqueness** | Any competitor could sign this. | Somewhat distinctive but replaceable. | Only this brand/product could say this. |
| **Instant clarity** | Need 10 seconds+ to parse. | Get it on second read. | One Mississippi, two Mississippi — got it. |
| **Word economy** | Bloated. Filler words and sentences. | Some fat, core is there. | Kaplan-clean. Remove a word and it breaks. |
| **Conflict/Tension** | Flat. No stakes or contrast. | Mild tension but predictable. | Genuine opposition that creates pull. |

## Evaluation Personas

AI evaluates each draft from 5 perspectives. Copy must hold up across all of them — not just one.

**The Scanner** — Glancing at their phone, walking past a billboard, skimming an inbox. Has zero patience and infinite alternatives. Gives you one Mississippi, maybe two. Asks: "Did I stop? Did I get it? Do I care?"

**The Skeptic** — Doesn't believe marketing claims. Has been burned by ads before. Immune to adjectives. Only responds to falsifiable proof. Asks: "Can I verify this? Is the writer putting their head on the chopping block, or hiding behind vague language?"

**The Creative Director** — Has reviewed 10,000 ads. Instantly spots derivative work, borrowed structures, and lazy shortcuts. Respects craft and originality. Asks: "Have I seen this before? Is this actually good, or just competent? Would I put this in my portfolio?"

**The Target Customer** — The actual human you defined in Step 1. In their specific context, with their specific current attitude. Asks: "Is this talking to me? Does this understand my situation? Do I want what's on the other side?"

**The Competitor** — Reads your copy and asks one question: "Could I have written this about my product?" If the answer is yes, the copy fails rule three. They're looking for the gap — the thing you can say that they can't.

## Iteration Loop

```
Brief / Objective
    ↓
Step 1: Define the Wire → Score → Below 4/5? → Rework → Re-score
    ↓ (4/5+)
Step 2: Core Message (Three Rules) → Score → Below 4/5? → Rework → Re-score
    ↓ (4/5+)
Step 3: Say It Well (Craft) → Score → Below 4/5? → Rework → Re-score
    ↓ (4/5+)
Step 4: Reality Test (Context) → Score → Below 4/5? → Rework → Re-score
    ↓ (4/5+)
Step 5: Final Review (all personas, all dimensions)
    ↓
    Pass (4/5+ avg) → Ship
    Fail → Identify weakest dimension → Loop back to relevant step
```

At each gate, output: the draft, scores for all 6 dimensions, which persona scored lowest and why, and what specifically to fix. Never advance with a vague "this is good enough."

## Classic Examples Worth Studying

| Ad / Line | Brand | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| "Worn by supermodels in London and dads in Ohio" | New Balance | Passes all three rules. Visual, true, nobody else can say it. |
| "1,000 songs in your pocket" | Apple iPod | Metonymy. Concrete beats technical. Sold millions. |
| "They don't write songs about Volvos" | Corvette | Six words. Enemy positioning. Only they can say it. |
| "I've never read The Economist. — Management trainee, aged 42" | The Economist | Conflict in two lines. No logo needed — red background is the brand. |
| "How can you make two months' salary last forever?" | De Beers | Juxtaposition of finite and infinite. Created diamond engagement ring demand. |
| "You're not you when you're hungry" | Snickers | Bestselling chocolate bar. Copy is the only differentiator. |
| "Tougher than an F-150. Faster than a Porsche 911." | Tesla | Facts as copy. Shows what it is by what it beats. |
| "The sports sedan for people who inherited brains instead of wealth" | Volvo | Makes the customer feel smart for buying the "uncool" car. |
| "Designed to be deleted" | Hinge | Counterintuitive. Only a dating app can say this. Memorable. |
